DEPARTMENT 86 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV13141    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: 86

MOTIONS (3) FOR APPROVAL OF

RECEIVER’S NINTH, TENTH, AND ELEVENTH INTERIM REQUESTS FOR FEES

 

Date:               9/12/24 (1:30 PM)

Case:                           Sound Equity High Income Debt Fund v. Top Flight Investments, et al. (21STCV13141)

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

 

I.                   MOTION FOR APPROVAL OF NINTH INTERIM REQUEST FOR FEES

 

Receiver Pacific Crest Realty Advisors, LLC’s UNOPPOSED Motion for Approval of Receiver’s Ninth Interim Request for Fees is GRANTED.

 

Receiver Pacific Crest Realty Advisors, LLC moves for approval of its interim fees incurred in the performance of its duties.

 

Pursuant to the Order Appointing Equity Receiver and Preliminary Injunction entered on 3/17/22, Receiver is authorized to prepare “periodic interim statements reflecting the Receiver’s fees and administration of the Receivership Estate.” (3/17/22 Receiver Order ¶ 11.) Receiver is obligated to submit its fees and expenses to the Court for approval and confirmation by way of a noticed interim request for fees, a stipulation among the parties, or the Receiver’s final account and report. (Ibid.)

 

Receiver seeks approval of $8,614.50 incurred in April 2024.

 

“Interim fees are subject to final review and approval by the court. The court retains jurisdiction to award a greater or lesser amount as the full, fair, and final value of the services rendered.” (Cal. Rule of Court 3.1183(a).) “Unless good cause is shown, objections to a receiver’s interim report and accounting must be made within 10 days of notice of the report and accounting, must be specific, and must be delivered to the receiver and all parties entitled to service of the interim report and accounting.” (Id. Rule 3.1183(b).) No objections were received.

 

A review of the invoice received in support of the motion reveals that Receiver paid vendor invoices; tended to escrow logistics, inspection issues, and completion of items in the buyer punch list for the property located at 3500 W Manchester Blvd #421, as well as worked with counsel with respect to litigation related to the property; tended to post-escrow activities for the property located at 68160 Calle Cerrito, including disconnection of utilities; and tended to removal of the property located at 816/818 N Mansfield Avenue from the housing stock with the Los Angeles Housing Department. (Constantinescu Decl. Ex. 1; 3/17/22 Receiver Order ¶ 3(b) [“The Receiver is granted the following powers and duties…[t]o operate, manage, maintain, preserve, market, and sell the Property, and to collect revenues, security deposits, storage fees, income, issues, and profits thereof….”].) Receiver billed $295.00 per hour for Sidney Constantinescu, $160.00 per hour for accounting tasks, and $50.00 per hour for administrative tasks, rates which are permitted in the March 17, 2022 order appointing Receiver. (3/17/22 Receiver Order ¶ 2(d).) Having reviewed the supporting declaration and invoice, the Court finds that the fees claimed by Receiver are reasonably related to the administration of the Receivership Estate.

 

The Court will sign the proposed order, electronically received 7/9/24.

 

II.                MOTION FOR APPROVAL OF TENTH INTERIM REQUEST FOR FEES

 

Receiver Pacific Crest Realty Advisors, LLC’s UNOPPOSED Motion for Approval of Receiver’s Tenth Interim Request for Fees is GRANTED.

 

Receiver seeks approval of $7,908.50 incurred in May 2024. No objections were received to this request.

III.     Respondents’ Motion to Dismiss and Petitioners’ Motion for CCP § 473(b) Relief from Late Filing

 

            Before the Court are two competing motions raising the same issues.  In the Motion to Dismiss for Failure to Comply with Public Resources Code Section 21167.4, respondents City of Los Angeles and Los Angeles City Council move for an order of dismissal of the first cause of action for Violations of the California Environmental Quality Act with prejudice. In the Motion for CCP § 473(b) Relief from Late Filing of Notice of Request for Hearing Pursuant to Public Resources Code § 21167.4, petitioners Jeb Kubrin and Marisa Kubrin, as co-Trustees of The Kubrin Living Trust, move for an order relieving petitioners from their failure to comply with Public Resources Code § 21167.4.

 

            As a preliminary matter, respondents’ requests for judicial notice are DENIED as “unnecessary to the resolution” of the issues before the Court. (Martinez v. San Diego County Credit Union (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 1048, 1075.)

 

            Section 21167.4(a) states: “In any action or proceeding alleging noncompliance with this division [where CEQA is codified], the petitioner shall request a hearing within 90 days from the date of filing the petition or shall be subject to dismissal on the court’s own motion or on the motion of any party interested in the action or proceeding.” “The meaning of the language used in section 21167.4, subdivision (a) is unambiguous. It requires superior courts to grant a motion to dismiss filed by an interested party when a CEQA petitioner has failed to file a request for hearing within 90 days from the date of filing the petition. Furthermore, dismissal is mandatory regardless of whether a request for hearing was filed before the motion to dismiss.” (Fiorentino v. City of Fresno (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 596, 605, footnote omitted.)

 

            Here, it is undisputed petitioners did not comply with section 21167.4. Petitioners did not file a request for a hearing with the Court until June 13, 2024, which was more than 90 days from November 17, 2023, the date of filing of the petition.[1] (See Munoz Decl. ¶ 11.) Accordingly, upon motion, the CEQA action is subject to mandatory dismissal.

 

The Court may grant relief from dismissal for failure to timely request a hearing date under Public Resources Code § 21167.4. (Comunidad en Accion v. Los Angeles City Council (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1131-32; McCormick v. Board of Supervisors (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 352, 362-63.) “The language of CEQA does not shield Public Resources Code section 21167.4 dismissals from the amelioratory provisions of section 473 of the Code of Civil Procedure.” (Miller v. City of Hermosa Beach (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 1118, 1135-37.)

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (CCP § 473(b).)

 

“‘A party who seeks relief under section 473 on the basis of mistake or inadvertence of counsel must demonstrate that such mistake, inadvertence, or general neglect was excusable because the negligence of the attorney is imputed to his client and may not be offered by the latter as a basis for relief.’ [Citation.] In determining whether the attorney's mistake or inadvertence was excusable, ‘the court inquires whether “a reasonably prudent person under the same or similar circumstances” might have made the same error.[’] [Citation.] In other words, the discretionary relief provision of section 473 only permits relief from attorney error ‘fairly imputable to the client, i.e., mistakes anyone could have made.’ [Citation.] ‘Conduct falling below the professional standard of care, such as failure to timely object or to properly advance an argument, is not therefore excusable. To hold otherwise would be to eliminate the express statutory requirement of excusability and effectively eviscerate the concept of attorney malpractice.’ [Citation.] [¶] The party seeking relief under section 473 must also be diligent. [Citation.] Thus, an application for relief must be made ‘within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.’ [Citation.] [¶] Where the mistake is excusable and the party seeking relief has been diligent, courts have often granted relief pursuant to the discretionary relief provision of section 473 if no prejudice to the opposing party will ensue. [Citations.]”

 

(Comunidad, 219 Cal.App.4th at 1132-33.)

 

            Petitioners’ counsel submits a declaration indicating that, after the Court entered an order to continue the trial setting conference and extend the time to certify the administrative record, he asked his assistant to update the firm’s calendar. (Slade Decl. ¶ 10.) After the dates were updated, the deadline to file a request for hearing under Public Resources Code § 21167.4 did not appear on the firm’s calendar. (Slade Decl. ¶ 10.) Petitioners’ counsel declares: “It is unclear now whether the deadline for filing the § 21167.4 notice was deleted from the calendar when the new dates were entered, or if it was accidentally never entered in the first place.” (Slade Decl. ¶ 10.) In subsequent communications concerning deadlines and scheduling, respondents’ counsel did not mention the § 21167.4 notice or a desire to expedite the CEQA hearing. (Slade Decl. ¶¶ 11, 13, 17.) However, respondents’ counsel indicated on April 2, 2024 during a CEQA status conference that respondents might file a motion to dismiss the CEQA cause of action. (Slade Decl. ¶ 15.) Petitioner’s counsel did not understand that respondents’ counsel was referring to the § 21167.4 notice requirement. (Slade Decl. ¶ 15.) On April 15, 2024, respondents served and filed an Answer indicating an affirmative defense based on petitioners’ failure to comply with section 21167.4. (Answer, First Affirmative Defense.) On April 25, 2024, during the continued status conference, counsel for respondents indicated that no decision had been made regarding whether a motion to dismiss would be filed. (Slade Decl. ¶ 16.) After respondents filed their Motion to Dismiss, petitioners filed their Motion for Relief and only then was the request for hearing made in an attachment. (Slate Decl. ¶¶ 19, 20 & Ex. F.)

 

            Here, the Court finds that petitioners is not entitled to relief from dismissal. While some calendar errors are excusable (see Nilsson v. City of Los Angeles (1967) 249 Cal.App.2d 976, 980), the purported calendar error invoked by petitioners in this case is not.  Petitioners fail to explain why, as of January 8, 2024—when the Court entered an order continuing the trial setting conference—petitioners had not already filed the request for hearing under Public Resources Code § 21167.4. The case was filed on November 17, 2023. Petitioners could have filed the request for hearing soon thereafter to avoid subjecting their CEQA case to mandatory dismissal. To the extent that petitioners were not ready to address the merits or were still waiting on the administrative record, this would not have prevented petitioners from filing the request for a hearing. The setting of the hearing would have occurred after the request for hear was made, and the Court could have considered the need to prepare the administrative record when setting the hearing. (County of Sacramento v. Superior Court (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 943, 951; Pub. Res. Code § 21167.4(c).)

 

            Under the circumstances, the Court questions whether petitioners’ counsel was even aware of the requirement to request a hearing within 90 days of filing the petition. Petitioners’ counsel does not aver to his practice of ensuring that the 90-day deadline is calendared (to the extent that waiting close to the 90 days is ever justified). Further, on April 2, 2024, when respondents’ counsel stated that respondents may file a motion to dismiss, petitioners’ counsel does not indicate what he understood respondents’ comment to the Court to mean. Petitioners’ counsel only states that it was not apparent to him that the City was referring to the §21167.4 notice requirement. (Slade Decl. ¶ 15.) Petitioners’ counsel does not appear to appreciate the importance of complying with Public Resources Code § 21167.4. A reasonable attorney should understand that the request for hearing must be filed or else a cause of action under CEQA is subject to mandatory dismissal. Petitioners’ failure to file the request for hearing within 90 days appears to be more than a mere mistake, but rather an unreasonable failure to understand the statutory requirement.  Indeed, it is notable that, during the 90-day period following the November 17, 2023 filing of the petition, numerous events occurred in this case to draw counsel’s attention, including: (1) the Notice of Trial Setting Conference sent from the Court to petitioners on November 27, 2023; (2) petitioners’ filing of proofs of service on December 4, 2023; (3) the Notice of CEQA Settlement Meeting filed and served on petitioner’s counsel on December 12, 2013; and (4) the Stipulation between petitioners and respondents filed on January 8, 2024.  Whether or not a calendar notation indicating the very last day to request a hearing was somehow omitted from counsel’s electronic calendar, there were many events that triggered counsel’s obligation to diligently ascertain the status of the case and learn no request for hearing had yet been made.

 

            For the foregoing reason, the Court finds that petitioners’ failure to file the request for hearing within 90 days of filing the Petition was not excusable neglect. Respondents’ Motion to Dismiss is GRANTED. Petitioners’ Motion for Relief is DENIED. The first cause of action for CEQA is DISMISSED.

 

IV.     Standard of Review

 

A.           First Cause of Action for Violations of CEQA[2]

 

In an action challenging an agency’s decision under CEQA, the trial court reviews the agency’s decision for a prejudicial abuse of discretion. (Pub. Res. Code § 21168.5.) “Abuse of discretion is established if the agency has not proceeded in a manner required by law or if the determination or decision is not supported by substantial evidence.” (Ibid.)

 

“[S]ubstantial evidence includes fact, a reasonable assumption predicated upon fact, or expert opinion supported by fact…. Substantial evidence is not argument, speculation, unsubstantiated opinion or narrative, evidence that is clearly inaccurate or erroneous, or evidence of social or economic impacts that do not contribute to, or are not caused by, physical impacts on the environment.” (Pub. Res. Code § 21080(e).) Under the substantial evidence test, the Court “review[s] the administrative record to see if it contains evidence of ponderable legal significance that is reasonable in nature, credible, and of solid value, to support the agency’s decision.” (Protect Tustin Ranch v. City of Tustin (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 951, 960.)  The Court does not weigh the evidence. (Ibid.)

 

When an appellant challenges “’the sufficiency of the evidence, all material evidence on the point must be set forth and not merely [its] own evidence.” (Toigo v. Town of Ross (1998) 70 Cal.App.4th 309, 317, quoting Jordan v. City of Santa Barbara (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1245, 1255.) The petitioner “must lay out the evidence favorable to the other side and show why it is lacking.” (Defend the Bay v. City of Irvine (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1261, 1266.) “Failure to do so is fatal. A reviewing court will not independently review the record to make up for appellant's failure to carry his burden.” (Ibid.)

 

B.           Second and Third Causes of Action Seeking Review of Approval of Conditional Use Permit

 

Under CCP § 1094.5(b), the pertinent issues are whether the respondent has proceeded without jurisdiction, whether there was a fair trial, and whether there was a prejudicial abuse of discretion. An abuse of discretion is established if the agency has not proceeded in the manner required by law, the decision is not supported by the findings, or the findings are not supported by the evidence. (CCP § 1094.5(b).)

 

“The issuance of a conditional use permit is a quasi-judicial administrative action, which the trial court reviews under administrative mandamus procedures pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1094.5…. [T]he trial court reviews the whole administrative record to determine whether the agency’s findings are supported by substantial evidence and whether the agency committed any errors of law. [Citations.]” (Neighbors in Support of Appropriate Land Use v. County of Tuolumne (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 997, 1005.)

 

In applying the substantial evidence test, “reasonable doubts must be resolved in favor of the decision of the agency.” (Breakzone Billiards v. City of Torrance (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1244.) The trial court “may reverse an agency's decision only if, based on the evidence before the agency, a reasonable person could not reach the conclusion reached by the agency.” (Ibid.) Substantial evidence is “relevant evidence that a reasonable mind might accept as adequate to support a conclusion” (California Youth Authority v. State Personnel Board (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 575, 584-85), or evidence of ponderable legal significance which is reasonable, credible and of solid value (Kuhn v. Department of General Services (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 1627, 1633).

 

“On questions of law arising in mandate proceedings, [the court] exercise[s] independent judgment.’” (Christensen v. Lightbourne (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1251.) The interpretation of statute or regulation is a question of law. (See State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Quackenbush (1999) 77 Cal.App.4th 65, 77.) However, the “City's interpretation of its own City Code ‘is entitled to deference’ in [the Court’s] independent review of the meaning or application of the law.” (City of Monterey v. Carrnshimba (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1091. “Under well-established law, an agency's view of the meaning and scope of its own ordinance is entitled to great weight unless it is clearly erroneous or unauthorized.” (Friends of Davis v. City of Davis (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1015.)

 

V.      Analysis of Writ Petition

           

A.           Request for Judicial Notice

 

Respondents’ request for judicial notice as to Exhibits B-G, sections of the Los Angeles Municipal Code, and Exhibit K, Section 15301 of Title 14 of the California Code of Regulations, effective October 6, 2005, to December 28, 2018, is GRANTED. (Evid. Code § 452(b).) 

 

            With respect to respondents’ Exhibits A and H-J, the requests are GRANTED. (Rodas v. Spiegel (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 513, 518 [records of administrative agencies judicially noticeable under Evid. Code § 452(c)]; Shapiro v. San Diego City Council (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 904, 907, fn. 2 [taking judicial notice of city council resolutions]; Evid. Code § 452(e) [judicial notice shall be taken of the “true signification of all English words and phrases].) Petitioners cite Western States Petroleum Assn. v. Superior Court (1995) 9 Cal.4th 559 for the assertion that extra-record evidence is not admissible. Western States, however, “addressed challenges to agency action as either unsupported by substantial evidence in the record, an abuse of discretion, or arbitrary and capricious.” (Hill RHF Housing Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2021) 12 Cal.5th 458, 489, citing Western States, 9 Cal.4th at 573, 574, 576.) Under such circumstances, the Supreme Court “concluded that to allow extrarecord evidence in support of such claims would be contrary to the deference associated with these standards of review.” (Hill RHF, 12 Cal.5th at 489.) “[E]xtra-record evidence can never be admitted merely to contradict the evidence the administrative agency relied on in making a quasi-legislative decision or to raise a question regarding the wisdom of that decision.” (Western States, 9 Cal.4th at 579.) “The interest in extending due deference to agency determinations that informed the analysis in Western States does not carry the same weight with regard to” claims where courts exercise independent judgment. (Hill RHF, 12 Cal.5th at 489.) Because the interpretation of “school” under the LAMC and how expansion of use under the Class 1 exemption under CEQA should be interpreted is a matter of the Court’s independent judgment, the Court takes judicial notice of Exhibits A and H-J.

 

B.           Request to Augment Record

 

With respect to Exhibits B (records from prior litigation) and C (real party’s past code violations),[3] the documents are not relevant to the definition of “school” under LAMC 12.14.U.24(c) or whether the Project is categorically exempt under CEQA. (CCP § 1094.5(e) [augmentation of record requires evidence to be relevant]; Mangini v. R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Co. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1057, 1063 [“Although a court may judicially notice a variety of matters (Evid. Code, § 450 et seq.), only relevant material may be noticed”].) Petitioners’ request to augment the record, or alternatively take judicial notice of Exhibits B and C, is DENIED. 

 

C.           Authorization of Conditional Use Permit Under Los Angeles Municipal Code

 

LAMC § 12.24.U[4] sets forth the uses and activities that may be permitted if approved by the CPC. (City RJN Ex. D at 31.) Section 12.24.U permits CUPs for schools as follows: “(a) Public schools, elementary and high (kindergarten through 12th grade)… (b) Private schools, elementary and high (kindergarten through 12th grade)… (c) Private schools [other than elementary or high (kindergarten through 12th grade) or nursery schools]….” (City RJN Ex. D at 37.)

 

Petitioners argue that the Project is not a “school” under § 12.24.U.24 because such a designation is reserved for academic, scholastic entities. While subdivisions (a) and (b) pertain to academic institutions, subdivision (c) is not so limited. Indeed, subdivision (c) allows for CUPs to be issued to private schools “other than elementary or high (kindergarten through 12th grade) or nursery schools.” The LAMC does not evidence any intent to limit the definition of school to academic institutions.  Indeed, the carve out in subdivision (c) for private schools that are not nursery schools, elementary schools, or high schools necessarily must refer to non-academic schools.[5]

 

While “school” is not defined in the LAMC, the Court refers to dictionary definitions to ascertain the word’s ordinary, usual meaning. (George v. Susanville Elementary School District (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 349, 356.) “School” is broadly defined to include an “organization that provides instruction” such as “an institution for the teaching of children” or “an establishment offering specialized instruction,” like a secretarial school or driving school. (City RJN Ex. I at 92.) It is undisputed that the Project teaches children how to swim, including reducing the risk of drowning and improving water safety skills. (AR 8.) The Project both teaches children and provides specialized instruction.

 

Petitioners argue that the City is using the “private school” categorization to evade home occupation regulation. Home occupation is defined as “[a]n occupation carried on by the occupant or occupants of a dwelling as a secondary use in connection with the main use of the property, subject to the regulations of Section 12.05 A.16” of the LAMC. (City Ex. B at 17 [§ 12.03]; see also City Ex. C at 21 [Conditions and Standards of Home Occupation in § 12.05.A.16].)

 

CUPs issued for “private schools” are still subject to the City’s regulation. No CUP shall be granted unless the City finds that “the project’s location, size, height, operations and other significant features will be compatible with and will not adversely affect or further degrade adjacent properties, the surrounding neighborhood, or the public health, welfare, and safety.” (City RJN Ex. D at 26 [§ 12.24.E.2].) Before issuing a CUP, the City is also required to find that “the project substantially conforms with the purpose, intent and provisions of the General Plan, the applicable community plan, and any applicable specific plan.” (City RJN Ex. D at 26 [§ 12.24.E.3].) In approving a project, the City may also impose conditions that relate to the interests set forth in the City’s findings. (§ 12.24.F.) Indeed, the City conditioned real party’s CUP on limiting class size to three students, operating from 8:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m. on Monday through Friday, and staffing class times so that traffic for drop-off and pick-up are not compounded. (AR 3, 8.)

 

Accordingly, petitioners’ hyperbolic assertion that the City could not have intended to allow any commercial operation to constitute a “school” misses the point. The City was entitled to prioritize school learning as a service that is “essential or beneficial” to the community and city (see City RJN Ex. D at [§ 12.24.E.1]) and accordingly authorize issuance of a CUP subject to appropriate standards in the City’s discretion. “Home occupations,” on the other hand, that do not involve teaching and instruction (e.g., accounting, hair styling, etc.) are subject to different conditions and standards than “private schools.”

 

For the foregoing reason, the plain meaning of “school” encompasses the Project. Petitioners fail to show that the Project cannot be a school under section 12.24.U.24(c).[6]

 

D.           Whether Project is Exempted Under CEQA

 

1.            Class 1 Exemption

 

Under CEQA, “[t]he lead agency has the burden to demonstrate that a project falls within a categorical exemption and the agency’s determination must be supported by substantial evidence.” (Citizens for Environmental Responsibility v. State ex rel. 14th Dist. Ag. Assn. (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 555, 568 [“CER”].) “‘[W]here the record contains evidence bearing on the question whether the project qualifies for the exemption … and the agency makes factual determinations as to whether the project fits within an exemption category … [judicial review is] whether the record contains substantial evidence to support the agency’s decision.”’ (Walters v. City of Redondo Beach (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 809, 817.) The Court “do[es] not weigh conflicting evidence, as that is the role of the public agency.” (Protect Tustin Ranch, 70 Cal.App.5th at 960.)

           

“Since a determination that a project falls within a categorical exemption excuses any further compliance with CEQA whatsoever, we must construe the exemptions narrowly in order to afford the fullest possible environmental protection.” (Save Our Carmel River v. Monterey Peninsula Water Management Dist. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 677, 697.)

 

Respondents contend that the Project qualifies for a Class 1 exemption to CEQA, codified in Title 14, Section 15301 of the California Code of Regulations (hereinafter, “Guidelines”).[7] “Class 1 consists of the operation, repair, maintenance, permitting, leasing, licensing, or minor alteration of existing public or private structures, facilities, mechanical equipment, or topographical features, involving negligible or no expansion of existing or former use.” (Guidelines § 15301.)

 

            The City justified the Class 1 exemption as follows: “The project consists of the continued use and operation of a private swim school at a private residence…. The private swim school will continue to operate towards the rear of the property at the existing swimming pool…. The continued use of an existing swim school at a private residence qualifies for a Class 1…Categorical Exemption. The use is an existing facility, and its operation occurs within the site’s swimming pool.” (AR 21-22.)

 

The foregoing constitutes substantial evidence supporting the categorical exemption. The dictionary defines “facility” as “something (such as a hospital) that is built, installed, or established to serve a particular purpose.” (City RJN Ex. J at 108.) A swimming pool is installed to facilitate swimming and therefore qualifies as a “facility.” The swimming pool also existed when the CUP application was sought. (AR 603, 1296.) The swimming pool therefore qualifies as an existing facility under the Class 1 exemption.

 

            The City also found that the CUP involved negligible or no expansion of existing or former use. The City found that the project “involves no physical construction/expansion of existing structures or swimming pools.” (AR 22.) The record contains substantial evidence supporting this conclusion. (AR 595 [testimony of City Planning stating “There’s been no expansion, no new construction, no demolition.”], 603 [no new construction proposed].)

 

            Petitioners argue that the swim school is not an existing use because it was enjoined from operating in 2018. (AR 1121-26.) However, the Class 1 exemption allows for consideration of a “former use” in determining whether a project involves negligible or no expansion of use. Before real party applied for a CUP on July 31, 2018, he had operated the swim school from 2011 until 2018. (AR 40, 1294-96.) The swim school thus qualifies as a former use.

 

            Petitioners also argue that the City should have compared the proposed swim school to its use as a single-family residence, because the CEQA analysis did not take place until after the initial public hearing on October 25, 2022 (AR 37-56) and because the prior use as a swim school was unpermitted. With respect to the proper basis for comparison, “[e]nvironmental conditions may vary from year to year and in some cases it is necessary to consider conditions over a range of time periods.” (Communities for a Better Environment v. South Coast Air Quality Management Dist. (2010) 48 Cal.4th 310, 327-28.) Indeed, the prior version of the Class 1 exemption required “negligible or no expansion of use beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination,” but the words “beyond that existing at the time of the lead agency’s determination” were deleted to reflect that under case law “a lead agency may look back to historic conditions to establish a baseline where existing conditions fluctuate.” (City RJN Ex. A at 14.) “Neither CEQA nor the CEQA Guidelines mandates a uniform, inflexible rule for determination of the existing conditions baseline. Rather, an agency enjoys the discretion to decide, in the first instance, exactly how the existing physical conditions without the project can most realistically be measured, subject to review, as with all CEQA factual determinations, for support by substantial evidence.” (Communities for a Better Environment v. South Coast Air Quality Management Dist. (2010) 48 Cal.4th at 327-28.) Based on the swim school’s operation for eight years, the City was entitled to consider such usage in determining that the CUP would not involve more than a negligible expansion of use.

 

            As for the fact that the swim school was unpermitted for eight years, CEQA baseline determinations do not depend on whether preexisting conditions may have been in violation of some statute or regulation. (Citizens for East Shore Parks v. State Lands Com. (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 549, 559 [“How present conditions come to exist may interest enforcement agencies, but that is irrelevant to CEQA baseline determinations—even if it means preexisting development will escape environmental review under CEQA”].

 

            For the foregoing reasons, substantial evidence supports the finding that the proposed swim school constituting the Project is a facility that involves negligible or no expansion of existing or former use.

 

2.            Unusual Circumstances Exception to Class 1 Exemption

 

“Once the agency establishes that the project is exempt, the burden shifts to the party challenging the exemption to show that the project is not exempt because it falls within one of the exceptions listed in Guidelines section 15300.2.” (Citizens for Environmental Responsibility v. State ex rel. 14th Dist. Ag. Assn. (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 555, 568.)

 

            Petitioners argue that there is a reasonable possibility that the Project will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances. “A categorical exemption shall not be used for an activity where there is a reasonable possibility that the activity will have a significant effect on the environment due to unusual circumstances.” (CEQA Guidelines § 15300.2(c).)

 

The unusual circumstances exception may be established in one of two ways. “A party invoking the exception may establish an unusual circumstance without evidence of an environmental effect, by showing that the project has some feature that distinguishes it from others in the exempt class, such as its size or location. In such a case, to render the exception applicable, the party need only show a reasonable possibility of a significant effect due to that unusual circumstance. Alternatively…a party may establish an unusual circumstance with evidence that the project will have a significant environmental effect.” (Berkeley Hillside Preservation v. City of Berkeley (2015) 60 Cal.4th 1086, 1105.)

 

            Petitioners do not show that the Project has some feature that distinguishes it from others in the Class 1 exemption. Accordingly, petitioners cannot avail themselves of the option to demonstrate “a reasonable possibility of a significant effect due to that unusual circumstance.” (Ibid.)

 

Petitioners argue that the City made no finding that the unusual circumstances exception was applicable, in which case the Court must assume that the City found that the Project involved unusual circumstances. (See Respect Life South San Francisco v. City of South San Francisco (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 449, 458.) No such assumption is necessary because the City did find that “there are no unusual circumstances that indicate this project would reasonably result in a significant effect on the environment.” (AR 23.) The City also found that the Project “will not generate significant impacts regarding traffic, air quality, water quality, or noise.” (AR 23.) The City notes that classes are limited to 3 students per class and 18 students per day spread out over 6 hours with no classes during the winter. (AR 23.) The City also notes that Environment Science Associates (“ESA”) recorded noise levels before and after swim class and found that class resulted in 2.5 dBA, which is barely perceptible to the human ear. (AR 23, 600.) Traffic concerns are addressed with a 15-minute buffer between classes to facilitate drop-off and instructions to inform the parents to park in real party’s driveway. (AR 44.) Neighbors have testified that the noise from the swim school was not disruptive and that parking is not a problem. (AR 617, 627, 688-90.) Substantial evidence supports the City’s finding that no unusual circumstances would reasonably result in a significant effect on the environment.

 

            Petitioners also argue that substantial evidence in the record supports a fair argument that the Project will have a significant impact on the environment due to the existence of unusual circumstances. While this standard may apply had the City failed to make any findings concerning unusual circumstances (Respect Life, 15 Cal.App.5th at 458), this is not the situation here. Petitioners must show substantial evidence that the Project will have a significant environmental impact. (Berkeley Hillside Preservation, 60 Cal.4th at 1105.)

 

            Petitioners argue that the City did not consider the fire hazard that 18 additional cars every weekday for 9 months of the year would pose to a narrow hillside street. However, this speculation is insufficient to demonstrate that any additional traffic from the swim school will result in a fire hazard. (Id. at 1104 [error to hold that “a potentially significant environmental effect itself constitutes an unusual circumstance”]; see also Walters v. City of Redondo Beach (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 809, 819-20.)

 

            Petitioners also maintain that the noise study was flawed because it was not conducted at real party’s property, among other reasons. However, the Court does not weigh the value of the study, as that was the role of the City in deciding whether to approve the Project. (Protect Tustin Ranch, 70 Cal.App.5th at 960.) In any event, even though noise samples were not recorded at the Project site, ESA took the samples during a swimming lesson given by real party and compared them to 50 hours of previously recorded swimming class footage taken at the Project site. (AR 25-26.) ESA’s staff determined that the recorded noise levels were representative of noise levels that occur during real party’s classes. (AR 26.) Even though some neighbors have complained about noise, “‘in the absence of specific factual foundation in the record, dire predictions by nonexperts regarding the consequences of a project do not constitute substantial evidence.’ [Citation.] ‘Unsubstantiated opinions, concerns, and suspicions about a project, though sincere and deeply felt, do not rise to the level of substantial evidence.’” (Banker's Hill, Hillcrest, Park West Community Preservation Group v. City of San Diego (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 249, 274.) Given that a noise study supports the finding that the Project will not have a significant impact on noise, in addition to other conditions imposed by the City to address noise, such as the prohibition against amplified music or loud non-amplified music (AR 4), subjective neighbor complaints do not constitute the type of substantial evidence that would demonstrate the Project will have a significant environmental impact.

 

            Petitioners fail to demonstrate that the “unusual circumstance” exception applies to the Class 1 exemption.

 

V.      Conclusion

 

The petition is DENIED. Pursuant to Local Rule 3.231(n), respondents City of Los Angeles and City Council of the City of Los Angeles shall prepare, serve, and ultimately file a proposed judgment.

III.     Analysis

 

“[T]he question whether a preliminary injunction should be granted involves two interrelated factors: (1) the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits, and (2) the relative balance of harms that is likely to result from the granting or denial of interim injunctive relief.”  (White v. Davis (2003) 30 Cal.4th 528, 554.)

 

A.           Evidentiary Matters

 

Sellers’ request to take judicial notice of the papers and pleadings in Park View Apt, LLC vs Seven Ark, LLC, et al., Case No. 18STCV03515 is DENIED as irrelevant. (Mangini v. R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Co. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1057, 1063 [“Although a court may judicially notice a variety of matters (Evid. Code, § 450 et seq.), only relevant material may be noticed”].)  Further, it is inadmissible character evidence. (See Evid. Code § 1101.)

 

Sellers’ requests to take judicial notice of Exhibits 1-3 attached to the declaration of Donald Rezak are GRANTED. (Evid. Code § 452(c); Pedus Building Services, Inc. v. Allen (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 152, 156, fn. 2.)

 

            Plaintiff’s objections to the supplemental declaration of Donald Rezak are OVERRULED.

 

B.           Standing

 

Sellers argue that plaintiff BSH has no standing to purchase the Property under the PSAs. For the purpose of BSH demonstrating a likelihood of success on the merits of its claims as the plaintiff, the Court disagrees.

 

With respect to assignability of the PSAs, BSH presents three “Assignment[s] of Commercial Real Estate Contract” whereby HSK assigned its rights under the PSAs to BSH. (Compl. ¶ 48 & Ex. F.) The PSAs provide that HSK may not assign its rights under the agreements “without the prior written consent of Seller….” (PSAs § 17.) It is undisputed that no such written consent was provided. However, the PSAs also provide: “Seller consents in advance to an assignment by Buyer to any entity controlling, controlled by, or under common control with Buyer.” (PSAs § 17.)

 

BSH’s managing member avers: “BSH is an entity under common control with the original buyer–HSK.  Specifically, the majority of the members of HSK and BSH overlap. HSK specifically created BSH for the purposes of acquiring the Property. Accordingly, HSK and BSH are under common control.” (Murillo Decl. ¶ 2; see also Supp. Murillo Decl. ¶ 2.) The truth of BSH’s assertion of common control may ultimately be determined in this litigation, but, for purposes of the instant motion, the Court finds the averments that HSK created BSH to acquire the property and that the majority of the members of HSK and BSH overlap sufficiently demonstrate a likelihood of success in establishing BSH’s standing as plaintiff.[2]

 

Further, regardless of whether BSH registered with the State of California after it verified the Complaint, it is undisputed that BSH is now in good standing and may continue to prosecute this action. (See Benton v. County of Napa (1991) 226 Cal.App.3d 1485, 1490 [corporation that was previously suspended may carry on litigation after receiving certificate of revivor, “even to the extent of validating otherwise invalid prior proceedings”].)

 

C.           Likelihood of Prevailing on Merits

 

The parties dispute the Closing Date under the PSAs. BSH maintains the Closing Date is October 28, 2024, 180 days from May 1, 2024, when HSK released the contingencies. (Compl. ¶ 47.) Sellers maintain the Closing Date is July 16, 2024, the date indicated in the Letter Agreement. (Rezak Decl. ¶ 22; 8/13/24 Nissani Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. B.)

 

The Court is persuaded by BSH’s interpretation. The PSAs define the “Closing Date” as “180 Days after the waiver of all Buyer’s Contingencies.” (PSAs, Section 10 of Summary and Definition of Basic Terms.) It is undisputed that HSK waived its contingencies on May 1, 2024, as memorialized in Amended Escrow Instructions. (Compl. ¶ 46 & Ex. E.) 180 days from May 1, 2024 is October 28, 2024. (Compl. ¶ 47.)

 

Sellers contend that the PSAs were amended by the Letter Agreement dated January 29, 2024. (Rezak Decl. ¶ 22; 8/13/24 Nissani Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. B.) With respect to amendments, the PSAs state: “This Agreement may not be modified, changed, supplemented or terminated, nor may any obligations hereunder be waived, except by written instrument signed by the party to be charged.” (PSAs § 18.8.) While the Letter Agreement may be a written instrument, the Court does not find that it modifies or changes the terms of the PSAs.   

 

The Letter Agreement in no way states that it is meant to amend the PSAs or identifies itself as a written instrument intended as such.  Indeed, the Letter Agreement describes July 16, 2024 as the “Close of Escrow” “[i]n accordance with your Agreement” and does not refer to July 16, 2024 as the “Closing Date.”  If the Letter Agreement were actually meant to amend a term in the PSAs—namely, the “Closing Date”—one might expect the Letter Agreement to have used the actual term it sought to amend.  Rather, the Letter Agreement simply sets forth the escrow company’s understanding (albeit erroneous) that the transaction was meant to close on July 16, 2024, as that date is 180 days after the January 18, 2024 effective date of the PSAs.  Indeed, the use of the phrase “Close of Escrow” would seem to indicate that the July 16, 2024 date was set forth from the perspective of the escrow company.  The Court thus finds that plaintiff is likely to prevail in its contention that the “Closing Date” is October 28, 2024 because the PSAs were not amended by the escrow company’s Letter Agreement.

 

            Further, “if a party to a contract expressly or by implication repudiates the contract before the time for his or her performance has arrived, an anticipatory breach is said to have occurred.” (Mammoth Lakes Land Acquisition, LLC v. Town of Mammoth Lakes (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 435, 467, quoting Romano v. Rockwell Internat., Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 479, 489.) Based on a Closing Date of October 28, 2024, Sellers’ insistence that HSK close the sale in accordance with a Closing Date of July 16, 2024 constitutes an anticipatory breach of the PSAs. Moreover, Sellers’ failure to pay off REIT’s lien and allow the Property to be subject to foreclosure appears to be an anticipatory breach of sections 4.2.1, 4.3.2, and 9.4 of the PSAs. Under section 16.1 and 16.5 of the PSAs, plaintiff demonstrates a likelihood that they are entitled to specific performance.

 

            REIT argues that it is a bona fide encumbrancer of the Property.  This argument fails.  To begin with, that doctrine has to do with lien priority. (Kahan v. City of Richmond (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 721, 740.)  Plaintiff’s right to purchase the Property is not a lien.  But even if one could logically compare these apples and oranges, the “bona fide encumbrancer” doctrine applies when the encumbrancer acquired a lien in good faith without knowledge or notice of a prior interest. (Ibid.) Here, HSK/BSH’s interest in the Property occurred upon execution of the PSAs in January 2024, which came after REIT’s recording of its Deed of Trust. (8/13/24 Beehler Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. B [Deed of Trust recorded on 12/30/21].) The bona fide encumbrancer doctrine is simply inapposite.

 

REIT also argues that BSH is equitably estopped from seeking to enforce the PSAs, as HSK had notice of default before it entered into the PSAs. (See Civ. Code § 3543 [“Where one of two innocent persons must suffer by the act of a third, he, by whose negligence it happened, must be the sufferer”].) However, the PSAs provided that Sellers would cause all liens to be removed. (PSAs § 4.2.1.) As part of due diligence, Sellers provided HSK with a title report that did not reflect any Notice of Sale. (Compl. ¶ 43.) Notably, the Notice of Sale was recorded on December 5, 2023 and HSK requested a preliminary title report on January 29, 2024, Sellers provided a title report from October 30, 2023, which pre-dated the recording of the Notice of Sale.  (See 8/13/21 Nissani Decl. ¶ 4 & Ex. C.)  Further, Sellers provided HSK with assurances that title would be clear before closing. (8/13/21 Nissani Decl. ¶ 5.) The Court thus finds BSH has sufficiently made out wrongdoing by Sellers to establish a likelihood of prevailing on the merits and overcoming any claim of estoppel.

 

            Lastly, Sellers argue that no injunction should issue against Rezak because the PSAs provide that there can be no personal liability for Sellers’ representatives or agents. Irrespective of whether Rezak may be found personally liable to BSH, it is undisputed that Sellers are acting through Rezak. (See Rezak Supp. Decl. ¶ 6.)   Further, the PSAs obligate Sellers to remove all liens and “not voluntarily alienate, lien, encumber or otherwise transfer all or any portion of the Property.”  (PSAs §§ 4.2.1, 9.4.) Accordingly, Rezak may be enjoined from assisting or acting in furtherance of foreclose on the Property.

 

            For the foregoing reasons, plaintiff demonstrates a likelihood that it is entitled to specific performance of the PSAs.

 

D.           Balance of Harms

 

The Court finds that the balance of harms weighs in favor of issuance of a preliminary injunction. Given the Property’s proximity to UCLA and plaintiff’s specific plans for use, the Property is unique. Damages do not constitute adequate relief. (Real Estate Analytics, LLC v. Vallas (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 463, 475, citing Civ. Code § 3387.)

 

            Further, given that PSAs provide for HSK/BSH to close by October 28, 2024, Sellers have not demonstrated any harm that would result from the delay of a foreclosure sale.

 

            Although REIT may have committed no wrongdoing in seeking to enforce its Deed of Trust, the Court finds that BSH is still entitled to a preliminary injunction.  The Court also finds that REIT’s interest in the repayment of its note are not compromised by the issuance of the injunction.  To begin with, BSH appears willing to fully pay REIT for the value of the note, including accrued interest. (9/6/24 Beehler Decl. ¶¶ 2, 3 & Ex. A, B.)  In any event, it is clear REIT would fully recover its principal and accrued interest from the approximately $30 million purchase price from the transaction upon closing, even accounting for the approximately $13 million encumbrance in a position superior to REIT’s deed of trust.

 

E.           Undertaking

 

The Court shall require an undertaking by plaintiff to account for any damage defendants “may sustain by reason of the injunction, if the court finally decides that the applicant was not entitled to the injunction.” (CCP § 529.) In this regard, the Court finds no support for the assertion that a bond of at least $7 million is required to protect REIT, as that grossly inflated figure accounts for the entirety of the principal and interest on REIT’s note.  Rather, the appropriate measure of harm is the pecuniary loss, if any, REIT would suffer by not obtaining the approximately $6.1 million due today and instead waiting until the end of this litigation to be fully paid on its note.  The Court will entertain arguments from the parties as to what amount of pecuniary loss, if any, that might be for purposes of an undertaking.

 

IV.     Conclusion

 

            The motion is GRANTED. Defendants FYJA, LLC, Le Conte Westwood Development, LLC, Evanston Road, LLC, Donald Rezak, and Freedom REIT are enjoining from proceeding on a foreclosure sale of the real property located at 10916-10928 Le Conte Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90024.

ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE

RE: PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

 

Date:               9/12/24 (1:30 PM) 

Case:                           Periodic Holdings v. Megan McMurray et al. (24STCV21046) 

  

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Periodic Holdings’ request for a preliminary injunction is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff seeks a preliminary injunction enjoining defendant Continental Stock Transfer & Trust (“Continental”) from canceling shares of WPFH in plaintiff’s possession and reissuing them in the names of defendants Megan McMurray and Chad Correia. (See Kasper Decl. ¶ 47; Richards Decl. ¶ 5; Hipple Decl. ¶ 16; Pls. Exs. 5, 10.) Plaintiff essentially argues that defendants McMurray and Correia misrepresented themselves and their ability to raise capital for plaintiff’s start-up company so that plaintiff would transfer its shares in WPFH as collateral for the investment. (Kasper Decl. ¶¶ 6-8, 43; Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 5.)  

 

“[T]he question whether a preliminary injunction should be granted involves two interrelated factors: (1) the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits, and (2) the relative balance of harms that is likely to result from the granting or denial of interim injunctive relief.”  (White v. Davis (2003) 30 Cal.4th 528, 554.) 

 

As a preliminary matter, on 8/23/24, the Court ordered plaintiff to serve all moving papers, any supplemental moving papers, the summons and complaint, and the order to show cause on defendants by 8/30/24. Defendant McMurray was served by substitute service on 8/28/24. However, no proof of service indicating that the supplemental brief filed on 8/30/24 was served on McMurray has been filed. Moreover, defendant Correia was untimely served on 9/4/24. No proof of service was filed as to defendant Continental. For the failure to serve and provide adequate notice alone, the requested preliminary injunction shall not issue.

 

Even considering all the papers filed, the Court finds no cause to issue the preliminary injunction. Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of prevailing on the merits. Plaintiff cites Corporations Code § 25401, which states: “It is unlawful for any person to offer or sell a security in this state, or to buy or offer to buy a security in this state, by means of any written or oral communication that includes an untrue statement of a material fact or omits to state a material fact necessary to make the statements made, in the light of the circumstances under which the statements were made, not misleading.” (See also Complaint ¶¶ 58, 59.) There is no evidence that defendants bought the WPFH shares. Plaintiff apparently transferred the shares to defendants McMurray and Correia not for any monetary compensation but based on an expectation that they would raise capital for his start-up. (Kasper Decl. ¶¶ 7-14.)

 

Plaintiff also alleges that it transferred the shares to McMurray and Correa due to defendant’s misrepresentations, deceit, and concealment. (Complaint, ¶ 55.) Plaintiff, however, fails to demonstrate justifiable reliance in order to successfully make out any fraud. (See Small v. Fritz Cos. Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 173 [element of fraud is justifiable reliance on misrepresentation].) “The reasonableness of the plaintiff's reliance is judged by reference to the plaintiff’s knowledge and experience.” (OCM Principal Opportunities Fund, L.P. v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 835, 864.)

 

Plaintiff’s principal avers that he was introduced to Correia by a mutual acquaintance named Randy, who he knew for two to two and a half years. (Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 4.) Randy described Correia as someone capable of raising funds and managing a family on behalf of his wife’s family. (Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 4.) Randy claimed to have known Correia for years and vouched for his experience in managing various brokerage accounts. (Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff later was introduced to McMurray, who he was led to believe was Correia’s wife. (Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 4.) Correia and McMurray represented to plaintiff’s principal that they could assist plaintiff in raising capital through their family office or other financial vehicles, such as stock loans, syndication, and direct introductions to investors. (Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendants never raised any capital for plaintiff. (Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 8.) Plaintiff’s principal later learned that Correia and McMurray are not married, that Correia misrepresented his name as “Chris,” and that Correia had been arrested for forgery and ID fraud. (Kasper Decl. ¶¶ 5, 31, 33, 34; Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶¶ 4, 9, 10.) McMurray is also purportedly facing criminal prosecution for ID fraud as well. (Supp. Kasper Decl. ¶ 10.) McMurray filed a complaint with the Securities and Exchange Commission, leading Continental to threaten to cancel plaintiff’s shares and issue them in the name of McMurray. (Pl. Ex. 10.)

 

Plaintiff appears to have relied on Randy’s recommendation of Correia, as well as Correia and McMurray’s usage of the language of investments in believing that they knew how to raise capital. (Supp. Brief at 11:1-3 [“Defendant Correia is knowledgeable about the finance and securities business and made statements to Plaintiff designed to have them reasonably believe progress on various opportunities was progressing”].) Plaintiff apparently did not investigate Correia and McMurray’s experience in raising start-up capital. As plaintiff envisioned raising $75 million and $125 million (Kasper Decl. ¶ 6), the Court does not find plaintiffs’ reliance to have been reasonable or justifiable under the circumstances, as plaintiff failed to conduct any reasonable inquiry into defendants’ background or claims.

 

Moreover, defendants purportedly required the capital to be “collateralized” by plaintiff’s shares in WPFH. (Kasper Decl. ¶ 5.) It is unclear how such collateralization would work, including under which circumstances and/or how the shares would be used as collateral or when, if ever, the shares would be released to plaintiff. The insufficient—if not nonsensical—description of the terms of plaintiff’s agreement to use the shares as collateral also renders this Court unable to find that the transfer of the shares to defendants was justifiable under the circumstances.

 

Plaintiff does not discuss any other causes of action on which it is likely to prevail. For the foregoing reasons, plaintiff has not demonstrated that it is likely to prevail on its claims.

 

Having failed to show a likelihood of prevailing on any cause of action, plaintiff’s request for a preliminary injunction is DENIED, notwithstanding any puported harm that may result from the denial of plaintiff’s request. (Jessen v. Keystone Savings & Loan Assn. (1983) 142 Cal.App.3d 454, 459 [“In a practical sense it is appropriate to deny an injunction where there is no showing of reasonable probability of success, even though the foreclosure will create irreparable harm, because there is no justification in delaying that harm where, although irreparable, it is also inevitable”].)

 

Plaintiff’s request for a preliminary injunction is DENIED. The Court’s Temporary Restraining Order and Order to Show Cause re: Preliminary Injunction issued on 8/23/24 are accordingly dissolved and discharged, respectively.
