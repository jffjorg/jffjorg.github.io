DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court may change tentative rulings at any time. Therefore, attorneys are advised to check this website to determine if any changes or updates have been made to the tentative ruling.

Counsel may submit on the tentative rulings by calling the clerk in Dept. O at 909-802-1126 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. Submission on the tentative does not bind the court to adopt the tentative ruling at the hearing should the opposing party appear and convince the court of further modification during oral argument.

The Tentative Ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such filing will be considered by the Court in the absence of permission first obtained following ex-parte application therefore.



Case Number: 21STCV10352    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

(1)   DEFENDANT SLX PROPERTIES, LLC’S MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFF’S THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT (ERRONEOUSLY TITLED SECOND VERIFIED COMPLAINT); AND REQUEST FOR DISMISSAL WITH PREJUDICE is GRANTED.

(2)   DEFENDANT THE VONS COMPANIES, INC.’S MOTION TO DISMISS THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT (ERRONEOUSLY TITLED SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT); AND REQUEST FOR DISMISSAL WITH PREJUDICE; MEMORANDUM OF POINTS AND AUTHORIES is GRANTED.[1]

 

KCAL’s joinder is also granted; The court requests KCAL to file a proposed order. The case is dismissed WITH prejudice.

 

Background

 

This case arises from a slip and fall case.

 

On March 17, 2021, Plaintiff Joseph Manuel Macias filed suit against Defendants Albertsons Companies, Inc.; KCAL P&C Insurance (“KCAL”); and Services SLX Property, LLC (“SLX”) for negligence and premises liability from injuries arising from an August 22, 2019 accident.

 

On April 21, 2021, Plaintiff filed his first amended complaint (FAC) for (1) general negligence and (2) premises liability (wherein he slipped water that was draining from the building pipes located/near the store).[2]

 

On April 23, 2021, Plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint naming the Vons Companies, Inc. as Doe 1.

 

On May 6, 2021, Plaintiff dismissed Albertsons Companies, Inc.

 

On May 17, 2021, Defendant KCAL filed its answer and that same day filed a cross-complaint (CC) against SLX for EXPRESS INDEMNITY; EQUITABLE INDEMNITY; CONTRIBUTION; BREACH OF IMPLIED WARRANTY AND DECLARATORY RELIEF.

 

On June 7, 2021, Vons filed its answer to the FAC and that same day filed a CC against KCAL, SLX, Jose Percy Sam; and Yuet Ngan Yuen De Sam for equitable indemnity.

 

On July 29, 2021, KCAL filed its answer to Vons’ CC.

 

On August 30, 2021, SLX filed its answer to Plaintiffs’ FAC.

 

On October 29, 2021, SLX filed its answer to Vons’ CC and that same day filed a CC against KCAL for (1) CONTRIBUTION (2) EQUITABLE INDEMNITY (3) IMPLIED INDEMNITY (4) EXPRESS INDEMITY.

 

On February 3, 2022, SLX filed its answer to KCAL’s CC.

 

On December 6, 2023, SLX filed an MSJ (ISSUE 1 – KCAL has a duty to defend SLX against the claims of Plaintiff as alleged in the 2nd COA for Express Indemnity against KCAL in SLX's Cross-Complaint; MSJ p. 2). (The hearing is set for 12/10/2024.)

 

On January 2, 2024,[3] Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (SAC)[4] wherein, seemingly dismissing his negligence based COAs, asserts the following two COAs:[5]

 

1.     Unruh Civil Rights Act

2.     Violations of the Disabled Persons Act (Civ. Code, § 54.1 et seq).[6]

 

On March 27, 2024, Vons filed the instant DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT x MTS. That same day, SLX filed a demurrer x MTS to the SAC.

Legal Standard

 

Defendants move to strike the proposed amended complaint pursuant to Section 436(b) and California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1320(i) and to dismiss the action under Section 581(f)(2).

 

Under section 436: “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435 [allowing motions to strike], or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: [¶] ... [¶] (b) Strike out all ... of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with ... an order of the court.” (§ 436; see also Richard v. Grobstein, Goldman, Stevenson, Siegel, LeVine & Mangel (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 157, 162.) A pleading filed in violation of prior court order/ruling may be stricken. (Leader v. Health Industries of American, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 613; Morales v. Camello (1970) 12 Cal.App.3d 370 372.)

 

When such a belated amended pleading is made, CRC Rule 3.1320(i), entitled ‘Motion to strike late-filed amended pleading,’ mandates the relief must be obtained by noticed motion. In this scenario, a trial court may strike the amendment as untimely and thereafter entertain the motion to dismiss. (Gitmed v. General Motors Corp. (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 824, 827-829.)

 

In turn, under section 581, subdivision (f)(2), the court may dismiss the complaint when “after a demurrer to the complaint is sustained with leave to amend, the plaintiff fails to amend it within the time allowed by the court and either party moves for dismissal.” (§ 581, subd. (f)(2).)  “The phrase ‘may dismiss’ means discretionary dismissal.” (Cano v. Glover (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 326, 329 (Cano).) However, if a court exercises discretion to dismiss, it must do so with prejudice. (Id. at pp. 329–330.)

 

Discussion[8]

 

The parties move to strike the amended complaint and dismiss the case on the following grounds:

 

1.     Plaintiff failed to file an amended complaint within the time period ordered by the Court on June 24, 2024 but did not file it until five weeks later, on August 21, 2024.

2.     Plaintiff failed to request permission from the Court to file his amended complaint beyond the date ordered by the Court.

3.     Plaintiff’s amended complaint (TAC) failed to comply with the Court’s June 24, 2024 Order that Plaintiff was permitted to re-allege the premises liability and negligence causes of action as set forth in his original complaint and First Amended Complaint. Instead, Plaintiff alleged new facts which he had pled in support of his previously filed Second Amended Complaint which the Court had stricken.

 

For reasons to be discussed below—namely that the lack of adherence to certain rules and deadlines besets the action—the court strikes the proposed amended complaint and subsequently dismisses the action.

 

On June 24, 2024, the court issued a nine-page ruling. The court carefully distinguished the FAC from the SAC and the court provided a thorough discussion of the relevant authority. The tentative ruling provided, in relevant part, the following:

 

[T]he SAC alleges that Plaintiff’s injuries were (largely) caused by ARCHITECTURAL DEFECTS/BARRIERS (i.e., INSTRUMENTALITY) concerning the patio conversion and uneven surface in the parking lot. Accordingly, while the complaints may all reference a shopping cart and a water leakage, the slope is an entirely NEW INSTRUMENTALITY, unrelated to the water alleged in the FAC. There are no allegations in Plaintiff’s original Complaint/FAC regarding any sloping or defect in the pavement of the disabled parking area or sidewalk where Plaintiff fell which would provide any notice to Defendant of potential violations of the Unruh Civil Rights Act, Disabled Persons Act, or any ADA violations. (See 6/24/24 Ruling, emphasis original.)

 

Accordingly, even though the “Unruh Civil Rights Act and Disabled Persons Act are time-barred and thus may not be asserted,” in the interest of justice and fairness, the court allowed for “Plaintiff to amend his complaint to re-allege his previous causes of action for negligence and premises liability as set forth in his original complaint and FAC. According to the minute order, an amended complaint was “due on or before July 15, 2024.” (See 6/24/24 Minute Order.) However, by July 15, 2024, Plaintiff did not file an amended complaint nor did Plaintiff heed to the prescribed scope of leave to amend.

 

A.     Indifference to Deadlines/Diligence

 

Plaintiff filed an amended complaint on 8/21/24, not by 7/15/24. Plaintiff offers no explanation in his oppositions as to the untimely filings and made no noticed motion for permission to file an amended complaint. (Leader v. Health Industries of American, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 612-613.) Such indifference to deadlines is not only exhibited in this motion, but in other parts of the litigation.  

 

First, in opposition to the demurrers and motions to strike to the SAC, Plaintiff asked the court to provide a “flexible approach to statutory deadlines.” (6/24/24 Ruling p. 7 of 9 of PDF.)

 

Second, this action was filed in March of 2021, over three and a half years ago. It is Plaintiff’s obligation to timely a prosecute a case, but the case is still at the pleading stage.

 

Third, the basis for the demurrers and motions to strike the SAC was based upon lack of due diligence/timeliness: the injury happened on 8/22/2019 such that any Unruh or ADA violations must have been asserted by 2/22/22, but they were instead filed on 1/2/24.

 

All in all, Plaintiff has not adhered to deadlines/scheduling in this case.

 

B.     Scope of Amendment

 

In his amended complaint, Plaintiff did not revert to the original basis of the FAC predicated upon a slippery surface caused by a pipe leak, but again referenced architectural barriers. (See e.g., Amended Complaint ¶16 [“The plaintiff fell and was injured while traversing the converted handicap parking stalls, which were not built pursuant to the pertinent commercial building codes.”].) In fact, in further circumvention of the court’s order wherein, when read in totality, the amended complaint not only keeps allegations about architectural barriers, but those alleged defects have also now become the proximate causes of the negligence and premises liability COAs. (See e.g., ¶17 [“DEFENDANTS failed to alter, convert, remodel, and renovate the former patio area in compliance with proper standards and/or violations. This failure was the direct and proximate cause of Plaintiff’s injuries.”]; ¶25 [“As a direct and proximate cause of the Defendants’ violation of the applicable building and safety codes, as alleged above, Plaintiff suffered bodily injury when using and traversing Defendants’ non-compliant reconverted patio, as well as difficulty, discomfort, and embarrassment.”].)

 

The court’s ruling did not merely state “leave to amend is granted”; it narrowed the scope of leave to amend.[9]

 

To the extent that Plaintiff argues that the court’s ruling never said anything about factual allegations but about the COAs, that shows a misunderstanding of the relation back doctrine. The relation back doctrine—which was the fundamental issue in the 6/24/24—requires an assessment of the facts. So, when the court ruled that Plaintiff can only plead the negligence and premises liability COAs, that inherently denotes that only facts previously asserted can be pled in the amended complaint. For Plaintiff to “interpret” otherwise is disingenuous.

 

Moreover, “[i]t is an elementary principle of modern pleading that the nature and character of a pleading is to be determined from its allegations, regardless of what it may be called….” (Jaffe v. Carroll (1973) 35 Cal.App.3d 53, 57.) Thus, regardless of what Plaintiff labels his causes of action, the nature and character of the proposed amended complaint focuses on alleged Unruh Civil Rights Act and Disabled Person Act violations.

 

Lastly, three of the parties understood the court’s scope of leave to amend, as evidenced by the motions and KCAL’s joinder.

 

C.    Motion to Strike

 

Based thereon—the lack of adherence to deadlines in the case and exceeding the scope of leave to amend—the court grants Defendants’ Motion to Strike the proposed amended complaint. (See Leader, supra, 89 Cal.App.4th at p. 613 [“Indeed, “by virtue of its inherent power to prevent abuse of its processes,” a trial court may strike an amended complaint “filed in disregard of established procedural processes,” and may strike an amended pleading “because no request for permission to amend was sought.”].)

 

D. Request for Dismissal

 

Based thereon-- the lack of adherence to deadlines in the case and exceeding the scope of leave to amend—the court grants Defendants’ request to dismiss the entire complaint as to Defendants.  

 

To the extent that Plaintiff is arguing a separate motion must be filed, that concern was addressed by the Leader court.

 

Plaintiffs' reliance on Gitmed v. General Motors Corp., supra, 26 Cal.App.4th 824, 31 Cal.Rptr.2d 625 (Gitmed) for this proposition is misplaced … Here, recognizing that the court could allow the amended complaint to be filed, defendants moved both to strike the pleading and to dismiss the action. The court expressly granted the motion to strike, then ordered the action dismissed. This satisfies the procedural concerns of Gitmed. No reason appears in this case to require that additional time and expense be incurred in filing a separate motion to dismiss after the court has ordered the amended complaint stricken. (Id. at p. 614.)

 

Similarly, the court has first ruled on the motion to strike (supra) and subsequently ruled on the request for dismissal.

 

To the extent that Plaintiff argues that the meet and confer effort pertained to the motion to strike and that Defendants “escalated to seeking dismissal” (Opp. p. 8:20-22), the court’s review of the meet and confer correspondence does not support such a characterization. (See e.g., SLX Motion, Exs. 7-8, starting p. 64 of 77 of PDF.) In an email from Plaintiff’s Counsel Ibarra to Defense Counsel Kim on 9/14/24, Counsel Ibarra states that he finds the “request for dismissal unwarranted and kind of offensive”—showing that the intentions of dismissing of the action were made known to Plaintiff. Moreover, even assuming the parties could have engaged in more meet and confer efforts, that would be futile as Counsel Ibarra’s vehemently maintains that Defendants misinterpret the court’s ruling.

 

Conclusion

 

In sum, the proposed amendment complaint is not one that merely seeks to add additional facts to make the complaint “better” or to provide a “complete statement of the case.” (SLX Opp. p. 9:16-18, quoting La Jolla Village Homeowners’ Assn. v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1131, 1143.)[10] Instead, Plaintiff is keeping the same facts and relying on those facts as the operative facts to serve as the basis for its action despite a clear court order saying that Unruh and ADA-based COAs—and the respective facts—are time barred. While Plaintiff argues that precluding such facts would “undermine the fundamental principles of fairness and justice,” (SLX Opp. p. 9:23-27), as stated before, fairness is not one sided: Defendants are also entitled to timely notice of the claims asserted against them.

 

Based on the foregoing, the motions to strike the proposed amended complaint are granted and, consequently, the Plaintiff’s complaint against the three defendants are dismissed.
