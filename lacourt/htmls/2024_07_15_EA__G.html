Case Number: 22PSCV00060    Hearing Date: July 15, 2024    Dept: G

Plaintiff Sharine Forbes’s Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Sharine Forbes’s Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

BACKGROUND

This is a wrongful termination action. In November 2020, Plaintiff Sharine Forbes reached out to Defendant Shehzad Ahmed, the chief officer and administrator for Defendant Pomona Valley Home Care, Inc. (PVHC), and inquired about employment in the healthcare industry. After discussing a potential position with Forbes, Ahmed reached out to Forbes on December 7, 2020, and asked whether Forbes had thought about the position and salary offered. On December 8, 2020, Forbes accepted Ahmed’s offer and started in the new position on January 22, 2021.

On February 18, 2021, Forbes decided to resign from employment with PVHC but was convinced by PVHC’s employees to stay. A few days later, Forbes’s employment was terminated by PVHC’s management after Forbes had a disagreement with a fellow employee. Forbes was subsequently rehired and a contract memorializing the terms of Forbes’s employment was executed on March 1, 2021. On April 1, 2021, Forbes’s employment was terminated after allegedly informing Ahmed that PVHC had been persistently violating HIPAA laws.

On January 19, 2022, Forbes filed a complaint against PVHC and Does 1-10, alleging the following causes of action: (1) breach of employment contract, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) wrongful termination in violation of public policy, (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (5) false promise, and (6) intentional misrepresentation.

On October 17, 2022, Forbes amended the Complaint to replace Doe 1 with Ahmed.

On April 10, 2203, PVHC filed a notice of bankruptcy filing and entry of automatic stay.

On September 7, 2023, Forbes dismissed the first, second and third causes of action against Ahmed.

On September 20, 2023, Forbes filed a notice of settlement.

On April 10, 2023, Forbes dismissed Ahmed from the present action.

On June 20, 2024, Forbes filed the present motion. A hearing on the motion is set for July 15, 2024, with a status conference re: bankruptcy set for July 23, 2024.

ANALYSIS

Forbes seeks to have judgment entered against Ahmed pursuant to the parties’ settlement agreement. For the following reasons, the court DENIES the motion.

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 provides a summary procedure that enables courts to enforce a settlement agreement by entering a judgment pursuant to the terms of the parties’ settlement. In relevant part, it provides as follows:

If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).)

A writing is considered signed if signed by the party or an attorney who represents the party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (b).)

Discussion

In this case, Forbes provides a copy of a written settlement agreement signed by Forbes and Ahmed in September 2023 along with an amendment signed in April 2024. (Alaric-Lorenzo Decl., Ex. A.) According to the agreement, Ahmed agreed to pay Forbes a total of $150,000 in monthly payments of at least $10,000 that commenced on October 25, 2023. (Alaric-Lorenzo Decl., Ex. A, § 2(a).) On May 30, 2024, Forbes’s counsel received an email from the United States Trustee that demanded Forbes repay $80,000 that Forbes received from PVHC on Ahmed’s behalf. (Alaric-Lorenzo Decl., ¶ 7.) On June 19, 2024, PVHC filed an adversarial proceeding in bankruptcy court against Forbes and Forbes’s counsel, seeking reimbursement of the $80,000. (Alaric-Lorenzo Decl., ¶ 8.)

Forbes now argues Ahmed breached the settlement agreement by making payments with funds from PVHC. But Forbes fails to establish damages as the declaration of Forbes’s counsel does not state (1) if Forbes has actually repaid the funds from PVHC or (2) if the bankruptcy court has ordered Forbes to repay the funds in PVHC’s bankruptcy proceedings. While Forbes may be entitled to enforce the settlement agreement if the funds already paid are lost, Forbes has yet to establish that has occurred.

Accordingly, Forbes’s motion is DENIED without prejudice.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Forbes’s motion to enforce the settlement agreement between Forbes and Ahmed.

Case Number: 23PSCV02093    Hearing Date: July 15, 2024    Dept: G

Defendants HLP ED Foundation, SVG Training Foundation, Inc., and San Gabriel Career Foundation, Inc.’s Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint

Defendants HLP ED Foundation, SVG Training Foundation, Inc., and San Gabriel Career Foundation, Inc.’s Motion to Strike Portions of the Second Amended Complaint

Defendant Cynthia Parulan-Colfer’s Motion to Strike Portions of Second Amended Complaint is GRANTED IN PART as to the Prayer for Attorney Fees and DENIED IN PART on all other grounds.

 

Plaintiffs Anthony Duarte and Hacienda La Puente Unified School District’s Petition for Order Granting Retroactive Leave to Assert New Causes of Action for Negligence and Aiding and Abetting Breach of Charitable Trust in Second Amended Complaint is DEEMED MOOT.

 

Defendants Parulan-Colfer, HLP ED Foundation, SVG Training Foundation, Inc. and San Gabriel Career Foundation, Inc. are ordered to file their Answer, as applicable, to the SAC’s remaining causes of action for Fraud (4th Cause of Action), Common Counts (7th Cause of Action), Unfair Business Practices (8th Cause of Action), Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage (9th Cause of Action ), and Declaratory Relief (10th Case Of Action) in ten (10) days.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an action arising from alleged misappropriation of non-profit funds. Defendant HLP ED Foundation (HLP ED) was a non-profit public benefit corporation initially founded in August 2008. Its alleged purpose was to provide scholarships for the students of Hacienda La Puente Unified School District (HLPUSD) and fund special projects that enhanced students’ academic and co-curricular activities. Since HLP ED’s founding, Defendant Cynthia Parulan-Colfer served as a director. In 2016 and 2018, Parulan-Colfer and another HLP ED board member allegedly used HLP ED’s funds to provide scholarships for Parulan-Colfer’s children outside the normal scholarship review process. In 2019, HLP ED’s funds were allegedly used for Parulan-Colfer’s retirement party. And in July 2020, HLP ED’s funds were allegedly used to pay Parulan-Colfer’s husband’s law firm for legal services.

 

In September 2020, HLP ED was dissolved and Parulan-Colfer allegedly transferred its remaining funds to other non-profits founded by Parulan-Colfer including Defendants SVG Training Foundation, Inc. (SVG Training Foundation) and San Gabriel Career Foundation, Inc. (San Gabriel Career Foundation). As a result, at least one of HLP ED’s scholarship recipients was unable to cash a scholarship check.

 

On July 12, 2023, Anthony Duarte and HLPUSD filed a complaint against Parulan-Colfer, HLP ED, SVG Training Foundation, San Gabriel Career Foundation, and Does 1-50, alleging the following causes of action: (1) breach of fiduciary obligations and duties, (2) breach of contract, (3) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (4) fraud, (5) conversion, (6) breach of charitable trust, (7) money had and received, (8) unfair business practices, (9) intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, (10) declaratory relief, and (11) accounting.

 

On October 3, 2023, Duarte and HLPUSD filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against the same defendants alleging the same causes of action with an additional twelfth cause of action for aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duties. On January 24, 2024, the Court sustained demurrers to the FAC by HLP ED, SVG Training Foundation, San Gabriel Career Foundation, and Parulan-Colfer in part with leave to amend.

 

On March 22, 2024, Duarte and HLPUSD filed a Second Amended Complaint (SAC) against the same defendants alleging the same causes of action with an additional thirteenth cause of action for aiding and abetting breach of charitable trust and a fourteenth cause of action for negligence. They also named Rob Bonta, the Attorney General of the State California, as an indispensable party.

 

On April 26, 2024, HLP ED, SVG Training Foundation, and San Gabriel Career Foundation (collectively, Entity Defendants) filed their demurrer. On the same day, Parulan-Colfer filed a demurrer and motion to strike while the Entity Defendants filed a notice of joinder to Parulan-Colfer’s demurrer and motion to strike. On April 29, 2024, the Entity Defendants filed their motion to strike. Prior to filing on April 8, 2024, counsel for Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants telephonically met and conferred with counsel for Duarte and HLPUSD and were unable to reach a resolution. (Brethen Decl., ¶ 2.)

 

A hearing on the present motions is set for July 15, 2024, along with a case management conference and Duarte and HLPUSD’s petition for retroactive leave to amend.

 

DEMURRERS


Parulan-Colfer demurs to Duarte’s first cause of action (breach of fiduciary obligations and duties), HLPUSD’s fifth cause of action (conversion), HLPUSD’s sixth cause of action (breach of charitable trust), Duarte and HLPUSD’s eleventh cause of action (accounting), HLPUSD’s thirteenth cause of action (aiding and abetting breach of charitable trust), and Duarte and HLPUSD’s fourteenth cause of action (negligence). In addition to the causes of action demurred to by Parulan-Colfer, the Entity Defendants also demur to HLPUSD’s second cause of action (breach of contract), HLPUSD’s third cause of action (breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing), and Duarte and HLPUSD’s twelfth cause of action (aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duties).

 

For the following reasons, the court SUSTAINS their demurrers in their entirety without leave to amend.

 

Legal Standard


A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).) When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, at p. 747.)

 

Breach of Fiduciary Duty (First Cause of Action)

 

Parulan-Colfer argues Duarte’s first cause of action for breach of fiduciary obligations and duties fails as a matter of law because Parulan-Colfer did not owe any fiduciary duty to Duarte. HLP ED also argues this cause of action fails as a matter of law because HLP ED did not owe any fiduciary duty to Duarte and HLPUSD. The court agrees.

 

Legal Standard

 

The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.)

 

Discussion

 

The SAC alleges HLP ED owed fiduciary duties to HLPUSD, Duarte, and student beneficiaries from soliciting and accepting charitable contributions. (FAC, ¶ 65-66.) The SAC also alleges Parulan-Colfer owed similar fiduciary duties by acting as HLP ED’s agent. (FAC, ¶ 66.) Parulan-Colfer argues Duarte lacks standing to assert breach of fiduciary duty claims because Duarte does not have standing to enforce a charitable trust. (Parulan-Colfer Demurrer, p. 2:13-3:17.) HLP ED makes the same argument with regard to both Duarte and HLPUSD. (Entity Def. Demurrer, p. 5:10-19.)

 

Business and Professions Code section 17510.8 recognizes the existence of “a fiduciary relationship between a charity or any person soliciting on behalf of a charity, and the person from whom a charitable contribution is being solicited.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17510.8.) It also recognizes that “[t]he acceptance of charitable contributions by a charity or any person soliciting on behalf of a charity establishes a charitable trust and a duty on the part of the charity and the person soliciting on behalf of the charity to use those charitable contributions for the declared charitable purposes for which they are sought.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17510.8.)

 

Here, as noted below, HLPUSD lacks standing to sue for breach of charitable trust as they do not have any contractual interest in HLP ED’s funds. As to Duarte, the court previously found Duarte lacked standing to sue for breach of charitable trust. (1/24/2024 Ruling, p. 3.) The court finds the SAC does not cure that deficiency. Instead, Duarte argues that there is no current case law that indicates a lack of standing to sue for breach of charitable trust precludes standing to sue for breach of fiduciary duty. (Parulan-Colfer Opp., p. 9:17-20.) Even so, the standing requirements for breach of charitable trust claims apply to any “action to enjoin, correct, obtain damages for or to otherwise remedy a breach of a charitable trust.” (Corp. Code, § 5142, subd. (a).) Because a breach of charitable trust necessarily includes a breach of the fiduciary duties arising pursuant to that trust, the court finds repackaging such claims as breach of fiduciary claims does not allow Duarte or HLPUSD to avoid the standing requirements of Corporation Code section 5142.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS the demurrer by Parulan-Colfer and HLP ED to the first cause of action without leave to amend.

 

Breach of Contract (Second Cause of Action)

 

HLP ED contends HLPUSD’s second cause of action for breach of contract fails because HLP ED’s articles of incorporation do not constitute a contract. The court agrees HLPUSD’s second cause of action fails because HLPUSD is not a third-party beneficiary of HLP ED’s articles of incorporation.

 

Legal Standard

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.) “[T]he vital elements of a cause of action based on contract are mutual assent (usually accomplished through the medium of an offer and acceptance) and consideration.” (Division of Labor Law Enforcement v. Transpacific Transportation Co. (1977) 69 Cal.App.3d 268, 275.)

 

Discussion

 

In previously sustaining HLP ED’s demurrer to this cause of action, the court found HLP ED’s articles of incorporation did not form a binding contract between HLP ED and HLPUSD. (1/24/2024 Ruling, p. 4.) While HLPUSD also alleged HLP ED’s articles of incorporation formed a binding contract between HLP ED and the state, HLPUSD failed to provide any legal authority in support of this contention. (1/24/2024 Ruling, p. 4.) The SAC repeats this allegation and provides citations to Trustees of Dartmouth College v. Woodward (1819) 17 U.S. 518 (Dartmouth College), and Heller Inv. Co. v. Southern Title & Trust Co. (1936) 17 Cal.App.2d 202, 211 (Heller). (SAC, ¶ 74.) HLP ED contends these authorities are inapposite. (Entity Def. Demurrer, p. 7:21-8:14.)  The court addresses each case below.

 

In Dartmouth College, the U.S. Supreme Court addressed whether a state legislature’s modifications to the charter of a charitable corporation violated the constitutional prohibition on the impairment of contracts. (Dartmouth College, supra, 17 U.S. at p. 624-626.) In answering in the affirmative, the Supreme Court held the charter was “plainly a contract to which the donors, the trustees and the crown (to whose rights and obligations New Hampshire succeeds) were the original parties.” (Id., at p. 643-644.) In Heller, the court addressed whether the legislature had the power “to change the contract of a stockholder with a corporation.” (Heller, supra, 17 Cal.App.2d at p. 206.) While the court’s opinion included block quotes from out-of-state cases that referenced Dartmouth College and quoted another Supreme Court decision for the suggestion that “[a] legislative grant to a corporation of special privileges, if not forbidden by the constitution, may be a contract,” the court’s analysis was limited to the legislature’s power to change a corporation’s structure. (Id., at p. 208, 210-212, quoting Attorney General v. Looker (1897) 111 Mich. 498, 505.)

 

The court notes, however, that neither Dartmouth College nor Heller address whether a third-party has standing to sue for breach of the contract between the corporation and the state as a third-party beneficiary. And, HLPUSD failed to direct the court to a single case in which a court has held so. Furthermore, even if the contractual relationship between the state and a corporation could give rise to third-party beneficiary standing, the court finds HLPUSD does not qualify as a third-party beneficiary.

 

“A contract, made expressly for the benefit of a third person, may be enforced by him at any time before the parties thereto rescind it.” (Civ. Code, § 1559.) “A third party may qualify as a beneficiary under a contract where the contracting parties must have intended to benefit that third party and such intent appears on the terms of the contract. [Citation.] However, it is well settled that Civil Code section 1559 excludes enforcement of a contract by persons who are only incidentally or remotely benefited by it.” (Jones v. Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 1717, 1724.) This is because “[a] third party should not be permitted to enforce covenants made not for his benefit, but rather for others. He is not a contracting party; his right to performance is predicated on the contracting parties’ intent to benefit him. [Citations.] As to any provision made not for his benefit but for the benefit of the contracting parties or for other third parties, he becomes an intermeddler.” (Murphy v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1976) 17 Cal.3d 937, 944.)

 

Here, HLP ED’s articles of incorporation do not specifically entitle HLPUSD to funds collected by HLP ED. Instead, the articles merely state that such funds are to be disbursed “to the benefit” of HLPUSD. (SAC, Ex. A, ¶ 2(II)(B).) And upon dissolution, HLP ED’s funds were to be sent to another nonprofit fund, foundation, or corporation. (SAC, Ex. A, ¶ 2(IV).) Because HLP ED’s articles of incorporation do not entitle HLPUSD to HLP ED’s funds, HLP ED is not a third-party beneficiary. Instead, the court finds HLP ED is an incidental beneficiary that received indirect benefits from HLP ED, and therefore has no standing to sue HLP ED for breach of contract.

 

Accordingly, HLP ED’s demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing (Third Cause of Action)

 

HLP ED maintains HLPUSD’s third cause of action for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing fails because the SAC does not establish the existence of a contract. (Entity Def. Demurrer, p. 9:21-26; Entity Def. Reply, p. 8:20-23.) Since the court has previously determined that HLPUSD cannot state a cause of action for breach of contract, the court finds this cause of action also fails.

 

Accordingly, HLP ED’s demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Conversion (Fifth Cause of Action)

 

Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants argue HLPUSD’s fifth cause of action for conversion fails because HLPUSD has no ownership interest in the funds at issue. The court agrees.

 

Legal Standard

 

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant's conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Burlesci v. Petersen (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1062, 1066.) “Conversion is an intentional tort.” (Multani v. Knight (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 837, 853.) “Because the act [of conversion] must be knowingly done, ‘neither negligence, active or passive, nor a breach of contract, even though it result in injury to, or loss of, specific property, constitutes a conversion.’” (Taylor v. Forte Hotels International (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1119, 1124.)

 

“[C]ases recognizing claims for the conversion of money ‘typically involve those who have misappropriated, commingled, or misapplied specific funds held for the benefit of others.’” (Voris v. Lampert (2019) 7 Cal.5th 1141, 1152, quoting PCO, Inc. v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro, LLP (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 384, 396.)

 

Discussion

 

In this case, the SAC alleges HLPUSD had an ownership interest in HLP ED’s funds which arose out of its status as a third-party beneficiary of HLP ED’s articles of incorporation. (SAC, ¶ 92.) Since the court rejected this argument above and found HLPUSD has no contractual interest in HLP ED’s funds, HLPUSD cannot state a cause of action for conversion.

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer and HLP ED’s demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Breach of Charitable Trust (Sixth Cause of Action)

 

Parulan-Colfer and HLP ED contend HLPUSD’s sixth cause of action for breach of charitable trust fails because HLPUSD lacks standing. The court agrees.

 

Legal Standard

 

Pursuant to Corporations Code section 5142, subdivision (a), an action for breach of charitable trust may be brought by “(1) [t]he corporation, or a member in the name of the corporation pursuant to Section 5710[,] (2) [a]n officer of the corporation[,] (3) [a] director of the corporation[,] (4) [a] person with a reversionary, contractual, or property interest in the assets subject to such charitable trust,” or the attorney general.”

 

Discussion

 

In this case, the SAC alleges HLPUSD’s standing arises from its contractual interest in HLP ED’s assets as a third-party beneficiary of HLPUSD’s articles of incorporation. (SAC, ¶ 97-98.) Parulan-Colfer and HLP ED contend HLPUSD does not have a contractual interest in HLP ED’s assets. (Parulan-Colfer Demurrer, p. 6:22-25; Entity Def. Demurrer, p. 5:14-23.) As noted above, the court has found HLPUSD is a not a third-party beneficiary of the contract between HLP ED and the state and thus has no contractual interest in the disposition of HLP ED’s funds.

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer and HLP ED’s demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Accounting (Eleventh Cause of Action)

 

Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants maintain Duarte and HLPUSD’s eleventh cause of action for accounting fails because they cannot establish some balance is due to them. The court agrees.

 

Legal Standard

 

“A cause of action for an accounting requires a showing that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting. [Citations.] An action for accounting is not available where the plaintiff alleges the right to recover a sum certain or a sum that can be made certain by calculation.” (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179.)

 

Discussion

 

In this case, Parulan-Colfer maintains Duarte and HLPUSD cannot show some balance is due to them as HLP ED’s articles of incorporation do not establish they are entitled to HLP ED’s funds. (Parulan-Colfer Demurrer, p. 8:4-20.) The Entity Defendants maintain this cause of action fails because they did not owe any fiduciary duty to Duarte and HLPUSD. (Entity Def. Demurrer, p. 11:19-26.)

 

With regard to Duarte, the court agrees. In the SAC, Duarte alleges a first cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, a fourth cause of action for fraud, a seventh cause of action for common counts, an eighth cause of action for unfair business practices, a tenth cause of action for declaratory relief, a twelfth cause of action aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duties, and fourteenth cause of action for negligence. Following this demurrer, only Duarte’s fourth, seventh, eighth, and tenth causes of action remain. Since these causes of action are based on a specific sum that HLP ED allegedly fraudulently induced, Duarte is not able to state a claim for accounting. (SAC, ¶ 29.)

 

As to HLPUSD, HLPUSD can no longer show some balance is due to HLPUSD as HLPUSD is not entitled to HLP ED’s funds and lacks standing to contest HLP ED’s distribution of funds.

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duties (Twelfth Cause of Action)

 

The Entity Defendants argue Duarte and HLPUSD’s twelfth cause of action for aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duties fails to plead sufficient facts to state a claim because HLP ED was legally obligated to transfer its property to the other Entity Defendants. (Entity Def. Demurrer, p. 12:1-8.) The court finds this cause of action fails for the reasons noted above since Duarte and HLPUSD cannot state a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duties.

 

Accordingly, the Entity Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Aiding and Abetting Breach of Charitable Trust (Thirteenth Cause of Action)

 

Parulan-Colfer and HLP ED contend HLPUSD’s thirteenth cause of action for aiding and abetting breach of charitable trust fails for the same reasons as HLPUSD’s cause of action for breach of charitable trust. (Parulan-Colfer Demurrer, p. 9:6-7; Entity Def. Demurrer, p. 12:10-13.) The court agrees.

 

Accordingly, their demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend for the same reasons noted above.

 

Negligence (Fourteenth Cause of Action)

 

Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants maintain Duarte and HLPUSD’s fourteenth cause of action for negligence fails because (1) the Court did not permit Parulan-Colfer to be named as a party pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.15, subdivision (a), (2) the cause of action is time-barred, and (3) the cause of action fails to allege sufficient facts to state a claim.

 

As an initial matter, Duarte and HLPUSD did not obtain leave to include this cause of action in the SAC when the Court sustained the previous demurrers to the FAC with leave to amend. (See Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023.) And even if they did, this cause of action is barred as it is essentially an attempt to restate and repackage the causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty and breach of charitable trust. (See Tribeca Companies, LLC v. First American Title Ins. Co. (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1114 [“[B]reach of fiduciary duty can be based upon either negligence or fraud, depending on the circumstances.”].)

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants’ demurrers to this cause of action are SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

MOTIONS TO STRIKE


Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants move to strike (1) punitive damages, (2) fiduciary duty allegations, (3) alter ego allegations, (4) injunctive relief, (5) requests for attorneys’ fees, and (6) references to irrelevant parties. For the following reasons, the Court GRANTS their motion as to the request for attorney fees and DENIES it on all other grounds.

 

Legal Standard

 

Upon a party’s motion or the court’s own motion, the court may strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) “The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437, subd. (a).)

 

An immaterial or irrelevant allegation includes “(1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense,” “(2)¿An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense,” or “(3)¿A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10.)

 

Punitive Damages

 

Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants first argue there are insufficient allegations to support the SAC’s request for punitive damages. The court disagrees.

 

Civil Code section 3294 allows punitive damages when a plaintiff establishes by clear and convincing evidence that a defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) For the purposes of determining punitive damages, fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).) “In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in section 3294, it must include specific factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for punitive damages.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) Furthermore, “[p]unitive damages may not be pleaded generally.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, the court previously held that Duarte and HLP ED have adequately alleged a cause of action for fraud against Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants. (1/24/2024 Ruling, p. 5.) Because well-pled allegations of fraud are sufficient to support a request for punitive damages, the court declines to strike punitive damages allegations from the SAC.

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants’ motion to strike on this ground is DENIED.

 

Fiduciary Duty Allegations


Parulan-Colfer next contends the court should strike the allegations that Parulan-Colfer owed a fiduciary duty to HLPUSD as a former employee. The court disagrees.

 

The SAC alleges Duarte and HLPUSD were entitled to rely on Parulan-Colfer’s fiduciary duties as “a former employee of [HLPUSD].” (SAC, ¶ 52.) The SAC further alleges that HLPUSD expected “Parulan-Colfer’s fiduciary duties as a former employee would be honored.” (SAC, ¶ 59.) Last, the SAC alleges Parulan-Colfer owed fiduciary duties as an employee which included “the common law duty of loyalty owed by a former employee with respect to engaging in unfair competition against a former employer.” (SAC, ¶ 64.)

 

In interpreting and liberally construing these allegations, the court finds they appear to denote Parulan-Colfer’s current employment status as opposed to suggesting Parulan-Colfer still owes fiduciary duties after the end of Parulan-Colfer’s employment. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 452 [“In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”].)

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer’s motion to strike on this ground is DENIED.

 

Alter Ego Allegations

 

Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants maintain the SAC’s alter ego allegations against them are insufficiently pled. The court disagrees.

 

“Two requirements must be met to invoke the alter ego doctrine: (1) ‘[T]here must be such a unity of interest and ownership between¿the corporation and its equitable owner that the separate personalities of the corporation and the shareholder do not in reality exist’; and (2) ‘there must be an¿inequitable result¿if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.’” (Turman v. Superior Court of Orange County (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 969, 980-981, quoting Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538.) When making alter ego allegations, pleading with particularity is not required as a Plaintiff need only allege “ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 236, quoting Burks v. Poppy Construction Co. (1962) 57 Cal.2d 463, 474.)

 

In this case, the SAC alleges there was a unity of interest between the Entity Defendants and Parulan-Colfer that eliminated any separation between the entities and Parulan-Colfer. (SAC, ¶ 54.) The SAC also alleges inequity would result if these entities were treated as separate. (SAC, ¶ 55.) While Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants demand further specificity, the Court finds the SAC’s general pleading of this requirements is sufficient to allege the Entity Defendants were Parulan-Colfer’s alter ego.

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants’ motion to strike on this ground is DENIED.

 

Injunctive Relief and Attorney Fees


Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants argue Duarte and HLPUSD are not entitled to attorney fees or injunctive relief.  However, the court notes injunctive relief is available for Duarte and HLPUSD’s eighth cause of action for unfair business practices pursuant to Business and Professions Code section 17203.

 

As for attorneys’ fees, Duarte and HLPUSD argue they are entitled to attorneys’ fees pursuant to Prentice v. North Am. Title Guaranty Corp., Alameda Division (1963) 59 Cal.2d 618 (Prentice). In that case, the court held a party may be entitled to attorney fees as form of damages “where a defendant has wrongfully made it necessary for a plaintiff to sue a third person.” (Id., at p. 621.) The court finds the Prentice case is inapplicable here since Duarte and HLPUSD are not claiming attorney fees as damages for a suit against a third party. Instead, Duarte and HLPUSD seek attorneys’ fees from Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants who they allege are alter egos of each other, and not third parties. Since Duarte and HLPUSD do not identify any other ground for the award of attorneys’ fees, the court strikes their request for attorneys’ fees without leave to amend.

 

Accordingly, Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED as to the request for injunctive relief and GRANTED as to the request for attorneys’ fees without leave to amend.

 

References to Irrelevant Third Parties


Last, the Entity Defendants argue the Court should strike all references regarding a recipient of HLP ED’s scholarship funds, HLP ED’s hiring of a CPA, and other unnamed donors. But while these allegations serve as the basis for Duarte and HLPUSD’s causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty and breach of charitable trust, the Court finds they also serve to provide support for Duarte and HLPUSD’s viable causes of action for fraud, common counts, unfair business practices, and interference with prospective economic advantage.

 

Accordingly, the Entity Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED as to these issues.

 

PETITION FOR RETROACTIVE LEAVE TO AMEND


Duarte and HLPUSD petition the court for retroactive leave to amend their SAC with a thirteenth cause of action for aiding and abetting breach of charitable trust and a fourteenth cause of action for negligence. Because the court sustained the above demurrers to these causes of action without leave to amend, the court deems this petition MOOT.

 

CONCLUSION


Based on the foregoing, the Entity Defendants and Parulan-Colfer’s demurrers to the SAC are SUSTAINED without leave to amend in their entirety.

 

Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants’ motions to strike are GRANTED IN PART as to the SAC’s prayer for attorney fees and DENIED IN PART on all other grounds.

 

Based on the analysis regarding the demurrer by Parulan-Colfer and the Entity Defendants, HLPUSD and Duarte’s petition for retroactive leave to amend is DEEMED MOOT.

 

Defendants Parulan-Colfer, HLP ED Foundation, SVG Training Foundation, Inc. and San Gabriel Career Foundation, Inc. are ordered to file their Answer, as applicable, to the SAC’s remaining causes of action for Fraud (4th Cause of Action), Common Counts (7th Cause of Action), Unfair Business Practices (8th Cause of Action), Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage (9th Cause of Action ), and Declaratory Relief (10th Case Of Action) in ten (10) days.
