DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

ALERT

Due to Coronavirus, please consider appearing by phone for Department M cases.

 

Department M strongly encourages the use of  LA CourtConnect* for ALL hearings, without need for prior approval, unless live testimony by a witness is required.

 

The contact information for LA CourtConnect* is:

 

 

 https://lacourt.portalscloud.com/VCourt/

 

 

*Parties with a fee waiver on file may be eligible to appear at no/reduced cost


Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Hearing Date:                         September 20, 2023

 

Moving Party:                         Cross-Complainant Airport Urgent Care & Industrial Medicine, a Professional Corporation

Responding Parties:                Cross-Defendants Pacific Medical Laboratory, Reza Zarghami, Jalal Zarghami aka J. Zarghami, Suzan Asilzadeh Dardashti

Motions to Compel Four Cross-Defendants’ Initial Responses to First Set of Form Interrogatories, First Set of Requests for Admission, First Set of Special Interrogatories, and First Set of Request for Production of Documents and Requests for Sanctions

            The court considered the moving papers.  No oppositions were filed.

RULING

            To the extent moving party acknowledges verified responses and production occurred prior to the filing of the motions the subject motions are moot with the exception of sanctions; for this reason the motions to compel interrogatories and request for production are DENIED.  Given the verified responses and production substantially predate the filing the request for sanctions are denied.  The court further finds that there is no statutory authority for a motion to compel responses to requests for admission; accordingly said motion is denied as well as the associated request for sanctions. 

            Given that the moving party has filed 18 motions as one paying only one motion fee the moving party is ordered to pay 17 filing fees to the court forthwith. Counsel for the moving party is advised to file separate motions and pay separate filing fees for any discovery motions in the future. Parties are ordered to make an effort to meet and confer verbally prior to bringing any further discovery motions to facilitate a productive resolution of any discovery dispute.

BACKGROUND

            On July 5, 2022, plaintiff Account Management Services, Inc. (“AMS”) filed a complaint against defendants Jamshid J. Shariati, M.D. aka James J. Shariati, M.D. (“Shariati”), Airport Urgent Care & Industrial Medicine, a Professional Corporation (“Airport”), and Does 1-10 for common counts. 

            On October 31, 2022, Shariati and Airport (collectively, “cross-complainants”) filed a cross-complaint against Pacific Medical Laboratory (“PML”), Reza Zarghami (“Reza”), Jalal Zarghami aka J. Zarghami (“Jalal”), and Suzan Asilzadeh Dardashti (“Dardashi”) (collectively, “cross-defendants”) for breach of oral contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, fraud, negligent misrepresentation, negligence, and destruction of goodwill.  

            Four discovery motions are presently before the court.  Airport moves for orders compelling each of the four cross-defendants to provide responses to four sets of discovery requests.  Specifically, each motion moves to compel initial responses to First Set of Form Interrogatories, First Set of Requests for Admission, First Set of Special Interrogatories, and First Request for Production of Documents.  Airport requests an award of monetary sanctions against cross-defendants in the total amount of $5,925.00.

            The court notes Airport improperly filed each of its motions to compel initial responses to four separate discovery requests as single motions, when they should have been filed as separate motions for each discovery request at issue, which would have been a total of sixteen motions. In addition, the court notes that the Discovery Act does not provide for a motion to compel initial responses to requests for admission; rather the proper mechanism is a motion to deem requests for admissions admitted. (C.C.P. §2033.280(b).)   

 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.

Request for Production of Documents

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of “good cause” is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487.

Request for Admissions

Pursuant to CCP § 2033.280(b), a party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).  “Failure to timely respond to RFA does not result in automatic admissions.  Rather, the propounder of the RFA must ‘move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction’ under § 2023.010 et seq.”  Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶ 8:1370, citing CCP § 2033.280(b).  The court “shall” grant the motion to deem RFA admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  CCP § 2033.280(c).

DISCUSSION

            Airport requests that the court compel cross-defendants to serve responses to cross-complainants’ form interrogatories, set one, requests for admission, set one, special interrogatories, set one, and requests for production of documents, set one.

            Airport contends it served the discovery requests on May 10, 2023.  Airport argues that although cross-defendants responded to its discovery requests, the responses are untimely because they were unverified and therefore non-code complaint.  On July 31, 2023, Airport sent cross-defendants a meet and confer correspondence informing cross-defendants that the discovery responses were late.  In response, cross-defendants’ counsel sent the untimely verifications for all four cross-defendants on August 7, 2023, and on August 11, 2023.  (Declaration of Koohski, ¶¶ 12-14, Exhs. H, I.)    Airport filed the instant motions on August 21, 2023.

            The motions are DENIED. To the extent verified responses and production occurred prior to the filing of the motion the motions subjects are moot with the exception of sanctions.

            Airport asserts that cross-defendants provided untimely responses to the four sets of discovery requests and that cross-defendants’ counsel sent cross-defendants’ verifications thereto.  (Declaration of Koohski, ¶¶ 12-14, Exhs. H, I.)  In light of cross-defendants’ production of responses and untimely verifications, there is no production to compel. To the extent Airport contends there are defects in the discovery responses served by cross-defendants, the proper mechanism is a motion to compel further responses, given initial responses have already been served, which Airport does not dispute.

            Based on the foregoing, Airport’s motions to compel cross-defendants’ responses to special interrogatories, form interrogatories, and requests for production are denied. Airport’s motions to deem RFAs admitted against cross-defendants are likewise denied.

            Airport requests sanctions against cross-defendants in the total amount of $5,925 for fees incurred in bringing the motions.  However, as discussed above, Airport filed the instant motions after cross-defendants had served verifications. As such, Airport was not obligated to incur fees in preparing the instant motions.  Where production of responses or verifications is made after the motions are filed, sanctions may still be at issue since the moving party incurred fees to bring the motions; however, that is not the case here. Accordingly, Airport’s requests for monetary sanctions are denied.

Motion to Quash Service of Summons

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is Denied.

BACKGROUND

            On June 28, 2023, plaintiff Samuel Tafolla filed a complaint against defendants Darren John Marcz and Does 1 to 50, inclusive for (1) motor vehicle and (2) negligence.

            This is a motor vehicle accident case that occurred on August 5, 2021, in which plaintiff alleges defendant Darren John Marcz negligently drove his car and caused physical injury to plaintiff. 

DISCUSSION

Defendant requests that the court quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction due to defective service of process.  Defendant contends he was highly unlikely to receive actual notice of summons based on attempted service because defendant has been either homeless or in a mental health facility since the date of the incident.  Although defendant briefly lived with his mother at her residence at 8 Chesterfield Rd. Rolling Hills Estates, CA in December 2022, defendant has not resided there since and currently lives in a mental health facility.  Consequently, defendant contends the attempted substituted service on his mother was improper and invalid.

In opposition, plaintiff alleges that on the date of the incident, defendant provided his address as 8 Chesterfield Rd. Rolling Hills Estates, CA in the face page of the traffic collision report.  Plaintiff further contends that although defendant’s mother stated that defendant lived with her briefly in December 2022, defendant lists 8 Chesterfield Rd. Rolling Hills Estates, CA as his address in his driver’s license.  Finally, plaintiff alleges a Westlaw search provides that defendant resided at 8 Chesterfield Rd. Rolling Hill Estates CA 90274 from January 1, 1988 to July 12, 2023.  Based on the above, plaintiff argues defendant’s primary residence is 8 Chesterfield Rd. Rolling Hills Estates, CA.

In response, defendant argues service is defective because plaintiff’s service processor did not declare under the penalty of the perjury what attempts were made to complete personal service on the defendant.  However, this argument is unsupported by evidence, since the Proof of Service on file with the Court includes a Declaration of Diligence, made under penalty of perjury, in which the registered process server claims he made three total attempts to serve defendant on July 5, 8, and 9, 2023 before serving by substituted service on Defendant’s mother on the final attempt. Defendant also argues that the service processor failed to provide defendant’s mother with a description of the legal documents.  Finally, defendant argues the court has no jurisdiction over him because defendant has not lived at his mother’s residence since December 2022. 

Under CCP § 415.20(b), “If a copy of the summons and complaint cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered to the person to be served, as specified in Section 416.60, 416.70, 416.80, or 416.90, a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at the person’s dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge of his or her office, place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and of the complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.”

Section 415.20, subdivision (b) allows a process server to leave the summons and complaint at the “dwelling house” or “usual place or abode” of the person being served.  Zirbes v. Stratton (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 1407, 1416.  “When a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process ‘the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service.’” Summers v. McClanahan¿(2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.  A proof of service containing a declaration from a registered process server invokes a presumption of valid service.  American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara¿(2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390. 

In Zirbes, the court found ineffective service of process as to defendant because she had established a separate legal household where she resided with her children which was a matter of public record, despite having her mother’s address as her listed residence.  Zirbes v. Stratton (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 1407, 1417.  Additionally, the court explained that “[t]he terms 'dwelling house' and 'usual place of abode' take their meaning from the Federal Rules.” Zirbes v. Stratton (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 1407, 1416.  Further, it is crucial that a connection be shown between the address at which substituted service is effectuated and the party to be served.  Corcoran v. Arouh (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 310, 314. 

Here, a connection exists between the address at which substituted service was effectuated and the party to be served at the time of service.  Defendant provided his mother’s residence in the September 10, 2021 collision report.  (Plaintiff’s Response, Exh. A.)  Additionally, defendant’s driver’s license as of September 5, 2023 and a Westlaw search conducted on September 6, 2023 list defendant’s address as that of his mother’s residence.  (Plaintiff’s Response, Exhs. B, C.)  Finally, even though defendant was homeless or in mental facilities after the date of the incident, he returned to live at his mother’s residence briefly in December 2022, demonstrating that his mother’s address is his place of abode.  (Marcz Decl., ¶ 7.)  Unlike in Zirbes and Corcoran, where the court found that the defendant had no legal connection with the address where substituted service was effectuated, here defendant has a strong connection with his mother’s residence.  Although defendant’s mother claims defendant has not lived with her since December 2022 and lacks knowledge of his whereabouts, that is not sufficient evidence to demonstrate that her address is not defendant’s place of abode.  Based on the foregoing, defendant resides at his mother’s residence for purposes of effectuating proper service of process.   

The motion is thus DENIED.

Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.
