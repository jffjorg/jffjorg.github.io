DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19GDCV01347    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

I.        INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff J&J Freight Solutions, LLC is a trucking company. It was started by Jie Mei Huang. Huang’s husband, Qinyi Yu, formed Golden J Group, Inc., a sister company that provides back up services to J&J in the form of assisting with port entry, trucking, repairing, warehousing, and repackaging services. Defendants Jianhua Li and Ninyan Tan are former employees. In this action, Plaintiffs allege that Li and Tan misappropriated their trade secrets to start a competing business, CTU Connection. In July 2020, a preliminary injunction was issued preventing Defendants from using Plaintiffs’ trade secrets. In October 2021, Plaintiffs added EMC Transportation, Inc. as a doe, alleging that it is a shell corporation and conduit for CTU.  

Defendants CTU Connection, Jianhua Li and Nenyin Tan filed their motion for summary judgment on March 18, 2024. Plaintiffs filed an opposition on May 23, 2024.

Before the Court is Defendants’ motion for summary judgment or in the alternative, summary adjudication. Defendants argue that there are no triable issues as to the entire complaint, citing agency law and trade law.

The motion is denied.

II. EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Plaintiffs’ Objections  to Jianhua Li Declaration: (5)

The following objections are overruled: 1, 2, 4, 5.

The following objections are sustained: 3.

Plaintiffs’ Objections  to Nenyin Tan Declaration: (4)

The following objections are overruled: 1, 3,4.

The following objections are sustained: 2.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

          Summary judgment is appropriately granted where it is shown that an action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(a).) “A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.”(CCP § 437p(1).) The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.¿ (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)¿

          A motion for summary judgment shall be granted where all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue of material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (CCP § 437c(c).) In so determining, the Court shall consider all evidence in the papers and all inferences reasonably deducible from that evidence. (Id.)

To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) The opposition papers “shall include a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating if the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed.” (CCP § 437(b)(3).)

Summary adjudication may be granted where it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim of damages, or an issue of duty. (CCP § 437c(f).)

A plaintiff has met “his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).) “[T]he burden [then] shifts to the defendant . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Id.) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

          Unopposed declarations must be accepted as true. (CCP § 437c(e).) However, affidavits must be liberally construed in favor of the opposition and strictly construed against the moving party in determining the existence of a "triable issue" of fact. (D'Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

IV.     ANALYSIS

          a. Undisputed Facts

          Defendant Jianhua Li was an operations manager for Plaintiff companies. (UMFs 2-3.) Defendants Li and Nenyin Tan do not have non-compete agreements with Plaintiffs. (UMF 5.) Defendants Li and Tan were not officers of Plaintiff companies. (UMFs 15-16.)

          Defendant CTU Connection is a separate business entity formed by Defendants. (UMFs 25, 28-31.)

          What is disputed is whether Defendants took Plaintiffs’ trade secrets, namely a customer list, among other information, and then used that list to develop a competing business and poach customers.

b. Misappropriation of Trade Secrets

A trade secret is information that “(1) Derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and (2) Is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.” (CC § 3426.1(d).) Misappropriation of a trade secret requires “(1) the plaintiff owned a trade secret, (2) the defendant acquired, disclosed, or used the plaintiff's trade secret through improper means, and (3) the defendant's actions damaged the plaintiff.” (Sargent Fletcher, Inc. v. Able Corp. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1658, 1665, internal citations omitted.)

Defendants urge that the customer list at issue is not a trade secret. In support, Defendants cite Barney v. Burrow (2008) 558 F.Supp.2d 1066, where it was found under California law that a customer list was not trade secret if the former employees had substantially developed the list and the former employer alleging misappropriation did not expend substantial efforts in creating the list. (Id. at 1080-1081.) Defendants further cite case law and statute for the proposition that California law disfavors retroactive non-competes and trade restraints. (See Whyte v. Schlage Lock Co. (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1464; BPC § 16600.)

Defendants offer the declaration of the two individual Defendants and of their attorney, Fred Wong. Defendant Nenyin Tan declares that she was assistant to the president of Plaintiffs. (Tan Decl. ¶¶ 4-5.) She declares that she developed CTU Connection’s customer lists and services  through her own efforts. (Tan Decl. ¶ 6.) She declares that she did not take Plaintiffs’ trade secrets nor owes Plaintiffs a duty to not compete. (Tan Decl. ¶¶ 7, 9-11, 20.) Defendant Jianhua Li declares similarly. (Li Decl. ¶¶ 5-11, 20.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs urge that there are triable issues of material facts as to this cause of action, arguing that Defendants took more than the customer list and customer lists can be trade secrets. Plaintiffs urge that Defendants took customer lists, driver lists, financial information, and rate sheets. (Plaintiffs Additional Undisputed Material Facts 35-40.) In support, Plaintiffs provide the declaration of three of their customers/business contacts (Mee Mee Liew Declaration, Mohamed Azwan Declaration, Wayne Wade Declaration), excerpts from depositions and the declaration of Jamie Huang, president of Plaintiff companies.

Of note, Jamie Huang declares that he personally prepared and created the rate sheet and the attached return policy. (Huang Decl. ¶ 2.) He declares that a rate sheet for defendant CTU Connection contains the exact same typographical errors and his return policy identically. (Huang Decl. ¶ 3.) Attached to the declaration of Steven Stiglitz are excerpts of Defendant Tan’s deposition testimony suggesting that CTU Connection modeled the rates it charged customers on the rates Plaintiff charged. (Stiglitz Decl. Exhs. A and B.)

As Plaintiffs have produced undisputed evidence that Defendants took more trade secret information than just the customer list and Defendants only presented argument as to the customer list, the motion for summary  judgment is denied. Summary adjudication is denied as to this cause of action.

          c. 2nd-6th Causes of Action

Defendants urge that the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act (Civil Code Section 3426.1 et seq.) preempts the second through sixth causes of action for conversion, breach of fiduciary duty, intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, unfair competition, and invasion of privacy. As the same argument is advanced as to each, the causes of action will be addressed at once.

          Here, Defendants urge that these causes of action are preempted by CUTSA as it concerns the same underlying conduct as the first cause of action. Citing KC Multimedia v. Bank of America, Defendants urge that the statute can function to preempt specific common law claims. (See K.C. Multimedia, Inc. v. Bank of Am. Tech. & Operations, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal. App. 4th 939, 957-959.) If the cause of action depends on a conclusion that Defendants misappropriated Plaintiffs’ trade secrets, the cause of action is preempted. (Id. at 959-960.)

          In support, Defendants cite to the operative Second Amended Complaint. (SAC.) The second cause of action alleges that Defendants converted Plaintiffs’ property by stealing customer information. (SAC ¶¶ 76-77.) The third cause of action alleges that Defendants breached their fiduciary duties by taking this information. (SAC ¶¶ 83-84.) The fifth cause of action alleges unfair competition based on misappropriation of trade secrets. (SAC ¶ 111.) The fourth cause of action, however, alleges interference with business by maligning and disparaging Plaintiffs. (SAC ¶ 98.) The sixth cause of action alleges invasion of privacy based on stealing of a social security number and subsequent disclosure of the number. (SAC ¶¶ 119-122.) The second, third and fifth causes of action arguably depend on the conclusion that Defendants misappropriated Plaintiffs’ trade secrets.

          Additionally, Defendants argue that the third cause of action fails because Defendants were not officers of Plaintiff companies. Citing GAB Business Services, Inc. v. Lindsey & Newsom Claim Services, Inc. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 409, 420, Defendants urge that to owe fiduciary duties to a corporation, an employee must exercise discretionary management power and be an officer in more than title only. (Id.) Here, the individual Defendants were not officers of Plaintiff companies. (UMFs 15-16.) As non-officer employees without discretionary management control, they cannot be held to owe fiduciary duties to the corporation.

          In opposition, Plaintiffs cite the January 18, 2022, Order on Demurrer that overrules this exact argument as to the second and fifth causes of action. (See 1/18/22 Notice of Entry of Order Exh A: Tentative Ruling.) In that ruling, the Court noted the presence of additional alleged misappropriation of non-trade secret material. (Id.) In so finding, the Court ruled that these causes of action were not preempted as other material was allegedly stolen, such as log-in information, identification numbers and forms.

          As to the third cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, Plaintiffs urge that though Defendants were not officers of Plaintiff-companies, they had access to confidential information and through that access, owed fiduciary duties. (Tri-Growth Centre City, Ltd. v. Silldorf, Burdman, Duignan & Eisenberg (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1139, 1150.) Indeed, Defendants did have access to this confidential information. (PSUMFs 35, 36, 45-46.) Further, Plaintiffs urge that Defendants breached their fiduciary duty by creating CTU Connection and soliciting their clients while Defendants still worked for Plaintiffs. (See Huong Que, Inc. v. Luu (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 401, 410-411, PSUMF 52.)

          As Plaintiffs have produced responsive evidence demonstrating triable issues of material fact that Defendants stole confidential information and that Defendants breached their fiduciary duties, summary adjudication is denied as to the remaining causes of action.

          d. Alter Ego Cannot Be Resolved on Summary Adjudication

          Summary adjudication is appropriate to resolve causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims of damages or an issue of duty. (CCP §437c(f)(1).) Alter ego, the theory that Defendant Tan and Li are alter egos of their corporation, CTU Connection and thus can be held liable for the actions of the corporation, does not fit into one of these categories.

          Summary adjudication on alter ego is not appropriate.

 

V.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is denied. Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is also denied.

Counsel for Plaintiffs to provide notice.

Case Number: 21BBCV00861    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT

I.        INTRODUCTION

Plaintiffs Julio Santos Mayorga; Heidy Santos Soto; Elisama Santos Soto, by and through her Guardian Ad Litem, Julio Santos Mayorga; and Ruth Santos Soto, by and through by Guardian Ad Litem, Julio Santos Mayorga ("Plaintiffs") filed suit against Defendant 7055 Coldwater Canyon Avenue, LLC (Coldwater), alleging that Plaintiffs were tenants in the property located at 7051 Coldwater Canyon Avenue, #8, North Hollywood, California, owned by Coldwater. Plaintiffs allege that the property had numerous habitability issues during their tenancy, includes leaks, flooding, mold, pest infestations, faulty electrical fittings, lack of heat, and other issues.

Plaintiff’s complaint against Coldwater, filed September 23, 2021, alleged 5 causes of action: (1) Breach of Warranty of Habitability; (2) Negligent Maintenance of Premises; (3) Nuisance; (4) Breach of Quiet Enjoyment; and (5) IIED. In October 2021, Defendant Shind, LLC (Shind) purchased the property from Coldwater. In April 2022, Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint, which added Shind as a Defendant and alleged four additional causes of action: (6) Violations of Unruh Civil Rights Act; (7) Violations of California Anti-Harassment Statute; (8) Retaliation; and (9) Unfair Competition. Plaintiffs alleged that after the filing of this lawsuit, Shind initiated eviction proceedings against Plaintiffs in retaliation.  Plaintiffs also allege that Shind harassed and discriminated against Plaintiffs based, at least in part, on their Latinx heritage. Several of these causes of action subject Shind to possible punitive damages. 

On January 29, 2024, Plaintiffs and Coldwater entered into a settlement agreement in which Coldwater agreed to pay Plaintiffs $100,000 as follows: $47,500 to Julio Santos Mayorga, $47,500 to Heidy Santos Soto, $2,500 to Elisama Santos Soto and $2,500 to Ruth Santos Soto. Before the Court is a motion to determine the good faith of this settlement. This motion was filed by Plaintiffs on February 27, 2024. Shind filed an opposition on April 17, 2024. Plaintiffs filed a reply on April 22, 2024. The motion was continued on May 1, 2024, for further briefing. Plaintiffs submitted their further brief on May 8, 2024. Defendant Coldwater’s counsel submitted a declaration on May 20, 2024. Shind submitted their reply on May 21, 2024.

          II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Plaintiffs move for an order finding that the settlement between Plaintiffs and Coldwater was made in good faith pursuant to CCP §§ 877 and 877.6. Coldwater’s counsel has submitted a declaration in support of Plaintiff’s position. 

"[Code of Civil Procedure] Section 877.6 was enacted by the Legislature in 1980 to establish a statutory procedure for determining if a settlement by an alleged joint tortfeasor has been entered into in good faith and to provide a bar to claims of other alleged joint tortfeasors for equitable contribution or partial or comparative indemnity when good faith is shown." IRM Corp. v. Carlson (1986) 179 Cal. App. 3d 94, 104.

CCP § 877.6(a)(1) provides, in relevant part, that, on noticed motion, "[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff … and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors …." "A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault." CCP § 877.6(c). Although a determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, "it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated" by the settlement. CCP § 877(a).

In Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499, the California Supreme Court identified the following nonexclusive factors courts are to consider in determining if a settlement is in good faith under section 877.6: "a rough approximation of plaintiffs' total recovery and the settlor's proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial. Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of nonsettling defendants."

The evaluation of whether a settlement was made in good faith is required to "be made on the basis of information available at the time of settlement." Tech-Bilt, 38 Cal.3d at 499. "'[A] defendant's settlement figure must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant's liability to be.' [Citation.]" Id. at 499.

"The party asserting the lack of good faith, who has the burden of proof on that issue (§ 877.6, subd. (d)), should be permitted to demonstrate, if he can, that the settlement is so far 'out of the ballpark' in relation to these factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute. Such a demonstration would establish that the proposed settlement was not a 'settlement made in good faith' within the terms of section 877.6." Tech-Bilt, 38 Cal.3d at 499-500.

"Thus, Tech-Bilt held that in determining whether a settlement was made in good faith for purposes of section 877.6, a key factor a trial court should consider is whether the amount paid in settlement bears a reasonable relationship to the settlor's proportionate share of liability. (Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at pp. 499-500 ….) This is because one of the main goals of section 877.6 is 'allocating costs equitably among multiple tortfeasors.' (Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 502 ….)." TSI Seismic Tenant Space, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 149 Cal. App. 4th 159, 166. "Accordingly, a court not only looks at the alleged tortfeasor's potential liability to the plaintiff, but it must also consider the culpability of the tortfeasor vis-à-vis other parties alleged to be responsible for the same injury. Potential liability for indemnity to a nonsettling defendant is an important consideration for the trial court in determining whether to approve a settlement by an alleged tortfeasor. [Citation.]" Id. at 166.         

III.     ANALYSIS

The first question this Court must address is whether Coldwater and Shind are “joint tortfeasors” or “co-obligors” within the meaning of section 877.6(a)(1).  If one takes these terms to mean co-defendants in the same action, then they likely are.  However, the law defines the term “joint tortfeasor” more narrowly.  As stated by the court in Commercial Standard Title Co. v. Superior Court (1979) 92 Cal. App. 3d 934: “By definition, joint tortfeasors are jointly and severally liable for the entire damage proximately resulting.”  (See also Black’s Law Dictionary: “Joint tortfeasors are parties that either commit the wrong together or, if done independently, their acts unite to cause one injury.”)  “Co-obligor” has generally been defined as “a party who is bound with one or more others to fulfill an obligation under a contract.”

Coldwater and Shind’s potential liability stem from different acts and different time periods. The demarcation point is the October 2021 sale of the property, with Coldwater’s potential liability preceding that event and Shind’s potential liability following that event.  Plaintiffs’ Counsel argues that, under Civil Code section 823, Shind, as Coldwater’s successor-in-interest, is also liable for Coldwater’s conduct before the sale of the property. However, there are two problems with this argument. First, the statute is permissive, not mandatory.  “Whatever remedies the lessee of any real property may have against his immediate lessor, for the breach of any agreement in the lease, he may have against the assigns of the lessor….” (Emphasis added.)  Furthermore, this argument is not supported factually. Whether Shind assumed Coldwater’s liability would likely depend upon the language of the purchase agreement between the parties.[1]

While causes of action 1 through 5 apply to both defendants, albeit for different time periods, causes of action 6 through 9 appear to apply solely to Shind. A compelling argument can be made that these defendants share no joint and several liability whatsoever. Coldwater’s liability begins either three or four years prior to the filing of this case, depending on the particular cause of action’s limitations period. Coldwater’s potential liability would appear to end on the date the property was sold. Shind’s potential liability begins on the day it assumed ownership of the property. If so, then Coldwater and Shind are not “joint tortfeasors” or “co-obligors” within the meaning of section 877.6(a)(1).  As such, the good faith settlement rules set forth in section 877.6 simply do not apply.

As noted above, one of the primary goals of section 877.6 is 'allocating costs equitably among multiple tortfeasors.'  Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 502.  This is a rule of basic fairness.  One tortfeasor should not be allowed to get out a case cheaply while a joint tortfeasor stays behind (possibly because of deeper pockets) to pick up the lion’s share of the tab. However, this rationale disappears when the liability of the two co-defendants is based on different causes of action and/or different time periods.  If these defendants are not joint tortfeasors or co-obligors within the meaning of section 877.6, then the good faith settlement rules do not apply.

However, if the court assumes that Coldwater and Shind are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors, the next question that must be answered is whether Shind has met its burden of proof. As noted above, "The party asserting the lack of good faith, who has the burden of proof on that issue (§ 877.6, subd. (d)), should be permitted to demonstrate, if he can, that the settlement is so far 'out of the ballpark' in relation to these factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute….” Tech-Bilt, 38 Cal.3d at 499-500. A review of

Plaintiffs’ counsel and counsel for Coldwater argue that the $100,000 settlement was made in good faith utilizing the services of a mediator. They further argue that the Unruh Act cause of action against Shind subjects it to the possibility of a far greater award of damages. The Court finds the declaration of Coldwater’s counsel, Mr. Kerr, persuasive.  He declares: “As a defense attorney who routinely handles landlord-tenant disputes, it is my experience that retaliation claims are worth significantly more than garden-variety habitability claims, and come with a much greater potential for a punitive damages award from a jury. The potential exposure to Defendant SHIND LLC is therefore significantly greater than the potential exposure to Defendant 7055 COLDWATER CANYON AVENUE, LLC.”

Mr. Kerr further declares: “$100,000 is more than a proportional share of the potential liability to Defendant 7055 COLDWATER CANYON AVENUE, LLC.” In support of this, Mr. Kerr cites the findings of certified industrial hygienist Brian Daly, who suggests that Plaintiffs’ habitability claims based on alleged mold exposure lack merit.” This Court finds that the $100,000 settlement amount bears a reasonable relationship to Coldwater’s proportionate share of liability. Furthermore, the Court finds that, given the Unruh act claims, Shind’s potential liability exceeds that of Coldwater. There is nothing in the record to suggest that the settlement was entered into in bad faith or with an intent to injure Shind’s interests.

In response, Shind’s counsel argues that Shind began making repairs and improvements to the property after it assumed ownership. He also argues that Shind did not discriminate, harass or retaliate against Plaintiffs based on a brief portion of Plaintiff Julio Santos Mayorga’s deposition testimony. The Court finds Mr. Mayorga’s apparent testimony that the eviction notices were not sent because of discrimination, harassment, or retaliation to be speculative and lacking in foundation. These are ultimate questions for the finder of fact and not something a witness can offer an opinion about. The Court finds that Shind has failed to meet its burden of showing that the proposed settlement is “so far out of the ballpark…as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute.”

IV.     CONCLUSION

          The Court grants the motion for determination of good faith settlement. 

Counsel for Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling.

Case Number: 23AHCV0179    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is a personal injury case arising from a car accident. Plaintiff Craig Renwick alleges that Defendant Dolores Marie Carrey hit him with her car as he was crossing the street on November 6, 2022.  

The complaint contains two causes of action: (1) motor vehicle negligence and (2) negligence.

Plaintiff Renwick filed the instant motion for summary adjudication on March 1, 2024. He seeks summary adjudication of Defendant’s three affirmative defenses. Defendant filed an opposition on May 23, 2024. Renwick submitted a reply on May 30, 2024.

The motion is GRANTED.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Summary adjudication may be granted where it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim of damages, or an issue of duty. (CCP § 437c(f).) A Plaintiff moving for summary adjudication of an affirmative defense has the initial burden to make a prima facie showing that that the affirmative defense is without merit.  (Continental Ins. Co. v. Columbus Line, Inc. (2003) 107 Cal. App. 4th 1190, 1199-1200;  See's Candy Shops, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 889, 900  [“The plaintiff bears the initial burden to show there is no triable issue of material fact as to the defense and that he or she is entitled to judgment on the defense as a matter of law. In so doing, the plaintiff must negate an essential element of the defense or establish the defendant does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain evidence needed to support the defense”];  Kendall-Jackson Winery, Ltd. v. Sup.Ct. (1999) 76 Cal. App. 4th 970, 977.)

          Unopposed declarations must be accepted as true. (CCP § 437c(e).) However, affidavits must be liberally construed in favor of the opposition and strictly construed against the moving party in determining the existence of a "triable issue" of fact. (D'Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

III.     ANALYSIS

i. Undisputed Material Facts

Pursuant to the separate statement, Carrey admitted in discovery that she had no facts or witnesses to support the first affirmative defense, comparative negligence. (UMFs 5-7.) Carrey admitted in discovery that she had no facts or witnesses to support the second affirmative defense, liability apportionment. (UMFs 8-10.) Carrey admitted in discovery that she had no facts or witnesses to support the third affirmative defense, proposition 51. (UMFs 11-13.)

In response to the separate statement, Defendant argues that she had already agreed to withdraw these defenses, on February 29, 2024. (Response to Separate Statement UMFs 5-14.)

ii. Motion for Summary Adjudication of Carrey’s Affirmative Defenses

Plaintiff contends that Defendant has provided factually devoid discovery responses in support of these affirmative defenses. A moving party may rely on factually devoid discovery responses to shift the burden of proof.¿¿(Collin v. CalPortland Co.¿(2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 582, 587;¿Union Bank v. Superior Court¿(1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590.)¿ However, the discovery must be sufficiently comprehensive¿to¿establish that the opposing party either does not then have, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence on a necessary element of their claim. (Collin, supra, 228 Cal.App.4th at 587 [“(D)efendant¿may show through factually devoid discovery responses that the plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”];¿Weber v. John Crane, Inc.¿(2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1441–1442 [defendant does not satisfy its burden of proof by producing discovery responses that do not exclude the possibility that plaintiffs may possess or may reasonably obtain evidence sufficient to establish their claim];¿Scheiding¿v. Dinwiddie Const. Co.¿(1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 80–81.)¿¿“There must be some ‘affirmative showing’ by the moving defendant that plaintiff could not obtain such evidence, before summary judgment would be proper.”¿¿(Krantz v. BT Visual Images, LLC¿(2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 164, 173.)

Plaintiff provides Carrey’s discovery responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and excerpts of Carrey’s deposition testimony. (See Genson Decl. Exhs. C, D.) Carrey responded to the discovery requests that she did not have any facts supporting her affirmative defenses, nor witnesses nor documents. (See Genson Decl. Exh. C: Special Interrogatories Nos. 23-25, 26-28, 29-31.)

In her opposition, Carrey does not oppose the merits of the motion but urges the Court that she had previously agreed to withdraw these defenses. (See Opposition p. 5: 21-28.) This is supported by the declaration of her counsel, Peter J. Gates, who attaches email correspondence with Plaintiff’s counsel indicating that Defendant will withdraw the defenses. (See Gates Decl. Exh. D.) From the record, it appears that Plaintiff’s counsel emailed the request to remove the defenses on February 13, 2024, Defense counsel responded on February 29, 2024, and this motion was filed by Plaintiff’s counsel on March 1, 2024. (Gates Decl. Exhs. C and D.) Indeed, Defendant also filed a motion for leave to amend her answer and remove the three affirmative defenses. (See Gates Decl. Exh. E.) Contrary to Defendant's assertions, this course of events does not render the motion moot.

Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication of Defendant’s 1st, 2nd and third affirmative defenses is granted.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

           Plaintiff Craig Renwick’s motion for summary adjudication is GRANTED. Counsel for Renwick to give notice.
