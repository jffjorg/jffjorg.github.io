DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STCV42104    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 2, 2024                     JUDGMENT: August 8, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Ozelia Harris v. Pomona Unified School District, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV42104           

 

(1)   MOTION FOR NEW TRIAL

(2)   MOTION FOR JUDGMENT NOTWITHSTANDING THE VERDICT

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1)(2) Plaintiff Ozelia Harris

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1)(2) Defendants Pomona Unified School District and Rebecca Norwood

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         11/03/20: Complaint filed.

·         05/30/24: Jury verdict rendered for Defendants and against Plaintiff.

·         08/08/24: Judgment entered on special verdict.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            In this action, Plaintiff alleges Defendants engaged in retaliation and harassment against her based on her use of medical leave and/or her perceived disability. On May 30, 2024, the jury returned a verdict for Defendants and against Plaintiff, finding that Plaintiff was not subjected to an adverse employment action and was not harassed based on a perceived disability.

 

Plaintiff moves for new trial and for judgment notwithstanding the verdict.  

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for New Trial is DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict is DENIED.

 

//

 

//

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion for New Trial

 

            Plaintiff moves for a new trial.

 

            A motion for new trial must be served and filed either “(1) After the decision is rendered and before the entry of judgment” or “(2) Within 15 days of the date of mailing notice of entry of judgment by the clerk of the court pursuant to Section 664.5, or service upon him or her by any party of written notice of entry of judgment, or within 180 days after the entry of judgment, whichever is earliest.” (Code Civ Proc. § 659.) The Court’s authority to rule on a motion for new trial expires “75 days after the mailing of notice of entry of judgment by the clerk of the court pursuant to Section 664.5 or 75 days after service on the moving party by any party of written notice of entry of judgment, whichever is earlier, or if that notice has not been given, 75 days after the filing of the first notice of intention to move for a new trial.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 660.)

 

Judgment was entered on the jury verdict on August 8, 2024. (Judgment; see also August 8, 2024 Minute Order.) Plaintiff served and filed her Notice of Intent to Move for New Trial on August 22, 2024, exactly 14 days later. Plaintiff therefore gave timely notice of intent to move for new trial. However, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 659a, Plaintiff was required to serve and file her papers in support of that motion within 10 days, i.e., no later than September 2, 2024. (Code Civ. Proc. § 659a; see also Code Civ. Proc. § 12 [extension of time when deadline falls on holiday].) The 10-day period for filing may be extended a further 10 days by stipulation or by order of the Court upon good cause shown by affidavits. (Code Civ. Proc. § 659a.) Here, however, Plaintiff filed and served her supporting papers on September 4, 2024, unsupported by any affidavit seeking an extension of the time to file the papers and without a stipulation by the parties to extend the time to respond. Although the timing requirements of section 659a are not jurisdictional (Kabran v. Sharp Memorial Hospital (2017) 2 Cal.5th 330, 342), the Court has discretion to disregard materials which are not timely filed. (Fernandez v. Consolidated Fisheries, Inc. (1953) 117 Cal.App.2d 254, 264.) As Plaintiff did not request an extension and has not demonstrated good cause for any retroactive extension, the Court refuses to consider Plaintiff’s supporting papers.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for New Trial is therefore DENIED.

 

Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict

 

            Plaintiff moves for judgment notwithstanding the verdict on the grounds that the jury’s findings that Plaintiff was neither subjected to an adverse employment nor harassed on the basis of a disability were unsupported by substantial evidence.

 

//

 

 

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 629 states that “[t]he court, before the expiration of its power to rule on a motion for a new trial, either of its own motion, after five days’ notice, or on motion of a party against whom a verdict has been rendered, shall render judgment in favor of the aggrieved party notwithstanding the verdict whenever a motion for a directed verdict for the aggrieved party should have been granted had a previous motion been made.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 629.)

 

            A JNOV motion ordinarily challenges whether the evidence was sufficient to prove the claims or defenses asserted by the opposing party and now embodied in the jury’s verdict. (Moore v. San Francisco (1970) 5 Cal.App.3d 728, 733-34; see Martin v. Ideal Packing Co. (1957) 156 Cal.App.2d 232, 235 [a JNOV motion is “in the nature of a demurrer to the evidence”].) It thus has the same function as a motion for nonsuit or directed verdict, the only difference being that the JNOV motion lies after a verdict for the opposing party has been rendered. (Beavers v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 310, 327; CC-California Plaza Assocs. v. Paller & Goldstein (1996) 51 Cal.App.4th 1042, 1050.)   

 

            All evidence supporting the verdict is presumed true, so the issue is whether the facts then constitute a prima facie case or defense as a matter of law.  (Fountain Valley Chateau Blanc Homeowner's Ass’n v. Department of Veterans Affairs (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 743, 750.) “The court may not weigh evidence, draw inferences contrary to the verdict, or assess the credibility of witnesses. The court must deny the motion if there is any substantial evidence to support the verdict.” (Begnal v. Canfield & Assocs., Inc. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 66, 72.  

 

            A JNOV motion is governed by the same rules that govern a motion for directed verdict or nonsuit. (Hauter v. Zogarts (1975) 14 Cal.3d 104, 110; Rollenhagen v. City of Orange (1981) 116 Cal.App.3d 414, 417 [disapproved on other grounds in Brown v. Kelly Broadcasting Co. (1989) 48 Cal.3d 711, 738].) Further, a JNOV motion “may be granted only if it appears from the evidence, viewed in the light most favorable to the party securing the verdict, that there is no substantial evidence in support.” (Sweatman v. Department of Veterans Affairs (2001) 25 Cal.4th 62, 68; Wolf v. Walt Disney Pictures & Television (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1107, 1137-38.) A JNOV in favor of a plaintiff is proper “only where, disregarding conflicting evidence on behalf of the [plaintiff] and giving to [defendant]'s evidence all the value to which it is legally entitled, therein indulging in every legitimate inference which may be drawn from that evidence, the result is a determination that there is no evidence of sufficient substantiality to support a verdict in favor of the [defendant].” (See Reynolds v. Wilson (1958) 51 Cal.2d 94, 99.)  

 

Jurisdiction to Rule on Motion

 

            The purpose of a JNOV motion “is to allow a party to prevail as a matter of law where the relevant evidence is already in.”  (DLI Properties LLC v. Hill (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th Supp. 1, 6; see Sukoff v. Lemkin (1988) 202 Cal.App.3d 740, 743 [“The purpose of a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict is not to afford a review of the jury’s deliberation but to prevent a miscarriage of justice in those cases where the verdict rendered is without foundation.”].)  Nonetheless, a trial court's discretion in granting a JNOV motion is severely limited.  (Garretson v. Harold I. Miller (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 563, 568.)

 

            A motion for JNOV must be decided by the trial court within 75 days after the clerk’s service of notice of entry of the judgment. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 629(b), 659, 660(c); see Palmer v. GTE California, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 1265, 1277.)  

 

[Code of Civil Procedure section 629] specifically limits a trial court’s power to rule on the motion in the following ways: First, the trial court must enter j. n. o. v. ‘before . . . expiration of its power to rule on a motion for a new trial,’ otherwise the judgment is invalid.  Section 660 indicates that ‘the power of the court to rule on a motion for a new trial shall expire [75] days from and after the mailing of notice of entry of judgment by the clerk of the court . . .’ Second, on the trial court's own motion it can grant j. n. o. v., but only after giving five days notice to the parties and only in accordance with the procedure just noted.  Third, the trial court is precluded from ruling on a motion for j. n. o. v. ‘until the expiration of the time within which a motion for a new trial must be served and filed . . .’ which according to section 659 is 15 days ‘of the date of mailing notice of entry of judgment . . .’    

 

(Sturgeon v. Leavitt (1979) 94 Cal.App.3d 957, 963 [decided before January 1, 2019, amendment extending time period for court to rule on new trial motion to 75-day period].)  Thus, a trial court “can grant j. n. o. v. so long as it acts before expiration of its power to rule on a motion for a new trial, [75] days, and¿after expiration of the time within which a motion for a new trial must be served and filed, 15 days.” (Id. at 964.)

 

            In this matter, notice of entry of judgment was given on August 8, 2024 on the date of entry. (See August 8, 2024 Minute Order.) Thus, by operation of law, the Court has power to rule on Plaintiff’s motion through October 22, 2024, which is 75 days from that date. (Code Civ. Proc. § 660(b)-(c).) Plaintiff’s motion, filed and served on August 20, 2024, before the Notice of Intention, is therefore timely.

 

Defendants’ Untimely Opposition

 

            Plaintiff served and filed her motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict on August 20, 2024. Defendants filed and served their opposition to this motion on September 13, 2024. Any papers in opposition to a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict must be served and filed within 10 days of service of the moving papers. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 629(b); 659a.) The 10-day period for filing may be extended a further 10 days by stipulation or by order of the Court upon good cause shown by affidavits. (Code Civ. Proc. § 659a.) No stipulation appears in the record, and Defendants, like Plaintiff on the new trial motion, do not account for their untimely filings. The Court therefore refuses to consider the untimely opposition to the motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict and will confine its ruling to the moving papers and the Court’s own knowledge of the proceedings in this case.

 

Retaliation

 

            Plaintiff first argues that the jury’s finding that Plaintiff was not subjected to an adverse employment action, and, therefore, that Defendants were not liable on the first cause of action for retaliation, was not supported by substantial evidence.

 

To prevail on a claim for retaliation under the Fair Employment and Housing Act, a Plaintiff must establish that “(1) he or she engaged in a 'protected activity,' (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer's action.” (Yanowitz v. L'Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1042 [internal citations omitted].) An adverse employment action must materially affect the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment to be actionable. (Id. at 1052.) However, a pattern of systematic injuries that do not individually affect the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment and that may not, individually, constitute adverse employment actions may constitute an adverse employment action collectively. (Id. at 1055-56.)

 

After trial, the jury found that Plaintiff engaged in protected activity by taking medical leave in January 2020 and by submitting a Uniform Complaint against Defendant Norwood to Defendant Pomona Unified School District on August 24, 2020. (Special Verdict Form Nos. 1A-1B.) The jury concluded, however, that Defendant Norwood did not engage in conduct which, taken as a whole, materially and adversely affected the terms and conditions of Plaintiff’s employment. (Id. No. 2.) Plaintiff argues that this finding was not supported by substantial evidence because she elicited testimony of numerous events which, taken as a whole, materially and adversely affected the terms and conditions of Plaintiff’s employment. Specifically, Plaintiff contends that the evidence showed that Defendant Norwood:

 

(1)   interfered with Plaintiff’s remote classroom by keeping the substitute teacher’s classroom active (Plaintiff’s Exh. 10 p.14:28-15:6; 19:25-20:3 [Testimony of Plaintiff; May 28, 2024 Trial Transcript]),

(2)   did not give Plaintiff supplies for distance learning (Id. p.109:7-14),

(3)   excluded Plaintiff from a staff meeting in May 2020 (Exh. 6 p.74:12-23 [Testimony of Defendant Norwood; May 21, 2024 Trial Transcript]),

(4)   repeatedly insisted that Plaintiff required support (Exh. 8 p.26:15-17, 122:8-13 [Testimony of Defendant Norwood; May 23, 2024 Trial Transcript]; Exh. 10 p.15:26-16:10, 18:16-18, 20:4-16),

(5)   repeatedly observed Plaintiff on Zoom (Trial Exhs. 75-1-3),

(6)   issued a written warning against Plaintiff on October 6, 2020 stating that the District had received several complaints from parents regarding the conduct of Plaintiff’s Zoom classes (Trial Exhs. 96 [complaints], 102-001 [Warning]; Plaintiff’s Exh. 7 p.34:26-37:28, 40:27 [Norwood Testimony; May 22, 2024 Trial Transcript]; Exh. 8 p.130:14-19 [Norwood Testimony; May 23, 2024 Trial Transcript]) and,

(7)   violated the District’s Progressive Discipline Policy and the Memorandum of Understanding between the District and its teachers by not giving a verbal warning before giving a written warning. (Exh. 8 p.164:5-13 [Norwood Testimony, May 23, 2024 Trial Transcript).

 

            While the evidence identified in the motion could arguably support a finding in Plaintiff’s favor, that same evidence could also support the finding that the jury reached—i.e., that, even taken collectively, the evidence did not establish that the terms and conditions of Plaintiff’s employment were materially and adversely affected. Much of the conduct described, including repeated observation of remote classes, failure to provide supplies for distance learning, insistence that Plaintiff needs support, and keeping a substitute’s remote classroom active, could fairly be described as “negligeable,” at best. Moreover, although Plaintiff argues that the written warning is premised on false assertions or events beyond Plaintiff’s control, Plaintiff offers no explanation for that conclusion. Further still, Plaintiff’s description of the testimony and evidence as to the calls from parents is highly misleading, as the testimony produced by Plaintiff describes numerous issues with remote classrooms reported by the parents. (See Trial Exh. 96.) It is not a false statement to characterize these calls as “complaints,” as they are labeled in the Warning and in Defendant Norwood’s testimony. (Exh. 102-001; Plaintiff’s Exh. 7 p.34:26-37:28, 40:27 [Norwood Testimony; May 22, 2024 Trial Transcript]; Exh. 8 p.130:14-19 [Norwood Testimony; May 23, 2024 Trial Transcript].)

 

As to the impact of the Warning itself, the Court’s August 11, 2022 finding that the Warning could constitute an adverse employment action by itself in ruling on Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is of no significance here. (See August 11, 2022 Minute Order.) That prior ruling applied the operative standards for ruling on a motion for summary judgment, where all evidence was required to be construed in the light most favorable to Plaintiff as the non-moving party. (See August 11, 2022 Minute Order; Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Here, however, the Court is required to apply the opposite construction, construing the evidence in the light most favorable to Defendants as the prevailing parties at trial. Applying that construction, the Court cannot conclude that there is no substantial evidence supporting the jury’s implicit verdict that the Warning does not establish an adverse employment action standing on its own.

 

Plaintiff’s argument, in essence, is that the jury erred in finding that this conduct did not collectively constitute an adverse employment action. That contention is insufficient on a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict where the evidence could at best be considered ambiguous. The Court is not empowered to disregard the findings of the jury as to the proper weight of the evidence presented. For that reason, Plaintiff has not demonstrated that the jury’s finding as to an adverse employment action was unsupported by substantial evidence.

 

//

Harassment

 

            Plaintiff also contends that the jury’s finding as to the issue of harassment was not supported by substantial evidence.

 

To prevail on a claim for disability harassment, Plaintiff must establish that (1) she was a member of a protected class; (2) she was subjected to unwelcome harassment; (3) the harassment was based on disability; and (4) the harassment unreasonably interfered with her work performance by creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment. (Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 876.)

 

Here, the jury found that Plaintiff was not subjected to harassing conduct based on a perceived disability. (Special Verdict No. 6.) Plaintiff contends, for the same reasons addressed above, that the jury’s finding was not supported by substantial evidence. For the reasons stated above, the Court finds this contention unpersuasive.

 

As both of Plaintiff’s challenges to the jury’s verdict are without merit, the Court finds that Plaintiff is not entitled to judgment notwithstanding the verdict.

 

Conclusion

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for New Trial is DENIED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict is DENIED.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants engaged in extensive self-dealing and misappropriation of corporate assets and improperly excluded Plaintiffs from participating in the operation of nominal Defendant Elysian Health, LLC.  

 

Defendants White, Applied Approach Services, Inc., Manion, & Bifulco demur to the ninth cause of action for dissolution in the Fifth Amended Complaint.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendants White, Applied Approach Services, Inc., Manion, & Bifulco’s Demurrer to the Fifth Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

            Defendants Gerry & Lisa Van Nortwick’s Joinder to the Demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Demurrer to Fifth Amended Complaint

 

Defendants White, Applied Approach Services, Inc., Manion, & Bifulco demur to the ninth cause of action for dissolution in the Fifth Amended Complaint.

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged. (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true”].) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet-and-confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of George S. Burns in support of the Demurrer states that counsel for the parties met and conferred via telephone on April 10, 2024 regarding the issues raised in the demurrer, but were unable to reach a resolution. (Declaration of George S. Burns ISO Demurrer ¶ 2.) Defendants have thus satisfied their statutory meet-and-confer obligations.

 

//

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of the Declaration of Zachary Ament filed in support of Plaintiffs’ ex parte application for a Temporary Restraining Order on November 17, 2022, and certain verified admissions contained therein. This document is not relevant to the Court’s ruling, and Defendants’ request is therefore DENIED. (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 (“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Ninth Cause of Action: Judicial Dissolution

 

            Defendants demur to the ninth cause of action for judicial dissolution of Elysian Health, LLC.

 

            Corporations Code section 17707.03 permits a member or manager of a limited liability company to commence an action seeking a decree of dissolution of the company when specified events occur. (Corp. Code § 17707.03(a).) Circumstances which warrant dissolution include, as relevant here, when “dissolution is reasonably necessary for the protection of the rights or interests of the complaining members” (Id. subd. (b)(2)), “the management of the limited liability company is deadlocked or subject to internal dissension” (Id. subd. (b)(4)), or “those in control of the limited liability company have been guilty of, or have knowingly countenanced, persistent and pervasive fraud, mismanagement, or abuse of authority.” (Id. subd. (b)(5).)

 

            As alleged in the pleadings, nominal defendant Elysian Health, LLC is a member-managed limited liability company (5AC ¶ 3.) Plaintiff Modern Management Solutions, Inc., (“MM”) is the manager of Elysian and holds a 42%-member interest. (¶ 1.) Plaintiff Ament is the principal and shareholder of MM, and is also a director of Elysian. (¶ 2.) The other members of Elysian are Defendants Applied, White, and LAVN Investment QSST U/A DTD 9/1/2009, through its trustee, Lisa Van Nortwick. (5AC ¶ 4-6.) Defendant Gerry Van Nortwick and Plaintiff Ament are directors of Elysian. (5AC ¶ 2, 6.) Defendant White is principal and shareholder of Applied and CFO and co-CEO of Elysian. (¶ 5.) Defendant Manion is Elysian’s Executive Director. (5AC ¶¶ 7-8.) Defendant Bifulco is Chief Operating Officer of Elysian. (5AC ¶ 9.)

 

`           Plaintiffs allege that Defendant White led the other Defendants in a scheme to usurp control of Elysian and misappropriate its assets and funds for their own gain, at the expense of the corporation’s ability to operate as a going concern. (See ¶¶ 24-26.) Plaintiffs claim that they were excluded from corporate meetings and affairs (¶¶ 27-28), that Defendant White exploited Ament’s grief over the death of Ament’s husband to improperly influence Plaintiff to support decisions favorable to Defendants (¶¶ 29-38), that Defendants engaged in self-dealing (¶¶ 39-40), and that Defendants excluded Plaintiffs from access to company records (¶ 41), hindered the corporation’s ability to regain essential licensing which had been revoked (¶¶ 42-46), improperly attempted to remove MM as manager (¶ 49), and started a separate business in competition with Elysian. (¶ 51.) Plaintiff MM therefore seeks dissolution of Elysian under subdivision (b)(2), (b)(4), and (b)(5) of Corporations Code section 17707.03. (5AC ¶ 102.)

 

            Defendants contend that the Fifth Amended Complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for dissolution because the circumstances described in the pleadings are not sufficient to warrant relief. Defendants’ sole authority for this position is Stuparich v. Harbor Furniture Manufacturing, a 2000 appellate opinion affirming dismissal of an action for dissolution on summary judgment because the evidence presented did not establish sufficiently grave circumstances to warrant the drastic remedy of dissolution. (Stuparich v. Harbor Furniture Manufacturing (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 1268, 1278-79.) Defendants have not brought a summary judgment motion. Defendants have brought a demurrer. Stuparich is therefore inapposite, and the Court is not persuaded by Defendants’ otherwise-unsupported argument.  Further, the facts offered by the Stuparich plaintiffs in support of their dissolution request amounted to complaints that they were denied meaningful participation in the corporation, that strong animosity had arisen between plaintiff and the majority shareholder, and that plaintiffs had an economic interest in protecting the company against losses incurred in recent years.  (Id., at p. 1277.)  The allegations here go well beyond the evidence offered in Stuparich and include serious contentions of self-dealing, interference with critical licensing necessary for company operations, and creating a competing company that would undermine Elysian’s success. 

 

            Defendants also contend that Plaintiffs are not entitled to assert a “deadlock of management” under subdivision (b)(4) because the Court has already ruled that MM was one of three members of the LLC. (E.g., July 31, 2023 Order Sustaining Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint p.9:4-9.) Although true, the claim that Elysian is “deadlocked” is only one ground asserted for dissolution, and this argument therefore does not suffice to defeat the ninth cause of action in light of the other grounds for dissolution listed in the Fifth Amended Complaint.  

 

            Finally, Defendants contend that there has been a misjoinder of parties because the ninth cause of action does not name Elysian as a defendant to that cause of action. None of the authority cited in the demurrer addresses whether the corporation that is the subject of a request for dissolution must be named as a defendant. (See Stuparich, supra; see also Stumpf v. C.E. Stumpf & Sons, Inc. (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 230.) In fact, the Court has unearthed at least one appellate opinion concerning buyout proceedings pursuant to Corporations Code section 17707.03 where the LLC was not a named party. (Friend of Camden, Inc. v. Brandt (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 1054, 1058 [directing trial court to dismiss buyout proceeding as moot and direct the parties to wind up activities following dissolution].) Defendants have therefore not demonstrated a misjoinder of parties in this respect.

 

            Defendants also assert that the ninth cause of action is improperly asserted against Defendant White and Defendant Gerry van Nortwick, who joins in this demurrer, because neither are alleged to be members of the LLC themselves. A bare conclusion such as this unsupported by legal authority establishing the proper parties is not sufficient to defeat a cause of action.

            For the reasons stated above, the Court finds Defendants’ challenges to the ninth cause of action to be without merit.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Fifth Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

            Defendants Gerry and Lisa Van Nortwick join the Demurrer, contending that the ninth cause of action is deficient for the reasons stated therein. As the Court has overruled the demurrer, the Van Nortwick Defendants’ Joinder to the Demurrer is DENIED.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a wrongful death and products liability action brought by the family of the decedent, Jerry Moss. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants sold a series of dietary supplements which contained hidden active ingredients that caused the decedent’s death.

 

Defendant eBay, Inc. moves to compel arbitration.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant eBay, Inc. moves to compel arbitration based on a user agreement containing an arbitration provision to which it contends Plaintiff Kelly Moss[1] assented. \

Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

Under California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. (Blake v. Ecker (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 728, 741 (overruled on other grounds by Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094).) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate, and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)

 

Defendant contends that Kelly consented to arbitration by agreeing to Defendant’s User Agreement when she purchased the products that are alleged to be the cause of the decedent’s death.

 

An agreement to arbitration must be mutual as “[t]here is indeed a strong policy in favor of enforcing agreements to arbitrate, but there is no policy compelling persons to accept arbitration of controversies which they have not agreed to arbitrate…”  (Freeman v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1975) 14 Cal.3d 473, 481.)  “This requirement applies with equal force to arbitration provisions contained in contracts purportedly formed over the Internet.”  (Long v. Provide Commerce, Inc. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 855, 862.)  “Federal law is wholly congruent with these principles.”  (Fleming v. Oliphant Financial, LLC (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 13, 19.) As the Court of Appeal stated in Sellers v. JustAnswer LLC:

 

Most courts now have identified at least four types of internet contract formation, most easily defined by the way in which the user purportedly gives their assent to be bound by the associated terms: browsewraps, clickwraps, scrollwraps, and sign-in wraps.  A “browsewrap” agreement is one in which an internet user accepts a website's terms of use merely by browsing the site. A “clickwrap” agreement is one in which an internet user accepts a website's terms of use by clicking an “I agree” or “I accept” button, with a link to the agreement readily available. A “scrollwrap” agreement is like a “clickwrap,” but the user is presented with the entire agreement and must physically scroll to the bottom of it to find the “I agree” or “I accept” button.... “Sign-in-wrap” agreements are those in which a user signs up to use an internet product or service, and the sign-up screen states that acceptance of a separate agreement is required before the user can access the service. While a link to the separate agreement is provided, users are not required to indicate that they have read the agreement's terms before signing up.’ [Citations.]” 

 

(Sellers v. JustAnswer LLC (2021) 73 Cal.App.5th 444, 463-64.) Scrollwrap and clickwrap agreements are generally found to be enforceable, and browsewrap agreements generally are not. (Id. at 466.) “[T]he Long court and federal courts have reached consistent conclusions when evaluating the enforceability of agreements at either end of the spectrum, generally finding scrollwrap and clickwrap agreements to be enforceable and browsewrap agreements to be unenforceable.”  (Sellers, supra, 73 Cal.App.5th at 466.) “[T]he onus must be on website owners to put users on notice of the terms to which they wish to bind consumers.” (Long, supra, 245 Cal.App.4th at 867.)

 

According to Defendant, Kelly created an eBay user account on March 21, 2002, and, in doing so, agreed to eBay’s then-operative User Agreement, which contained an arbitration provision. (Declaration of Nic Conlin ISO Mot. ¶ 9; Exh. 4.) Defendant provides no evidence of Kelly’s assent to that agreement beyond the assertion that an agreement is reflected in eBay’s user account records. (Id.) In August 2012, Defendant updated its User Agreement and sent a message to all users’ My eBay Message Centers advising them of the changes to the Agreement. (Id. ¶¶ 10-11; Exh. 5.) Defendant contends that users receive notices sent to their registered email alerting them that “a message . . . was posted to the My eBay Message Center,” but does not state that the email notification reflected or referenced changes to the Agreement. (Id. ¶ 10.) The revised Agreement required users to mail a written Opt-Out notice on or before November 9, 2012 if they wished to reject the modified Agreement to Arbitrate included as part of the User Agreement. (Id. ¶¶ 12-14; Exh. 6.) Defendant also states that users must agree to the User Agreement as part of executing a transaction on the website, as the button to complete a purchase is accompanied by text which states “By placing your order, you agree to eBay’s User Agreement and Privacy Notice.” (Conlin Decl. ¶ 7.) The “User Agreement” and “Privacy Notice” are hyperlinked to the documents bearing those titles. (Id.) Although Defendant provides a screenshot of the button and notice, Defendant does not provide the context in which it appears. (Id.)

 

Defendant does not clearly indicate the form of the User Agreement with respect to the framework described in Sellers. That said, the confirmation page suggests that the agreement is, at best, a sign-in-wrap, rather than a clickwrap or scrollwrap. Under Sellers, the Court must therefore look to the context in which notice of the Agreement was presented and determine whether a reasonably prudent offeree would be on inquiry notice of the terms at issue. (Sellers, supra, 73 Cal.App.5th at 461.) The Court is not satisfied by Defendant’s production. Defendant offers a bare assertion that Kelly agreed to the 2002 Agreement, a second-hand description of an email “notification” regarding revisions to that agreement posted on a separate website message board without any detail in the email as to the contents of the revisions, and a highly-cropped and contextless screenshot of a webpage with a link to a User Agreement. To make matters worse, this webpage is described as the current version of the checkout page, not the 2022 page that Kelly actually confronted when she bought the products through eBay.  (Conlin Decl., ¶ 7.)  This meager showing is not sufficient to demonstrate even on a prima facie basis that Kelly assented to the arbitration agreement or was on constructive—let alone actual—notice of its existence.

 

Defendant has not demonstrated that Plaintiff Kelly Moss was on notice of any agreement to arbitrate, and therefore has not demonstrated her consent to such an agreement. Although not necessary to its conclusion, the Court also notes that Plaintiff denies any knowledge of the 2012 email notification of a revised arbitration agreement, the website message about a revised arbitration clause and need to opt out, or the revised 2012 User Agreement. (Declaration of Kelly Moss, ¶¶ 4-5.) Because there is no evidence that Plaintiff received notice of the relevant arbitration agreement or that she agreed to be bound by it, Defendant is not entitled to an order compelling arbitration.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.
