DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24NNCV00523    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANTS DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFFS’ COMPLAINT and DENYING DEFENDANTS MOTION TO STRIKE.

 I.         INTRODUCTION

This action arises from an incident in which Plaintiffs Alberto Erick Shum Cen, Yan Li, and their minor son, Anson Cen (“Anson”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege that Anson sustained severe injuries from an unstable lazy Susan and hot teapot at Defendants’ restaurant. 

Plaintiffs filed this action against Defendants Boston Lobster Inc., a California corporation, and Li Shou Ma (“Defendants”) on March 27, 2024, asserting the following causes of actions:(1) Negligence, (2) Premises Liability, (3) Breach of Implied Warranty of Safety, (4) Strict Product Liability, (5) Vicarious Liability, (6) Attractive Nuisance Doctrine, (7) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, and (8) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

On July 9, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer along with a motion to strike. Plaintiffs filed an opposition to the demurrer on July 29, 2024, and an opposition to the motion to strike on August 14, 2024.

Moving party’s arguments

Defendants demur to the entire complaint, arguing that Plaintiff Anson Cen, a minor born on October 18, 2018, lacks the legal capacity to sue because no guardian ad litem has been properly appointed. Defendants assert that although the complaint identifies Alberto Erick Shum Cen and Yan Li as guardian’s ad litem, there is no formal petition or court order confirming this appointment, thereby rendering the complaint defective under Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(b).

Additionally, Defendants demur to the third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh and eighth causes of action.

Opposition arguments

Plaintiffs oppose Defendants’ demurrer, arguing that they have the capacity to sue as the Complaint identifies Anson’s parents as his guardian’s ad litem, which they assert is sufficient to establish this capacity. Plaintiffs further contend that they have sufficiently alleged the third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.

Reply arguments

Defendants reiterate the same arguments in reply.

Meet and confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, on June 5, 2024, Defendants' counsel, Alvin Andrade, contacted Plaintiffs' counsel, Peter S. Deng, by telephone to discuss the possible demurrer and motion to strike portions of Plaintiffs complaint. Defendants' counsel inquired whether Plaintiffs would consider withdrawing the challenged causes of action and the punitive damages allegations to avoid filing the demurrer and motion to strike. Plaintiffs' counsel indicated he would need to consult with colleagues and would respond regarding possible withdrawal (Andrade Decl., ¶¶ 4-5). Following this discussion, Plaintiffs’ counsel agreed to an automatic 30-day extension for Defendants to file their responsive pleadings. Defendants' counsel filed the declaration for extension on June 6, 2024, thereby extending the due date for the demurrer and motion to strike to July 10, 2024 (Andrade Decl., ¶¶ 5-6).

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

A.    General Demurrer – Failure to State Facts Sufficient to Constitute a Cause of Action

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s Complaint based on (1) lack of capacity to sue as to the entire complaint and, alternatively, (2) failure to state sufficient facts for the third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.

Lack of capacity to sue

Defendants demur to the entire complaint, arguing that Plaintiff Anson Cen, a minor born on October 18, 2018, lacks the legal capacity to sue because no guardian ad litem has been properly appointed. Defendants contend that, although the complaint identifies Alberto Erick Shum Cen and Yan Li as guardians ad litem, there is no formal petition or court order establishing this appointment. Thus, Defendants argue that the complaint is defective, and the demurrer should be sustained under Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(b). (Demurrer pp.7-8)

In opposition, Plaintiffs asserts that the complaint clearly designates Alberto and Yan as guardian’s ad litem, which, Plaintiffs argues, establishes the necessary capacity to sue.

The Court finds that Defendants argument regarding Plaintiff Anson Cen's lack of capacity to sue based on the alleged absence of a formal appointment of guardian ad litem does not justify sustaining a demurrer to the entire complaint. Although Defendants contend that the complaint is defective under Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(b) due to a lack of formal petition or court order for guardian’s ad litem, the Court finds that the designation of Alberto Erick Shum Cen and Yan Li as guardians ad litem in the complaint suffices at this stage. The issue does not appear on the face of the complaint to warrant dismissal. Therefore, the Court overrules Defendants' demurrer on this ground.

Third cause of action: Breach of Implied Warranty of Safety

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for breach of implied warranty of safety, arguing that it is duplicative of the negligence and premises liability causes of action. Defendants assert that the allegations and relief sought in this claim are identical to those in the negligence and premises liability claims. Additionally, Defendants contend that breach of implied warranty of safety is a fictitious cause of action without recognized legal elements. (Demurrer pp.9-10)

Plaintiffs argues that the complaint sufficiently details Defendants breach of an implied warranty of safety owed to their patrons. Plaintiffs asserts that, by operating the restaurant, Defendants implicitly warranted that all aspects of the dining environment would be safe for use.

The Court finds that no recognized cause of action exists for breach of implied warranty of safety. Plaintiff’s reliance on Soule v. General Motors Corp. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 548 is misplaced, as this case does not establish such a cause of action. Furthermore, a demurrer may be sustained when a cause of action is duplicative of another cause of action and “adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery.” (Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 501; see also Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290). Here, the third cause of action repeats substantially similar allegations as those in the negligence and premises liability claims, specifically that “Defendant negligently provided a dangerous and defective condition” (Complaint ¶ 50) and that the injuries resulted as a “direct and proximate result of the breach” (Complaint ¶ 51).

Thus, the Court SUSTAINS the Defendants demurrer as to this cause of action without leave to amend.

Fourth cause of action: Strict Product Liability

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action, arguing that the complaint fails to state a cause of action. To establish strict products liability, a plaintiff must allege that the defendant “produced, manufactured, sold, or was in some way responsible for the product.” (Garcia v. Joseph Vince Co. (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 868, 874.)¿¿A defendant may be liable if the defendant “play[ed] an integral role in the producing and marketing enterprise of [the light fixtures, lighting components, and other lighting products and accessories] and . . . profit[ed] from placing the products into the stream of commerce.” (Bay Summit Community Association v. Shell Oil. Co. (1996) 51 Cal.App.4th 762, 773-74.) 

Here, the complaint fails to meet these elements as it does not allege that Defendants produced, manufactured, or sold the lazy Susan and metal teapot. While the complaint claims that the lazy Susan and metal teapot were defective and posed a danger to consumers—including Plaintiff Anson Cen—due to instability and lack of adequate safety features (Complaint ¶ 56), it does not allege that Defendants were involved in the production, manufacturing, or sale of these items. Consequently, the complaint lacks the necessary allegations to support a strict products liability claim against Defendants.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the Defendants demurrer as to this cause of action with 20 days leave to amend.

Fifth cause of action: Vicarious Liability

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action, arguing that the complaint fails to state a cause of action. Under California law, A principal may be vicariously liable for injury committed by an act of its agent where (1) the principal directly authorizes the act to be committed, (2) the agent commits the act in the scope of their agency and in performing service on behalf of the principal, or (3) the principal ratifies its agent's conduct after the fact by electing to adopt the agent's conduct. Doe v. Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 953, 969.

Defendants argue that vicarious liability is not a standalone cause of action but rather a theory of liability. They contend that Plaintiffs has already alleged Defendants liability for the actions of their employees in the negligence and premises liability causes of action. Since the underlying allegations and relief sought in the vicarious liability claim are identical to those in the negligence and premises liability claims, Defendants assert that this cause of action is redundant and should be dismissed. (Demurrer p.10)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argues that the employees of Defendants acted negligently within the scope of their employment, thereby establishing a basis for vicarious liability. Plaintiffs specifically alleges that Defendants employees were acting within the scope of their employment (Complaint ¶ 64) and that the act of placing the hot teapot on an unstable lazy Susan was performed by an employee of Defendants (Complaint ¶ 65).

The Court finds that the vicarious liability allegations are duplicative of the claims already set forth in the negligence and premises liability causes of action. As such, the vicarious liability claim does not introduce distinct factual or legal elements beyond those asserted in the negligence and premises liability causes of action.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Defendants demurrer to the fifth cause of action without leave to amend on the grounds that it is duplicative.

Sixth cause of action: Attractive Nuisance Doctrine

Defendants demur to the sixth cause of action arguing that it fails for multiple reasons: (a) the doctrine no longer applies in California as an independent basis for liability; (b) when applicable, it was linked to negligence theory to address the duty of care but was never intended as a separate cause of action, making it improperly redundant (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1401; Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290; Award Metals, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 1128, 1135); and (c) even if the doctrine were still viable, it would not apply here because Plaintiffs is alleged to be an invitee/patron, not a trespasser (Complaint ¶¶ 11, 14-16, 69-74).

Plaintiffs, in opposition, maintains that the complaint articulates a clear scenario of attractive nuisance, asserting that the hazardous condition posed by the lazy Susan constitutes such a claim.

The Court agrees with Defendants and finds that attractive nuisance no longer serves a cause of action in California. Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action without leave to amend.

 Seventh cause of action: Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (NIED)

“The law of negligent infliction of emotional distress in California is typically analyzed by reference to two theories of recovery: the ‘bystander’ theory and the ‘direct victim’ theory. The negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort, but the tort of negligence. The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply. Whether a defendant owes a duty of care is a question of law.” (Spates v. Dameron Hosp. Ass’n (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 213, ellipses, quotation marks, brackets, and paragraph breaks omitted.) 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs NIED claim does not meet the limited circumstances in which direct victim claims for NIED have been upheld. The California Supreme Court has recognized three specific scenarios for direct victim NIED claims: (1) negligent mishandling of corpses (Christensen v. Sup. Ct. (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868); (2) misdiagnosis of a disease that could potentially harm another (Molien v. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (1980) 27 Cal.3d 916); and (3) negligent breach of a duty arising from a preexisting relationship (Burgess v. Sup. Ct. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1064). Defendants argue that none of these circumstances are present in this case.

Plaintiffs maintain that the complaint clearly supports NIED. Plaintiffs argue that this foreseeability of emotional distress resulting from the incident substantiates their NIED claim, and they allege that witnessing the scalding of their minor child and handling the immediate aftermath caused them serious emotional distress (Complaint ¶ 78). Plaintiffs also allege Defendants owed a duty of care to them and their minor child to act in a way that would not foreseeably cause emotional distress (Complaint ¶ 76). They further claim that their distress was severe, manifesting in physical symptoms and disruptions in daily life, necessitating medical attention and ongoing psychological care (Complaint ¶ 79).

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have conflated the bystander and direct victim theories of NIED by grouping all Plaintiffs together under a single cause of action. Each theory applies to different Plaintiffs—the parents may proceed under the bystander theory, while the minor could proceed under a direct victim theory. However, the complaint fails to distinctly allege each theory for the respective Plaintiffs, resulting in a lack of clarity as to which theory applies to each party.  Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer to this cause of action with 20 days leave to amend.

Eighth cause of action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.) 

Defendants demur to this cause of action, arguing that Plaintiffs have failed to allege conduct rising to the level of “outrageousness” required for an IIED claim. Defendants contend that there are no factual allegations showing conduct so extreme that it exceeds all bounds of decency.

Plaintiffs argue in opposition that Defendants’ failure to remedy known hazards or adequately warn patrons of potential dangers, particularly given the vulnerability of the patrons involved, constitutes extreme and outrageous conduct. Plaintiffs cite their allegations that “Defendants’ acts and omissions amount to extreme and outrageous conduct,” causing them mental anguish and severe emotional distress (Complaint ¶¶ 84, 87).

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have not provided sufficient factual allegations to support a claim of extreme and outrageous conduct that exceeds the bounds of what is typically tolerated in a civilized community. The Complaint lacks specific facts to establish “extreme and outrageous” conduct or to demonstrate that Defendants acted with intent to cause Plaintiffs emotional distress.

The Court SUSTAINS Defendants' demurrer as to this cause of action with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Motion to strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id,, § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

Given that the Court sustained the demurrer to the Complaint with leave to amend, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as MOOT.

       IV.       ORDER

            The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to the third, fourth, fifth, sixth seventh and eighth cause of action with 20 days leave to amend as to causes of action 4, 7 and 8.

Defendants' motion to strike is DENIED as moot.

            Defendants are to give notice.
