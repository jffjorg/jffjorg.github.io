DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV11027    Hearing Date: October 24, 2023    Dept: 39

Luis Venegas, et al. v. Browning-Ferris Industries of California, Inc., et al.

Case No. 21STCV11027

 

Order #1 of 3

Motion for Leave to Amend

 

            Plaintiffs filed this employment discrimination and sexual harassment case against Browning-Ferris Industries of California, Inc. (“BFI”) and the supervisor at issue, Peter Pouwels.  The Court granted summary adjudication of every cause of action against BFI except as follows:

 

            1.         The first cause of action (sexual harassment) by all Plaintiffs

 

            2.         The third cause of action (negligent hiring/retention/supervision) by all Plaintiffs to the extent the claim is predicated upon allegations of sexual harassment

 

            3.         The fifth cause of action (failure to prevent harassment/discrimination) by all Plaintiffs to the extent the claim is predicated upon sexual harassment

 

            4.         The sixth cause of action (retaliation under FEHA) by Luis Venegas to the extent the claim is predicated upon sexual harassment

 

            5.         The seventh and eight causes of action (retaliation under the Labor Code) by Luis Venegas to the extent the claim is predicated upon alleged safety violations

 

            6.         The ninth cause of action (wrongful termination in violation of public policy) by Jake Perry. 

 

            7.         The ninth cause of action (wrongful termination in violation of public policy) by Joshua Garcia to the extent the claim is predicated upon sexual harassment and/or racial harassment by Fred Jones. 

 

(See Court’s Minute Order, dated July 10, 2023.)  The Court denied summary adjudication of the first and second causes of action against Peter Pouwels.  (Ibid.)  The Court also denied summary adjudication of the claim for punitive damages against Pouwels.  (Ibid.)   

 

            This action was filed on March 22, 2021.  The Court ruled on Defendants’ motions for summary adjudication over two years later, on July 10, 2023.  Fact discovery has closed; expert designations/disclosures have been completed; and the expert discovery deadline is November 17, 2023.  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated July 26, 2023.)  The Court previously denied a motion to reopen discovery.  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated September 25, 2023.)  Now, Plaintiffs’ counsel seeks leave to file a first amended complaint to add factual allegations and causes of action. 

 

            The Court grants the motion in part and denies the motion in part.  Specifically, the Court rules as follows:

 

            Paragraph #43 – Granted.  Plaintiffs merely seek to add a factual allegation in support of an existing cause of action, specifically, the ninth cause of action (wrongful termination in violation of public policy) by Joshua Garcia to the extent the claim is predicated upon sexual harassment and/or racial harassment by Fred Jones.  While the Court questions whether this is necessary given the notice pleading standard, there is no prejudice to this amendment, so the motion is granted in this respect.   

 

            Paragraph #45 through Paragraph #53 – Granted in Part; Denied in Part.  Plaintiffs seek to add factual allegations that they were constructively terminated.  The Court grants the motion to the extent Plaintiffs seek to add allegations that they were constructively terminated as a result of the alleged sexual harassment, as those allegations do not unduly expand the scope of the existing complaint, given the first cause of action.  The Court previously suggested it would permit such an amendment to clarify this theory of damages.  (See Declaration of Christopher DeClue, Exh. #4, pp. 17-18.)  Therefore, the motion is granted in this respect.  However, the Court denies the amendment with respect to other factual allegations of constructive termination, e.g., safety violations.  The Court resolved those issues in ruling on Defendants’ motions for summary adjudication, and the new allegations seek to expand the scope of the case, unduly prejudicing Defendants.  Plaintiffs had ample opportunity to develop and add these theories before discovery closed and failed to develop sufficient evidence.  Therefore, the motion is denied in this respect.    

 

            Fifth Cause of Action – Denied.  Plaintiffs seek to add “race and/or national origin” as a predicate to the fifth cause of action.  The Court resolved the issue in ruling on Defendants’ motions for summary adjudication, and the new allegations seek to expand the scope of the case, unduly prejudicing Defendants.  Plaintiffs had ample opportunity to develop and add these theories before discovery closed and failed to do so without good cause.  Therefore, the motion is denied in this respect.   

 

            Ninth Cause of Action – Granted in Part; Denied in Part.  Plaintiffs seek to add causes of action for wrongful termination in violation of public policy by seven additional plaintiffs based upon a constructive termination theory.  The motion is granted only to the extent Plaintiffs seek to raise constructive wrongful termination claims based upon allegations of sexual harassment.  These claims do not unduly expand the scope of the existing case, which has been predicated upon allegations of sexual harassment from the outset.  Because there is no undue prejudice to Defendants, the motion is granted in this respect.  However, the motion is denied to the extent Plaintiffs seek to raise constructive termination claims based upon other factual allegations, like “unsafe . . . working conditions” and “unfair working conditions.”  The Court resolved the issues in ruling on Defendants’ motions for summary adjudication, and the new allegations seek to expand the scope of the case, unduly prejudicing Defendants.  Plaintiffs had ample opportunity to develop and add these theories before discovery closed and failed to develop sufficient evidence.  Therefore, the motion is denied in this respect.  

 

            Tenth Cause of Action – Denied.  Plaintiffs seek to add a cause of action for racial harassment by three plaintiffs.  The Court resolved the issue in ruling on Defendants’ motions for summary adjudication, and the new allegations seek to expand the scope of the case, unduly prejudicing Defendants.  Plaintiffs had ample opportunity to develop and add these theories before discovery closed and failed to do so without good cause.  Therefore, the motion is denied in this respect.  

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Based upon the foregoing, the Court orders as follows:

            2.         The Court authorizes Plaintiffs to file a first amended complaint that clarifies the allegations in support of the first cause of action (sexual harassment) and adds constructive wrongful termination claims based solely upon allegations of sexual harassment.  The Court also authorizes Plaintiff Joshua Garcia to add one factual allegation in support of his wrongful termination claim.

Order #2 of 3

Motion to Bifurcate

 

Plaintiffs filed this employment discrimination and sexual harassment case against Browning-Ferris Industries of California, Inc. (“BFI”) and the supervisor at issue, Peter Pouwels.  Defendants move to bifurcate this case to order separate trials for each plaintiff.  Under Code of Civil Procedure section 598, “The court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby . . . make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case[.]”  (Code Civ. Proc.,  § 598.)  Judicial economy would not be served by having separate trials concerning sexual harassment by the same supervisor.  Defendants argue that there is misjoinder by all ten plaintiffs filing a single action.  That is not the case when the plaintiffs all allege that they were sexually harassed by the same supervisor, Peter Pouwels.  Defendants also argue that a joint trial would involve irrelevant and prejudicial “me, too” evidence.  This is not the case when the “me, too” evidence involves the same supervisor.  Finally, Defendants’ counsel argues that a joint trial would involve at least 50 witnesses and over 200 hours of testimony, rendering the trial unmanageable.  If that is the case, the Court will transfer this case to a long cause courtroom at the appropriate time.  Ten separate trials would take even longer, given the time it would take to select juries and the repetitious testimony.  

 

The Court has considered Defendants’ remaining arguments and finds none to be persuasive.  Therefore, Defendants’ motion to bifurcate the trial is denied.  Plaintiff’s counsel shall provide notice and file proof of such with the Court.

Case Number: 21STCV28966    Hearing Date: October 24, 2023    Dept: 39

Scott Erickson, et al. v. Bartlett Loft

Case No. 21STCV28966

Motion to Strike

Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint

Demurrer to Cross-Complaint

 

            Plaintiffs are spouses and own a residential unit governed by Defendant, a homeowners’  association.  (Second Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 3-4.)  Plaintiff Ryan Erickson resides at the unit, but Plaintiff Scott Erickson does not.  (Id., ¶ 3.)  Plaintiffs allege that Defendant “has targeted the Plaintiffs and engaged in selective enforcement of the Governing Documents to Plaintiffs’ financial and emotional detriment most notable since the beginning of 2019.”  (Id., ¶ 24.)  Plaintiffs allege that the building manager, Peter Ong, failed to address at least 26 written incident reports, including “harassing, disturbing, threatening, intimidating, slandering, and racially discriminating behavior” by another tenant, Christopher Tripp (“Tripp”).  (Id., ¶ 25.)  Plaintiffs allege that Tripp made a series of racist and obscene comments to Plaintiff Ryan Erickson.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiffs also allege that Ong failed to address Tripp’s illegal activities, including suspected drug use and/or drug trafficking.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiffs allege that Ong failed to address Plaintiffs’ reasonable repair requests that led to overcharges and verbal harassment by Defendant’s repairman, Mr. Stivers, in addition to failing to repair holes from cameras Tripp installed, which subjected Plaintiff Ryan Erickson to exposure to toxic fumes.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiffs allege that they were denied use and enjoyment of the unit.  (Id., ¶ 27.) 

 

            Tripp is deceased, and Plaintiffs filed this action against the homeowners’ association, Bartlett Loft (“Bartlett”).  Bartlett filed a cross-complaint against the owner of the unit occupied by Tripp, Amit Tidhar (“Tidhar”) on March 27, 2023.  Now, Tidhar moves to strike the cross-complaint for failure to seek leave to file it, and Bartlett moves for leave to amend to file the cross-complaint.  The Court grants Bartlett’s motion for leave to amend; orders that the cross-complaint of March 27, 2023, shall not be stricken; and denies Tidhar’s motion to strike, per Code of Civil Procedure section 426.50.  There is no undue prejudice, as the trial date is not until July 9, 2024, which affords ample time to complete discovery.  To the extent Tidhar raises issues with the merits of the cross-claims, the Court will address those issues in ruling on the demurrer.   

 

Bartlett’s cross-complaint asserts five causes of action: (1) Breach of governing documents, (2) Negligence, (3) Equitable indemnity, (4) Equitable contribution, and (5) Declaratory relief.  Tidhar demurs to each cause of action.  “It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)  The Court overrules the demurrer for the following reasons:

 

1.         Statute of Limitations – Tidhar argues that the claims are time-barred.  Plaintiffs allege that Tripp’s conduct occurred “as early as February 2019” and that they were denied “use and enjoyment of the unit” from January 2019 to June 2021.  The cross-claims were filed on March 27, 2023, which was within two years of this date.  Therefore, the demurrer is overruled on this basis.  This issue must be resolved on a motion for summary adjudication or a motion in limine.   

 

2.         Breach of Governing Documents – Barlett is entitled to plead a breach of contract claim in general terms.  (Ochs v. PacifiCare of Cal. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 782, 795.)  In this case, Bartlett alleges that Tripp violated various provisions of the governing documents, which are detailed in the cross-complaint (as well as the operative complaint).  (See Cross-Complaint, ¶ 15.)  Bartlett alleges that Tidhar’s “failure to restrain Mr. Tripp from engaging in that conduct . . . constitutes a nuisance, in violation of the Governing Documents.”  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 24.)  Among other things, Bartlett alleges that Tidhar violated section 3 of the governing documents, which prohibits owners from “do[ing] or permit[ting] anything to be done which would interfere with the rights, comfort and convenience of the other Owners/Residents & Tenants as protected by Article III.”  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 8.)  Bartlett alleges: “On several occasions, Plaintiffs complained to [Bartlett] about Mr. Tripp’s actions and conduct.  [Bartlett] promptly forwarded those complaints to [Tidhar’s] attention.”  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 16.)  These allegations are sufficient to state a cause of action for breach of contract.  Contrary to Tidhar’s argument, this cause of action is not predicated upon a vicarious liability theory.  Rather, Bartlett is proceeding against Tidhar for his violation of the governing documents by permitting Tripp to pose a nuisance.  Therefore, the demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.   

 

3.         Negligence – The Court sustains the demurrer because Bartlett does not allege that Tidhar owes a duty to Bartlett (as opposed to Plaintiffs).  Bartlett relies on a line of cases—including Chee v. Amanda Goldt Property Management (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1360—which states that a landlord may be held liable when a known danger causes injury to a plaintiff.  This case law stands for the proposition that Tidhar may owe a duty to the injured parties, i.e., Plaintiffs.  However, counsel for Bartlett alleges no basis to find any duty from Tidhar to Bartlett as a result of Plaintiffs’ injury.  Similarly, Barlett alleges no injury or damages to Bartlett as a result of Tidhar’s alleged failure to act.  At heart, this cause of action is nothing more than an equitable indemnity claim because Bartlett is seeking to recover for its damages stemming from Plaintiffs’ claims against Bartlett as a result of the known dangers posed by Tripp.  Therefore, the Court sustains the demurrer to the second cause of action without leave to amend. 

 

4.         Equitable Indemnity and Equitable Contribution – To state a claim for indemnity against Tidhar, Bartlett must allege that it may be held liable for harm to Plaintiffs that is attributable, in whole or in part, to Tidhar’s actions or omissions.  (Platt v. Coldwell Banker Residential Real Estate Services (1990) 217 Cal. App. 3d 1439, 1445, fn. 7.)  Defendant alleges Tidhar caused Bartlett’s alleged harm by failing to address Tripp’s misconduct.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 38-39.)  These allegations state a claim for equitable indemnity against Tidhar.  For the same reason, Defendant’s fourth cause of action for contribution suffices.  (See General Elec. Co. v. State of Cal. ex rel. Dept. Pub. Wks. (1973) 32 Cal.App.3d 918, 825.)  Therefore, the demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action is overruled.

 

5.         Declaratory Relief – Barlett’s claim for declaratory relief is not appropriate because Bartlett has causes of action for breach of contract, equitable indemnity, and equitable contribution.  A declaratory relief action is inappropriate when a plaintiff has an adequate remedy on other causes of action at trial.  (See Hood v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 319, 324; California Ins. Guarantee Assn. v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1617, 1624.) 

 

            The declaratory relief statute should not be used for the purpose of anticipating and determining an issue which can be determined in the main action.  The object of the statute is to afford a new form of relief where needed and not to furnish a litigant with a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues.

 

(General of America Ins. Co. v. Lilly (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 465, 470.)  The Court has discretion to decline to issue a declaratory judgment under these circumstances.  (See AICCO, Inc. v. Insurance Company of North America (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 579, 590.)  Therefore, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Based upon the foregoing, the Court orders as follows:

 

            1.         The Court grants Bartlett’s motion for leave to file a cross-complaint and orders that the cross-complaint, dated March 27, 2023, shall not be stricken.

 

            2.         The Court denies Tidhar’s motion to strike.

 

            3.         The Court sustains Tidhar’s demurrer with respect to the second and fifth causes of action without leave to amend.

 

            4.         The Court overrules Tidhar’s demurrer with respect to the first, third, and fourth causes of action.

 

            5.         Tidhar shall file an answer within thirty (30) days.

 

            6.         Counsel for Tidhar shall provide notice and file proof of such with the Court.

Case Number: 22STCV35924    Hearing Date: October 24, 2023    Dept: 39

Daniel Chavira v. Hyundai Motor America, Inc., et al.

Case No. 22STCV35924

Motion for Attorney’s Fees

 

            Plaintiff Daniel Chavira (“Plaintiff”) filed a case under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act against Hyundai Motor America and Commerce Hyundai (collectively, “Defendants”), which settled.  Now, Plaintiff’s counsel seeks the following: (1) Attorney’s fees in the amount of $15,095; (2) A multiplier of 1.30 which equals an additional $4,528.50; and (3) Costs in the amount of $874.70.  Defendants do not oppose the motion.

 

            The determination of reasonable amount of attorney fees is within the sound discretion of trial courts. (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095; Akins v. Enterprise Rent-A-Car Co. (2000) 79 Cal. App. 4th 1127, 1134.)  The burden is on the party seeking attorney fees to prove reasonableness of the fees.  (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal. App. 4th 603, 615.)  The Court has broad discretion in determining the amount of a reasonable attorney’s fee award which will not be overturned absent a “manifest abuse of discretion, a prejudicial error of law, or necessary findings not supported by substantial evidence.”  (Bernardi v. County of Monterey (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 1379, 1393-94.)  The Court need not explain its calculation of the amount of attorney’s fees awarded in detail; identifying the factors considered in arriving at the amount will suffice. (Ventura v. ABM Industries Inc. (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 274-75.)

 

            Although Plaintiff’s counsel’s rates are high for a case of this nature, the record reflects efficiency in the handling of this case, which makes up for the high rates.  Defendants do not oppose the motion.  Finally, overall, a request for approximately $15,000 to handle a case of this nature is reasonable.  Therefore, the Court grants the motion and approves attorneys’ fees in the amount of $15,095.  The Court also approves costs in the amount of $874.70.  However, the Court denies the request for a multiplier, as this case does not satisfy the criteria for a multiplier.

            2.         The Court dismisses this case with prejudice.
