DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV02326    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF NICHOLAS THALER PH.D.

           I.         INTRODUCTION

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff Vaneza Alfaro (“Plaintiff”) filed her complaint against Defendant Liliana Deluca (“Defendant”), alleging injuries resulting from an automobile collision. Plaintiff retained Dr. Nicholas Thaler, Ph.D. (“Dr. Thaler”), on March 23, 2023, to assess her injuries.

On April 16, 2024, Defendant served a deposition notice for Dr. Thaler, seeking his testimony and the production of documents. Dr. Thaler’s deposition was scheduled for September 6, 2024. However, on September 4, 2024, after a dispute over the production of documents, Plaintiff objected to the deposition proceeding as scheduled.

Defendant now moves for the Court to compel Dr. Thaler’s deposition.

Moving Party’s Position

Defendant moves to compel the deposition of Nicholas Thaler, Ph.D., to appear for a deposition and produce the documents set forth in the subpoena. Defendant contends that the deposition is warranted under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2025.450, 2025.480, 1987.1, and 1987.2, as Plaintiff’s counsel has improperly refused to allow the deposition to proceed despite a properly served subpoena.

Defendant argues that Dr. Thaler is a treating physician that Plaintiff was examined by for psychological issues before the lawsuit was filed. Plaintiff now seeks to preclude the deposition by designating Dr. Thaler as a retained expert. Defendant asserts that this is baseless and meritless. Defendant further argues that Plaintiff waived any objection to the testimony by failing to timely object. 

Opposition

Plaintiff opposes the motion on the grounds that Dr. Thaler is a retained expert, and his deposition cannot take place prematurely before expert designations, which are not due until January 20, 2025. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.210, Plaintiff asserts that Dr. Thaler’s deposition is premature, as he was retained to form and express opinions in anticipation of litigation.

Plaintiff further contends that Defendant is seeking an unfair advantage by attempting to depose Dr. Thaler before Plaintiff has received all relevant information necessary for Dr. Thaler to form his opinion, specifically the raw data from Defendant's expert, Dr. Delis. Plaintiff also rebuts Defendant’s waiver argument, stating that just two days before the scheduled deposition, Defendant notified Plaintiff that the essential data would not be available. This delayed notification prevented Plaintiff from making a timely objection, in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.410.
Reply

In response, Defendant asserts that Dr. Thaler is a treating physician and that the lack of exchanged raw data is irrelevant to the current deposition request. Defendant also highlights that Plaintiff previously agreed to produce Dr. Thaler for deposition.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

            Code Civ. Proc. §2025.450

(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

. . .

(g)(1) If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(2) On motion of any other party who, in person or by attorney, attended at the time and place specified in the deposition notice in the expectation that the deponent's testimony would be taken, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of that party and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Meet and Confer

The parties attempted to meet and confer regarding Dr. Thaler’s deposition on several occasions prior to the filing of this motion (Opposition, Exh. 2, 3). Initially, Plaintiff agreed to the deposition under the stipulation regarding the exchange of raw data between experts. However, as the deposition date approached, Plaintiff withdrew their agreement, citing the lack of essential raw data from Defendant’s expert, Dr. Delis (Opposition, p. 3). Despite communications to resolve this issue, including Defendant’s claim that Plaintiff failed to timely object to the deposition, the parties could not reach an agreement. Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

 

Compel Deposition

The Court finds that Dr. Thaler cannot be deposed at this stage in his capacity as a retained expert witness for Plaintiff. According to the Opposition, Plaintiff retained Dr. Thaler on March 23, 2023, seven months prior to filing the Complaint on October 23, 2023, specifically to determine whether Plaintiff sustained a traumatic brain injury as a result of the collision underlying this matter (Opposition, p. 2). Dr. Thaler was retained “for the purpose of forming and expressing an opinion in anticipation of litigation or in preparation for the trial" (Opposition, p. 2).

Plaintiff further asserts that while there was an initial agreement to proceed with Dr. Thaler’s deposition, this agreement was made under the stipulation regarding Plaintiff’s neuropsychological examination. Under the stipulation, Defendant’s expert, Dr. Delis, was required to exchange raw data directly with Plaintiff’s retained expert, Dr. Thaler (Opposition, p. 3). Plaintiff contends that Dr. Thaler was retained to provide an expert opinion based on testing, not for treatment purposes.

To date, Plaintiff claims they have not received Dr. Delis’ report or raw data, despite multiple requests. Plaintiff argues that the exchange of raw data is essential for the experts to form fully informed opinions. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s motion to compel Dr. Thaler’s deposition seeks to take unfair advantage by pursuing an unauthorized early deposition of a retained expert while denying Plaintiff access to the necessary information for a meaningful deposition.

Plaintiff also contends that their delayed objection to the deposition was caused by Defendant’s late notification, just two days before Dr. Thaler’s scheduled deposition, that Dr. Delis’ report and data would not be available in time (Opposition, p. 3). Given these circumstances, Plaintiff maintains that Dr. Thaler’s deposition is premature.

In contrast, Defendant contends that Plaintiff has previously agreed to Dr. Thaler’s deposition and is now improperly seeking to delay it by labeling him an expert witness. (Motion pg. 2) Defendant highlights that Dr. Thaler’s designation as an expert should not shield him from deposition when the information sought pertains to his role as a treating physician¿. (Reply pg. 1) Defendant also emphasizes that Plaintiff failed to object to Dr. Thaler’s deposition and as such is required to be compelled for deposition. (Reply pg, 7-9)

 In light of these facts, the Court concludes that Dr. Thaler, as a retained expert, cannot be compelled to testify at this time. Defendant will have the opportunity to depose Dr. Thaler after the exchange of expert witness information. Accordingly, the motion to compel is DENIED.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            Defendant’s motion to compel deposition of Dr. Thaler is DENIED.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

Case Number: 24AHCV00190    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES – GENERAL Set One, FORM INTERROGATORIES – EMPLOYMENT Set One, FORM INTERROGATORIES PRE-TRIAL SUPPLEMENTAL Set One, REQUEST OF PRODUCTION- PRE TRIAL-SUPPLEMENTAL Set One, REQUEST OF PRODUCTION – Set One, REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION BE DEEMED ADMITTED Set One   

I.                INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff, Chrisoula Kousoulis (“Plaintiff”), filed this action against Defendant USC Arcadia Hospital (“Defendant”), alleging causes of action for (1) Failure to Prevent Harassment, Discrimination, or Retaliation, (2) Constructive Discharge, (3) Hostile Work Environment, (4) Defamation, and (5) Publication of Private Facts - Invasion of Privacy. Plaintiff worked as a Nuclear Medicine Technician at Defendant’s facility from December 2019 until her resignation in September 2022. Plaintiff claims that her supervisor bullied and harassed her due to her decision not to receive the COVID-19 vaccine for personal reasons.

During discovery, Defendant served Plaintiff with six discovery requests: (1) Form Interrogatories – General Set One, (2) Form Interrogatories – Employment Set One, (3) Form Interrogatories – Pre-Trial Supplemental Set One, (4) Request for Production – Pre-Trial Supplemental Set One, (5) Request for Production Set One, and (6) Requests for Admission Set One. Defendant has filed six motions to compel Plaintiff to serve verified responses and produce documents and seeks monetary sanctions of $960 for each respective motion. The Court will address these motions in one tentative ruling as follows:

            II.        REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS – RFA Set One

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.)¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared . . . .”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).)  

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).) 

It is well-settled that “unsworn responses are tantamount to no responses at all.”  (Appleton v. Superior Court (1888) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636; Zorro Inv. Co. v. Great Pacific Securities Corp. (1977) 69 Cal.App.3d 907, 914.)   

Defendant USC Arcadia Hospital served its RFAs (Set One) on May 26, 2023, making Plaintiff’s responses due by June 26, 2023. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.) On June 19, 2023, Plaintiff served responses; however, these responses were not verified, rendering them incomplete under California law. (Croutch Decl., Exh. B.) Despite communication between the parties the responses remained unverified. Subsequently, Plaintiff’s counsel, Mr. Brian M. Heit, filed a motion to be relieved as counsel on May 22, 2024. (Croutch Decl., ¶¶ 5-6.) This motion was denied after Plaintiff failed to appear at the hearing on July 3, 2024. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 5.)

Since Plaintiff failed to serve verified responses as required by statute, Defendant filed this motion to deem the RFAs admitted. As of the date of this hearing, Plaintiff has not served verified responses. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280(b), if a party fails to serve timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order that the matters specified in the RFAs be deemed admitted. The court must grant this motion unless verified responses are served before the hearing.

Because no verified responses have been filed as of the date of the hearing, Defendant’s motion to deem the RFAs admitted must be granted.

Accordingly, Defendant’s request to deem the RFAs admitted is GRANTED.

 

III.      FORM INTERROGATORIES – Form Interrogatories General – Set One, Form Interrogatories Employment – Set One and Pre-Trial Supplemental Interrogatories

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(a), if a party fails to respond to interrogatories, they waive any objections, including those based on privilege or work product protection. When a party does not provide timely responses, the propounding party is entitled to move for an order compelling responses. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(b), the Court must order the responding party to provide responses without objections.

Here, Defendant served Plaintiff with discovery requests on May 26, 2023, for Form Interrogatories - General Set One and Form Interrogatories Employment – Set One (Croutch Decl., ¶ 3) Plaintiff served responses on June 19, 2023, but these responses were unverified. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 4) Despite several meet and confer attempts, no verified responses were provided by Plaintiff to date. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 6) 

Here, Defendant properly served the Pre-Trial Supplemental Interrogatories Set One on June 13, 2024. Plaintiff has not served responses by the deadline of July 15, 2024, nor has Plaintiff requested an extension. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 4.) As Plaintiff failed to provide any responses, Defendant’s motion to compel is justified under the statute. As of date of the hearing, Plaintiff has not served responses, and thus the motion to compel responses without objections is appropriate.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants motion to compel for Form Interrogatories – General Set One, Form Interrogatories Employment – Set One and Pre-Trial Supplemental Interrogatories Set One

Given that Plaintiff failed to provide verified response to Form Interrogatories – General Set One and Form Interrogatories Employment – Set One, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Compel. 

 

IV.       REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION – RFP Pre-Trial Supplemental Set One and RFP Set One

A propounding party may move to compel responses to requests for production of documents where the responding party fails to provide responses. (CCP § 2031.300.) The responding party must provide responses within thirty days after the demand is served. (CCP § 2031.030(c)(2) & (3).) The responding party waives all objections, including privilege and work product, by failing to timely respond to requests for production of documents. (CCP § 2031.300.) 

Here, Defendant served the Pre-Trial Supplemental Requests for Production Set One on June 13, 2024, and Plaintiff has failed to provide any responses or request additional time. (Croutch Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) Defendant is therefore entitled to an order compelling responses to request for production. 

Here, Defendant served Plaintiff with discovery requests on May 26, 2023, for Requests for Production Set One. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 3) Plaintiff served responses on June 19, 2023, but these responses were unverified. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 4) Despite several meet and confer attempts, no verified responses were provided by Plaintiff to date. (Croutch Decl., ¶ 6) 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel responses to the Request for Production Set One and Pre-Trial Supplemental Requests for Production Set One.

 

V.        SANCTIONS

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].) 

CCP § 2023.030 authorizes the Court to issue sanctions against a party engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process. Failure to respond to discovery, evasive responses, and objections lacking substantial justification are “misuses of the discovery process.” (CCP § 2023.010, subd. (d)-(f).) 

In this case, Plaintiff failed to provide timely, verified responses to (1) Request for Admission Set One, (2) Form Interrogatories General Set One, (3) Form Interrogatories Employment Set One, (4) Pre-Trial Supplemental Form Interrogatories Set One, (5) Requests for Production Set One, and (6) Pre-Trial Supplemental Requests for Production Set One. Despite multiple meet and confer efforts, Plaintiff has neither remedied these deficiencies nor provided any substantial justification for failing to do so. Defendant has filed these six motions to compel Plaintiff to serve verified responses and has requested monetary sanctions of $960 for each motion.

Defendant’s counsel seeks $960 in sanctions for each motion, totaling $5,760. Defendant requests attorney’s fees as follows: (a) two hours, billed at $300 per hour, for research and preparation of each motion, amounting to $600; (b) one hour, billed at $300 per hour, to attend the hearing on the motions; and (c) a filing fee of $60 for each motion.

However, after reviewing the complexity and nature of the motions, the Court finds this request excessive. These motions are straightforward, and no opposition has been filed. The Court concludes that $960 for each of the six motions is excessive. Accordingly, the Court grants a reduced award as follows: $60 filing fee for each of the six motions, three hours total for all the motions at $300 per hour for research and preparation, and one hour to attend the hearing at $300, as all motions will be heard together on October 30, 2024.

Thus, the total sanctions amount awarded is $1,560, which includes $360 for the six filing fees, $900 for preparation, and $300 for attending the hearing.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants request for sanctions in awarding reduced amount of $1,560.

 

VI.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Motion by Defendant to compel (1) Request for Admission Set One, (2) Form Interrogatories General Set One, (3) Form Interrogatories Employment Set One, (4) Pre-Trial Supplemental Form Interrogatories Set One, (5) Requests for Production Set One, and (6) Pre-Trial Supplemental Requests for Production Set One is GRANTED.

Motion by Defendant for compelling monetary sanctions is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $1,560.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide responses and pay sanctions within 20 days.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order.
