DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV46934    Hearing Date: August 9, 2024    Dept: 73

08/09/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

BARRIOS v. B.V. TELACU NW FIVE, INC., et al. (20STCV46934)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Daniel Balaban (Balaban & Spielberger, LLP)

Counsel for Waste Management Defendants:  Benjamin Sampson (Bordin Semmer LLP)

Counsel for Telacu Defendants/moving party: Sonali Olson (Olson Law Group, APC)

 

(1)   MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT (filed by Telacu Defendants on 06/05/2024)

(2)   MOTION TO SEAL DOCUMENTS CONTAINING SETTLEMENT AMOUNT (filed by Telacu Defendants on 06/17/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

 

Defendants’ Motion to Seal Documents Containing Settlement Amount is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 8, 2020, Plaintiff Jerson Barrios (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Telacu NW Five, Inc.; Telacu 1270, Inc.; Telacu Development Corporation, Inc.; The East Los Angeles Community Union; Waste Management of California, Inc.; and Waste Management Collection and Recycling, Inc.

 

The Complaint alleges that while in the course of Plaintiff’s employment with Garibay Landscaping, Inc., Plaintiff sustained significant injuries during an incident on May 2, 2020. Plaintiff alleges he was standing on the outer ledges on the top of a dumpster, as he and others usually did, to empty the contents of the reusable trash bags full of leaves, shrubbery, branches, which was being handed to him by another member of the Garibay Landscaping crew. Plaintiff alleges he suddenly lost his balance and fell down to the ground hitting the back of his head, neck, back and the rest of his body onto the asphalt/concrete floor, suffering a skull fracture, respiratory failure, spinal injuries and shoulder abrasion, among other injuries. Defendants Telacu NW Five, Inc.; The East Los Angeles Community Union; and Telacu Real Estate Services, Inc. (collectively the “Telacu Defendants”) own and maintain the property where the incident occurred.

 

On June 5, 2024, the Telacu Defendants filed the Motion to Determine Good Faith Settlement stating that they reached a settlement with Plaintiff. No opposition was filed.

 

On June 17, 2024, the Telacu Defendants also filed the Motion to Seal Documents Containing the Settlement Amount. No opposition was filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Motion to Determine Good Faith Settlement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6, subdivision (a)(1), provides that, on noticed motion, “[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff . . . and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors . . . .” “A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc. § 877.6(c).) Although determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, “it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated” by the settlement.¿ (Id. at (c).)¿ 

 

In City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261, the Court provided guidance on an unopposed motion for a good faith settlement determination:¿ 

 

This court notes that of the hundreds of motions for good faith determination presented for trial court approval each year, the overwhelming majority are unopposed and granted summarily by the trial court. At the time of filing, in many cases the moving party does not know if a contest will develop. If each motion required a full recital by declaration or affidavit setting forth a complete factual response to all of the¿Tech-Bilt¿factors, literally thousands of attorney hours would be consumed and inch-thick motions would have to be read and considered by trial courts in an exercise which would waste valuable judicial and legal time and clients' resources. It must also be remembered that¿Tech-Bilt¿was decided on a contested basis. We are unaware of any reported decision which has reversed an¿uncontested¿good faith determination and we, therefore, conclude that only when the good faith nature of a settlement is disputed, it is incumbent upon the trial court to consider and weigh the¿Tech-Bilt¿factors. That is to say, when no one objects, the barebones motion which sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case is sufficient. 

 

((1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.) 

 

The instant motion is unopposed. The Telacu Defendants have provided evidence that they reached a settlement with Plaintiff through out of court negotiations. (Cain Decl. ¶¿2.)

 

On March 13, 2024, parties had a mediation with Hon. Thierry Colaw (ret.). (Cain Decl. ¶¿5.) While the parties did not settle during mediation, the parties continued to communicate and negotiate through the efforts of the mediator and the Telacu Defendants and Plaintiff were able to reach a settlement on or about May 8, 2024. (Ibid.)

 

The confidential settlement agreement provides that the Telacu Defendants and their insurer have agreed to pay an agreed upon settlement amount contingent on a stipulation of good faith settlement between the parties. Plaintiff to be responsible for all liens. There is a confidentiality clause in the agreement, each party to bear their own costs and fees, and a 1542 Waiver. (Cain Decl. ¶¿2.)

 

In Telacu Defendants’ motion, they provided a background of the case and the parties’ positions relative to their dispute. (See Motion, pp. 3-6.) All parties to this action have been notified of the subject settlement, including the settlement amount, and counsel for the non-settling Defendant Waste Management of California, Inc. has stipulated that the settlement is in good faith. (Cain Decl. ¶¿3.) The foregoing is sufficient for an unopposed motion for good faith settlement determination. 

 

The motion is granted. 

 

B.      Motion to Seal Documents Containing Settlement Amount

 

“Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c).) A party may move to seal records pursuant to California Rules of Court, rules 2.550-2.551. “A party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record. The motion or application must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b).) To grant an order permitting a record to be filed under seal, the court must find facts that establish:  

 

(1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; 

(2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; 

(3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; 

(4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and 

(5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest. 

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)  

 

The Telacu Defendants request that the Court permanently seal the un-redacted version of the supporting Declaration of Jinny Cain to the Defendants’ Motion for Order Determining Good Faith Settlement pursuant to C.C.P. § 877.6 and the exhibits attached thereto. The only portions redacted from the Good Faith Settlement Motion contain the amount of settlement.

 

The Telacu Defendants’ grounds are that Plaintiff and the Telacu Defendants reached a settlement as the result of out of court negotiations; the settlement agreement contains a confidentiality provision; and the settlement agreement is contingent on a court order finding the settlement was entered in good faith. (Cain Decl. ¶¿2.)

 

A contractual obligation not to disclose the terms of a confidential settlement agreement can constitute an overriding interest to justify sealing under appropriate circumstances. (See Universal City Studios, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1273, 1283.) 

 

There are sufficient facts to support the filing of the settlement documents under seal. There is an overriding interest supporting sealing in permitting the parties to proceed to settlement and prevent a breach of the settlement agreement, which is contingent on this Court’s order determining it was made in good faith. If the document is not sealed, the settlement agreement will be breached. No less restrictive means are apparent to achieve the overriding interest.  

 

The unopposed motion is granted. 

 

DISPOSITION 

 

Defendants’ Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

 

Defendants’ Motion to Seal Documents Containing Settlement Amount is GRANTED.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, Defendants submitted a declaration attesting that the parties met and conferred. (Park Decl. ¶¶ 6-14.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement.

 

B.      Legal Standard

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.”¿ CCP § 2030.010(a).¿ “Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.”¿ CCP § 2030.260(a).¿ 
¿ 

“The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered. (2) An exercise of the party's option to produce writings. (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.”¿ CCP § 2030.210(a).¿ “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”¿ CCP § 2030.220(c).¿ 

¿ 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(a).¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(b).¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).¿ 

¿ 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(c).¿“[T]he time limitations set forth in section 2030 and section 2034, subdivision (a) [are] mandatory and [when] not met, the trial court's order is in excess of its jurisdiction.”¿(Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”   

 

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

 

C.      Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses for Production of Documents, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One

 

                                                              i.      Separate Statement

 

For motions to compel further discovery responses, moving parties are required to file separate statements in support. (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a).) The Court is required to analyze each objection made to determine whether valid objections were made and this type of analysis requires a separate statement. The California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subdivision (a), states: 

 

“A separate statement is a separate document filed and served with the discovery motion that provides all the information necessary to understand each discovery request and all the responses to it that are at issue. The separate statement must be full and complete so that no person is required to review any other document in order to determine the full request and the full response. Material must not be incorporated into the separate statement by reference.”

 

In Mills v. U.S. Bank (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 871, 893, the Court of Appeal analyzed a procedural deficiency in a separate statement where “Plaintiffs filed a separate statement in support of their motion to compel, but it did not comply with the requirements of former rule 335 (now rule 3.1020). Plaintiffs' separate statement did not set forth U.S. Bank's full response to each of the interrogatories and requests for production at issue. Instead, the separate statement repeatedly grouped together several discovery requests, and then stated ‘in relevant part’ U.S. Bank's response to the group as a whole, and it significantly truncated some of the lengthier responses.” When determining if the trial court abused its discretion in denying the motion to compel further for this procedural deficiency, the Court of Appeal stated, “We conclude that because Plaintiffs did not comply with the requirements of former rule 335, the trial court was well within its discretion to deny the motion to compel discovery on that basis. (Cf. Neary v. Regents of University of California (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 1136, 1145, 230 Cal.Rptr. 281 [describing trial court's denial of motions to compel discovery because of a nonconforming separate statement].)” (Id. at 893.)     

 

Here, Plaintiff filed separate statements for its Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses for Production of Documents, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One. However, similar to Mills, Plaintiff’s separate statements contain multiple deficiencies and do not comply with the requirements in California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345. In the separate statement filed with Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses for Production of Documents, Set One, Plaintiff’s separate statement only refers to Plaintiff’s meet and confer letter as to why supplemental responses are necessary for RFP Nos. 13-26. However, pursuant to California Rule of Court Rule 3.1345(c), material must not be incorporated into the separate statement by reference. Further, Plaintiff’s separate statements for both its Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses for Production of Documents, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One contain multiple mistakes, such as misstating the responses and requests, which Plaintiff attributes to copy and paste errors. However, the deficiencies in Plaintiff’s separate statements make it extremely difficult for the Court to analyze Plaintiff’s requests and Defendants’ objections.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses for Production of Documents, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One, are DENIED without prejudice.

 

A.      Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One

 

Defendants move for an order that the matters specified in Requests for Admission, Set One, be deemed admitted, or in the alternative, for an order compelling further response.  

 

When a party fails to serve a timely response to a request for admission, the party propounding the request for admission may move for an order to deem the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2033.280, subd. (b).) A party who fails to provide a timely response waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction… on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

Since Plaintiff timely served a response, the Court does not deem the truth of the matters specified in the requests admitted.

 

Defendants move to compel further responses to Requests for Admissions Set One, Nos. 10-12, 14, 16-17.

Request for Admission No. 10 states “Beginning January 2021, YEDAM stopped purchasing “Dani Mackenzie” merchandise from DM Cosmetics.” Plaintiff Choi objects on the grounds of lack of foundation. In her opposition, Choi asserts there is no underlying pleading basis that can support this request. However, the Court agrees with Defendants that this admission is probative as to the Cross-Complaint’s fifth, sixth, seventh and eight causes of action and would show whether Yedam was selling Dani Mackenzie merchandise without consent. Thus, the Court grants Defendants’ request to compel further response as to this request.

Request for Admission No. 11 states “CHO, YEDAM, OLIVE HOUSE and LAMIS TANIA misappropriated DM TRADE SECRETS (“DM TRADE SECRETS” shall mean “1) the formula of Candles, Diffuser, Scent Oil; 2) the list of manufacturers and suppliers of DM Cosmetics; 3) list of customers; 4) distribution and sales methods, all of which are owned by DM Cosmetics, Inc.”).” Plaintiff Choi objects on the grounds that Defendants have not identified the trade secret. The Court disagrees that the trade secret has not been identified with reasonable particularity and finds the trade secret identified in Request for Admission No. 11 sufficient. Thus, the Court grants Defendants’ request to compel further response as to this request.

Request for Admission No. 12 states “DANIEL LEE and ANGELA LEE are the owners of the trade name and trade mark of “DANI MACKENZIE” (Registration No. 6,151,717).” Plaintiff Choi objects on the grounds that it is vague, ambiguous, compound, overbroad, oppressive and unduly burdensome. The Court disagrees, and the request is not compound because it refers to both Daniel Lee and Angela Lee, not either or. Thus, the Court grants Defendants’ request to compel further response as to this request.

Request for Admission No. 14 states “The value of YEDAM was evaluated about over Fifty Million dollars ($50,000,000.00) as of 12/31/2020” and Request for Admission No. 16 states “For Year 2020, YEDAM had net profits of $1,060,000.00.” Plaintiff Choi objects on the grounds these requests are “Not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Privileged financial matters. Violates the right to privacy.” However, Choi has not sought a protective order, therefore, her privacy objection is not proper. Further, the requested admission is relevant to show the valuation and net profits of Yedam, and it is relevant to support Defendant Lee’s claims relating to his unpaid salary and ownership interests in Yedam. Thus, the Court grants Defendants’ request to compel further response as to this request.

Request for Admission No. 17 states “YEDAM, INC. had no certificate of qualification to transact intrastate business or to pay taxes based on conducting intrastate business in the State of California at any time.” Plaintiff Choi objects on the grounds these requests are “Vague and ambiguous. Not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” Defendants assert they are seeking to ascertain whether YEDAM was qualified to conduct business, as a corporation, in California. The Court finds this request relevant. Thus, the Court grants Defendants’ request to compel further response as to this request.

In sum, the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One, is GRANTED. Further responses are due within 30 days. 

 

B.      Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Both parties request the Court impose monetary sanctions. Since the Court grants Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Admissions, Set One, the Court grants Defendants’ request for attorneys’ fees incurred in bringing that motion. Defendants request reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs in the amount of $2,250.00. (Park Decl. ¶ 17.)

 

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ request. Plaintiff Choi is also ordered to pay sanctions to Defendants, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $2,250.00, within 30 days. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Defendant Daniel Lee’s Requests for Admissions, Set One, is GRANTED. Plaintiff Choi shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order. Plaintiff Choi is also ordered to pay sanctions to Defendants, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $2,250.00, within 30 days. 

 

Defendants’ Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Defendant Daniel Lee’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Defendant Daniel Lee’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, are DENIED without prejudice.

Dept. 73

DAVIS v. LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT, et al. (24STCV07170)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Jared Watkins (Cypress LLP)

Counsel for Defendant Inclusive Education and Community Partnership, Inc./moving party:  Sonali Olson (Olson Law Group)

 

Demurrer WITH MOTION TO STRIKE FIRST AMENDED complaint (filed 6/24/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant IECP’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action against Defendant IECP is OVERRULED. 

 

Defendant IECP’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff Pharrell Davis, a minor by and through his guardian ad litem, Justin Davis, filed his First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Los Angeles Unified School District; Inclusive Education and Community Partnership, Inc. and Does 1-20. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Assault;

2.      Battery; and

3.      Negligence.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff Pharrell Davis (“Plaintiff” or “Pharrell”) was born in 2012 and was ten years old at the time of the alleged assault. Justin Davis is Plaintiff’s father.  During all times relevant, Plaintiff was a student at Hillcrest Drive Elementary (“Hillcrest”), a school within the Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD”). At all times mentioned herein, LAUSD employed and/or contracted with Defendant Inclusive Education and Community Partnership, Inc. (“IECP”) to support and supervise children with disabilities at Hillcrest.

 

The FAC alleges that on November 14, 2022, during lunch on the schoolyard at Hillcrest Drive Elementary, Plaintiff and another student (Doe 2) were playing football. The two students got into a physical altercation and a school aide, Jose, intervened. Plaintiff alleges that Jose was assigned to supervise the other student (Doe 2) based on that student’s “disability(ies), behavorial problems” as part of the student’s Individualized Education Plan. Plaintiff alleges that Jose violently grabbed Plaintiff by his sweatshirt and slammed his head and body to the blacktop. Plaintiff alleges he suffered a concussion with ongoing post-concussive syndrome. Plaintiff alleges school officials witnessed the physical assault, failed to prevent it, and failed to provide medical aid or comfort to Pharrell.

 

On June 24, 2024, Defendant IECP filed a demurrer and motion to strike portions of the FAC arguing:

·       The First Cause of Action for Assault fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against IECP. Plaintiff fails to allege vicarious liability on behalf of Jose.

·       The Second Cause of Action for Battery fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against IECP. Plaintiff fails to allege the intent element.

·       Defendant IECP also moves to strike allegations of punitive damages as well as irrelevant allegations from the FAC

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing that the FAC sufficiently sets forth facts to constitute its first and second causes of action.

 

Defendant IECP filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiff failed to plead facts to constitute its first and second causes of action as to IECP.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating that the parties discussed the grounds of the demurrer and motion to strike via telephone on June 17, 2024. (Witzman Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation and the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

B.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant IECP requests judicial notice of: (1) Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint and (2) IECP’s meet and confer letter.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)

 

The Court grants Defendant IECP’s requests. The Court notes that while the Court may take judicial notice of the document, the Court may not take judicial notice of the truth of its contents. (See Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.)

C.      Objections to Evidence

 

 

Defendant IECP objects to the declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel Jared L. Watkins in opposition to IECP’s demurrer and motion to strike, specifically paragraph 4: “Documents produced by IECP in connection with its discovery responses show, among other things, that:

• IECP trains its employees on nonviolent crisis intervention and physical intervention.

• The Master Contract (“Agreement”) between Defendants IECP and Los Angele Unified School District (“LAUSD”) empowers IECP employees to engage in emergency intervention to control unpredictable, spontaneous behavior that pose clear and present danger of serious physical harm to students that cannot be immediately prevented by a less restrictive response.

• The Agreement prohibits interventions that cause pain or trauma.”

 

The Court sustains Defendant IECP’s objection.

 

D.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

E.      First Cause of Action for Assault

 

“The essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed [he or] she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.”  (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-69.)

 

The FAC alleges the following. “On or about November 14, 2022, during lunch at Hillcrest, Defendant DOE 1 (Jose) acted, intending to cause harmful contact with Pharrell’s person by grabbing Pharrell by his sweatshirt and slamming his head and body onto the blacktop causing him to suffer serious personal injuries.” (FAC, ¶ 38.) “Pharrell reasonably believed that he was about to be touched in a harmful manner and it reasonably appeared to Pharrell that Defendant DOE 1 (Jose) was about to carry out the threat.” (FAC, ¶ 39.) “Pharrell did not consent to Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) conduct.” (FAC, ¶ 40.) “Defendant IECP is vicariously liable for Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) tortious conduct under the doctrine of respondeat superior because Defendant DOE 1 was an employee and agent of IECP acting within the course and scope of his employment and agency at the time of the subject assault.” (FAC, ¶ 41.) “Pharrell was harmed by Defendants’ tortious conduct, and Defendants’ conduct was a substantial factor in causing Pharrell’s harm in that a reasonable person would consider Defendants’ conduct to have contributed to Pharrell’s harm and it was more than a remote or trivial factor.” (FAC, ¶ 43.)

 

The Court finds these allegations sufficient to satisfy the elements of assault.

 

In its motion, Defendant IECP argues that the FAC fails to allege any facts supporting vicarious liability against IECP.

 

An employer is vicariously liable for an employee’s tort under the doctrine of respondeat superior if the tort was committed within the scope of the employment.¿ (Montague v. AMN Healthcare, Inc. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1515, 1520.)¿

 

An employee’s willful or malicious tort may fall within the scope of employment even without the employer’s authorization. (Flores v. AutoZone West, Inc.¿(2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 373, 379.) Whether an employee's wrongful acts were committed during the scope of employment is judged by a two-prong, alternative “test”: (1) whether the act was either required by the employer or “incidental” to the employee's duties (“nexus” test); or (2) whether the employee's misconduct was reasonably foreseeable by the employer (even if not “required” or “incidental”). (Montague, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at 1521.)

 

“The employee need not have intended to further the employer's interest for the employer to be liable if there is a ‘causal nexus’ between the intentional tort and the employee's work.” (Crouch v. Trinity Christian Center of Santa Ana, Inc.¿(2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 995, 1015.) This nexus is not simply “but for” causation. (Lisa M. v. Henry Mayo Newhall Memorial Hospital¿(1995) 12 Cal.4th 291, 298.) “The connection or causal nexus required for respondeat superior liability is the tort must have been engendered by or arise from the work. [Citation omitted.] The required connection has been described as (1) ‘the incident leading to injury must be an “outgrowth” of the employment’; (2) ‘the risk of tortious injury is “inherent in the working environment” ’; (3) the risk of tortious injury is ‘ “typical of or broadly incidental to the enterprise [the employer] has undertaken” ’ or (4) ‘the tort was, in a general way, foreseeable from the employee's duties.’” (Id. [Citations omitted].)

“[E]mployers have been held liable for the wrongful and unauthorized acts of their employees where they were committed in the course of a series of acts of the agent which were authorized by the principal.” (Thorn v. City of Glendale (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1382.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges that “Jose was specifically assigned to supervise the student with whom Pharrell was playing (“DOE 2”) as part of an Individualized Education Plan. On information and belief, because of DOE 2’s disability(-ies), behavioral problems, and the need to monitor his interactions with other students, DOE 2 required individualized and particularized supervision and instruction.” (FAC, ¶ 17.)

 

Defendant IECP argues that the alleged conduct of “slamming” Plaintiff to the ground is not required nor incident to Jose’s duties nor reasonably foreseeable in the role of supporting a disabled student.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that an employer may be vicariously liable for its employee’s assault and battery committed while breaking up a fight in the interest of the employer. For example, in De Rosier v. Crow (1960) 184 Cal. App. 2d 476, a waitress employed by a bowling alley and bar was acting in the course and scope of her employment when she committed assault and battery while attempting to stop a fight between patrons and the owner. (Id. at 481.)

 

In Defendant IECP’s reply, it argues that in De Rosier, the employer defendants knew that the waitress had a propensity too be violent when she was drinking alcohol, and the defendants served the waitress alcohol on the evening of the attack.

 

The Court finds that the allegations in the FAC are sufficient to support vicarious liability against IECP. The FAC alleges that Jose was specifically assigned to “monitor Doe 2’s interactions with other students” “because of Doe 2’s disability(-ies) and behavioral problems.” (FAC, ¶ 17.) Therefore, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant knew Doe 2 had “behavioral problems” requiring supervision. Thus, intervening in a physical altercation between two students on behalf of his employer appears to be within the scope of Jose’s employment with IECP.

 

As such, the demurrer to the first cause of action in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint against IECP is OVERRULED. 

 

F.       Second Cause of Action for Battery

 

“The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant’s conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.”  (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-69.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges the following. “On or about November 14, 2022, during lunch at Hillcrest, Defendant DOE 1 (Jose) grabbed Pharrell by his sweatshirt and slammed his head and body onto the blacktop causing him to suffer serious personal injuries. In doing so, Defendant DOE 1 (Jose) touched Pharrell with the intent to harm him. In addition to Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) use of physical violence, his repeated cursing at Pharrell further exhibited his intent.” (FAC, ¶ 47.) “Plaintiff did not consent to Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) touching him or his sweatshirt.” (FAC, ¶ 48.) “Plaintiff was harmed by Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) conduct.” (FAC, ¶ 49.) “A reasonable person in Pharrell’s situation would have been offended by the touching.” (FAC, ¶ 50.)

 

Defendant IECP argues the FAC is devoid of facts that would show any intent by Jose to harm Plaintiff while intervening in the fight. Defendant IECP argues that Plaintiff has alleged that due to Jose having to intervene to stop the fight, Plaintiff fell to the ground.

 

However, the Court does not find Defendant IECP’s assertion persuasive. The FAC does not allege that Plaintiff fell to the ground. Instead, the FAC alleges intentional conduct by Jose, specifically that he “grabbed Plaintiff by his sweatshirt and slammed his head and body onto the blacktop causing him to suffer serious personal injuries.” (FAC, ¶ 47.) The Court finds the FAC’s allegations sufficient to state a battery cause of action.

 

As such, the demurrer to the second cause of action in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint against IECP is OVERRULED. 

 

G.     Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Defendant IECP requests the Court to strike the following allegations in the FAC:

1.      Page 2, paragraph 1, lines 6 and 7, the words: “violently” and “slammed”

2.      Page 3, paragraph 7, line 16, the word: “slammed”

3.      Page 4, paragraph 22, line 18, the word: “slammed”

4.      Page 4, paragraph 26, line 23, the word: “obvious”

5.      Page 5, paragraph 27, line 1, the word: “slamming”

6.      Page 6, paragraph 38, line 10, the words: “slamming” and “serious”

7.      Page 7, paragraph 45, in its entirety: “At all times mentioned in this Complaint, Defendants acted with malice within the meaning of California Civil Code § 3294. Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) conduct was intended by him to cause injury to Pharrell or was despicable conduct carried out by him with a willful and conscious disregard of the safety of students, particularly Pharrell. Defendants IECP and LAUSD are liable for punitive and exemplary damages because their respective officers, directors, or managing agents had advance knowledge of Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) unfitness and each employed and retained him with a conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others, including Pharrell.”

8.      Page 7, paragraph 47, the words: “slammed” and “serious”

9.      Page 8, paragraph 56, in its entirety: “At all times mentioned in this Complaint, Defendants acted with malice within the meaning of California Civil Code § 3294. Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) conduct was intended by him to cause injury to Pharrell or was despicable conduct carried out by him with a willful and conscious disregard of the safety of students, particularly Pharrell. Defendants IECP and LAUSD are liable for punitive and exemplary damages because their respective officers, directors, or managing agents had advance knowledge of Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) unfitness and each employed and retained him with a conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others, including Pharrell.”

Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) conduct was intended by him to cause injury to Pharrell or was despicable conduct carried out by him with a willful and conscious disregard of the safety of students, particularly Pharrell. Defendants IECP and LAUSD are liable for punitive and exemplary damages because their respective officers, directors, or managing agents had advance knowledge of Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) unfitness and each employed and retained him with a conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others, including Pharrell.”

12.  Page 13, paragraph 3, in its entirety: “For punitive and exemplary damages according to proof against Defendants IECP and DOE 1;”.

 

a.       Punitive Damages

 

The adequacy of a claim for punitive damages is properly tested by a motion to strike. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of punitive damages where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied. Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Id., subd. (c)(3).) 

 

To hold a corporate employer liable for punitive damages, an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation must have “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294.) “Ratification is a question of fact. The burden of proving ratification is upon the party asserting its-existence. But ratification may be proved by circumstantial as well as direct evidence.” (Greenfield v. Spectrum Inv. Corp.,1985 174 Cal. App. 3d 111, 118 (disapproved of on other grounds by Lakin v. Watkins Associated Indus. 1993 6 Cal. 4th 644) (internal quotations and citations omitted).) Ratification can be shown by a managing agent’s failure to fully investigate, penalize, or terminate an employee who was guilty “oppression, fraud or malice.” (Id. at 121.)

 

Defendant IECP argues there is no basis for punitive damages against IECP. Defendant IECP asserts that the FAC only contains boilerplate language alleging legal conclusions without any factual support, such as “Defendants acted with malice” and “Defendant DOE 1’s (Jose) conduct was intended by him to cause injury to Pharrell or was despicable conduct carried out by him with a willful and conscious disregard.”

 

However, the Court disagrees and finds the FAC sufficiently pled facts to support a claim for punitive damages. The FAC alleges that Jose violently grabbed Plaintiff and slammed him to the blacktop and then called Plaintiff a “motherfucker.” Thus, Jose’s alleged physical and verbal misconduct shows he acted with malice and oppression towards Plaintiff. Further, the FAC alleges that IECP ratified Jose’s misconduct by failing to fully investigate it and failing to penalize or terminate Jose’s employment. (FAC, ¶¶ 29-30.) Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a claim for punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, Defendant IECP’s motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED.

 

b.      Irrelevant and Improper allegations

 

Defendant IECP argues that portions of the First Amended Complaint should be stricken because they are improper, false, and have no relevance to the legal claims.

 

For example, Defendant IECP argues that the Court should strike the words “violently slamming” because it is Plaintiff’s “attempt to inflame passions” and prejudice against IECP. (Motion, p. 7.)

 

However, Defendant IECP fails to cite to any authority to support its contention that these allegations should be stricken. Defendant IECP does not cite to any cases that support striking words like “violently” or “slamming.” These words and allegations are relevant and provide context to Plaintiff’s claims. The Court does not find Defendant’s arguments persuasive and denies the motion to strike these allegations.

 

In sum, Defendant IECP’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant IECP’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action against Defendant IECP is OVERRULED. 

 

Defendant IECP’s motion to strike is DENIED.
