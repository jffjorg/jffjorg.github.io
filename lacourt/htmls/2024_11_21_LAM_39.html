DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV02273    Hearing Date: November 21, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 21, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV16488

MOTION

Demurrer to Cross-Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of Cross-Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Zee Spezzano

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Marco Campo and Earleen Campo

 

MOTION

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Zee Spenzzano (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants and Cross-Complainants Marco Campo and Earleen Campo (“Defendants”) regarding a dispute over a residential lease.  Defendants cross-complained against Plaintiff.  Plaintiff demurs to Defendants’ cross-complaint.  Plaintiff also moves to strike portions of the cross-complaint.  Defendants oppose the motions.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

First Cause of Action: Violation of Los Angeles Municipal Code Section 45.33  

Los Angeles Municipal Code section 45.33 (section 45.33, which is part of the Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance, prohibits a landlord from harassing a tenant.  Harassment is “defined as a landlord’s knowing and willful course of conduct directed at a specific tenant that seriously alarms or annoys the tenant, and that serves no legitimate purpose . . . .”   (L.A. Mun. Code, § 45.33.)  In the instant case, Defendants allege Plaintiff took away Defendants’ storage space, to which Defendants were entitled pursuant to terms in the lease, under the pretext of building an accessory dwelling unit; Plaintiff had no plans to build an accessory dwelling unit, and instead intended to drive Defendants to break their lease so that Plaintiff could lease the premises at a higher rent; Plaintiff installed cameras in the shared backyard space to surveil Defendants’ children; Plaintiff installed a motion-activated floodlight to shine into Defendants’ bedroom; and Plaintiff demanded Defendants provide documentation that the lease did not require and threatened failure to do so would lead to an eviction.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 48-50.)  For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice to ward off Plaintiff’s challenge to the cross-complaint by way of this motion.  Plaintiff may obtain further information regarding Defendants’ claims through discovery.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

Second Cause of Action: Private Nuisance

To state a claim for private nuisance, Defendants must allege Plaintiff interfered with Defendants’ use and enjoyment of the rental property; Plaintiff’s invasion of Defendants’ use and enjoyment of the property caused actual damage; and the interference was unreasonable as to its nature, duration, or amount.  (San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1996) 13 Cal.4th 893, 938.)  As set forth above in paragraphs 48 through 50, Defendants detail the conduct in which Plaintiff engaged that constituted nuisance behavior.  Additionally, Defendants allege Plaintiff failed to inspect and take all necessary steps to remove mold from the property. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 56.)  Defendants’ allegations state a claim for private nuisance.

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ claim for private nuisance is duplicative. That is immaterial, as Defendants are permitted to plead in the alternative.  (Mendoza v. Continental Sales Co. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1402.) 

Third Cause of Action: Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

To state a claim for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, Defendants must allege Plaintiff leased property to Defendants and thereafter interfered with Defendants’ rights to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes of the tenancy.  (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 589 [“the landlord’s act or omission must substantially interfere with a tenant’s right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy”].)  In addition to the allegations made in paragraphs 48 through 50 and 56 in the cross-complaint, Defendants allege Plaintiff’s conduct rendered the property unfit for the purposes for which Defendants leased it and deprived Defendants of any beneficial enjoyment of the property.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 64, 65.)  These allegations support Defendants’ claim for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment. 

For the same reasons, Defendants’ fourth cause of action for breach of lease agreement suffices.  Defendants are entitled to plead breach of contract in general terms.   (Ochs v. PacifiCare of Cal. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 782, 795.) 

Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action: Negligence and Negligence Per Se, Respectively

“Ordinarily, negligence may be pleaded in general terms and the plaintiff need not specify the precise act or omission alleged to constitute the breach of duty.”  (Lopez v. S. Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal. 3d 780, 795.)  Defendants allege Plaintiff failed to exercise due care in the management of the property, a reflected in the allegations discussed above.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 74-76.)  These allegations suffice for pleading purposes.  While Plaintiff argues the sixth cause of action is duplicative of the fifth, as set forth above, that is immaterial.

Motion to Strike

Defendant also moves to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages.  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (College Hospital, supra, (1994) 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.) 

Defendants fail to allege specific facts showing Plaintiff acted with oppression, fraud, or malice where, with respect to the second cause of action, Defendants simply state Plaintiff’s acts were “intentional, willful, and malicious” and Defendants “are also entitled to receive punitive damages, pursuant to Cal. Civ. Code § 3294.”  As such, Defendants must amend their claim for punitive damages. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s demurrer to Defendants’ cross-complaint is overruled.

Plaintiff’s motion to strike is granted, but Defendants have leave to amend.  Defendants are to file an amended cross-complaint within 20 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV02273C    Hearing Date: November 21, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 21, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV02273

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Jane Doe

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Defendant”) for sexual harassment.  Plaintiff alleges that two other employees of Defendant, Defendant David Weitzel (“Weitzel”) and Defendant Eric Pagel (“Pagel”), sexually harassed Plaintiff while Plaintiff worked for Defendant.  Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative first amended complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the first, second, and third causes of action therein.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision(f)(1) permits a party to move for summary adjudication “as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim of damages, . . . or that  one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs.  A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of  cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  

EVIDENCE

            Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections are sustained.

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Hostile Work Environment Sexual Harassment

            Under the Fair Employment and Housing Act, or FEHA, an employer is strictly liable for a supervisor’s harassment of a subordinate.  (State Dept. of Health Services v. Superior Court (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1026, 1042.)  For purposes of FEHA, a supervisor is anyone with authority to “hire, transfer, suspend, lay off, recall, promote, discharge, assign, reward, or discipline other employees, or the responsibility to direct them, or to adjust their grievances, or effectively to recommend that action, if, in connection with the foregoing, the exercise of that authority is not of a merely routine or clerical nature, but requires the use of independent judgment.”  (Gov. Code, § 12926, subd. (t).)  A lead worker who has responsibility to direct an employee's day-to-day duties may be a supervisor under FEHA.  (Chapman v. Enos (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 920, 930-931 (Chapman).)

            While relying on Plaintiff’s deposition, Defendant argues neither Pagel nor Weitzel were Plaintiff’s supervisors.  At deposition, Plaintiff testified Art Saldivar, and later Mehdi Emrani (“Emrani”), supervised her.  (Declaration of Erin J. Cox, Exhibit A, pp. 105, 113, 826-827.)  This evidence meets Defendant’s burden of showing Defendant is not strictly liable for harassment of Plaintiff by Pagel or Weitzel. 

In opposition, Plaintiff does not contend either Pagel or Weitzel supervised her.  Instead, Plaintiff argues Defendant is strictly liable for harassment by Pagel or Weitzel if either Pagel or Weitzel supervised anyone for Defendant.  In support of this argument, Plaintiff cites Chapman. In that case, the Court of Appeal held an employee was the supervisor of another employee and, therefore, their employer was strictly liable for that employee’s harassment of the other employee, “because he had the responsibility to direct her work.”  (Chapman, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 929.)  In the instant case, unlike in Chapman, Plaintiff fails to show either Pagel or Weitzel had the authority to direct her work.  That Pagel or Weitzel may have directed the work of others is irrelevant to the determination of whether Defendant is strictly liable for their harassment of Plaintiff.  Plaintiff, therefore, fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant is strictly liable for harassment of Plaintiff by Pagel or Weitzel.

            If Defendant is not strictly liable for the alleged harassment of Plaintiff by Pagel or Weitzel, Plaintiff must show Defendant knew of previous harassment by Pagel or Weitzel, or failed to take prompt, reasonable, and effective remedial action.  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (j)(1); State Dept. of Health Services v. Superior Court (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1026, 1041.)  Defendant advances evidence Plaintiff reported to Defendant’s ethics hotline on November 9, 2020 Pagel assaulted Plaintiff.  (Declaration of Erin J. Cox, Exhibit A, p. 897.)  On the same date, Plaintiff informed Emrani, her supervisor, Pagel assaulted her.  (Id., Exhibit A, pp. 919-921.)  On November 10, 2020, Emrani submitted a request to Defendant’s human resources department to open an investigation into Pagel’s assault of Plaintiff.  Pursuant to instructions from human resources, Emrani reported Pagel’s assault of Plaintiff to another employee relation system on November 12, 2020.  (Id., Exhibit D, pp. 54-57, 70-71.) 

Defendant assigned the matter to Kimberlee Whitman (“Whitman”), a human resources consultant, who interviewed Plaintiff on November 19, 2024, and confirmed Plaintiff’s allegations against Pagel.  Whitman interviewed Plaintiff again on December 15, 2020 and spoke to her on January 12, 2021; then, at Plaintiff’s request, Whitman interviewed Plaintiff again on January 20, 2021.  (Declaration of Erin J. Cox, Exhibit A, p. 966, Exhibit B, pp. 48, 307, 335-337, 401.)  On December 1, 2020, because of the pending investigation, Defendant had put Pagel on administrative leave when Pagel returned from vacation.  (Id., Exhibit 166.) 

As part of her investigation, Whitman interviewed Emrani on November 24, 2020 and June 23, 2021; Weitzel on December 2, 2020 and January 21, 2020; Pagel on December 9 and 28, 2020; and other potential witnesses on December 1, 2020, January 29, 2021, and February 21 and 24, 2021.  (Declaration of Erin J. Cox, Exhibit B, pp. 83, 87, 101, 166, 184, 226, 356, 371, 374, 382.)  Whitman also obtained Skype messages between Plaintiff, Pagel, and Weitzel from Defendant’s internal forensics team and reviewed other documents Plaintiff and other witnesses provided.  (Id., Exhibit B, pp. 77-78, Exhibit 17, Exhibit 195.) 

On April 23, 2021, Whitman asked Plaintiff to provide her cell phone so that Defendant could extract data pertinent to its investigation, but Plaintiff refused to do so.  (Declaration of Erin J. Cox, Exhibit 310, 312.)  On July 15, 2021, Whitman contacted Plaintiff to request a meeting; but Plaintiff did not meet with Whitman.  (Id., Exhibits 313, 314, 315, 322, 323.)  Whitman completed her investigation report on September 9, 2021,based on which, Defendant gave Pagel a final notice.  (Id., Exhibits 17, 167.)  Defendant eliminated Weitzel’s position in August 2020, after which he did not work for Defendant.  (Id., Exhibit I, pp. 213-216.)  As such, Defendant took no corrective action against Weitzel. 

Defendant’s evidence, detailed above, demonstrates, when Plaintiff report Page’s harassment of her, Defendant promptly investigated the matter and took corrective action.  Therefore, Defendant met its burden of showing it is not liable for harassment by Pagel or Weitzel because Defendant was not negligent in its handling of the matter.

In opposition, Plaintiff makes no argument Defendant was negligent, or remiss, in dealing with Plaintiff’s allegations Pagel or Weitzel harassed her such that Defendant is liable for sexual harassment.  Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of material fact as to her first cause of action.

Second Cause of Action: Failure to Prevent Harassment

Plaintiff’s claim for failure to prevent harassment is derivative of her claim for harassment.  As Defendant has met its burden of showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on her claims for discrimination and harassment against Defendant, Defendant likewise has met its burden of  showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on the cause of action for failure to prevent harassment.  Therefore, as Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of material fact exists as to her claim for harassment, Plaintiff’s claim for failure to prevent harassment likewise fails.  (Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 288.) 

Third Cause of Action: Retaliation

To prevail on a claim for retaliation, Plaintiff must establish “by a preponderance of the evidence, that retaliation for an employee's protected activities was a contributing factor in a contested employment action.  . . .  Once the plaintiff has made the required showing, the burden shifts to the employer to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.”  (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 718.) 

Defendant advances evidence Plaintiff began a leave of absence in April 2021 and filed a complaint of retaliation with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission on April 29, 2021.  In her complaint, Plaintiff stated she had been subjected to adverse actions since she reported the incidents of harassment, including reassignment of clients under her jurisdiction without her input, threat of removal from lucrative accounts, and exclusion from important client communications necessary for Plaintiff to carry out her duties and obligations.  These are not materially adverse employment actions.  “A change that is merely contrary to the employee's interests or not to the employee's liking is insufficient.  Workplaces are rarely idyllic retreats, and the mere fact that an employee is displeased by an employer's act or omission does not elevate that act or omission to the level of a materially adverse employment action.”  (Light v. Department of Parks & Recreation (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 75, 92, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Defendant has met its burden by showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on her claim for retaliation, because Defendant did not subject Plaintiff to a materially adverse employment action.  Defendant then shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her claim for retaliation.

In opposition, Plaintiff concedes her claim is based on being excluded from client relationships that would require her to work with Pagel.  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment, pp. 18-19.)  This was a change to Plaintiff’s employment that was not to Plaintiff’s liking, and Plaintiff contends the change was against her interests.  But that does not render it an adverse employment action.  Thus, Plaintiff has failed to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant subjected Plaintiff to an adverse employment action.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 24STCV16488    Hearing Date: November 21, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 21, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV16488

MOTION

Demurrer to Cross-Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of Cross-Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Zee Spezzano

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Marco Campo and Earleen Campo

 

MOTION

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Zee Spenzzano (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants and Cross-Complainants Marco Campo and Earleen Campo (“Defendants”) regarding a dispute over a residential lease.  Defendants cross-complained against Plaintiff.  Plaintiff demurs to Defendants’ cross-complaint.  Plaintiff also moves to strike portions of the cross-complaint.  Defendants oppose the motions.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

First Cause of Action: Violation of Los Angeles Municipal Code Section 45.33  

Los Angeles Municipal Code section 45.33 (section 45.33, which is part of the Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance, prohibits a landlord from harassing a tenant.  Harassment is “defined as a landlord’s knowing and willful course of conduct directed at a specific tenant that seriously alarms or annoys the tenant, and that serves no legitimate purpose . . . .”   (L.A. Mun. Code, § 45.33.)  In the instant case, Defendants allege Plaintiff took away Defendants’ storage space, to which Defendants were entitled pursuant to terms in the lease, under the pretext of building an accessory dwelling unit; Plaintiff had no plans to build an accessory dwelling unit, and instead intended to drive Defendants to break their lease so that Plaintiff could lease the premises at a higher rent; Plaintiff installed cameras in the shared backyard space to surveil Defendants’ children; Plaintiff installed a motion-activated floodlight to shine into Defendants’ bedroom; and Plaintiff demanded Defendants provide documentation that the lease did not require and threatened failure to do so would lead to an eviction.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 48-50.)  For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice to ward off Plaintiff’s challenge to the cross-complaint by way of this motion.  Plaintiff may obtain further information regarding Defendants’ claims through discovery.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

Second Cause of Action: Private Nuisance

To state a claim for private nuisance, Defendants must allege Plaintiff interfered with Defendants’ use and enjoyment of the rental property; Plaintiff’s invasion of Defendants’ use and enjoyment of the property caused actual damage; and the interference was unreasonable as to its nature, duration, or amount.  (San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1996) 13 Cal.4th 893, 938.)  As set forth above in paragraphs 48 through 50, Defendants detail the conduct in which Plaintiff engaged that constituted nuisance behavior.  Additionally, Defendants allege Plaintiff failed to inspect and take all necessary steps to remove mold from the property. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 56.)  Defendants’ allegations state a claim for private nuisance.

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ claim for private nuisance is duplicative. That is immaterial, as Defendants are permitted to plead in the alternative.  (Mendoza v. Continental Sales Co. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1402.) 

Third Cause of Action: Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

To state a claim for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, Defendants must allege Plaintiff leased property to Defendants and thereafter interfered with Defendants’ rights to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes of the tenancy.  (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 589 [“the landlord’s act or omission must substantially interfere with a tenant’s right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy”].)  In addition to the allegations made in paragraphs 48 through 50 and 56 in the cross-complaint, Defendants allege Plaintiff’s conduct rendered the property unfit for the purposes for which Defendants leased it and deprived Defendants of any beneficial enjoyment of the property.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 64, 65.)  These allegations support Defendants’ claim for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment. 

For the same reasons, Defendants’ fourth cause of action for breach of lease agreement suffices.  Defendants are entitled to plead breach of contract in general terms.   (Ochs v. PacifiCare of Cal. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 782, 795.) 

Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action: Negligence and Negligence Per Se, Respectively

“Ordinarily, negligence may be pleaded in general terms and the plaintiff need not specify the precise act or omission alleged to constitute the breach of duty.”  (Lopez v. S. Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal. 3d 780, 795.)  Defendants allege Plaintiff failed to exercise due care in the management of the property, a reflected in the allegations discussed above.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 74-76.)  These allegations suffice for pleading purposes.  While Plaintiff argues the sixth cause of action is duplicative of the fifth, as set forth above, that is immaterial.

Motion to Strike

Defendant also moves to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages.  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (College Hospital, supra, (1994) 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.) 

Defendants fail to allege specific facts showing Plaintiff acted with oppression, fraud, or malice where, with respect to the second cause of action, Defendants simply state Plaintiff’s acts were “intentional, willful, and malicious” and Defendants “are also entitled to receive punitive damages, pursuant to Cal. Civ. Code § 3294.”  As such, Defendants must amend their claim for punitive damages. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s demurrer to Defendants’ cross-complaint is overruled.

Plaintiff’s motion to strike is granted, but Defendants have leave to amend.  Defendants are to file an amended cross-complaint within 20 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
