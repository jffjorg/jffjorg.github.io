DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV13225    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: 50

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

MARIBEL AVILA,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

ALFRED HONG, MD, FACOG, et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

23STCV13225

Hearing Date:

September 5, 2024

Hearing Time:

2:00 p.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OF DEFENDANT JONATHAN AZIZZADEH, M.D.

 

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Maribel Avila (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on June 9, 2023 against Defendants Alfred Hong, MD, FACOG; Jamie Chia Lin, MD; Jonathan Azizzadeh, MD; Joseph Matthew Nussbaum, MD; Ben B Ha, MD, FACOG; and CHA Hollywood Presbyterian Medical Center. The Complaint alleges one cause of action for medical negligence.

Jonathan Azizzadeh, M.D. (“Azizzadeh”) now moves the Court for an order granting summary judgment in his favor as to Plaintiff’s Complaint. Plaintiff opposes. 

Evidentiary Objections

The Court rules on Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections as follows:

Objection No. 1: overruled  

Objection No. 2: sustained as to subsections e through l of paragraph 4, overruled as to the remainder.

Plaintiff cites to Garibay v. Hemmat (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 735, 737, where “Plaintiffs Alejandra Garibay (Garibay) and Toby C. Barron, Jr., appeal[ed] from a judgment entered after the trial court granted summary judgment on plaintiffs’ claim for medical malpractice arising from surgery performed on Garibay by defendant Mehdi Hemmat, M.D. (Dr. Hemmat). Defendant’s summary judgment motion relied solely on the opinion of a medical expert witness that Dr. Hemmat did not commit medical malpractice. The medical expert witness based his opinion on facts derived from his review of hospital and medical records.” The Court of Appeal noted that “[t]hese records were not properly admitted into evidence under the business records exception to the hearsay rule, however, and did not accompany the declaration or the summary judgment motion. We find that the defendant moving for summary judgment failed to meet his burden of production of evidence, and reverse.” (Ibid.)

The Garibay Court noted that “Dr. Hemmat’s summary judgment motion was not supported by his deposition testimony or declaration, or by any other admissible evidence. The sole support for the summary judgment motion was Dr. Frumovitz’s medical expert witness declaration. Dr. Frumovitz’s declaration stated that he had reviewed medical records from Dr. Hemmat’s office and from Kaiser Harbor City Hospital. The declaration also referred to an operative note by Dr. Moosadazehi, who performed the second bilateral tubal ligation on Garibay. Neither the medical records nor the operative note were in evidence.” (Garibay v. Hemmat, supra, 161 Cal.App.4th at pp. 741-742.) The Court found that “Dr. Frumovitz had no personal knowledge of the underlying facts of the case, and attempted to testify to facts derived from medical and hospital records which were not properly before the court. Therefore his declaration of alleged facts had no evidentiary foundation. An expert’s opinion based on assumptions of fact without evidentiary support has no evidentiary value.” (Id. at p. 743.)  

Here, Azizzadeh submits the Declaration of James A. Macer in support of the motion. Dr. Macer states that in forming the opinions expressed in his declaration, he relied on certain materials specified in paragraph 4 of his declaration. (Macer Decl., ¶ 4.) It appears that Azizzadeh did provide copies of the materials referenced in subsections a-d of paragraph 4 of Dr. Macer’s declaration, specifically, the Complaint, Plaintiff’s Medical Records from Hollywood Presbyterian Medical Center, Plaintiff’s Pathology Report from Hollywood Presbyterian Medical Center, and Plaintiff’s Medical Records from Los Angeles General Medical Center. (Macer Decl., ¶ 4; see Gonzalez Decl., ¶¶ 3-6, Exs. B-E.) By contrast, in Garibay v. Hemmat, supra, 161 Cal.App.4th at page 737, the Court of Appeal found that “[t]he summary judgment motion was insufficient because there were no facts before the court on which the expert medical witness could rely to form his opinion.” (Emphasis added.) In addition, in the reply, Azizzadeh notes that his separate statement only references Exhibits B, C, D, and E, which were provided in connection with the motion, as set forth above. (Gonzalez Decl., ¶¶ 3-6.)

Legal Standard

“[A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) If the moving party carries this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿

“A defendant or cross-defendant has met that party’s burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action. Once the defendant or cross-defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

Discussion

A.    Allegations of the Complaint

In the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that “on June 8, 2022, Plaintiff underwent a caesarian section and bilateral tube ligation at the facilities of CHA HOLLYWOOD PRESBYTERIAN MEDICAL CENTER. The procedure was performed by Defendant ALFRED HONG, MD, FACOG, the attending provider and JONATHAN AZIZZADEH, MD assisted.” (Compl., ¶ 20.) Plaintiff alleges that “[t]he procedure and the following care and treatment were negligently [sic] resulting in a post-partum hemorrhage.” (Compl., ¶ 20.) Plaintiff also alleges that “[o]n June 8, 2022, Defendants ALFRED HONG, MD, FACOG, JAMIE CHIA LIN, MD, and JONATHAN AZIZZADEH, MD, negligently performed a D&C and total abdominal hysterectomy. During the procedure, Plaintiff MARIBEL AVILA’s colon was negligently perforated.” (Compl., ¶ 21.) Plaintiff alleges that “[o]n June 13, 2022, despite ongoing abdominal distention and pain, elevated WBC, tachycardia, nausea, vomiting, and other ongoing symptoms, without performing or ordering any further blood work or imaging to further determine the cause of MARIBEL AVILA’s symptoms, [sic] was discharged from CHA HOLLYWOOD PRESBYTERIAN MEDICAL CENTER.” (Compl., ¶ 23.)

Plaintiff alleges that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of the acts and omissions of the Defendants, Plaintiff MARIBEL AVILA sustained serious and severe personal injuries, as well as pain, suffering, and mental and emotional anxiety. Said injuries have caused and will continue to cause Plaintiff MARIBEL AVILA to sustain pain, physical disability, disfigurement, anxiety, and disruption of the nervous system, all to her general damages according to proof at the time of trial.” (Compl., ¶ 24.) Plaintiff alleges that “[a]s a further direct and proximate result of the acts and omissions of the Defendants, Plaintiff MARIBEL AVILA was compelled to and did employ the services of physicians, surgeons, nurses and the like to handle and care for Plaintiff MARIBEL AVILA’s treatment, thereby incurring medical, professional and incidental expenses.” (Compl., ¶ 25.) “Specifically, on or around June 15, 2022, Plaintiff MARIBEL AVILA was admitted to LAC+USC Medical Center where she underwent an emergency exploratory laparotomy [sic] was diagnosed with multiple colon perforations. Due to the severity of injuries MARIBEL AVILA had to undergo multiple extensive surgeries and procedures, suffered from septic shock, kidney failure, and other injuries.” (Compl., ¶ 26.)

B.    Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Medical Negligence

“The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage.” (Lattimore v. Dickey (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968 [internal citations omitted].)

In the instant motion, Azizzadeh asserts that “there are no triable issues of material fact because, as outlined in the declaration of expert James A. Macer, M.D., there was no breach in the standard of care and no act or omission on the part of JONATHAN AZIZZADEH, M.D. that caused or contributed to the alleged injuries of Plaintiff.” (Mot. at p. 3:22-25.)

Azizzadeh submits the Declaration of James A. Macer, M.D., a physician licensed to practice medicine by the State of California. (Macer Decl., ¶ 1.) In his supporting declaration, Dr. Macer states that “[o]n January 8, 2022, Plaintiff Maribel Avila [DOB: 10/25/1980] presented to Hollywood Presbyterian Medical Center for a planned C-section and bilateral tube ligation procedure. The procedure was performed by Alfred S. Hong, M.D. with Jonathan Azizzadeh, M.D. assisting. It began at or around 8:11 a.m. and was performed under epidural anesthesia and the infant was delivered without issue.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(a).) “Bilateral tubal ligation was performed, after which Dr. Hong noted there was excellent hemostasis.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(b).) “An ovarian cyst, about 5cm in size, was observed. This was first ruptured using the Bovie cautery with clear serosanguinous fluid, and then clamped at the base with a Peon clamp. The cyst was then excised.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(c).) “Surgical pathology of Ms. Avila’s right and left fallopian tube revealed unremarkable tubal mucosa and muscular wall in complete cross sections. Pathology of her right ovary revealed a benign corpora luteum cyst.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(d).)

Following the procedure, “Dr. Hong was notified Ms. Avila had posthemorrhagic hydrocephalus.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(g).) “At or around 3:05 p.m., obstetrician-gynecologist Dr. Jamie C. Lin evaluated Ms. Avila. He was notified of Ms. Avila’s vital signs and condition, with a total of 1486cc blood loss. Ms. Avila’s hemoglobin was low at 7.1. Dr. Lin recommended that the patient be returned to the operating room.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(k).) “A decision was made to take Ms. Avila to the operating room for a suction dilation and curettage (‘D&C’) procedure, with possible hysterectomy. The risks and benefits of the procedure were discussed with Ms. Avila, and she agreed to proceed with the surgery under general anesthesia.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(l).) “The procedure was performed by Dr. Lin and Dr. Hong, with Dr. Azizzadeh assisting.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(m).)

“A decision was made to convert the D&C procedure into an exploratory laparotomy...” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(m).) “Upon entering the peritoneum, bleeding was observed. In a post-operative note, Dr. Hong notes that upon entry into the abdomen, it was observed that the hysterotomy sutures were all intact, and that the bleeding was not coming from the prior uterine incision site. The bilateral tubal ligation sutures were also intact, as well as the sutures from the right ovarian cystectomy. When the peritoneum was opened, the uterus was visualized and appeared to be swollen and bruised, with possible bleeding into the myometrium, possibly due to multiple large myomas (benign growths in the smooth muscles of the uterus). The decision was made to proceed with a hysterectomy due to bleeding. Bilateral round ligaments were doubly clamped, transected, and ligated. Excellent hemostasis was observed.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(n).) “A supracervical hysterectomy was then performed.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(o).)

“Excellent hemostasis was observed throughout the procedure. All sponge, lap, instrument, and needle counts were correct at the end of the procedure. The patient remained intubated and was transferred to a recovery room. Surgical pathology on the uterus noted (1) prominent implantation site intermediate trophoblasts within myometrium, compatible with exaggerated placental site; (2) leiomyomata (up to 2 cm); and (3) gestational endometrium.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(p).)

“The following day, Ms. Avila was administered two blood transfusions. She was extubated to normal oxygen. She was awake, alert, and following commands. Her white blood cell count trended high at 14.0 K/uL and the following day it was at 17 K/uL. Therefore, an infectious disease consult was ordered. Dr. Joseph S. Nussbaum of Infectious Disease assessed the patient and noted an isolated post-operative fever and leukocytosis after acute blood loss anemia. He thought both the fever and leukocytosis could be due to the brisk bleeding she encountered, but infection would be difficult to exclude. Therefore, Dr. Nussbaum recommended continuing the antibiotic treatment of ceftriaxone and metronidazole that she was already on.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(r).) “Ms. Avila had no further bleeding on June 10, 2022, two-days post-op. She continued to report she was feeling better the following days and was discharged home on June 13, 2022 in stable condition.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(s).)

“On June 15, 2022, Ms. Avila presented to the Los Angeles General Medical Center Emergency Department with complaints of shortness of breath since the day prior.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(t).) “She was admitted for an exploratory laparotomy, extended right hemicolectomy, abthera placement, and rigid proctoscopy procedure, which they proceeded with urgently.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(u).) “Post-operatively, Ms. Avila was admitted to Surgical ICU alert and following commands. Ms. Avila was on levophed and vasopressors, although her pressor requirements were decreasing with fluid resuscitation. Her abdomen remained open for takeback to the operating room the following day, to assure no further ischemia and to examine the integrity of anastomosis.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(v).) Dr. Macer states that “[t]he surgical pathology from Plaintiff’s June 15, 2022 procedure at Los Angeles General Medical Center indicates that a 3 cm defect to the patient’s cecum and a 2 cm defect to the patient’s ascending colon were seen during the urgent exploratory laparotomy. These locations are unusual locations for an injury to occur from a C-section, bilateral tube ligation, D&C, or hysterectomy procedure.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 10.)

“In the morning of June 16, 2022, Ms. Avila was awake and following commands. She was taken to the operating room and underwent an exploratory laparotomy, small bowel serosal injury repair x 2, blake drain replacement x 3, delayed closure of abdominal wall procedure, and superficial wound vac placement.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(x).) “Ms. Avila was admitted at Los Angeles General Medical Center for over a month. While there, she suffered from septic shock and developed an acute kidney injury. She was discharged on July 23, 2022.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 5(y).)

Dr. Macer states that “[t]here was no breach in the standard of care by Dr. Azizzadeh in his role in treating and caring for Plaintiff Maribel Avila. The standard of care of an assistant surgeon is to assist the lead surgeon in any way he can with respect to the procedure being performed – in this case a Caesarian section, bilateral tube ligation, dilation and curettage, and total abdominal hysterectomy. The assistant’s role includes helping the surgeon best visualize the operative field, suctioning to keep the field clear, cutting suture material, and helping to close the surgical incision, among other responsibilities. There was no breach in the standard of care by Dr. Azizzadeh in his performance as assistant surgeon in either of Plaintiff’s procedures on June 8, 2022. Nothing in the operative reports indicates that Dr. Azizzadeh breached the standard of care as an assistant surgeon during either procedure on June 8, 2022.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 7.) Dr. Macer further states that “[w]ith regard to the second procedure, Dr. Azizzadeh was a second assist and his involvement would have been minimal. Based on my review of the materials, Dr. Azizzadeh met the standard of care of an assistant surgeon in both the C-section and bilateral tubal ligation procedure and in the D&C and hysterectomy procedure.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 7.)

Dr. Macer also states that “[i]t is my opinion that, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, there was no act or omission on Defendant Jonathan Azizzadeh, M.D. [sic] that caused or contributed to Plaintiffs injuries. Nothing in the medical record reflects that Dr. Azizzadeh failed to meet his responsibilities as assistant surgeon in either procedure. There is no evidence that Dr. Azizzadeh caused or contributed to any of the patient’s injuries in his role as assistant surgeon to her June 8, 2022 procedures.” (Macer Decl., ¶ 9.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Azizzadeh has met his initial burden of demonstrating that Plaintiff’s cause of action against him for medical negligence is without merit. In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that “there is a triable issue of material fact on the issues of liability and causation such that Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment should be denied.” (Opp’n at p. 3:16-18.)

Plaintiff submits the Declaration of Howard C Mandel, M.D., a medical doctor currently licensed to practice medicine in the State of California. (Mandel Decl., ¶ 1.) Dr. Mandel states that “[o]n June 8, 2022, Maribel Avila underwent the Caesarian Delivery with Bilateral Tubal Ligation and right Ovarian cystectomy performed by Dr. Hong with Dr. Azizzadeh assisting. The delivery was reported to have an estimated blood loss of only 600 cc’s [much less than the average Caesarian Delivery] and to have been routine and without any complications…” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 5(b).) “Dr. Lin’s late entry note written at 18:19 states that she was ‘Called by charge nurse regarding postpartum bleeding, came to evaluate the patient at around 2:30 PM’…Maribel Avila goes into DIC and is transferred back to the PACU at 15:15 then to the OR where she undergoes an emergency D&C and then subtotal hysterectomy at 16:48. Dr. Alfred Hong and Dr. Jonathan Azizzadeh both assist Dr. Lin in this procedure.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 5(g)-(h).) “Dr. Azizzadeh testified that he assisted in the surgery until Dr. Hong arrived.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 5(j).)

            Dr. Mandel states that “Ms. Avila was discharged from Hollywood Presbyterian Hospital on June 13, 2022,” and that “[u]pon discharge Plaintiff experienced abdominal pain and swelling, vomiting, difficulty eating and difficulty ambulating, and lose [sic] of consciousness.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 5(m)-(n).) “On June 15, 2022, Plaintiff presented to the emergency department at Los Angeles County Hospital and was admitted for an exploratory laparotomy. Four injuries were documented by the surgeons who explored Maribel Avila including a 3 cm defect to the patient’s cecum, a 2 cm defect to the patient’s ascending colon, as well as two areas of the small bowel. Plaintiff was hospitalized at Los Angeles County Hospital until July 23, 2022….Plaintiff was readmitted to White Memorial Hospital in August 2022 and November 2022 with diagnoses of septic shock and renal failure.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 5(o)-(p).)

            Dr. Mandel states that “[b]ased upon my knowledge, training, experience, and review of the relevant materials, it is my opinion, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, that the care and treatment provided by Dr. Jonathan Azizzadeh to Plaintiff fell below the standard of care in the community.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 6.) Dr. Mandel states that “[t]here is no evidence that the nurses, Dr. Alfred Hong, Dr. Jonathan Azizzadeh or Dr. Jamie C. Lin ever instituted a massive transfusion policy or protocol or standard of care [sic] adequate transfusions and replacement of clotting factors and platelets that could have avoided the emergency hysterectomy.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 6(a).)

            Dr. Mandel states that “[t]he evidence demonstrates that Dr. Azizzadeh assisted in the hysterectomy for approximately thirty-five to forty minutes. During this time, as an assistant surgeon, Dr. Azizzadeh had the responsibility intraoperatively to assure that Dr. Lin had proper visualization as well as to make sure that during dissection and vascular coagulation that the bowel was not in harm’s way. The fact that there was a ‘3 cm defect to the patient’s cecum and a 2 cm defect to the patient’s ascending colon’ as well as two areas of the small bowel that were injured is clear evidence that either there was poor visualization, careless dissection or vascular coagulation, that resulted in Ms. Avila’s injuries.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 6(b).)

            Dr. Mandel further states that “it is my opinion that the care and treatment by Dr. Azzizadeh [sic] was a substantial factor in causing or contributing to Ms. Avila’s injuries. My opinions are based on the following: (a) Maribel Avila was critically injured on June 8th 2022, to a medical probability during the hysterectomy performed by Dr. Jamie C. Lin with Dr. Alfred Hong and Dr. Jonathan Azizzadeh assisting. To a medical probability in their haste operating on a patient in shock one or more of these obstetricians caused substandard iatrogenic injuries which resulted in 4 bowel lacerations, fulminant peritonitis, her near death, and chronic problems that continue to plague her today. (b) The failures of both the nurses and Dr. Jamie C. Lin, Dr. Alfred Hong and Dr. Jonathan Azizzadeh to institute a massive transfusion policy exaggerated her critical condition. If Maribel Avila had been as required by the standard of care adequately transfused and had blood products infused, to a medical probability there would have been significantly better coagulability and hence less ooze and less leaking from small blood vessels. To a medical probability the Bovie electrocautery would have been utilized less thereby decreasing the risk of iatrogenic bowel injury.” (Mandel Decl., ¶ 7.)

            In the reply, Azizzadeh asserts that “[t]he declaration of Plaintiff’s expert contains only conclusory opinions with no reasonable explanation for the basis of his opinion, and ought to be excluded.” (Reply at p. 6:12-13.) Azizzadeh cites to Kelley v. Trunk (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 519, 523, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[i]n professional malpractice cases, expert opinion testimony is required to prove or disprove that the defendant performed in accordance with the prevailing standard of care, except in cases where the negligence is obvious to laymen.” (Internal citation omitted.) The Kelley Court noted that “[s]ummary judgment is appropriate in every case where the statutory standard is met, and the absence of material issues for trial established. However, that standard is not satisfied by laconic expert declarations which provide only an ultimate opinion, unsupported by reasoned explanation.” (Id. at pp. 524-525.) However, the Court finds that here, Dr. Mandel’s opinion is supported by reasoning and explanation.

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has raised triable issues of material fact as to whether Azizzadeh breached the standard of care and as to causation. Accordingly, the Court denies Azizzadeh’s motion for summary judgment.

            Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Azizzadeh’s motion for summary judgment is denied.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this Order.
