DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV05731    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: 73

06/27/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

ZZ FASHION CORP. v. DRESS BARN ONLINE, INC., et al. (23STCV05731)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Russell Allyn, Michael Fuoroli (Buchalter)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Thomas Sands (Sand Law Groups, APLC)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed 04/23/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiff $48,750.83 in attorneys’ fees and costs. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On March 15, 2023, Plaintiff ZZ Fashion Corp. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Dress Barn Online, Inc. (“Dress Barn”), Stein Mart Online, LLC (“Stein Mart”) and Retail Ecommerce Ventures, LLC alleging the following causes of action:

C/A 1: Breach of Contract;

C/A 2: Account Stated; and

C/A 3: Money Had and Received.

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff is a California corporation that manufactures clothing. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) On or about April 2, 2021 and April 5, 2021, respectively, Plaintiff entered into a pair of Vendor Direct to Customer Agreements (the “Agreements”) with Stein Mart and Dress Barn. (Id., ¶ 13.) Pursuant to the Agreements, Defendants agreed to pay Plaintiff certain sales and other commissions in connection with the promotion and sale of Plaintiff’s clothing and accessories on Defendants’ branded e-commerce websites. (Id., ¶ 14.) Plaintiff alleges it performed all promises, covenants, and conditions it agreed to perform in accordance with the terms of the Agreement, but Defendants breached the Agreements by failing to timely pay Plaintiff the sums due. (Id., ¶¶ 15, 20.) Plaintiff further alleges the total outstanding amount owed by Defendants under the Agreements is approximately $131,617.11. (Id., ¶ 21.)

On March 28, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication against Dress Barn and Stein Mart as to Plaintiff’s claim for breach of contract against them.

 

On April 11, 2024, based upon the order granting summary adjudication and the stipulations of the parties as to liability and damages, the Court entered judgment against all Defendants. (Allyn Decl. ¶ 15.)

 

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for attorneys’ fees against Defendants. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

B.     Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees

 

In any action “on a contract” that provides for the prevailing party’s recovery of attorneys’ fees, the prevailing party “shall be entitled to [an award of] reasonable attorneys’ fees.”¿(Civ. Code, § 1717.)

 

Here, Plaintiff presents a copy of the Vendor Direct to Customer Agreements underlying Plaintiff’s complaint.  (Allyn Decl., Exhs. 1 & 2.)  The contracts include an attorneys’ fees provision which states, “If any action, suit or other proceeding is instituted concerning or arising out of this Agreement, the prevailing party shall recover from the nonprevailing party all such party’s reasonable attorneys’ fees incurred in each and every such action, suit or other proceeding, including any and all appeals and petitions therefrom.” (Allyn Decl., Exhs. 1 & 2.) 

 

On March 28, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication against Dress Barn and Stein Mart as to Plaintiff’s claim for breach of contract against them. On April 11, 2024, based upon the order granting summary adjudication and the stipulations of the parties as to liability and damages, the Court entered judgment against all Defendants. (Allyn Decl., ¶ 15.)

 

Therefore, Plaintiff is a prevailing party in this action. Since the contracts provide that Plaintiff is entitled to recover costs and attorneys’ fees in connection with prevailing on its enforcement of the right to payment under those contracts, Plaintiff is entitled to its reasonable attorneys’ fees in this action. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal. 4th 1122, 1131.)

 

C.     Hourly Rate/Reasonable Hours Incurred

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) 

 

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.”  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) 

 

While not per se objectionable, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney fee request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable.  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.) 

 

Plaintiff seeks to recover a total of $47,960.50 in attorneys’ fees for work completed by several attorneys and paralegals with varying hourly rates.

 

In Plaintiff’s counsel declaration, Russell Allyn sets forth that he billed at a rate of $525 an hour; that attorney Michael Fuoroli billed at a rate of $340 an hour; and that attorney Mathew Kramer billed at a rate of $325 an hour. (Allyn Decl., ¶¶ 17-18.) Mr. Allyn attests to their experience and reasonableness of the rates. (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds that the rates requested by Plaintiff is reasonable and commensurate with rates charged by attorneys with comparable skill and expertise. Based on the evidence before it, the Court is not inclined to reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

Plaintiff submitted invoices and statements to support its requested attorneys’ fees. (Allyn Decl., Exhs. 4 & 5.) The Court notes that upon review of Plaintiff’s attached billing records, the invoice shows 0.9 hours billed for Joshua Mizrahi at a rate of $455 an hour and 0.5 hours billed for Anthony J. Napolitano at a rate of $510 an hour. Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration does not identify or attest to the reasonableness of Mr. Mizrahi or Mr. Napolitano’s rates. Therefore, the Court is not inclined to include these fees totaling $664.

 

Plaintiff’s billing application shows the total amount of attorneys’ fees that Plaintiff’s counsel billed to its client in this matter from December 2022 through March 2024. The Court has considered the sought attorney fees based on the stated tasks and does not find them unreasonable in light of the work completed by counsel in this case. Further, verified cost bills are “prima facie evidence the costs, expenses and services listed were necessarily incurred…” (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682). In light of the uncontroverted evidence in support and its own experience, the Court finds the hourly rates and work billed by counsel reasonable. (See Morris v. Hyundai Motor America (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 24, 41, as modified (Oct. 11, 2019), review denied (Jan. 2, 2020).) 

 

Plaintiff anticipated additional attorneys’ fees to review Defendants’ Opposition, prepare Plaintiff’s Reply, and to prepare for and attend the motion hearing, at a rate of $525 an hour for 2 hours, totaling $1,050. (Allyn Decl., ¶ 20.) Since the motion is unopposed, the Court will only include an hour of these anticipated fees for Plaintiff to prepare for and attend the motion hearing.

 

Plaintiff also filed a Memorandum of Costs seeking the recovery of its filing and service costs in connection with this matter. The “prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b).) Thus, the Court further awards Plaintiff’s requested costs for $1,979.33. 

 

Accordingly, this Court grants Plaintiff a reduced amount of attorneys’ fees totaling $46,771.50 and costs totaling $1,979.33. The Court awards Plaintiff a total amount of $48,750.83 in attorney fees and costs. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiff $48,750.83 in attorneys’ fees and costs.

PUIG v. GOODPEOPLE ROASTING LLC, et al. (23STCV27674)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Scott Ernest Wheeler (Law Office of Scott Ernest Wheeler)

Counsel for Defendants/ opposing party: N/A

 

(1)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSE TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, PROPOUNDED ON DEFENDANT GP GROUP LLC (filed on 05/17/2024)

(2)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE, PROPOUNDED ON DEFENDANT GP GROUP LLC (filed on 05/17/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Defendant GP Group LLC are GRANTED. Defendant GP Group LLC shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against GP Group LLC and their attorney of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $1,750. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 13, 2023, Plaintiff Eric Puig brought this action individually and on behalf of all other aggrieved employees against Defendants Goodpeople Roasting LLC and GP Group LLC.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Defendants own and operate one or more coffee bars, that also operate as restaurants, bars, and Marijuana dispensaries, in Southern California. (Compl., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff, along with other non-exempt employees of Defendants, worked and performed duties and tasks at these coffee bars and additionally, received wages from Defendants. (Ibid.) Defendants implemented common policies and practices within their coffee bars that have resulted in systematic violations of California’s wage and hour laws. (Compl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiff brings this representative action to recover civil penalties relating to systematic violations of California wage and hour laws by Defendants.

 

On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed Motions to Compel responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Defendant GP Group LLC. Plaintiff seeks monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)  

 

If a motion to compel responses is filed, the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290 (c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).) 

 

B.      Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff served for Special Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Production of Documents, Set One on Defendant GP Group LLC on December 23, 2023. (Wheeler Decl. ¶¶ 2, Exh. A.) Responses were due on or before January 26, 2024. (Id. ¶ 3.) On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant GP Group LLC a correspondence regarding its lack of responses. (Id. ¶ 6, Exh. B.) Since then, no responses have been served. (Id. ¶¶ 7-8.)  Therefore, because responses have not been served, the motions to compel are granted.  

 

Plaintiff requests $1,750.00 in monetary sanctions for counsel’s work on its Motion to Compel Request for Production of Documents, Set One against Defendant GP Group LLC and their counsel of record, representing 2 hours spent on the motion at an hourly rate of $875. (Id. ¶ 9.)  Plaintiff also requests $2,625.00 in monetary sanctions for counsel’s work on its Special Interrogatories, Set One against Defendant GP Group LLC and their counsel of record, representing 3 hours spent on the motion at an hourly rate of $875. (Id. ¶ 9.) Thus, Plaintiff requests a total of $4,375.00 in monetary sanctions.

 

The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Defendant GP Group LLC has failed to respond. However, the amount requested is excessive due to the type of motion at issue, the fact no opposition was filed, and the fact counsel can appear at the hearing remotely. Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the total amount of $1,750 (2 hours of attorney time to file both motions and appear at the hearing.)

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Defendant GP Group LLC are GRANTED. Defendant GP Group LLC shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant GP Group LLC and their attorney of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $1,750. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s order and file a proof of service of such.

MONTEBELLO (EDENS), LLC v. TASH
DENTAL CORPORATION, et al. (24STCV13022)



 



Counsel for
Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Joshua Moon,
Candice Harper (Buchalter)



Counsel for
Defendants/moving party:  Paul Marks (Neufeld
Marks)



 



demurrer TO complaint (filed 06/03/24)



 



TENTATIVE RULING



 



The Court OVERRULES Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.



 



I. BACKGROUND



 



On May 23,
2024, Plaintiff Montebello (Edens), LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this unlawful
detainer action against Defendants Tash Dental Corporation and Raymond Tash DDS
a Professional Corporation.  



 



The Complaint
alleges the following. On or about
August 15, 1995, Plaintiff's predecessor-in-interest leased to Defendants'
predecessor-in-interest the premises identified as Store No. 8 and commonly
known as 880-B West Beverly Boulevard, Montebello, California 90640
("Premises"), pursuant to a written Lease. (Compl., ¶ 8.) In or
around October 2022, the Original Lease, as amended, was further amended and
assigned to Defendant Tash Dental Corporation. (Ibid.) Plaintiff is
informed and believes that Defendant Raymond Tash DDS, also occupies the
Premises pursuant to an oral assignment or sublease, which was entered into
without Landlord's knowledge or consent, in violation of the terms of the
Lease. (Compl., ¶ 9.) It is alleged that Defendant
failed to make all the payments of rent due under the lease. (Compl., ¶ 13.)
On May 10, 2024, Plaintiff caused to be served on Defendants a written notice
stating the amount of leasehold obligations due and requiring Defendants to pay
the whole thereof or deliver up possession of the Premises within five days
after service of the notice. (Ibid.) At the time of service of the
notice, Defendants had not paid its rental obligations in the total amount of $102,490.13. (Compl., ¶ 12)



 



On June 3, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer on the
grounds that Plaintiff failed to allege facts sufficient to state an unlawful
detainer cause of action.



·       Defendants
assert that the lease attached to the Complaint does not name Plaintiff as the
landlord. Instead, the landlord names Montebello LLC, a Delaware Limited
Liability Company as the landlord. Thus, Plaintiff has not established that it
had the right to collect rent.



 



On June 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing Defendants’
demurrer is procedurally improper and not based on any legal authority.



·       Defendants
demurrer was filed late and should be denied because it is untimely.



·       Plaintiff has
sufficiently alleged a landlord-tenant relationship by alleging its interest as
owner of the Premises.



o  
In unlawful detainer actions, it is incumbent upon the plaintiff
to show the existence of a landlord-tenant relationship for the subject
property at issue. However, for the purposes of the pleading stage, a plaintiff
can meet its burden of showing a landlord-tenant relationship by alleging that
he or she procured a written lease from its predecessor-in-interest. (Berryman
v. Gibson (1908) 7 Cal.App. 679, 682.)



·       Defendants
and its counsel should be sanctioned for filing a frivolous demurrer.



 



Defendants have not filed a reply.



 



II. ANALYSIS



 



A.     Legal
Standard for Demurrer



 



A demurrer
tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn
v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers,
courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be
apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian
v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the
pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v.
Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the
court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual
allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is
whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn,
supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)



 



B.     Meet and
Confer Requirement



 



“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to
this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by
telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for
the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would
resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., §
430.41, subd. (a).)  



 



As an initial matter, the Court notes that Defendants
have failed to provide a meet and confer declaration with their demurrer.



 



Plaintiff does not raise this issue in its opposition. Plaintiff’s
counsel states in his declaration that the parties met and conferred by
telephone on June 12, 2024, after Plaintiff received Defendants’ demurrer. (Moon
Decl., ¶ 5.)



 



However, Defendants should have met and conferred prior
to filing the instant motion. The Court finds Defendants’ efforts fall short of the
good-faith effort requirements set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section
430.41(a). However, failure to meet and confer is not a proper ground for
overruling a demurrer. (CCP § 430.41(a)(4).) Thus,
the Court continues its discussion on the merits of the motion.



 



C.     Timeliness



 



First,
Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ motion should be denied because it is
untimely.



 



A demurrer
must be filed and served at least 16 court days before the hearing (Code Civ.
Proc. §1005(b)), which is increased by 2 calendar days when notice thereof is
served electronically (Code Civ. Proc. § 1010(a)(3)(B).)



 



Here, the
proof of service accompanying the demurrer indicates that it was served on June
3, 2024. (Moon Decl., ¶ 4.) With a hearing date of June 27, 2024, the last day
Defendants could serve the demurrer by electronic means was May 31, 2024.



 



However, the court has discretion to consider an untimely demurrer as long as the substantial rights of the parties
are not affected.  (Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742,
750.) 



 



Plaintiff has not shown that the substantial
rights of the parties were affected. Thus, the Court exercises its discretion
to consider the untimely demurrer.



 



D.     Unlawful
Detainer Action



 



To state a cause of action
for unlawful detainer based on nonpayment of rent, the plaintiff must allege
facts demonstrating, “’(1) the tenant is
in possession of the premises; (2)
that possession is without permission; (3) the tenant
is in default
for nonpayment of rent; (4) the tenant has
been properly
served with a
written three-day notice;
and (5)
the default continues
after the three-day notice period has
elapsed.’ [Citation.]” (Fitness Int'l,
LLC v. KB Salt Lake III, LLC (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 1032, 1045.)



 



Defendants
demur to the entire Complaint on the grounds Plaintiff failed to allege facts sufficient to state an unlawful detainer
cause of action. Specifically, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not
sufficiently alleged a landlord-tenant relationship. Defendants assert that the
lease attached to the Complaint does not name Plaintiff as the landlord.
Instead, the landlord names Montebello LLC, a Delaware Limited Liability Company,
as the landlord. Thus, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not established that
it was a party to the lease and had the right to collect rent.



 



Here, the Complaint alleges the
Lease for the Premises was entered into between Defendants' predecessor-in-interest
and Plaintiff's predecessor-in-interest. (Compl., ¶ 8.) The Complaint also
alleges that Plaintiff is the owner of the real property. (Compl., ¶ 7.)



 



The Court finds Plaintiff’s allegations
are sufficient. In unlawful
detainer actions, the plaintiff must allege the existence of a landlord-tenant
relationship for the subject property at issue. At the pleading stage, a
plaintiff can meet its burden of showing a landlord-tenant relationship by
alleging that he or she procured a written lease from its
predecessor-in-interest. (Berryman v. Gibson (1908) 7 Cal.App. 679,
682.) Thus, Plaintiff’s allegation that it is a predecessor-in-interest to the
lease and is the owner of the real property is sufficient at this stage.



 



Accordingly,
Defendants’ demurrer to the Complaint is overruled.



 



E.     Sanctions



 



A party and
its attorney may be sanctioned or ordered to show cause by the Court why it
should not "pay any reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees,
incurred by another party as a result of bad-faith actions or tactics that are
frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay." (Code Civ. Proc.
§ 128.5, subd. (a).) This includes filing motions that are "totally and
completely without merit or for the sole purpose of harassing the opposing
party." (Code Civ. Proc. § 128.5, subd. (b)(1)-(2).)



 



Plaintiff’s
opposition to the demurrer includes a request for sanctions against Defendants
in the sum of $2,525.00. Plaintiff conclusively states that Defendants’ motion
was filed in bad faith and brought for the purpose of delay. While the Court
finds that Defendants’ argument lacks merit, the Court declines to impose
sanctions.



 



III. DISPOSITION



 





The Court OVERRULES Defendants’ demurrer as to Plaintiff’s
entire Complaint.
