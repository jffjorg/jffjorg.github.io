DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01878    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

RAYMOND THOMAS,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22VECV01878

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

AEOLUS AIR CHARTER, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          September 4, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Aeolus Air Charter, Inc. and Lett’s Jett, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Raymond Thomas

Motion to Tax Costs

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The Court strikes Item 16 (Other) in the amount of $3875 for mediation fees from plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

BACKGROUND

On November 4, 2022, plaintiff Raymond Thomas filed a complaint against Aeolus Air Charter, Inc. for wrongful death based on negligence, strict products liability, and products liability/negligence.

On December 28, 2022, the case was deemed related to 22STCV03393.

On November 8, 2023, plaintiff filed amendments designating Let’s Jett, Inc., Conner Jadwin, and Dave Ventrella as Does.

On April 10, 2024, the related cases were consolidated with 22STCV03393 being the lead case for discovery purposes.

On June 5, 2024, defendants filed a stipulation for purposes of liability that defendant Aeolus Air Charter, Inc., aka Let’s Jett, Inc. was the sole cause of the subject accident.  The stipulation noted that defendants dispute the nature and extent of damages (if any) that will be claimed by Raymond Thomas and that the action will be tried on damages only.

On June 27, 2024, after a jury trial, the Court ordered judgment entered for plaintiff Raymond Thomas against defendants Aeolus Air Charter, Inc. and Let’s Jett, Inc. for damages of $850,000 ($135,000 past loss, $715,000 future loss).

On July 16, 2024, defendants filed a notice of intent to move for new trial or, in the alternative, for a conditional remittitur.

On July 17, 2024, plaintiff Thomas filed a memorandum of costs.

On July 25, 2024, the Court granted defendants’ request for stay of enforcement of judgment.

On August 1, 2024, defendants filed a motion to tax costs.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.  This means that the prevailing party is entitled to all of his costs unless another statute provides otherwise.  Absent such statutory authority, the court has no discretion to deny costs to the prevailing party.”  Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal. App. 4th 111, 128-29 (citations and internal quotations omitted); CCP §1032(b) (“Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding”).

CCP §1033.5(c) states, in relevant part:  “Any award for costs shall be subject to the following: 

            (1) Costs are allowable if incurred, whether or not paid. 

(2) Allowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation. 

(3) Allowable costs shall be reasonable in amount. 

(4) Items not mentioned in this section . . . may be allowed . . . in the Court’s discretion.”

“If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.  On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.”  Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal. App. 4th 761, 774 (citation omitted).   Id.  “The court’s first determination, therefore, is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item, and whether it appears proper on its face.  If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show them to be unnecessary or unreasonable.”  Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal. App. 4th 111, 131 (citations omitted).  “[I]t is not enough for the losing party to attack submitted costs by arguing that he thinks the costs were not necessary or reasonable.  Rather the losing party has the burden to present evidence and prove that the claimed costs are not recoverable.”  Seever v. Copley Press, Inc. (2006) 141 Cal. App. 4th 1550, 1557.

DISCUSSION

            Defendants request that the Court strike plaintiff’s memorandum of costs filed on July 17, 2024 at Item 16 (Other) for $3,875 for mediation fee incurred on July 18, 2022.

            Defendants argue that the mediation cannot have been reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation because the mediation occurred two months before the complaint was filed.  Defendants argue that rather than being necessary to the conduct of the litigation, the mediation was conducted in the hopes of avoiding litigation. 

            In opposition, plaintiff contends that mediation expenses are recoverable in the court’s discretion.  Plaintiff asserts that the mediation was "essentially court-ordered as [Judge Hill] ordered the parties to either participate in private mediation or a multi-hour in-person settlement between counsel and the parties.”  Plaintiff argues that the parties fulfilled that obligation by participating in, and paying for, private mediation after the related case had already been filed and before the herein case was filed.  Plaintiff further argues that the parties would have had to participate in the mediation anyways if Judge Hill had not determined that the parties had complied with the standing order by already participating in a mediation.  Plaintiff cites to Gibson v. Bobroff (1996) 49 Cal. App. 4th 1202, 1209 which states that “mediation is fundamental to the conduct of litigation as it encourages the parties to settle their disputes before trial and exposes parties who fail to agree to a reasonable settlement proposal to the risk of a discretionary court determination that they should pay their opponent’s share of the failed mediation.”  Further, the court stated:  “We hold that when an unsuccessful mediation has been court-ordered, reasonably necessary expenses incident thereto may, in the sound discretion of the trial court, be awarded after trial to the prevailing party.”  Id.

            In reply, defendants contend that the mediation was not court-ordered as it took place in September 2022 prior to when plaintiff filed the herein case and well before the case was transferred to Judge Hill in November 2022.  Defendants reiterate their argument that pre-litigation mediation fees are not recoverable as they are not reasonably necessary to the conduct of litigation.  Defendants further argue that the Gibson case cited by plaintiff is inapplicable as that case specifically addresses that the mediation was court-ordered.

            The Court rules as follows:  Defendants have shown that the mediation costs in the amount of $3875 do not fall under CCP §1033.5(c)(2) as they were not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation.  The mediation was held before the complaint was filed and was merely convenient and/or beneficial to the preparation of litigation.  Indeed, had the Court never ordered mediation in this case, the funds had already been spent and the mediation conducted.  The Court shall not allow the fortuity of the Court’s later order to convert an already expended non-litigation costs to a compensable litigation cost.

             The motion is thus GRANTED.       

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.  The Court strikes Item 16 (Other) in the amount of $3875 for mediation fees from plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.
