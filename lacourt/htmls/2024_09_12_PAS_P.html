DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV01872    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE)

I.        INTRODUCTION

     Plaintiff Melissa Gutholm (plaintiff) moves for an order compelling further responses to special interrogatories, set one, served upon defendant the Young Men’s Christian Association of West San Gabriel Valley (defendant.) Plaintiff does not seek sanctions.  

 III.     LEGAL STANDARD

  In California, discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.) “Under the discovery statutes, information is discoverable if it is unprivileged and is either relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to reveal admissible evidence.” (John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.) “The court shall limit discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).)

 

In responding to an interrogatory, a party shall respond in one of the following ways: providing an answer (1) containing the information sought to be discovery, (2) exercising the party’s option to produce writings, or (2) objecting to the particular interrogatory. On receipt of a response, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the interrogatories if the demanding party deems any of the following apply: (1) an answer is evasive or incomplete, (2) an exercise of the option to produce documents is unwarranted or inadequate, or (3) the objection is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.300, subd. (c).) “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.” (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).)

 IV.     ANALYSIS

  A.    Meet and Confer

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

The court finds the meet and confer requirement has been met. Plaintiff’s counsel, Adam Kargman, declares that he communicated with counsel on several occasions and as a result of those communications defendant supplemented their responses once. Counsel does not declare the parties communicated any further after the supplemental responses. Nevertheless, the court finds the initial meetings sufficient to support the parties have met and conferred.

  B.    Compliance of Responses

The special interrogatories at issue are numbers 1, 2, 10, 18-26, 31-42, and 50-56.

 

SROG Nos. 1, 2: Plaintiff requested the dates (month, day, year) perpetrator worked and/or was employed for Defendant. Defendant provided the year. To the extent Defendant does not know any further information about the dates it should so state. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).) The motion is granted.

 

SROG Nos. 10: Plaintiff requested Defendant “IDENTIFY” (e.g., contact information) the perpetrator’s supervisor. Defendant responded it may have been “Mike Spain” with no further information. To the extent Defendant has no ability to “IDENTIFY” Mike Spain in the manner requested by plaintiff it should so state. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).) The motion is granted.

 

SROG No. 19: Plaintiff requested the dates Plaintiff attended camp, but Defendant only provided the year. To the extent Defendant does not know any further information about the dates it should so state. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).) The motion is granted.

           

SROG Nos. 18, 20, 21, 22, 23, 40, 41: Plaintiff requested Defendant “IDENTIFY” individuals who with knowledge as to different aspects of the case. Defendant responded with names but without contact information or that the information was unknown.  To the extent defendant lacks further information and conducted a reasonable good faith search, it should so state. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).) The motion is granted.

 

SROG No. 24: Plaintiff requested information regarding the layout of the camp. Defendant responded with a description which identifies certain land features and appears to refer to a document produced which may assist plaintiff. The court’s review reveals that defendant has not identified the document with any specificity and the effort to incorporate said document is thus inadequate. Defendant shall identify with specificity the document it is referring to.  The motion is granted.

 

SROGS Nos. 31-37, 39: Plaintiff has submitted contention interrogatories wherein she asks for defendant to provide more information including whether they contend something, the facts supporting that contention, identifying individuals who support the contention, identifying documents which support the contention. Defendant provides objection only responses. This is patently insufficient. The interrogatories are clear and the objections (expert opinion, legal conclusion, speculation etc.) are not grounds to withhold discoverable information. Defendant does not assert that any of the information sought is privileged or work product. The motion is granted.

 

SROG Nos. 38: Plaintiff request information about defendant’s conduct in 1981. Defendant states that information is unknown. To the extent defendant conducted a reasonable good faith search, it must so state. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).) The motion is granted.

 

SROG No. 42: Plaintiff requests information about the duties of a lifeguard in 1981. Defendant responded that it lacks documents from that period and thus lacks information to provide any detail beyond that it includes safety of other in or around water. This is sufficiently compliant as it includes information and the reason why no further information can be provided. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).) The motion is denied.

 

SROG Nos. 50-53: Plaintiff has submitted contention interrogatories to which Defendant responded “unknown”. However, a contention interrogatory pertains to the Defendant’s present position on an issue. Defendant has provided an answer which has no bearing on the question, i.e., it lacks information to know its own position. This is insufficient. The motion is granted.

 

SROG Nos. 54-56: Plaintiff request information about defendant’s policies in 1981. Functionally, Defendant objected entirely on the grounds that the question was overbroad or called for speculation. The interrogatories are clear and the objections are not grounds to withhold discoverable information. Defendant does not assert that any of the information sought is privileged or work product. The motion is granted.

 

Apart from SROGS 31-37, and 39 (discussed therein), as Defendant responded to each of the requests at issue notwithstanding the objection. Accordingly, the court does not address the propriety of those objections.

 

V.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER 

The motion is GRANTED in part. The motion to compel further responses to special interrogatory number 42 is DENIED. In all other respects the motion is GRANTED.

[TENTATIVE]MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO
FORM INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE)

 

I.        INTRODUCTION

         

Plaintiff Melissa Gutholm
(plaintiff) moves for an order compelling further responses to form interrogatories,
set one, served upon defendant the Young Men’s Christian Association of West
San Gabriel Valley (defendant.) Plaintiff does not seek sanctions.  

 

III.     LEGAL
STANDARD

 

In
California, discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of
disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” (Williams v. Superior Court
(2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.) “Under the discovery statutes, information is
discoverable if it is unprivileged and is either relevant to the subject matter
of the action or reasonably calculated to reveal admissible evidence.” (John B.
v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186.) “Discovery may relate to the
claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the
action.” (Ibid.) “The court shall limit discovery if it determines that the
burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the
likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible
evidence. The court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for
protective order by a party or other affected person.” (Code Civ. Proc., §
2017.020, subd. (a).)

 

In
responding to an interrogatory, a party shall respond in one of the following
ways: providing an answer (1) containing the information sought to be
discovery, (2) exercising the party’s option to produce writings, or (2)
objecting to the particular interrogatory. On receipt of a response, the
demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the
interrogatories if the demanding party deems any of the following apply: (1) an
answer is evasive or incomplete, (2) an exercise of the option to produce
documents is unwarranted or inadequate, or (3) the objection is without merit
or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.300,
subd. (c).) “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge
sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but
shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by
inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information
is equally available to the propounding party.” (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220, subd. (c).)

 

IV.     ANALYSIS

 

A.   
Meet and Confer

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to
compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to
obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western
Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) “A meet and confer declaration
in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith
attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code
Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

The court finds the meet and confer
requirement has been met. Plaintiff’s counsel, Adam Kargman, declares that he
communicated with counsel on several occasions and as a result of those
communications defendant supplemented their responses once. Counsel does not
declare the parties communicated any further after the supplemental responses. Nevertheless,
the court finds the initial meetings sufficient to support the parties have met
and conferred.

 

B.    Compliance
of Responses

The special interrogatories at
issue are numbers 12.1, 15.1, 16.1, 16.2 and 17.1

 

FROG No. 12.1: Plaintiff asks for identifying
information for witness. Defendant provided witness names with no further
information. To the extent defendant has no ability to provide contacting
information in the manner requested by plaintiff it should so state.
(Code Civ. Proc., §2030.220,
subd. (c).) The motion is granted.

 

FROG No. 15.1: Plaintiff asks for defendant
to state all facts, persons, and identifying documents supporting each denial
of a material allegations and each special and affirmative defense. Defendant
provides objection only responses. This is patently insufficient. Defendant
asserts that the information sought is privileged or work product. However, defendant
does not clarify whether every single fact, person and identifying information
falls within the privilege. To suggest that all this information would be
privileged is a dubious argument claim at best. To the extent there is some
information which is unprivileged, defendant is obligated to answer. (Code Civ.
Proc., § 2030.240) The other asserted objections are not grounds to deny
discovery, e.g., discovery is incomplete. The motion is granted.

 

FROG No. 16.1: Plaintiff has
submitted contention interrogatories wherein she asks for defendant to provide
more information about any other person defendant claims is responsible.
Defendant provided another person, the perpetrator, but does not state all
facts upon which it bases its contention. Specifically, defendant refers to
information “told to it in 2022.” It is unclear if this is reference to a
document, or what information defendant was told. The response is insufficient
because the foundational facts which defendant is relying on are not included.
The motion is granted.  

 

FROG No. 16.2: Plaintiff has
submitted contention interrogatories wherein she ask for defendant to provide
more information about whether it contends plaintiff was not injured and the
supporting facts, persons and documents. Defendant responded that it lacked
information about plaintiff’s injuries.  Defendant
has not answered the first question regarding whether it contends plaintiff was
not injured. The motion is granted.

 

FROG No. 17.1: Plaintiff requests
further explanation on any request for admission including the facts, persons,
and documents supporting that explanation. With respect to part (b) which
requests all facts upon which you based your response, defendant responded, “Please
see applicable objection and/or explanation in Request for Admissions
responses, which are self-explanatory and legally sufficient.” However, the
only proper response to a request for admission is to admit or deny the
admission and/or object to the request. (Code Civ. Proc., §2033.210.) There is
no basis to explain the request for admission, thus the existence of Form
Interrogatory No. 17.1. To the extent defendant is relying on certain facts, it
must respond with those facts to properly answer the interrogatory. (Code Civ.
Proc., §2030.210, subd (a).) The motion is granted.

 

V.      CONCLUSION
AND ORDER

 

The motion is GRANTED.  Defendant to provide code complaint responses
without objections within 30 days of today.

Plaintiff to give notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 23AHCV01999    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER FINDING PLAINTIFF JOHN CHICHESTER’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT CLAUDIA MADDOX MOOT AND AWARDING SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT REQUESTED

                          I.          INTRODUCTION

This personal injury case arises from a vehicle colliding with a cyclist. Plaintiff John Chichester (“Plaintiff”) alleges that while riding his bicycle on January 24, 2023, at Grand Avenue and Madeline Drive in Pasadena, CA, Defendant Claudia Maddox (“Defendant”)’s vehicle struck him. On or about August 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Complaint against Defendant alleging general negligence and motor vehicle negligence.

On March 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel the deposition of Defendant, or alternatively for a protective order, and for monetary sanctions. On July 5, 2024, Defendant opposed the motion, and on July 11, 2024, Plaintiff replied.

On July 18, 2024, the Court continued the instant motion and ordered counsel to meet and confer. The Court noted that the approval for the guardian ad litem had been granted and signed.

On July 31, 2024, a stipulation and order regarding the deposition of Defendant was filed and signed by the Court, stating that Defendant does not have to appear for deposition and will not offer testimony in this case including at trial. The stipulation and order also states that the instant motion shall remain on calendar only for the Court to determine sanctions. Thus, only sanctions remain at issue.

On August 28, 2024, Defendant filed another opposition to the instant motion. As Defendant already filed an opposition to the motion, the Court declines to consider the second unauthorized opposition.

                        II.          LEGAL STANDARD

            CCP §2025.450 provides the following:

(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following: 

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. 

. . . 

(g)(1) If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(2) On motion of any other party who, in person or by attorney, attended at the time and place specified in the deposition notice in the expectation that the deponent's testimony would be taken, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of that party and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. 

            Further, CCP § 2023.030 authorizes the court to impose a monetary sanction, issue sanction, evidence sanction, or terminating sanction against any party who engages in conduct that is misuse of discovery. (Id., § 2023.030(a)-(d).) Misuses of the discovery process include failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery, and failing to confer in person, by telephone, or by letter with an opposing party or attorney in a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning discovery, if the section governing a particular discovery motion requires the filing of a declaration stating facts showing that an attempt at informal resolution has been made. (CCP § 2023.010(d), (i).)

                     III.          ANALYSIS

The Court only addresses the issue of sanctions as the request to compel the deposition is moot based on the July 31, 2024 stipulation and order. Plaintiff submits the Declaration of Marc Appell in support of his motion. Appell attests that he served a Notice of Deposition on November 7, 2023, and on December 14, 2023, defense counsel Ms. Flowers advised that she was not available for Defendant’s deposition on December 19, 2023. She requested a new date after the first week of January. Appell agreed to reschedule the deposition on January 10 provided that verified responses to written discovery were received without objection. (Appell Decl., ¶¶ 2, 5.) On December 18, 2023, Ms. Flowers advised Appell that Defendant was diagnosed with mild cognitive impairment. She then requested that Defendant not be deposed because this case is just a “value dispute.”  She also requested an extension to respond to discovery until January 12. Appell agreed to the extension but said that objections were most likely waived. (Appell Decl., ¶ 6.)

On January 10, 2024, Ms. Flowers advised Appell that Defendant’s condition had deteriorated, and she most likely would not be able to sign verifications. She was determining if Defendant’s daughter could obtain power of attorney to sign on her behalf. (Appell Decl., ¶ 7.) After settlement negotiations and discovery disputes regarding other outstanding written discovery, Plaintiff served a new Notice of Deposition of Defendant on February 29, 2024. (Appell Decl., ¶¶ 10-19.) Defendant served an objection to the Deposition on March 5, 2024. The objection was based on unavailability and that Defendant is “incapacitated and too ill to testify.” (Appell Decl., ¶ 20.) Appell immediately emailed Ms. Flowers and stated that the objection was not proper. Plaintiff’s argument is that Defendant cannot dispute liability based on statements attributable to Plaintiff or Defendant yet lack capacity to be deposed. The email requested an immediate meet and confer or Plaintiff would file a motion to compel. (Appell Decl., ¶ 21.) Appell received an auto reply that Ms. Flowers was on leave, so he had the email forwarded to Ms. Flowers’ supervisor. Appell subsequently received an email from Scott Stratman saying that he would review the file and follow up with him. Mr. Stratman then requested a phone conference on March 8, 2024. (Appell Decl., ¶ 22.) Appell spoke with Mr. Stratman on March 8, 2024. Appell agreed to waive Defendant’s deposition provided Defendant agrees not to testify at trial, and not to dispute liability, injuries, or damages based on anything Defendant said, or Plaintiff allegedly said to Defendant at the scene. Mr. Stratman said he understood and would get back to him. (Appell Decl., ¶ 23.)

On March 15, 2024, Appell sent Mr. Stratman an email following up and asked him to please get back to him ASAP or he would have to file a motion, to which no response was received. (Appell Decl., ¶ 24.) On March 19, 2024, Appell again sent Mr. Stratman an email following up. He requested a response by March 20, 2024, or Plaintiff would file the appropriate motion. (Appell Decl., ¶ 24.) No response was received as of the filing date of the motion on March 25, 2024. (Appell Decl., ¶ 25.)

In opposition, Defendant provides no explanation for the lack of responses after counsel attempted to confer regarding the second deposition and Defendant’s incapacity. Plaintiff’s counsel also warned that a motion to compel would be brought, and yet no response was provided.

Based on the lack of responsiveness regarding the deposition of Defendant and her incapacity such that a motion to compel should not be filed, the Court finds that the conduct of Defendant is a misuse of the discovery process. Accordingly, sanctions are warranted as the conduct necessitated motion practice. Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $3,318.91 based on 5 hours to prepare the motion, review the opposition and reply, and appear at the hearing, at the hourly rate of $650, plus a $68.91 filing fee. (Appell Decl., ¶¶ 28-29.) The Court finds this amount reasonable and awards the full amount requested.

                     IV.          CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court finds the motion MOOT and imposes sanctions in the total amount of $3,318.91 against Defendant and counsel of record.

Moving Party is ordered to provide notice of this ruling.
