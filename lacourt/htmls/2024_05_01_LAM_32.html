DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV17687    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: 32

 

EVELEN MARTINEZ, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

CHILDREN’S HOSPITAL OF LOS ANGELES, et al.,

                       

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV17687

  Hearing Date:  May 1, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant children’s hospital of los angeles’ motion to exclude witnesses

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 27, 2022, Plaintiffs filed this medical malpractice action on behalf of their deceased son, who died after several MRI scans. Plaintiffs allege that Decedent was improperly exposed to the chemical gadolinium throughout the course of the MRIs, resulting in severe burns, health complications, and eventually death. Plaintiffs allege that Decedent had a history of hypersensitivity to gadolinium and that Defendants were put on notice of Decedent’s condition and should have been aware of the risks of gadolinium exposure in children. The complaint alleges causes of action for (1) medical malpractice, (2) medical malpractice-lack of informed consent, (3) medical battery, and (4) a survival action.

            On April 2, 2024, Defendant Children’s Hospital of Los Angeles filed the instant motion to exclude Plaintiffs’ expert witnesses from testifying at trial or alternatively, for an order compelling the experts to appear for deposition.  Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition.

LEGAL STANDARD

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document … described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document … described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(a).)

“Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery” constitutes a “misuse[] of the discovery process.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010(d).) The court is authorized to impose monetary, issue, or evidence sanctions against a party engaging in misuse of the discovery process. (Id., § 2023.030.)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiffs’ March 4, 2024 expert designation identifies Richard Semelka, MD and Jenaya Calderilla, DO. (Kjar Decl., Ex. A.) Accordingly, on March 5, 2024, Defendant served notices for the depositions of Drs. Semelka and Calderilla to take place on March 28, 2024. (Id., ¶ 5, Ex. B.) Plaintiffs did not object to the deposition notices yet failed to produce the experts for deposition on March 28, 2024. (Id., ¶¶ 6-10.)

            Plaintiffs are ordered to produce their experts for deposition within 20 days of this order. If Plaintiffs fail to abide by this order, the experts will be precluded from testifying at trial. Sanctions are warranted as Plaintiffs have provided no substantial justification for the failure to produce their own designated experts for deposition. Given the simplicity of the motion and lack of opposition, the Court awards $735, representing 3 hours at $225 per hour (see Kjar Decl. ¶ 11), plus a $60 filing fee.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Children’s Hospital of Los Angeles’ motion is GRANTED in part. Evidentiary sanctions are denied at this stage, however Plaintiffs are ordered to produce Drs. Semelka and Calderilla for deposition within 20 days of this order. The Court sanctions Plaintiffs in the total amount of $735, to be paid within 30 days of this order.

plaintiff and cross-complainants’ motion for summary adjudication

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On December 8, 2022, Plaintiff Nicholas Sabbagh filed this action against Defendant Culver 12 LLC, alleging causes of action for (1) unlawful retention of security deposit, (2) breach of lease, (3) negligence, and (4) conversion. Plaintiff alleges that he rented a unit from Defendant and paid a $15,000 security deposit which was supposed to be returned upon termination of the tenancy. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant improperly applied expenses against the deposit, resulting in nothing being returned to Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges that this amounts to theft of the security deposit.      

            On January 5, 2023, Defendant Culver filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Sachin Tejas Bettadapur, Alexander Amir Rassouli, and Ashan Naik, alleging breach of lease. Plaintiff and the other Cross-Defendants resided in the same unit. Culver alleges that the tenants caused extensive damages to the unit which required more than $18,000 to repair. Culver therefore applied the full security deposit of $15,000 towards the repair. The tenants also allegedly kept a dog in violation of the no-pet provision in the lease. Culver sues for the damage caused to the premises by the tenants and their dog.  

            On March 15, 2023, Bettadapur, Rassouli, and Naik, the tenants who shared the unit with Plaintiff, filed their own cross-complaint against Culver, which is substantively identical to Plaintiff’s complaint.

            On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff, Bettadapur, Rassouli, and Naik (collectively, Moving Parties or tenants) filed a motion for summary adjudication of the first, second, and fourth causes of action in their respective complaints. The Court denied this motion as untimely on January 3, 2024.

            Moving Parties refiled their motion on February 5, 2024. Culver filed its opposition on April 15, 2024. Moving Parties filed their reply on April 23, 2024.  

LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the plaintiff moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to establish each element of the cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Once the plaintiff has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

Culver’s Objections to Moving Parties’ Evidence

Moving Parties’ Objections to Culver’s Evidence

DISCUSSION

I. Summary Adjudication of Multiple Pleadings

            Culver argues that Moving Parties improperly move for summary adjudication of claims in both Plaintiff’s complaint and the other tenants’ cross-complaint. Culver also argues that Moving Parties reference themselves as “Plaintiffs” even though the only plaintiff is Plaintiff Sabbagh, and the remaining tenants are cross-complainants. Lastly, Culver argues that Plaintiff Sabbagh cannot testify as to the harm suffered by the other co-tenants.

            Culver cites no authority suggesting that it is improper to move for summary adjudication of claims in multiple pleadings, especially where, as here, the claims are substantively identical. The requirement for summary adjudication is that it “completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(f).) There is no additional requirement that the claims come from a single pleading. The use of “Plaintiffs” has not resulted in any confusion or misidentification. It is clear who the Moving Parties are: the four tenants who resided in the subject premises. Plaintiff Sabbagh is competent to testify to the fact that the four tenants collectively paid and lost the $15,000 security deposit. Therefore, these are not reasons to deny the motion.

II. Separately Filed Evidence

            “If evidence in support of or in opposition to a motion exceeds 25 pages, the evidence must be separately bound and must include a table of contents.” (Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1350(g).) Culver argues that the motion must be denied because Moving Parties attached their evidence to their points and authorities rather than filing it separately. Culver does not claim that this made it more difficult to review Moving Parties’ evidence, nor does Culver otherwise articulate any prejudice. Therefore, the Court proceeds on the merits.    

III. Unlawful Retention of Security Deposit

A landlord may impose a deposit at the beginning of a tenancy to reimburse various costs, including “[t]he repair of damages to the premises, exclusive of ordinary wear and tear, caused by the tenant or by a guest or licensee of the tenant.” (Civ. Code, § 1950.5(b).) “The landlord may claim of the security only those amounts as are reasonably necessary for the purposes specified in subdivision (b).” (Id., § 1950.5(e).) “The elements of an action for wrongful retention of a security deposit under Civil Code section 1950.5 are: (1) the plaintiff paid a security deposit; (2) the security deposit was for a residential property; (3) the plaintiff used the property as a dwelling; and (4) the amounts deducted by the defendant were not reasonably necessary.” (Peviani v. Arbors at California Oaks Property Owner, LLC (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 874, 899.) A landlord “must return the entire deposit to the tenant” if he fails to abide by the summary deduct-and-retain procedure of Section 1950.5. (Granberry v. Islay Investments (1995) 9 Cal.4th 738, 745.)   

Here, it is undisputed that Moving Parties paid a security deposit for a residential property that they used as a dwelling. (Plntf.’s Undisputed Facts (UF) 2-4.) The primary issue is whether Culver improperly retained or deducted the deposit. Moving Parties contend that Culver has indisputably failed to comply with Section 1950.5, subdivisions (f) and (g) and therefore should have returned the entire deposit.

a. Right to Inspection (Subdivision (f))

“Within a reasonable time after notification of either party’s intention to terminate the tenancy, or before the end of the lease term, the landlord shall notify the tenant in writing of the tenant’s option to request an initial inspection and of the tenant’s right to be present at the inspection.” (Civ. Code, § 1950.5(f)(1).) 

Here, Moving Parties provided notice in June 2022 that they would not be renewing the lease. (UF 6.) Moving Parties argue that Culver did not provide any notice afterwards of a right to inspection prior to Moving Parties vacating the unit. (UF 8.) As a result, Moving Parties argue they did not have the opportunity to cure defects to avoid deductions from the security deposit. However, Section 25(C) of the lease provides that “[a]fter giving or receiving notice of termination of a tenancy . . . Tenant has the right to request that an inspection of the Premises take place prior to termination of the lease.” (See Plntf.’s Ex. 1.) A reasonable trier of fact may find that this constitutes the written notice required by subdivision (f), and that the tenants simply did not exercise the right to inspect. The statute provides that the notice must be provided either “[w]ithin a reasonable time after notification of either party’s intention to terminate the tenancy, or before the end of the lease term.” (Civ. Code, § 1950.5(f)(1).) The notice in the lease was provided before the end of the lease term. Therefore, there is a triable issue as to Culver’s compliance with subdivision (f).    

b. Itemized Statement and Return of Deposit (Subdivision (g))

“No later than 21 calendar days after the tenant has vacated the premises . . . the landlord shall furnish the tenant, by personal delivery or by first-class mail, postage prepaid, a copy of an itemized statement indicating the basis for, and the amount of, any security received and the disposition of the security, and shall return any remaining portion of the security to the tenant.” (Civ. Code, § 1950.5(g)(1).) “[T]he landlord and the tenant may also agree to have the landlord provide a copy of the itemized statement along with the copies required by paragraph (2) to an email account provided by the tenant.” (Ibid.)

Here, Culver sent the itemized statement by email rather than personal service or regular mail. (UF 13.) The parties had not previously agreed to exchange the itemized statement by email. (UF 15.) Culver did not return the security deposit within 21 days of the tenants vacating the premises. (UF 12.) Culver does not dispute these facts, nor does Culver dispute that this constitutes a violation of subdivision (g). Therefore, Moving Parties have satisfied their initial burden of establishing a violation of subdivision (g).

Culver argues that this is an immaterial technical violation because the tenants still received the itemized statement and do not contest its contents, and Culver did not act in bad faith. However, Culver cannot escape liability by unilaterally deciding that a statutory requirement is not important enough to be followed. The Legislature has expressly required the itemized statement and supporting documentation to be personally delivered or sent by mail unless the parties agree to email. Nothing indicates that this requirement is optional. It is part of the summary deduct-and-retain procedure that a landlord must follow in order to retain any portion of the security deposit. (See Granberry, supra, 9 Cal.4th at p. 745.) Furthermore, bad faith is not required to establish a violation of the statute. Civil Code section 1950.5(l) provides for additional penalties and damages if the landlord’s retention of a security deposit is found to be in bad faith. Subdivision (l) does not mean that a landlord’s retention is wrongful only if done in bad faith. Therefore, whether Culver acted in good or bad faith does not affect whether it violated the statute and does not serve to raise a triable issue.     

Culver also argues that the tenants made compliance impossible by refusing to provide their addresses or informing Culver how much each tenant is owed. However, the tenants clearly offered to provide “any info from us to confirm where to send [the deposit].” (Plntf.’s Ex. 5.) Culver’s PMQ acknowledged receiving this text. (Plntf.’s Ex. 8 (Kawa Depo.) 52:14-24.) There is no evidence that Culver asked for the address or that the tenants refused to provide it. Furthermore, the statute provides that “[i]f the tenant does not provide an address, mailings pursuant to this subdivision shall be sent to the unit that has been vacated.” (Civ. Code, § 1950.5(g)(6).) Lastly, the lease provides that “[a]ny security deposit returned by check shall be made out to all Tenants named on this Agreement.” (Plntf.’s Ex. A, § 4(C).) Therefore, Culver did not need to divide the amount between each tenant in order to return the deposit.

Ultimately, Culver has not presented “substantial responsive evidence” raising a triable issue. (See Sangster, supra, 68 Cal.App.4th at p. 166.) Therefore, Moving Parties have established as a matter of law that Culver violated at least one provision of Section 1950.5 and therefore wrongfully retained the security deposit. (See Granberry, supra, 9 Cal.4th at p. 745.)    

IV. Breach of Contract

To establish breach of contract, a plaintiff must show: (1) the contract existed, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)

Here, the lease is indisputably a valid contract, and Moving Parties indisputably performed their obligation to provide a security deposit at the start of the lease. Moving Parties argue that Culver breached the lease because the lease required Culver to provide an itemized statement and supporting documentation “as required by California Civil Code § 1950.5(g)” (Plntf.’s Ex. 1, § 4(B)), and Culver did not do so.  

Culver argues that it could not have breached the contract by failing to notify Moving Parties of their right to a pre-moveout inspection because the lease expressly notified the tenants of such inspection at Paragraph 25(C). However, this does not address the requirement under Section 1950.5(g) to properly provide an itemized statement and return the unused portion of the security deposit within 21 days. Culver does not dispute that its method of transmitting the itemized statement, and failure to remit the deposit within 21 days, violated subdivision (g). Culver argues that its violation of the technical requirements of the statute does not amount to a breach of contract. However, the contract required Culver to act in accordance with Section 1950.5(g). (Plntf.’s Ex. 1, § 4(B).) Therefore, failure to abide by the statute would constitute a breach of contract. Moving Parties naturally suffered damage in the loss of their security deposit. Accordingly, Moving Parties have established a breach of contract as a matter of law.    

V. Conversion

The elements of conversion are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the personal property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages. (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202, 208.) The court in Aljabban v. Fontana Indoor Swap Meet, Inc. (2020) 54 Cal.App.5th 482, 508-09 found that the withholding of a security deposit supported the plaintiff’s conversion claim. The $15,000 deposit is an identifiable sum of money that is capable of being converted. (See PCO, Inc. v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro, LLP (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 384, 395.)

Culver argues that there are triable issues as to whether it “wrongfully” attempted to retain the property. Culver points out that it never stated the security deposit would be retained to pay for damages caused by the tenants. (Culver’s Additional Facts (AF) No. D.) Instead, Culver contends that it tried to return the deposit, but the tenants did not provide a forwarding address or bank information. (Ibid.) However, as discussed above, the tenants offered to provide the necessary information, and both the statute and lease provide for ways to remit the deposit without that information. Culver’s retention of the deposit was a wrongful act because it was done in violation of the statute. The fact that Culver never intended to keep the deposit to pay for repairs does not negate the fact that Culver retained the deposit past 21 days in violation of the statute. (See Welco, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 208 [conversion is a strict liability tort with no consideration of good faith or motive].)

Culver also argues that it sent a check for $15,000 to Moving Parties’ attorney, who then refused to cash it because Moving Parties desired to proceed with the litigation. As a result, Culver argues that it is the attorney who has “dominion and control” over the money, not Culver. This position is unsupported by law or fact. Culver’s attempt to pay $15,000 after the lawsuit was filed does not negate the conversion that already occurred. Furthermore, evidence of settlement offers or related communications is inadmissible to prove the invalidity of a claim. (Evid. Code, §§ 1152, 1154.) Therefore, there is no triable issue that Culver converted the security deposit.

VI. Affirmative Defenses

            A plaintiff moving for summary judgment does not have the initial burden of disproving any and all affirmative defenses. (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564-65.) If the plaintiff satisfies its initial burden of establishing each element of a claim, the defendant opposing summary judgment may raise a triable issue using an affirmative defense. (Ibid.; Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(1).) Culver relies on its affirmative defenses based on estoppel, waiver, unclean hands, and mitigation of damages.

           

a. Estoppel

            “To prove equitable estoppel, one must show (1) a representation or concealment of material facts; (2) made with knowledge, actual or virtual, of the facts; (3) to a party ignorant, actually and permissibly, of the truth; (4) with the intention, actual or virtual, that the ignorant party act on it; and (5) the ignorant party relied on it.” (Oldcastle, supra, 170 Cal.App.4th at p. 571.)

            Culver relies on its argument that Moving Parties withheld their forwarding address and banking information such that Culver could not remit the deposit. As discussed above, there is no evidence that Moving Parties intentionally concealed the information from Culver. There is also no evidence that Moving Parties withheld the information with the intent that Culver would rely on the omission and retain the deposit. Lastly, there was no concealment of “material facts” because Culver could have remitted the deposit even without the information. (See Civ. Code, § 1950.5(g)(6); Plntf.’s Ex. A, § 4(C).) Therefore, Culver has failed to raise a triable issue on its estoppel defense.

            b. Waiver

            “Waiver requires the intentional relinquishment of a known right upon knowledge of the facts.” (Los Angeles Unified School Dist. v. Torres Construction Corp. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 480, 504.) “[W]aiver may also stem from conduct ‘which, according to its natural import, is so inconsistent with an intent to enforce the right as to induce a reasonable belief that such right has been relinquished.’” (Savaglio v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 598.)      

            Culver reiterates its argument that Moving Parties “refused” to provide the requisite information. The evidence does not show Moving Parties “refusing” to provide anything, and in fact shows the opposite. (See Plntf.’s Ex. 5.) The fact that Moving Parties asked for their deposit back, offered to provide the information needed for that to occur, and hired an attorney to sue for recovery of the deposit, is consistent with their right to the deposit. There is no evidence that Moving Parties intentionally relinquished their right to the deposit or acted inconsistently with that right. Therefore, Culver has failed to raise a triable issue on its waiver defense.

            c. Unclean Hands

            The doctrine of unclean hands is a defense that bars a plaintiff from seeking relief if he has committed wrongdoing himself because it would be inequitable to allow a wrongdoer to benefit from his own wrongdoing. (Kendall-Jackson Winery, Ltd. V. Sup. Ct. (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 970, 978.) For unclean hands to apply, the plaintiff’s wrongdoing must be directly related to the claims at issue. (Id. at p. 979.)

            For the same reasons discussed above, there is no evidence that Moving Parties engaged in any wrongdoing related to their claims. There is no evidence indicating intentional concealment or deception preventing Culver from returning the deposit. Rejecting an offer of payment after litigation has begun is not evidence of wrongdoing, and the offer is inadmissible to prove the invalidity of Moving Parties’ claims. (Evid. Code, § 1152, 1154.)

            d. Mitigation of Damages

            “The doctrine of mitigation of damages holds that ‘[a] plaintiff who suffers damage as a result of either a breach of contract or a tort has a duty to take reasonable steps to mitigate those damages and will not be able to recover for any losses which could have been thus avoided.’” (Valle De Oro Bank v. Gamboa (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 1686, 1691.)

            Culver argues that Moving Parties failed to mitigate their damages because they rejected multiple offers from Culver to pay back the $15,000 deposit. For the reasons established above, Moving Parties’ rejection of Culver’s offer after litigation began cannot be used against them. Culver cites no authority for the proposition that the doctrine of mitigation requires a plaintiff to accept offers of compromise during litigation. Furthermore, mitigation refers to the plaintiff’s obligation to “avoid continuing or enhanced damages through reasonable efforts.” (Valle De Oro Bank, supra, 26 Cal.App.4th at p. 1691.) Here, Moving Parties lost their $15,000 deposit when Culver failed to return it. There were no continuing or enhanced damages for Moving Parties to avoid. Therefore, Culver has failed to raise a triable issue on its mitigation defense.  

CONCLUSION

            The motion for summary adjudication filed by Plaintiff, Bettadapur, Rassouli, and Naik is GRANTED.

6.     Communications with dealer, factory representative and/or call center concerning the subject vehicle.

7.     Warranty claims submitted to and/or approved by Defendant concerning the subject vehicle.

8.     All other requests for further production are DENIED.

9.     Defendant shall provide supplemental responses in compliance with this order within 45 days of this order.     

10.  Production shall be subject to a protective order.

11.  The Court does not award sanctions as it finds both sides acted with substantial justification.


Case Number: 24STCV00523    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: 32

 

KEILYN MEDINA ALCERRO,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

HARDAGE HOSPITALITY,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  24STCV00523

  Hearing Date:  May 1, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant’s motion for protective order

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 8, 2024, Plaintiff Keilyn Medina Alcerro filed this employment discrimination action against Defendant Hardage Hospitality and Does 1 through 50. Plaintiff alleges that she was abruptly fired after suffering a work-related injury and requesting accommodation. Plaintiff alleges that she was falsely accused of sexual harassment as a pretext to terminate her.

            On March 21, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for a protective order absolving it of the obligation to respond to certain discovery requests to the extent they seek the personal information of any employee who observed, was subjected to, or reported sexual battery against Plaintiff. (Notice of Mtn. 2:4-9.) Alternatively, Defendant requests a protective order allowing it to designate the information as “Confidential” or “Attorney’s Eyes Only.” (Id. at 2:9-12.) Plaintiff filed its opposition on April 18, 2024. Defendant filed its reply on April 24, 2024.  

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

The recipient of a discovery request may move for a protective order. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.090, 2031.060, 2033.080.) “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Ibid.) “The issuance and formulation of protective orders are to a large extent discretionary.” (Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 316.) “[T]he burden is on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for whatever order is sought.” (Id. at p. 318.)

DISCUSSION

“The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.” (Williams v. Sup. Ct. (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) If the court finds a privacy interest, the court must balance the privacy concerns against the need for the information. (Id. at p. 552.) Discovery of private information is governed by the more stringent standard of direct relevance in order to prevent a fishing expedition of “tangentially pertinent sensitive information.” (Boler v. Sup. Ct. (1987) 201 Cal.App.3d 467, 472.) “The burden is on the party seeking the constitutionally protected information to establish direct relevance.” (Davis v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1017.)

            Confidential personnel files at a person’s place of employment are within a zone of privacy. (See Board of Trustees v. Superior Court of Santa Clara County (1981) 119 Cal.App.3d 516, 528-30.) At the same time, “[t]he disclosure of the names and addresses of potential witnesses is a routine and essential part of pretrial discovery.” (Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1249-50.) Here, Plaintiff alleges that she was wrongly terminated, while Defendant maintains that it legitimately terminated Plaintiff due to sexual harassment. Having raised that defense, Defendant cannot prevent Plaintiff from testing its veracity and proving that Defendant’s proffered justification is pretextual. The information is directly relevant, and Plaintiff’s need for the information outweighs the privacy concerns. Therefore, Defendant cannot be wholly absolved from responding to the subject discovery.

            Due to the nature of the case and the information sought, a protective order limiting usage of the material may be warranted. However, a “Highly Confidential” or “Attorney’s Eyes Only” designation is unwarranted because Plaintiff is entitled to personally view the evidence in order to assist with the preparation of her own case. Plaintiff’s counsel should not be left to speculate as to the significance of the evidence without the benefit of Plaintiff’s own account or explanation. Defendant cannot accuse Plaintiff of sexual assault as a justification for terminating her and then prevent her from reviewing the purported evidence of same. Defendant’s summary assertion that “only Plaintiff’s lawyers need that information in order to effectively conduct discovery” is unavailing. (See Reply 5:3-4.) Defendant cannot unilaterally decide what information Plaintiff needs to see to effectively litigate the case.       

            Defendant’s confidence that it has produced sufficient information to prove the alleged harassment has no bearing on Plaintiff’s right to view the evidence. The merits of the case have yet to be resolved. Plaintiff is entitled to contest Defendant’s legal theories and question Defendant’s evidence. Defendant has provided no indication that Plaintiff is at risk of using the information to retaliate against the witnesses who reported the alleged incident. There is no evidence that Plaintiff has made any threats or taken any action that could be interpreted as retaliatory. The fact that Plaintiff filed this lawsuit is not evidence that she intends on retaliating against those responsible for her termination. Plaintiff is entitled to assert that her termination was unlawful and to select the legal theories upon which to sue. Defendant’s belief that the complaint is meritless does not preclude Plaintiff from building her case. This motion is not the proper mechanism to litigate the merits of the case, and no finding can be made at this stage about whether Defendant has adequately proven that Plaintiff harassed a coworker.

            In sum, Plaintiff is entitled to discover the information at issue and cannot be prevented from personally viewing the discovery. However, a protective order is warranted to limit usage of the information to this litigation.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s motion for protective order is GRANTED in part as set forth above. Production shall be subject to a protective order. Sanctions are denied as the parties acted with substantial justification.
