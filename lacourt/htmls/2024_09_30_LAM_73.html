DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCV01048    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 73

9/30/2024 

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

KLEIN v. LASSENS NATURAL FOODS & VITAMINS, LLC (24STCV01048)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Sepideh Hersel (Chat Law Group, LLP), Ashley Laiken (Laiken Law Group, APC)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party: David Clinton (Clinton & Clinton)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE THIS COURT’S MAY 15, 2024 RULING TO DISMISS DOE DEFENDANTS (filed 05/23/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion.  The Court amends its May 15, 2024 order dismissing all Doe Defendants to reinstate Doe Defendants 1-50.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff Patricia Klein filed this action against Defendant Lassens Natural Foods & Vitamins, LLC and Does 1-50.

 

The Complaint alleges causes of action for:  

1.      General Negligence

2.      Premises Liability

 

The Complaint alleges that on or about January 13, 2022, Plaintiff tripped and fell as a result of a dangerous condition on Defendant’s property, that was created by Defendants, causing injuries and damages in an amount to be proven at the time of trial.

 

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Set Aside, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves for an order granting relief from this Court’s order dated May 15, 2024 order dismissing all Doe defendants. This request is made based upon Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b), as the agreement to dismiss the Doe defendants was due to inadvertence, mistake or excusable neglect of counsel for Plaintiff.

 

Defendant did not file an opposition. Plaintiff did not file a reply.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves for an order granting relief from this Court’s order dated May 15, 2024 order dismissing all Doe defendants.

 

A party may apply for an order granting relief under § 473(b) “upon any such term as may be just…from a judgment, dismissal, order or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect.”

 

On May 15, 2024, the Court held a Case Management Conference in this matter. Plaintiff’s counsel, Sepideh Hersel, submitted a declaration stating that she appeared for the hearing via telephonic appearance. (Hersel Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel states she was asked by the Court whether all Defendants had been served, and whether the Court could dismiss all Doe defendants. (Ibid.) Plaintiff’s counsel agreed to dismiss all Doe defendants. (Ibid.) However, she states that upon reflection, she had made a mistake to dismiss Doe defendants prematurely as discovery is still underway and in the event that discovery reveals a liable party, Plaintiff could be prejudiced if she is unable to add parties to the case as a Doe defendant. (Hersel Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds that amendment of the May 15, 2024 order is warranted. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 576, “[a]ny judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.” Additionally, “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473, subd. (a)(1).) In Santa Catalina, the court dismissed doe defendants pursuant to a pretrial conference order. (Santa Catalina Island Co. v. Superior Court, (1967) 248 Cal. App. 2d 729, 730-731.) Thereafter, Plaintiffs and the named defendants obtained an order amending the pretrial conference order to dismiss all doe defendants except Does 1 and 2. (Id.) Petitioner, served as a doe defendant, moved to quash service for lack of jurisdiction. (Id.) The motion was denied. (Id.) The Court of Appeal held that Code of Civil Procedure section 576 authorized the trial court to amend the original pretrial conference order dismissing all doe defendants with the order reinstating does 1 and 2. (Id. at 731.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel filed this motion on May 23, 2024, only eight days after the May 15, 2024 hearing. Thus, Plaintiff pursued this motion with diligence. In addition, Plaintiff submitted a declaration by her counsel indicating the dismissal was a mistake. The Court finds that permitting amendment of the May 15, 2024 order to allow the reinstatement of Doe Defendants 1-50 would be in the furtherance of justice.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion.  The Court amends its May 15, 2024 order dismissing all Doe Defendants to reinstate Doe Defendants 1-50.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion.  The Court amends its May 15, 2024 order dismissing all Doe Defendants to reinstate Doe Defendants 1-50.


Case Number: 24STCV06351    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 73

09/30/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

DOE v. LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT, et al. (24STCV06351)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: John Manly (Manly Stewart Finaldi)

Counsel for Defendant LAUSD/moving party:  Avi Burkwitz (Peterson Bradford Burkwitz Gregorio Burkwitz & Su, LLP)

 

Demurrer TO FIRST AMENDED complaint (filed 7/18/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant LAUSD’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first cause of action is OVERRULED. 

 

On March 14, 2024, Plaintiff Jane FA Doe, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Jane MA Doe (“Plaintiff”), filed this against Defendants Los Angeles Unified School District, a local public entity (“LAUSD”); Unidentified Perpetrator, an individual; and Does 1-100.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action in her First Amended Complaint:

1.     Negligence

2.     Negligent Supervision

3.     Negligent Hiring and Retention

4.     Negligent Failure to Warn, Train or Educate

5.     Sexual Harassment in Education Setting

6.     Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

7.     Gender Violence

8.     Sexual Battery

9.     Sexual Assault

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff Jane FA Doe is a female minor, who was born on July 23, 2007. Plaintiff is a non-verbal Special Education student, diagnosed with severe Autism, who requires an aide to assist her during school hours at Van Nuys Middle School. Plaintiff was under LAUSD's supervisory personnel's care, control, and supervision. Unidentified Perpetrator was employed, retained, and/or allowed to be on-campus to supervise, mentor, aide, and/or teach children at Van Nuys Middle School. Plaintiff is informed and believes that Unidentified Perpetrator sexually abused Plaintiff by touching Plaintiff's genitals over her clothes in a classroom during school. Plaintiff is informed and believes that Plaintiff's assigned one-on-one aide witnessed Unidentified Perpetrator touching Plaintiff inappropriately. Plaintiff is informed and believes that Unidentified Perpetrator was arrested pursuant to this incident but not prosecuted. Plaintiff is also informed and believes that Unidentified Perpetrator was transferred to another school within LAUSD but was not terminated by Defendant LAUSD.

 

On July 18, 2024, Defendant LAUSD filed a demurrer to the FAC, arguing:

·       The First Cause of Action for Breach of Mandatory Duties/Violation of Statute/Negligence Per Se fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action

·       Here, the Plaintiffs rely on Penal Code §11166 to support a claim for negligence per se and breach of mandatory duties. Plaintiff's legal conclusion fails to allege facts that support the threshold question of whether the penal code contains a mandatory duty and allege any facts that support an allegation of whether that duty was breached other than relying on circular and conclusory language. Therefore, Plaintiff’s reliance on negligence per se is misguided and grounds for a demurrer.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       LAUSD cannot demur to only a portion of Plaintiff’s negligence action. "A demurrer must dispose of an entire cause of action to be sustained." (Fremont Indem. Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)

o   Here, Defendant explicitly requests the Court to sustain its demurrer to the negligence per se portions of Plaintiff’s Negligence claim.

o   This is improper and the demurrer must be overruled on this ground.

o   LAUSD does not seek to strike these paragraphs by way of a Motion to Strike, which would have been the proper vehicle by which to challenge these claims.

·       Negligence Per Se Is Properly Pleaded Against DOE 1 for Violating Mandated Reporting, As It Is Vicariously Liable For Its Agents' Failures

o   Here, the negligence per se claims in the First Cause of Action arise from LAUSD’s employees and/or agents who failed to report suspected or known child abuse. FAC, ¶¶ 42-50.

·       Plaintiff Need Not Identify Who Breached LAUSD’s Mandatory Duties

 

LAUSD filed a reply maintaining that the Court should sustain its demurrer to the first cause of action. Any alleged violation of Penal Code §11166 would presumably be addressed as a cause of action for breach of a mandatory duty and not a claim for negligence/negligence per se.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for LAUSD submitted a declaration stating that the parties discussed the grounds of the demurrer via telephone on July 3, 2024. (D’Egidio Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation and the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

B.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

C.     First Cause of Action for Negligence

 

According to LAUSD’s notice of demurrer, LAUSD asserts that Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Breach of Mandatory Duties/Violation of Statute/Negligence Per Se fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.”  (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

“The negligence per se doctrine, as codified in Evidence Code section 669, creates a presumption of negligence if four elements are established: (1) the defendant violated a statute, ordinance, or regulation of a public entity; (2) the violation proximately caused death or injury to person or property; (3) the death or injury resulted from an occurrence of the nature of which the statute, ordinance, or regulation was designed to prevent; and (4) the person suffering the death or the injury to his person or property was one of the class of persons for whose protection the statute, ordinance, or regulation was adopted.”  (Spates v. Dameron Hospital Association (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 218, cleaned up.)  “The doctrine of negligence per se is not a separate cause of action[] but creates an evidentiary presumption that affects the standard of care in a cause of action for negligence.”  (Johnson v. Honeywell International Inc. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 549, 555, cleaned up.)

 

As an initial matter, the Court finds that LAUSD’s demurrer fails to dispose of the entire first cause of action. Demurrers do not lie as to only parts of causes of action, where some valid claim is alleged.  (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119 (“A demurrer must dispose of an entire cause of action to be sustained.”).) Negligence per se is not a separate cause of action (see Johnson v. Honeywell International, Inc. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 549, 555; Millard v. Biosources, Inc. (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1353 n.2.), but it is a separate theory of negligence upon which recovery may be based. (See e.g., Randi W. v. Muroc Joint Unified School Dist. (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1066, 1086-87.) Here, LAUSD’s notice states that Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Breach of Mandatory Duties/Violation of Statute/Negligence Per Se fails. However, LAUSD cannot demur to only the negligence per se portion of the first cause of action for negligence. As the Court stated in PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1682:

 

"[W]e do not reach the merits of the petition because Ibershof's demurrer was not procedurally proper. A demurrer does not lie to a portion of a cause of action. ([citation omitted)) Thus, Ibershof could not properly demur to the Hoffer claim portion of the causes of action; and the trial court could not properly sustain the demurrer as to only that portion." (emphasis added).

 

Therefore, the Court does not address the merits of LAUSD’s demurrer because the Court cannot sustain a demurrer to only a portion of a cause of action.

 

As such, the demurrer to the first cause of action in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant LAUSD’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first cause of action is OVERRULED.
