DEPARTMENT 51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling, please email the clerk at SMCdept51@lacourt.org (and “cc” all other parties in the same email) and notify all other parties in advance that you will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you do not have access to the internet, you may call the clerk at (213) 633-0351.

 

If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear and argue the motion, and the Court may decide not to adopt the tentative ruling. Please note that the tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question which are not authorized by statute or Rule of Court.

 

2. For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its discretion.

3. DO NOT USE THE ABOVE EMAIL FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE RULING.  The Court will not read or respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.

 




Case Number: 19STCV42136    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: 51

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

 

HEARING DATE:   October 15, 2024                                           

 

CASE NAME:           RM Glass, Inc. v. Bede USA, Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                19STCV42136

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES

 

MOVING PARTY:  Defendants Bede USA, Inc., Business Alliance Insurance Company, and Ronald Tesmer[1]

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): None as of October 9, 2024

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.      An Award of $865,406.00 in attorneys’ fees.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Motion for Award of Attorneys’ Fees is GRANTED;

2.      The court awards $731,502.00 in attorneys’ fees to the Bede Defendants.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On November 22, 2019, Plaintiff RM Glass, Inc. (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Defendants Bede USA, Inc. dba Bede Windows & Doors, LA Plaza Village, LLC, Benchmark Contractors, Inc., Hudson Insurance Company, Suretec Indemnity Company, Business Alliance Insurance Company, and Ronald John Tesmer with eleven causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Foreclosure on Mechanic’s Lien – Leasehold Interest; (3) Enforcement of Stop Payment Notice; (4) Enforcement of Payment Bond; (5) Quantum Meruit; (6) Common Count for Goods and Services Rendered; (7) Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (8) Violation of Prompt Payment Statutes; (9) Foreclosure of Mechanic’s Lien; (10) Contractor’s License Bond; and (11) Declaratory Relief.

 

Plaintiff alleges that Bede submitted a Benchmark Purchase Order for windows and sliding glass doors. Bede then invited Plaintiff to submit a bid; Bede and Plaintiff then entered a subcontract. Plaintiff alleges that it discovered Bede had misrepresented the prevailing wage rate. The final invoice was significantly more than the original contract price due to additional hours for assembling, fabricating, and glazing the windows. Defendant delayed payment.

 

On May 27, 2020, Plaintiff filed a request for dismissal as to Defendant LA Plaza Village, LLC only.

 

On June 16, 2020, Plaintiff filed a request for dismissal as to Defendant Benchmark Contractors, Inc. only.

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

“It is well established that the determination of what constitutes reasonable attorney fees is committed to the discretion of the trial court, whose decision cannot be reversed in the absence of an abuse of discretion.¿ [Citations.]¿ The value of legal services performed in a case is a matter in which the trial court has its own expertise. . . . The trial court makes its determination after consideration of a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.¿ [Citations.]”¿ (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623624.) 

 

Under Public Contract Code §7107, in any action for the collection of funds wrongfully withheld, the prevailing party shall be entitled to attorney’s fees and costs. (Public Contract Code §7107(f).) Similarly, Cal. Civ. Code Proc. Article two, which includes §8812, provides “in an action for collection of the amount wrongfully withheld, the prevailing party is entitled to costs and reasonable attorney's fees.” (Cal. Civ. Code §8818(b).) 

 

Under Brawly v. J.C. Interiors Inc., courts have the discretion to determine that there is no prevailing party, even if a statute does not expressly say so. (James L. Harris Painting & Decorating, Inc. v. West Bay Builders, Inc. (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1214, 1220 [citing Brawley v. J.C. Interiors, Inc. (2008), 161 Cal. App. 4th 1126].) Case law holds that this applies to prompt payment statutes as well. (Id.) 

 

The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. A computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award. The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided. (Serrano v. Priest¿(1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.)¿ Such an approach anchors the trial court’s analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney’s services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary. (Id. at 48, n.23.)

¿ 

The factors considered in determining the modification of the lodestar include the nature and difficulty of the litigation, the amount of money involved, the skill required and employed to handle the case, the attention given, the success or failure,¿and other circumstances in the case. (EnPalm, LLC v.¿Teitler¿Family Trust¿(2008) 162 Cal. App. 4th 770, 774 (emphasis in original).) A negative modifier was appropriate when duplicative work had been performed. (Thayer v. Wells Fargo Bank,¿N.A.¿(2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 819.)

Prevailing Party

 

Statutorily, “prevailing party” includes: “the party with a net monetary recovery, a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant.” (Cal. Civ. Code §1032(a)(4).)

 

Here, Defendants are clearly the prevailing parties. Notably, the Bede Defendants brought a motion for summary judgment (MSJ) which, after the court imposed evidentiary sanctions against Plaintiff, the court granted. Indeed, the Bede Defendants moved the court to reconsider its prior ruling denying the MSJ because of this. Because the court dismissed the FAC in their favor because of these motions, the Bede Defendants are prevailing parties under Civ. Code §1032(a)(4).

 

However, Defendants Benchmark and Hudson were not parties to the MSJ. Indeed, Plaintiff voluntarily dismissed Benchmark on June 16, 2020 – well before the Bede Defendants filed the MSJ. Defendant Hudson did not file a joinder to Bede’s MSJ, did not file a joinder to the motion for reconsideration, and did not file a joinder to the instant motion for attorneys’ fees. It is serendipitous that Defendant Hudson benefits from the Bede Defendants’ motion practice. Nonetheless, they are a prevailing party because the court dismissed the entire FAC. (Order Granting Motion for Reconsideration, May 21, 2024.)

 

Accordingly, Defendants are the prevailing party.

 

Attorneys’ Fees At Issue

 

As a threshold matter, the court addresses which Defendants’ attorneys’ fees are at issue. First, the Bede Defendants’ fees are clearly at issue since they brought the instant motion. Second, Defendant Benchmark is not a moving party to this motion and did not timely move for its attorneys’ fees when Plaintiff dismissed it back in 2020. (CRC 3.1702(b)(1); Sanabria v. Embrey (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 422, 427 [noting that a voluntary dismissal is effectively a judgment under this rule.) The court therefore declines to award Benchmark’s fees or analyze their reasonableness. Third, Defendant Hudson also did not move for attorneys’ fees. Rather, the Bede Defendants appear to move on Hudson’s behalf because they are supposedly paying Hudson’s attorneys’ fees. (Mot. 3:19-27.) While the Bede Defendants refer to a contractual relationship with Defendant Hudson, they did not provide authority to the court indicating this is a valid substitute for Defendant Hudson to bring its own motion or joinder to the instant motion. Additionally, the court is inclined not to consider Defendant Hudson’s attorneys’ fees because the notice of motion does not indicate that the Bede Defendants are seeking them.

 

Accordingly, the court will consider the Bede Defendants’ attorneys’ fees only.

 

Reasonableness of Fees Requested

 

i.                    Time Spent

The court applies the lodestar method when determining reasonableness of fees. Here, the average rate of an attorney in Los Angeles County varies depending on several factors including the nature and difficulty of the litigation, the amount of money involved, the skill required and employed to handle the case, the attention given, the success or failure,¿and other circumstances in the case. (EnPalm, LLC v.¿Teitler¿Family Trust¿(2008) 162 Cal. App. 4th 770, 774 (emphasis in original).)

 

A verified fee bill is “prima facie evidence the costs, expenses and services listed were necessarily incurred, and when they are properly challenged the burden of proof shifts to the party claiming them as costs.” (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) Still, the court is the ultimate arbiter in deciding whether expenses are reasonable.¿¿ 

 

The Bede Defendants request 963.6 hours billed by Mr. Dawson and 565.6 hours billed by Mr. Friesen. (Dawson Decl., Exhibit A; Friesen Decl. ¶ 7.) Both requests are problematic. First, Mr. Dawson’s bill includes unreasonable entries such as time for a motion never filed, time for ex parte applications that were not warranted, and time for conferring with Mr. Friesen (co-counsel for the same client), and performing administrative tasks (such as trouble shooting exhibit software). (See generally, Dawson Decl., Exhibit A.) Second, Mr. Friesen’s billing descriptions are woefully vague and excessive for an attorney with 40 years’ experience. (See generally, Friesen Decl., Exhibit 1.) To be sure, “trial courts are not required to identify each charge they find to be reasonable or unreasonable, necessary or unnecessary. . . . A reduced award might be fully justified by a general observation that an attorney over litigated a case.” (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 101.) Moreover, “trial courts need not, and indeed should not, become green-eyeshade accountants. The essential goal in shifting fees (to either party) is to do rough justice, not to achieve auditing perfection. So trial courts may take into account their overall sense of a suit, and may use estimates in calculating and allocating an attorney’s time. (Fox v. Vice (2011 563 U.S. 826, 838.) As such, the court finds based upon its experience and knowledge of this type of litigation and the professed specialization of counsel, the court will apply a 10% negative multiplier to reduce the total hours expended in the billing records provided. Otherwise, the court notes that the Bede Defendants vigorously defended Plaintiff’s claim and obtained a favorable result.

 

Accordingly, 867.24 of Mr. Dawson’s hours and 509.04 of Mr. Friesen’s hours are reasonable.

 

ii.                  Hourly Rate

The Bede Defendants provide evidence supporting the hourly rates requested for Mr. Dawson and for Mr. Friesen. (Dawson Decl. ¶¶ 13-14, 21; Friesen Decl. ¶¶ 2, 4, 7.) Counsels’ experience warrants the requested hourly rates of $550 and $500 per hour, respectively.

 

Accordingly, the hourly rates requested are reasonable.

2.      The court awards $731,502.00 in attorneys’ fees to the Bede Defendants payable within 30 days notice of this order.
