DEPARTMENT S25 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23LBCV01099    Hearing Date: May 30, 2024    Dept: S25

Background

On June 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Borba Investment Realty, Inc., Charles B. Roselli, and Does 1 through 100. On March 13, 2024, Plaintiff alleged the following causes of action of the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”):

1. Violation of California Civil Code § 1942.4;

2. Tortious Breach of the Warranty of Habitability;

3. Private Nuisance;

4. Business and Professions Code § 17200, et seq.;

5. Negligence;

6. Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment;

7. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress;

8. Negligence Per Se;

9. Violation of Consumer Legal Remedies Act, California Civil Code § 1750, et seq.;

10. California Fair Employment and Housing Act, Gov. Code § 12955, et seq.; and

11. Violation of Long Beach Tenant Harassment Ordinance.

Plaintiff alleges that this action arises out of Plaintiff’s alleged tenancy at Defendant Roselli’s property located at 535 W. Third Street, Unit 12, Long Beach, California (“Subject Property”). (FAC ¶ 4.) Plaintiff alleges residing at the Subject Property from October 2018 to February 2023 and it was inhabitable due to cockroaches, plumbing issues, weatherproofing and an inoperable electric garage door opener. (FAC ¶¶ 22, 25.) Plaintiff alleges that repair requests communicated to Defendant Roselli were ignored or only partially repaired. (FAC ¶¶ 33, 34.)

Procedural History

On October 25, 2023, Defendant Borba Investment Realty, Inc. (“Defendant Borba”) filed a Demurrer and Motion to Strike to the original complaint. That same day, Defendant Charles B. Roselli (“Defendant Roselli”) filed a Demurrer to the original complaint. On February 15, 2014, this Court, per the Honorable Mark C. Kim, issued its

Minute Order following the hearing. The Court sustained Defendant Borba’s demurrer without leave to amend and sustained Defendant Roselli’s demurrer with leave to amend.

On March 14, 2024, this Court, per the Hon. Mark C. Kim, granted judgment of dismissal following sustaining Defendant Borba’s demurrer without leave to amend. On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to set aside/vacate dismissal. The hearing for the motion to set aside/vacate dismissal is set for October 10, 2024.

On April 15, 2024, Defendant Roselli filed the instant demurrer with motion to strike. As of May 24, 2024, no opposition or reply has been filed.

Legal Standard

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading. It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50, subd. (a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within]. Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. Per Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (a), a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).)

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. (Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn (1987) 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769.) And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).)

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

Finally, Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).)

Meet and Confer

Defendant Roselli’s counsel declares that he sent a detailed email to Plaintiff’s counsel on April 12, 2024, prior to filing the instant demurrer and motion to strike. (Day Decl., ¶¶ 11, 12; Exh. B.) In effort to meet and confer, Defendant Roselli’s counsel called Plaintiff’s office on April 12, 2024 and left a voice mail; Defendant Roselli’s counsel also sent emails requesting Plaintiff’s counsel call him to discuss the grounds for the demurrer and motion to strike. (Day Decl., ¶¶ 13-15, Exh. C.) When Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond by the deadline listed by Defendant Roselli’s counsel, the instant demurrer and motion to strike was filed. (Day Decl., ¶¶ 15, 16.) The Court finds that Defendant Roselli’s counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirements prior to filing the instant demurrer and motion to strike.

The February 15, 2024 Minute Order also noted Plaintiff counsel’s failure to participate in the meet and confer process regarding the demurrer and motion to strike that were filed to the original complaint. (2/15/2024 Minute Order pp. 3, 4.) This Court ordered Plaintiff’s counsel to participate in future meet and confer conversations. Plaintiff’s counsel has again failed to participate in the meet and confer process. (Ibid.)

Discussion

Defendant Roselli states that in Plaintiff’s FAC contains only three differences from Plaintiff’s original complaint. The three differences are:

1. The words “First Amended” are hand-written on the caption page;

2. The name Borba Investment is deleted from Paragraph 4 in the definition of “Defendants”; and

3. The First Amended Complaint contains the addition of one “new” Paragraph 134 in the Seventh Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

(See Day Decl., ¶¶ 8-10.)

The Court notes on additional difference. The FAC also contains the addition of one “new” Paragraph 169 in the Tenth Cause of Action for FEHA. (FAC ¶ 169.)

This Court sustained with leave to amend Defendant Roselli’s demurrer and motion to the strike the original complaint because Plaintiff did not attach the property lease (“subject lease”). (2/15/2024 Minute Order p. 4; Day Decl., Exh. “A”.) This Court ordered Plaintiff to attach the lease to an amended pleading. (Ibid.) Despite this Court’s earlier directive, Plaintiff has not included the subject lease to the FAC.

Defendant Roselli demurs to the private nuisance, breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, IIED, CLRA, FEHA, and tenant harassment ordinance causes of action in the FAC. Defendant Roselli raises the same arguments as he did in the demurrer to the original complaint. Despite this Court’s earlier directive that Plaintiff must attach the subject lease to an amended pleading, Plaintiff has not done so. The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend on the ground that Plaintiff did not attach the subject lease from which the parties’ duties under the subject lease are alleged to arise from.

Defendant Roselli also argues the IIED cause of action fails to allege extreme and outrageous conduct, the FEHA cause of action fails to allege facts showing discrimination, and the tenant harassment cause of action fails to allege facts showing harassment. The Court finds that Plaintiff has not alleged enough facts to state sufficient to state causes of action for IIED, FEHA, and the tenant harassment ordinance.

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend on each of these additional grounds; Plaintiff must plead additional facts in an amended pleading if he wishes to pursue these claims.

Motion to Strike

Defendant Roselli’s motion to strike is moot in light of the ruling on the demurrer.

Tentative Ruling

Defendant Roselli’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend for 20 days. Defendant Roselli’s motion to strike is moot.

Case Number: 23LBCV01593    Hearing Date: May 30, 2024    Dept: S25

Procedural Background

On August 23, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants Frank Colaruotolo Consulting, Inc. (“FCC”), Pacific Crane Maintenance Company, LLC (“PCMC”), and Does 1-10. On April 11, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”), alleging two causes of action: (1) Negligence against Defendants FCC, Frank Colaruotolo, and Does 2 through 10; and (2) Negligence against Defendants PCMC and Does 2 through 10.

Plaintiffs Martin Ruiz and Graciela Ruiz are the surviving biological father and mother of the decedent, respectively. (FAC ¶¶ 1, 2.) Plaintiffs are suing for the loss of their adult son, decedent Edgar Ruiz. Decedent was killed during a forklift incident on January 18, 2022. (Forouzan Decl., ¶ 2.)

Since the filing of Plaintiffs’ original complaint, a related case has been filed by Nico Ruiz against Defendants FCC and CSI Services, Inc., case number 24LBCV00081. Both this case, 23LBCV01593, and case number 24LBCV00081 have been deemed related by this Court. (See 3/14/2024 Notice of Ruling Re: Related Cases and Upcoming Hearing Dates in Case No. 24LBCV00081; Forouzan Decl., ¶ 4.) On May 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a motion to consolidate this case, 23LBCV01593, with the related case no. 24LBCV00081. The hearing on the motion to consolidate is set for July 11, 2024.

Plaintiffs did not know about the existence of CSI Services, Inc. until after the filing of the related case and learned of its existence through counsel for Nico Ruiz on March 18, 2024. (Forouzan Decl., ¶¶ 5, 14 28.) The two-year statute of limitation had passed on Plaintiffs’ case. On March 18, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel met and conferred with Defendant FCC’s counsel via email to request a stipulation for leave to name CSI Services, Inc. as a Doe Defendant. (Forouzan Decl., ¶¶ 5-6; Exh. A.) Two days later, Defendant FCC’s counsel responded and requested a week to consider the proposal. (Forouzan Decl., ¶ 7.) After waiting a week with no response from Defendant FCC’s counsel, Plaintiff initiated efforts to appear ex parte in order to request for leave to

amend. However, Plaintiffs elected to proceed via the instant motion to provide notice to all parties. The instant motion was filed on April 15, 2024. No opposition has been filed.

Legal Standard

“Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 576.)

As a condition of granting leave to amend, the trial court may require a plaintiff to bring a motion that is compliant with rule 3.1324 of the California Rules of Court (Rule 3.1324), including the evidentiary requirements, and doing so does not constitute an abuse of discretion. (Hataishi v. First American Home Buyers Protection Corp. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1454, 1469.)

A party requesting leave to amend must submit a motion which includes: (1) a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings; (2) a statement of which allegations would be deleted by the amendment, and where they are located in the previous pleading; and (3) a statement of what allegations would be added by the amendment, and where they are located in the proposed pleading. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a).) The motion must be accompanied by a declaration stating: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) why the request was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)

Code Civ. Proc. § 474 provide the process for naming fictitiously named defendants:

“When the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant, he [or she] must state that fact in the complaint, or the affidavit if the action is commenced by affidavit, and such defendant may be designated in any pleading or proceeding by any name, and when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly.”

To amend the complaint to add fictitiously named defendants under Code of Civ. Proc. § 474, the complaint must have alleged that plaintiff was ignorant of the true names and capacities of defendants sued by fictitious names [e.g., DOES 1-10], and that the names under which such defendants were sued were fictitious. (See Kerr-McGee Chemical Corp. v. Superior Court (1984) 160 Cal. App. 3d 594, 597-598.) The complaint must also contain charging allegations alleging that the fictitiously named defendants were responsible for the acts complained of by plaintiff. (See Winding Creek v. McGlashan (1996) 44 Cal. App. 4th 933, 941-943.)

When an amendment is sought after the statute of limitations has run, the amended

complaint will be deemed filed as of the date of the original complaint, provided the following:

1. The original complaint stated a valid cause of action against the now-identified Doe;

2. The Plaintiff was “genuinely ignorant” of the defendant’s identity;

3. The amended complaint is based on the “same general set of facts” and refers to the “same accident and same injuries,” as in the operative complaint.

(See Austin v. Massachusetts Bonding & Ins. Co. (1961) 56 C2d 596, 600-601, 15

CR 817, 819; San Diego Navy Broadway Complex Coalition v. California Coastal Comm'n (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 563, 579; Eghtesad v. State Farm Gen. Ins. Co. (2020) 51 Cal.App.5th 406, 415 (citing text)).

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiffs have met the requirements for leave to amend to name a fictitiously named Doe after the passage of the statute of limitations.

The FAC alleges that all defendants, including all fictitiously named Doe defendants, were the alter egos of one another or acting in a single enterprise, thereby stating a valid cause of action against CSI Services, Inc.. (FAC ¶ 11.) CSI Services, Inc. has the same agent for service of process, Defendant Frank Colaruotolo, as previously named Defendant Frank Colaruotolo Consulting, Inc. (Forouzan Decl., ¶ 9, 29, 30; Exh. B.)

Plaintiffs were genuinely ignorant of CSI Services, Inc. Plaintiffs were able to discover Defendant PCMC through prelitigation investigations; however, they could not discover CSI Services, Inc. through their investigation. (See Forouzan Decl., ¶¶ 11, 13; Exh. C.) Plaintiffs did not learn about CSI Services, Inc. until after a conversation with counsel for the related case on March 18, 2024. (Forouzan Decl., ¶ 14, 28.)

Plaintiffs seek leave to replace Doe 2 with a newly discovered entity. Thus, the amendment is based on the same general set of facts and refers to the same accident and same injuries.

Defendants have not filed an opposition and have not shown there will be prejudice to them by allowing Plaintiffs’ leave to amend.

Good cause has been shown and Plaintiffs have complied with Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324. The motion for leave to amend is GRANTED.

Case Number: 23LBCV02389    Hearing Date: May 30, 2024    Dept: S25

Procedural Background

On December 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendant for breach of contract and common counts. Plaintiff alleges payment of $34,000 to Defendant for the purchase of a functional 2017 Truck Chassis VIN LRJC46372H1000934 (“Truck”) on or about August 12, 2022. (Compl., p. 3, BC-1.) Plaintiff discovered that the Truck sold to Plaintiff was not legally operable on California highways and could not be registered with the DMV. (Compl., p. 3, BC-2.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant refused to rescind sale of or to refund Plaintiff for the purchase of the Truck. (Compl., p. 3, BC-2.)

On February 5, 2024, Defendant propounded following discovery: (1) Request for Production of Documents (Set One), (2) Form Interrogatories (Set One), and (3) Special Interrogatories (Set One) (hereinafter “Defendant’s discovery requests”). Plaintiff’s discovery responses were due on March 8, 2024. (See Seyfnia Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond to Defendant counsel’s March 15, 2024 email requesting responses be served no later than March 20, 2024. (See Seyfnia Decl., ¶ 7; Exh. D.) Further, Plaintiff did not request an extension to respond past the deadline. (See Seyfnia Decl., ¶ 8.)

On March 21, 2024, Defendant filed these two motions seeking compliance with the discovery requests and requested $3,973.30 in monetary sanctions be imposed on Plaintiff and its counsel, jointly and severally.

Legal Standard

If a party to whom interrogatories and document demands are directed fails to respond at all, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a court order compelling answers thereto. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.) All that needs to be shown is that the discovery was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. The moving party is not required to show a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the matter informally before filing this motion. A motion to compel initial discovery responses need not show good cause, meeting and conferring, or timely filing, and need not be accompanied by a separate statement. (See Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pac. Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The failure to timely respond also waives all objections.¿¿

On motion, a party may be relieved from its waiver of objections if: (1) the party subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance “with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240” and (2) “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a)(1)-(2).)

Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d) provides that a misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond to or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(h) states that a misuse of the discovery process includes making or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery. A court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process or any attorney advising such conduct under Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771.)

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiff’s opposition is untimely; nonetheless, Plaintiff has now provided responses to the propounded discovery. These responses render Defendant’s motions moot. The Court declines to award monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel in this instance; however, further continued failure by Plaintiff to timely respond to discovery requests or engage in the discovery process, including any request for further responses by Defendant, may result in future monetary sanctions.
