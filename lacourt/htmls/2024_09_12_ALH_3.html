[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET THREE

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

September 12, 2024

 

)

 

 

          Plaintiff Luz Mercado (“Plaintiff”) moves for an order compelling defendant ADP Technology Servies, Inc. (“Defendant”, erroneously sued as “Automatic Data Processing, Inc.”) to serve further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Three. Plaintiff also seeks sanctions in the amount of $9,426.02 against Defendant and its counsel of record.

Plaintiff propounded Special Interrogatories (Set Three) on February 12, 2024, and in response, Defendant filed a motion for a protective order. Special Interrogatories (Set Three) consists of 49 special interrogatories seeking information about complaints submitted by Defendant’s employees to Defendant’s Associate Relations Department. On March 15, 2024, Defendant served responses consisting only of objections. The Court denied Defendant’s motion for a protective order on May 3, 2024, and Plaintiff filed this motion on May 23, 2024, claiming that Defendant has not indicated if it intends to provide substantive responses to her 49 special interrogatories.

On September 3, 2024, Defendant filed a declaration from counsel stating that supplemental responses were served on June 14, 2024. (Perelshteyn Decl., ¶ 6.) These supplemental responses are purportedly attached as Exhibit A, but the document attached as Exhibit A are supplemental responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production (which are the subject of another motion scheduled to be heard on October 1, 2024).  Further supplemental responses to SROG Nos. 78 to 83 were served on July 25, 2024. (Id., Ex. E.)

Defendant first argues that Plaintiff’s motion should be denied because she failed to meet and confer before filing this motion, even though Defendant “informed her that it would supplement its responses” at the IDC. The Court is unpersuaded that Defendant would have supplemented its responses without Plaintiff’s motion practice because Defendant’s protective order was denied on May 3, 2024, the parties participated in an IDC on May 15, 2024, and yet Defendant did not serve supplemental responses until June 14, 2024. (Perelshteyn Decl., ¶ 6.)

Defendant also argues that its responses are sufficient because they refer Plaintiff to specific Bates-labeled documents which contain the necessary information, as permitted by Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230. The Court cannot analyze the adequacy of Defendant’s supplemental responses to SROG Nos. 34-77 and determine whether the motion should be denied without having those supplemental responses before it. The Court is also unpersuaded by Defendant’s suggestion that the supplemental responses moot the motion. Adopting Defendant’s position would permit a party faced with a motion to compel further responses “to delay its adjudication indefinitely by successive supplemental but inadequate responses, each prompting a further motion to compel, each of which in turn would be defeated by piecemeal supplementation.” (County of San Benito v. Superior Court (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 243.)

Accordingly, the Court continues this hearing to October 1, 2024. Defendant is ordered to file a copy of its supplemental responses to Special Interrogatories (Set Three) no later than 10 court days before the hearing.

 

 

Dated this 12th day of September, 2024

 

 

 

 

       William A. Crowfoot

Judge of the Superior Court

          Application for Default Judgment

Plaintiff Ally Bank (“Plaintiff”) seeks a default judgment against defendants Marine Nersisyan (“Nersisyan”), Devine Motorworkz Corp. (“Body Shop”), and Grigor Cholakyan, individually and dba Devine Motorworkz (“Cholakyan”). This case arises from Nersisyan’s purchase of a 2013 Mercedes-Benz G Class (“Subject Vehicle”) pursuant to a retail installment sales contract (“Contract”). Nersisyan defaulted on the Contract and currently owes $73,766.17. To complicate matters, Nersisyan also transferred the Subject Vehicle to Body Shop and Cholakyan, who refuse to surrender the Subject Vehicle to Plaintiff.

On August 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed two requests for court judgment on Form CIV-100. One of the forms seeks a judgment of $73,766.17 against Nersisyan for the amounts owed under a Retail Installment Sale Contract. The other Form CIV-100 seeks $47,972 against Body Shop and Cholakyan, which is the market value for the Subject Vehicle.

The declaration provided by Plaintiff’s representative, James Singleton, does not set forth the basis for Plaintiff’s recovery against Nersisyan for $73,766.17. The payment history attached as Exhibit E reflects a balance of $70,496.59.

          The Court also notes that the proposed judgment is confusing because as currently phrased, it appears to allow Plaintiff a double recovery for the Subject Vehicle. The proposed judgment should specify that all three defendants are jointly and severally liable for $47,972, less any amount obtained from the sale of the Subject Vehicle, with Nersisyan separately liable for any amount exceeding such value due to the terms of the Contract.

          Accordingly, the hearing is continued to ______ at 8:30 a.m. in Department 3 of the Alhambra Courthouse so that Plaintiff may file an amended proposed judgment and additional evidence supporting its claim for damages against Nersisyan. All revised and supplemental papers must be submitted no later than 5 court days before the hearing.

          Application for Writ of Possession

          Plaintiff’s application for a writ of possession is missing a proposed order. Therefore, the Court continues the hearing on the application to _____, the same day as the default prove-up hearing. The Court notes that a writ of possession will become unnecessary once Plaintiff proves up its damages for a default judgment because the proposed default judgment already includes a request for possession of the Subject Vehicle. However, if Plaintiff still wishes to pursue a writ, Plaintiff must submit a proposed order no later than 5 court days before the next hearing.
