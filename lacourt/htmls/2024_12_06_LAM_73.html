DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV15571    Hearing Date: December 6, 2024    Dept: 73

12/06/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

JANET DIXON v. THE PEOPLE CONCERN (23STCV15571) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Janet Dixon (Pro Per) 

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Norman Ayotte (Jones & Ayotte) 

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT (filed 10/15/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 5, 2023, Plaintiff Janet Dixon filed the instant action against Defendant The People Concern.

 

On June 12, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file First Amended Complaint.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following. Plaintiff is a resident of Defendant The People Concern, which is a temporary housing shelter. Plaintiff informed Defendant’s staff that she does not eat meat, such as beef, chicken or turkey. On April 27, 2023, a member of Defendant’s staff served Plaintiff dinner that contained chicken. The staff told Plaintiff the chicken was vegan. However, Plaintiff alleges the chicken was not vegan and that after eating the food, she became very ill. Plaintiff alleges she was ill 3 days following the incident and it took her a day and a half to recover. She alleges that she reported the issue to Defendant’s staff, but they still continue to serve her meat.

 

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint, arguing:

·       Plaintiff seeks leave to file an amendment to the complaint and a Second Amended Complaint in order to set forth the basis for punitive damages against the Defendant as well as verify the complaint.

·       Plaintiff’s Proposed Second Amended Complaint is based on the same general set of facts as the Complaint and First Amended Complaint; refers to the same incident(s) beginning on or about April 20th, 2023, and continuing thereafter; and seeks recovery for the same injuries suffered by Plaintiff as alleged in the original Complaint and First Amended Complaint.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s motion is procedurally defective

·       Plaintiff did not fulfill the requirements of California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324.

o   Plaintiff does not state when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered. (CRC Rules 3.1324(b)(3).) Plaintiff’s supporting declaration also fails to offer any explanation as to why the amendment was not made earlier. (California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324(b)(4). The motion fails to state meaningfully why the amendments are necessary and proper. (CRC Rules 3.1324(2))

o   Lack of robustness in a pleading is not a justifiable basis for filing an amended complaint.

o   None of the alleged acts of wrongdoing by Defendant occurred later than March 19, 2024, which predates her filing of her First Amended Complaint.

·       Defendant requests sanctions be imposed against Plaintiff for bringing a frivolous motion

 

Plaintiff filed a reply stating that Defendant’s opposition is untimely and should be disregarded. Plaintiff also maintains that her motion to file SAC is warranted.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Timeliness of Opposition

 

As an initial matter, Plaintiff argues in her reply that Defendant’s opposition was untimely and should be disregarded.

 

Opposition papers are required to be filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b). However, a court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP section 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d); Code Civ. Proc., § 475.)

 

Here, Defendant filed its opposition on November 21, 2024. The Court finds Defendant’s opposition is timely as it was filed at least nine court days prior to the hearing date of December 6, 2024. Thus, the Court considers Defendant’s opposition.

 

B.      Legal Standard

 

The trial court has discretion to allow amendments to pleadings in the furtherance of justice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) “There is a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the pleadings at any stage of the proceeding.” (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.) “An application to amend a pleading is addressed to the trial judge’s sound discretion.” (Ibid.)   

 

Under Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b) of the California Rules of Court, a separate declaration must accompany a motion for leave to amend and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).) A party seeking leave to amend must attach a copy of the proposed pleading to the motion for leave to amend. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a).)  

 

C.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for an order granting Plaintiff leave to file a Second Amended Complaint.

Plaintiff substantially complied with CRC Rule 3.1324. Plaintiff provided a declaration in support of the instant motion, an Appendix of Proposed Changes to the First Amended Complaint and separately filed the proposed Second Amended Complaint. Plaintiff’s declaration states the effect of the proposed amendments and claims the proposed amendments will allow for punitive/exemplary damages. (Dixon Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff also states that she discovered on or about September 5, 2024, after receiving the Defendants’ answers, that she mistakenly did not verify the complaint and that did not sufficiently state any grounds needed to recover, or attempt to recover, punitive/exemplary damages. (Dixon Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

The courts have a strong policy of allowing motions for leave to amend. “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend….” (Morgan v. Sup. Ct. (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527.)  A court can deny leave to amend after long, inexcusable delay, where there is cognizable prejudice, such as discovery needed, trial delay, critical evidence lost, or added preparation expense.  (Solit v. Tokai Bank (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448; Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761; Green v. Rancho Santa Margarita Mortgage Co. (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 686, 692; Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)

 

Defendant does not argue in its opposition that it will be prejudiced by the motion for leave to amend. Trial in this matter is not set until June 16, 2025. There is no indication that there was unreasonable delay in seeking leave or that Defendant would be prejudiced. Plaintiff has indicated the proposed amendments allow for punitive/exemplary damages, which is necessary and proper to provide Plaintiff the full range of remedies available to her.

 

Therefore, since there has been no showing Defendants will be prejudiced by the motion for leave to amend and Plaintiff has substantially complied with the requirements of Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b), Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint is GRANTED.

MENDEZ v. DINO’S BURGERS FAST FOOD, INC., et al. (23STCV29774)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Eli Banayan (Bana Legal Group, PC)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Timothy R. Casey (Law Offices of Timothy R. Casey)

 

(1)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES TO DEFENDANT DINO’S BURGERS FAST FOOD, INC., SET ONE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 07/15/24)

(2)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES TO DEFENDANT DINO’S BURGERS FAST FOOD, INC., SET ONE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 07/15/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

(1)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 4.1, 12.2 and 17.1 to Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. shall provide verified responses within 30 days. The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. in the reduced amount of $2,670. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

(2)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2 and 17 to Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. shall provide verified responses within 30 days. The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. in the reduced amount of $2,670. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 6, 2023, Plaintiff Diego Lopez Mendez filed this action against Defendants Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.; Dino’s Main St, Inc.; Konstantinos Pantazis and George Pantazis.

 

On January 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

 

C/A 1: Failure To Provide Meal Periods

C/A 2: Failure To Provide Rest Periods

C/A 3: Failure To Pay All Wages Due Upon Termination: Waiting Time Penalties

C/A 4: Failure To Pay All Wages Earned

C/A 5: Failure To Pay Minimum Wage

C/A 6: Failure To Issue Accurate Itemized Wage Statements

C/A 7: Failure To Timely Produce Personnel File

C/A 8: Violations Of California’s Unfair Competition Act

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff was employed by Defendants as a dishwasher from in or about 2017, until the termination of Plaintiff’s employment on August 29, 2023. Plaintiff alleges multiple violations of California’s Labor Code.

 

On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Motion to Compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and request for sanctions. No opposition was filed.

 

On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Motion to Compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and request for sanctions. No opposition was filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel declares that a meet and confer letter was sent to Plaintiff’s counsel on June 12, 2024, explaining why the discovery responses were deficient and requesting further responses. (Banayan Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff’s counsel also states another correspondence was sent on June 26, 2024 asking Defense Counsel if Defendant was amenable to participating in an Informal Discovery Conference to address the issues. (Id., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff did not receive a response. (Id., ¶ 8.) The Court finds that counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2). 

 

B.      Legal Standard

 

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) 

 

It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

  

If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure fully to answer. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

 

Furthermore, to the extent there is any doubt in whether these records should be discoverable, California’s liberal approach to discovery provides that doubt should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.) 

 

C.      Form Interrogatories, Set One

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 4.1, 12.2 and 17.1.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. provided improper, evasive and incomplete answers to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 4.1, 12.2 and 17.1. The interrogatories at issue are as follows:

 

Form Interrogatory No. 4.1: “At the time of the INCIDENT, was there in effect any policy of insurance through which you were or might be insured in any manner (for example, primary, pro-rata, or excess liability coverage or medical expense coverage) for the damages, claims, or actions that have arisen out of the INCIDENT? If so, for each policy state:

(a) The kind of coverage;

(b) the name and ADDRESS of the insurance company;

(c) the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of each named insured;

(d) the policy number;

(e) the limits of coverage for each type of coverage contained in the policy;

(f) whether any reservation of rights or controversy or coverage dispute exists between you and the insurance company; and

(g) the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the custodian of the policy.”

 

Defendant’s Response to Form Interrogatory No. 4.1: “Plaintiffs has previously exhausted all demands for disability insurance and gas been denied on Each occasion, in addition he has exhausted all Workman’s Compensation claims which were found to be frivolous, See responses to Request for Production of documents.”

 

Form Interrogatory No. 12.2: “Have YOU OR ANYONE ACTING ON YOUR BEHALF obtained a written or recorded statement from any individual concerning the INCIDENT? If so, for each statement state:

(a) the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the individual interviewed;

(b) the date of the interview; and

(c) the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who conducted the interview.”

 

Defendant’s Response to Form Interrogatory No. 12.2: “Yes (a) Fernando Alvarez (Asked not to give his personal information out) (b) Eddie Serrano (asked to keep his personal information private) (c) Juan Mattas (asked to keep his personal information private)”

 

Form Interrogatory No. 17.1: “Is your response to each request for admission served with these interrogatories an unqualified admission? If not, for each response that is not an unqualified admission:

(a) state the number of the request;

(b) state all facts upon which you base your response;

(c) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and

(d) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.”

 

Defendant’s Response to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1: “Plaintiff was and is a continuing alcoholic that case to work intoxicated and brough beer onto The premises even after being told not to do so. His conduct was observed by each of the employees, and he was Actually, seen going into the parking lot with women and drinking with them in their automobile even after being Confronted in the act by management. This conduct never ceased”

 

Plaintiff asserts that Defendant must provide clear and specific responses to the Plaintiff’s requests, including identifying individuals with knowledge, producing relevant documents, and providing factual explanations.

 

Upon review of Defendant’s responses, the Court agrees. As to Form Interrogatory No. 4.1, Defendant is asked whether or not it had insurance policies in effect at the time of the incident, and what the information is regarding the policy. However, Defendant’s answer is nonresponsive. As to Form Interrogatory No. 12.2, Defendant identifies individuals without providing complete answers. The requested information is relevant as Plaintiff must be able to conduct discovery and depositions of individuals with discoverable information. If Defendant objects on privacy grounds, Defendant has the burden of justifying its objections. As to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1, the Court finds Defendant’s response is nonresponsive and does not discuss any of Plaintiff’s request for admissions.

 

The Court notes that Defendant has failed to oppose the motion. “A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion…is meritorious.”¿ (Id.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.” (Id.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.” (Id.)

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 4.1, 12.2 and 17.1. 

 

a.      Form Interrogatories, Set One - Sanctions

 

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.) 

 

Sanctions may be imposed for misuse of discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030, subd. (a).) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.010, subd. (d).) Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348 provides that a court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery even if no opposition was filed.  

 

Here, the Court finds that Defendant engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by failing to make good faith efforts to respond to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One. Plaintiff requests $3,970 in sanctions for counsel’s work on this motion, including 4 hours spent on the instant motion, an anticipated 2 additional hours to review the opposition, prepare a reply and appear at the hearing, and $70 filing fee. The Court finds sanctions are warranted. However, since no opposition was filed, the Court awards sanctions in the reduced total amount of $2,670 (4 hours of attorney time at the rate of $650 an hour and $70 filing fee).

 

D.     Special Interrogatories, Set One

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2 and 17.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. provided improper, evasive and incomplete answers to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2 and 17. The interrogatories at issue are as follows:

 

Special Interrogatory No. 2: “Please IDENTIFY PLAINTIFF’S dates of employment with YOU.”

 

Defendant’s Response to Special Interrogatory No. 2: “Said information is within the knowledge of the Plaintiff and has not been properly identified in Responses to discovery or in the base of the complaint.”

 

Special Interrogatory No. 17: “Please state the number of rest period premium payments YOU paid to PLAINTIFF during the July 1, 2023 to present.”

 

Defendant’s Response to Special Interrogatory No. 17: “Plaintiff was paid for all rest periods.”

 

The Court finds that further responses are warranted. Defendant was Plaintiff’s employer and the requested information is in Defendant’s possession. However, Defendant’s answers to both interrogatories is non-responsive and not code compliant. Defendant has failed to oppose the motion.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc.’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2 and 17.

 

a.      Special Interrogatories, Set One – Sanctions

 

The Court finds that Defendant engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by failing to make good faith efforts to respond to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One. Plaintiff requests $3,970 in sanctions for counsel’s work on this motion, including 4 hours spent on the instant motion, an anticipated 2 additional hours to review the opposition, prepare a reply and appear at the hearing, and $70 filing fee. The Court finds sanctions are warranted. However, since no opposition was filed, the Court awards sanctions in the reduced total amount of $2,670 (4 hours of attorney time at the rate of $650 an hour and $70 filing fee).

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

(1)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 4.1, 12.2 and 17.1 to Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. shall provide verified responses within 30 days. The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. in the reduced amount of $2,670. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

(2)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2 and 17 to Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. is GRANTED. Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. shall provide verified responses within 30 days. The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Dino’s Burgers Fast Food, Inc. in the reduced amount of $2,670. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order.

BOA LOGISTICS, LLC v. LUCKY B EXPRESS, INC. (24STCV03193) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  John Larson (Larson & Gaston, LLP) 

Counsel for Defendant:  None

 

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 12/04/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 7, 2024, Plaintiff Boa Logistics, LLC filed the instant action against Defendant Lucky B Express, Inc.

 

Plaintiff alleges causes of action for Breach of Contract and Negligence.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff is a licensed property broker by the Federal Motor Carrier Administration. It arranges freight transportation for its customers. Defendant Lucky B. Express, Inc. ("Lucky B") is a motor carrier operating interstate to provide services required by Plaintiff. Plaintiff and Lucky B entered into a contract which provided that, inter alia, Lucky B would provide transportation services as directed by Plaintiff. Lucky B agreed to transportation of temperature-controlled shipments and ensure that shipments would arrive successfully. On January 27, 2022, Lucky B transported a load directed by Plaintiff. The load was picked up in good condition in California, but upon arrival at Trader Joe's in Pennsylvania, it was rejected due to evidence of mouse infestation on the pallets. This breach required the destruction of the contaminated goods, costing $74,858.08 in product loss and $2,600 for disposal, for a total principal amount of $77,458.08.

 

On November 21, 2024, default was entered against Defendant.  

 

On December 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a request for Default Judgment. Plaintiff is requesting a judgment against Defendant in the principal amount of $77,458.08. In addition to the principal amount, Plaintiff is entitled to: (1) Interest in the sum of $17,867.24, calculated at the rate 10% per annum on the principal amount of $77,458.08 ($21.22 per day times 842 days from the Loss and Damages Claims form (BOL) dated 8/15/2022; (2) court costs of $1,043.54; and (3) attorney’s fees of $5,731.07 for a total of $102,099.93.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.      Application

 

SUBMITTED: CRC 3.1800 

 

1.      Use of JC Form CIV-100                                                                             No

2.      Dismissal or judgment of non-parties to the judgment                      Yes 

3.      Declaration of non-military status for each defendant                      Yes 

4.      Summary of the case                                                                                 Yes 

5.      585(d) declarations/admissible evidence in support                      Yes 

6.      Exhibits (as necessary)                                                                               Yes 

7.      Interest computation (as necessary)                                                    Yes 

8.      Cost memorandum                                                                                    Yes

9.      Request for attorney fees (Local Rule 3.214)                                     No

 

Damages Summary: 

 

Damages 

$ 77,458.08 

Punitive Damages  

 N/A

Interest 

$ 17,867.24   

Attorneys’ fees 

$ 5,731.07

Costs 

$ 1,043.54       

TOTAL 

$ 102,099.93

Other relief requested 

 N/A

 

 

The Court does not find that Plaintiff has complied with all requirements for default judgment.

 

JC Form CIV-100 must be used to request court judgment. While Plaintiff filed a CIV-100 form on November 21, 2024 requesting entry of default, Plaintiff must also file a CIV-100 form to request default judgment with Section 2 of the form filled out.

 

On December 12, 2024, in support of its request for default judgment, Plaintiff only filed form JUD-100, a memorandum of costs, a summary of the case and a declaration in support. The Court notes that Plaintiff does not indicate how it calculated its attorneys’ fees.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED for failure to complete the CIV-100 form.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

ISRAEL v. KADOSH, et al. (BC630262)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Bijan Israel (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Jeff Lesser (Jeff Lesser Law)

 

DEFENDANT AND CROSS-COMPLAINANT AVNER KADOSH’S MOTION TO SET ORDER OF DISMISSAL OF CROSS-COMPLAINT (filed 10/18/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court GRANTS Cross-Complainant’s motion.

 

The Court amends the October 2, 2024 order to reinstate the Cross-Complaint. The Court orders Cross-Complainant to file a declaration in opposition to the dismissal of the Cross-Complaint due to the 5-years statute of limitation.

 

I. BACKGROUND

On August 10, 2016, Plaintiff Bijan Israel (“Bijan”)—as a self-represented litigant—filed this lawsuit against Defendant Avner Kadosh (“Kadosh”) for battery. 

On September 12, 2016, Kadosh filed a cross-complaint against Bijan and Sima Israel aka Sima Sharaf alleging the following causes of action: 

1.      Breach of Written Contract 

2.      Open Book Account 

3.      Account Stated 

4.      Goods Sold and Delivered 

5.      Unjust Enrichment 

On November 15, 2017, Bijan amended the complaint to substitute Doe #1 with Defendant Jeff Lesser, who is Kadosh’s counsel of record   

On December 4, 2017, Bijan dismissed his complaint without prejudice.   

On January 3, 2018, Bijan filed a second lawsuit (BC688916) against Defendants Kadosh, Jeff Lesser and Judge Diedre Hill. Bijan alleged a cause of action against Kadosh and subsequently dismissed the other cause of action for Abuse of Process. On June 12, 2018, Bijan dismissed Defendant Hill from the second lawsuit (BC688916.) 

On June 29, 2018, the court (Dept. 73) related this action (which is now just the Kadosh’s cross-complaint) with Bijan’s second lawsuit (BC688916.)  On August 27, 2018, the cases were consolidated with this action (BC630262) designated as the lead case. 

On October 2, 2024, the Court held an Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal of the Complaint and Cross-Complaint. The Court, pursuant to an oral request made by Plaintiff, dismissed the Complaint without prejudice. In its minute order, the Court stated that Defendant/Cross-Complainant failed to file a declaration in opposition to the dismissal of the Cross-Complaint due to the 5-years statute of limitation. The Court found no good cause as to why the case should not be dismissed and dismissed the Cross-Complaint without prejudice.

On October 18, 2024, Kadosh filed the instant Motion to Set Aside, arguing:

·       Kadosh requests an order setting aside the Order of Dismissal made on October 2, 2024, which dismissed the Cross-Complaint in its entirety

·       The Cross-Complaint alleged that there were multiple invoices for fabric sold to Bijan Israel, Sima Israel and their business. Kadosh attached invoices to the Cross-Complaint showing Cross-Defendants owed him money.

·       The Cross-Complaint was not related to Bijan’s battery claim in his Complaint.

·       On August 14, 2024, the Court had ordered that Kadosh file a declaration regarding why the Cross-Complaint should proceed if the main action of Israel was dismissed and that the declaration be filed prior to the October 2, 2024 hearing.

·       Kadosh’s counsel states he was hospitalized and did not calendar that a declaration needed to be filed prior to the October 2, 2024 hearing.

·       Kadosh’s counsel states in his declaration that based on his mistake, inadvertence or excusable neglect he had not filed a declaration in response to the Court’s OSC re dismissal of the Cross-Complaint.

 

Cross-Defendant Bijan filed an opposition, arguing:

·       There is no declaration by Kadosh or evidence indicating that Kadosh did not wish for the Cross-Complaint to be dismissed

·       There is no showing of any sort of extrinsic fraud to justify setting aside the subject dismissal order

·       Kadosh should be estopped from moving to set aside the subject dismissal order because Bijan had relied upon the finality

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

Cross-Complainant Kadosh moves for an order granting relief from this Court’s order dated October 2, 2024 order dismissing the Cross-Complaint.

 

On August 12, 2024, the Court held a hearing in this matter. According to the Court’s minute order, Plaintiff agreed to dismiss the case. (Court’s 8/12/14 Order, p. 1.) The Court’s order also stated “Cross-Complainant intends to proceed with the cross-complaint and indicates to the court that parties had waived the 5 years statute of limitations.” (Ibid.) Thus, the Court ordered Cross-Complainant to file a declaration in opposition to the dismissal due to the 5 years statute of limitations before the October 2, 2024 hearing date. (Ibid.)

 

On October 2, 2024, the Court held an Order to Show Cause hearing Re: Dismissal of the Complaint and Cross-Complaint. The Court’s minute order states that “Cross-Complainant failed to file a declaration in opposition to the dismissal due to the 5-years statute of limitation. The Court finds no good cause as to why the case should not be dismissed.” (Court’s 10/02/14 Order.) Therefore, the Court dismissed the Cross-Complaint without prejudice.

 

Cross-Complainant brings this instant motion arguing the dismissal of the Cross-Complaint was due to the mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect of his counsel.

 

A party may apply for an order granting relief under § 473(b) “upon any such term as may be just…from a judgment, dismissal, order or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect.”

 

The Court had dismissed the Cross-Complaint due to Cross-Complainant’s failure to file a declaration in opposition to the dismissal. In his instant motion, Cross-Complainant submitted a declaration by his counsel, Jeff Lesser, stating that the failure to file the declaration in opposition to the dismissal was due to attorney fault. Lesser states he had been recently hospitalized and did not calendar that a declaration needed to be filed prior to the October 2, 2024 hearing.

 

The Court finds that amending the October 2, 2024 order dismissing the Cross-Complaint would be in the furtherance of justice.

 

Thus, the Court amends the October 2, 2024 order to reinstate the Cross-Complaint. The Court orders Cross-Complainant to file a declaration in opposition to the dismissal of the Cross-Complaint due to the 5-years statute of limitation.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court GRANTS Cross-Complainant’s motion.

 

The Court amends the October 2, 2024 order to reinstate the Cross-Complaint. The Court orders Cross-Complainant to file a declaration in opposition to the dismissal of the Cross-Complaint due to the 5-years statute of limitation.
