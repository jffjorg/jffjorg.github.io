DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV30828    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 73

10/04/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

YEDAM, INC., et al. v. DANIEL LEE, et al. (21STCV30828)

 

Counsel for Cross-Complainants/moving party:  Dale Park (Law Offices of Dale J. Park)

Counsel for Cross-Defendants/opposing party: Frederick Lee (Frederick W. Lee Law Firm APC)

 

CROSS-COMPLAINANTS DANIEL LEE’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (SET ONE) PROPOUNDED ON CROSS-DEFENDANT EUN KYUNG CHOI AND REQUEST FOR COSTS (filed on 06/17/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Cross-Defendant Eun Kyung Choi is GRANTED. Cross-Defendant Choi shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order. Choi is also ordered to pay sanctions to Cross-Complainant Lee, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $2,700.00, within 30 days. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 20, 2021, Plaintiffs Yedam, Inc. (“Yedam") and Eun Kyung Choi (“Choi") brought this breach of loyalty and unfair business practices action against Daniel N. Lee (“Lee") and Jin Sook Lee (together, “the Lees"), and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“DM Cosmetics").

 

On March 15, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting 5 causes of action for: (1) Breach of Duty of Loyalty; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Unfair Business Practices; and (5) Declaratory Relief.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Yedam imports and purchases products including candles and scented oils that are sold at various retail locations such as department stores in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 9.) On or about February 2017 Defendant Daniel Lee began working for Yedam in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 10.) In his capacity as an employee, Defendant Daniel Lee was significantly involved in dealing with: a) manufacturers that produced candles, scented oils or related products; b) the manufacturers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; c) the buyers at the department stores in Korea that purchased such products from Yedam; d) the buyers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; and e) the retail locations in both the United States of America and South Korea where such items were sold at. (FAC, ¶ 22.) Notwithstanding his duty of loyalty, Defendant Daniel Lee, in conjunction with his wife Defendant Angela Lee, have misused Yedam’s proprietary information in order to enrich themselves financially. (FAC, ¶ 24). Defendants’ actions have caused Buyers for Department Stores in South Korea to cease purchasing products from Plaintiff Yedam and/or have caused the closure of retail locations in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 26).

 

The Lees and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“Cross-Complainants" or “Defendants”) cross-complained against Yedam, Choi, Olive House LLC, and Lamis Tania, LLC (“Cross-Defendants"). On August 10, 2023, Cross-Complainants filed their third amended cross-complaint (“TACC”) asserting 8 causes of action against Cross-Defendants: (1) Breach of Oral Contract I; (2) Breach of Oral Contract II; (3) Breach of Oral Contract III; (4) Breach of Oral Contract IV; (5) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets; (6) Trademark Infringement; (7) Unfair Competition in Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq. and California Trademark Law; and (8) Declaratory Relief.

 

The TACC alleges the following. Prior to 2017, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Choi owned Yedam. (TACC, ¶ 11.) Yedam was experiencing serious financial problems. (Ibid.) In February of 2017, Choi approached Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lee asking for help. (Id., ¶ 12.) Choi proposed Lee serve as Yedam’s general manager, whereby Lee would oversee the Korean market and Choi would oversee the Chinese market. (Ibid.) In exchange, Lee would be paid a $10,000 per month salary. (Ibid.) In addition, Choi would give Lee 49% ownership of Yedam if Lee could help Yedam “overcome [its] financial hardship and become profitable." (Ibid.) Lee agreed and moved from the US to Korea to serve as Yedam’s general manager. (Id., ¶ 13.) To help Yedam solve its financial issues, Lee loaned Yedam $70,000.00 in operating capital. (Id., ¶ 14.) Choi promised that the $70,000.00 would be paid back when Yedam become profitable, along with 7% interest per annum from April 1, 2017. (Id., ¶ 12.) Lee successfully managed Yedam thereafter, and Yedam went from net losses in 2016 and 2017 to net profits of over a million dollars per year by the end of 2019. (Id., ¶ 15.) However, in July of 2019, Yedam faced supply chain issues when the “Milk House Candle" company in the United States refused to renew its exclusive distribution contract with Yedam. (Id., ¶ 16.) In response, the Lees created a new brand of merchandise named “Dani Mackenzie" under DM Cosmetics, Inc. (Id., ¶ 17.) The Lees trademarked Dani Mackenzie in the United States in May of 2020. (Id., ¶ 18.) Around that same time, Choi proposed that Yedam purchase Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics in exchange for Yedam having the right to use the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 19.) Choi told the Lees that there was no need to have a written licensing and distribution agreement because Choi would transfer 49% of Yedam’s ownership interest to Lee by the end of December 2020. (Id., ¶ 20.)

 

Dani Mackenzie became highly popular in Korea, and Yedam’s sales and profit soared to the point that Yedam was valued at over $50 million. (Id., ¶ 22.) On December 20, 2020, Lee demanded that Choi transfer 49% of Yedam to him. (Id., ¶ 23.) Lee also demanded that Choi and Yedam repay the $70,000 operating capital loan with 7% interest from April 1, 2017. (Ibid.) However, Choi terminated Lee from Yedam shortly thereafter and refused to convey the 49% ownership share. (Id., ¶ 25.) Further, Choi and Yedam stopped purchasing Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics and started using other suppliers to manufacture products bearing the Dani Mackenzie trade name. (Id., ¶ 28.) On March 18, 2021 and April 14, 2021, Cross-Complainants demanded that Cross-Defendants cease and desist manufacturing, supplying and/or selling products under the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 32.)

 

A.      Motion to Compel Further Answers to Request for Production of Documents (Set One)

 

On June 17, 2024, Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee filed the Motion to Compel Further Answers to Request for Production of Documents (Set One) against Choi, arguing:

·       Lee moves to compel further responses to Requests for Production, Set One, Nos. 1-13

·       Choi provided responses that were improper, deficient, incomplete, and/or without merit

·       Lee requests that the Court award monetary sanctions against Choi and her attorney of record in the sum of $2,700.00.

Choi filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Moving party fails to establish relevant specific facts which warrant the production of documents

·       Lee’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to the Requests for Production of Documents are tied to Form Interrogatories 12.4, 12.5, 12.6 and 15.1. The Court had denied further response to these form interrogatories based on the term “incident”

·       Sanctions should be awarded to Choi in the amount of $6,247.50

 

Lee filed a reply arguing:

·       Responding Party’s attempts to tie the previous form interrogatories to the prior request for production of documents, and to the present Requests is, again, irrelevant and without merit because the present Motion is based on Propounding Party’s Cross-Complaint, and not Responding Party’s Complaint.

·       Choi’s boilerplate objections fails to respond to respond with any substance

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, counsel for Cross-Complainant Lee submitted a declaration attesting that the parties met and conferred. (Park Decl. ¶¶ 6-16.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement.

 

B.      Legal Standard

¿  

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”   

 

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

C.      Discussion

 

Lee requests further responses to Requests Nos. 1-13.

 

Requests Nos. 1-13 seek any and all documents and ESI identified or mentioned in response to Form Interrogatories, Set No. One, Nos. 12.3-12.6, 15.1, 50.1, 50.30-50.6.

 

Choi responded with the following objections: “Asked and Answered”, “Duplicative”, and “Not applicable by virtue of response to [the concurrent Form Interrogatory].”

 

The Court finds that further response is warranted. Choi’s boilerplate objections are too general and unmeritorious because they do not state how or why Choi believes responses are not applicable. Even where a responding party cannot comply with a request for production, a “representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.) The statement shall include the reason why the responding party is unable to comply and “set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Ibid.) Further, Choi fails to show that these requests are duplicative or asked and answered.

 

Based on the foregoing, Lee’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED.

 

D.     Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Both parties request the Court impose monetary sanctions. Since the Court grants Lee’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Production of Documents, Set One, the Court also grants Lee’s request for attorneys’ fees incurred in bringing the motion. Lee requests reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs in the amount of $2,700.00. (Park Decl. ¶ 18.)

 

The Court GRANTS Lee’s request. Choi is ordered to pay sanctions to Cross-Complainant Lee, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $2,700.00, within 30 days. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Cross-Complainant Daniel Lee’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, propounded on Cross-Defendant Eun Kyung Choi is GRANTED. Cross-Defendant Choi shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order. Choi is also ordered to pay sanctions to Cross-Complainant Lee, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $2,700.00, within 30 days.

LOPEZ-REYES v. COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES (24STCP00384)

 

Counsel for Petitioner/opposing party: Rigoberto Lopez-Reyes (Pro Per)

Counsel for Respondent/moving party:  Dawyn Harrison (Los Angeles County Counsel)

 

Demurrer TO THE PETITION OF WRIT OF MANDATE (filed 7/26/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Respondent’s demurrer to the Petition of Writ of Mandate is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a petition against County of Los Angeles Department of Regional Planning.  

 

The Petitioner alleges the County of Los Angeles Planning Department leadership is in violation of the following State Laws: Title 7 chapter 4.3 section 65915(a)(1), 65915(d)(1) and 65915(e)(1) because they are physically precluding the construction of a housing development by denying a building permit. The Petitioner is seeking relief under CCP 1094.5 and is requesting an order from the Court and monetary damages.

 

On July 26, 2024, Respondent filed a demurrer to the Petition, arguing:

·       The entire Petition is uncertain, since a traditional writ under section 1085 and an administrative writ under section 1094.5 review different actions by an agency.

·       There are no allegations to support either request for relief.

·       There are no facts alleged, or any evidence presented that Respondent violated the Government Code sections that Petitioner claims it did.

 

Petitioner filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The petition sets forth sufficient facts that Respondent is in violation of various state laws

·       Respondent has refused Petitioner’s application and is preventing him from moving forward with his housing project

 

Respondent filed a reply maintaining that the Court should sustain its demurrer.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Respondent submitted a declaration stating that the parties discussed the grounds of the demurrer via telephone on July 24, 2024 and via email on July 25, 2024. (Cooper Decl., ¶¶ 3-6.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation and the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

B.      Discussion

 

Respondent demurs to the Writ of Mandate Petition on the grounds that the Petition is uncertain and fails to state sufficient facts.

 

Under CCP section 1094.5(b), the pertinent issues are whether the respondent has proceeded without jurisdiction, whether there was a fair trial, and whether there was a prejudicial abuse of discretion.  An abuse of discretion is established if the agency has not proceeded in the manner required by law, the decision is not supported by the findings, or the findings are not supported by the evidence.  (CCP § 1094.5(b).) 

 

“Where the writ is issued for the purpose of inquiring into the validity of any final administrative order or decision made as the result of a proceeding in which by law a hearing is required to be given, evidence is required to be taken, and discretion in the determination of facts is vested in the inferior tribunal, corporation, board, or officer, the case shall be heard by the court sitting without a jury.” (CCP §1094.5(a).)

 

A respondent may demur to a petition for writ relief.  (See generally Woods v. Superior Court (1981) 28 Cal.3d 668, 673 (demurrer to petition for writ of administrative mandamus properly overruled where petitioner sought review of adjudicatory decision of the Department of Social Services which was alleged to be invalid because it was based on an invalid regulation).)  “All that is required is that plaintiff state facts entitling him to some type of relief, and if a cause of action for mandamus or certiorari has been stated, the general demurrer should have been overruled.”  (Scott v. City of Indian Wells (1972) 6 Cal.3d 541, 546 (plaintiff stated claim for administrative writ relief under CCP 1094.5 based on allegations that respondent issued conditional use permit for project without providing abutting landowners, including plaintiff, notice of the project and potential permit, without considering their interests and without hearing their views); see also Carloss v. County of Alameda (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 116.)

 

Here, the Petition states that relief is being sought under CCP 1094.5. (Petition, p. 1.) However, there are no allegations or indication that there was a final adjudicative action of an administrative body as required by CCP 1094.5. Petitioner’s allegations that Respondent unlawfully denied his application for proposed housing for homeless veterans does not provide a basis for bringing a Writ of Mandate under CCP 1094.5. Therefore, Court finds the Petition is uncertain and Respondent cannot reasonably respond. "If a pleading is so uncertain that a party cannot reasonably respond, the pleading is properly subject to a special demurrer." (Khoury v. Maly's of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) The inability to reasonably respond occurs when a party cannot determine which issues to admit or deny, or which claims are made against him. (Id. at 616, 618 (sustaining demurrer on the grounds that demurred cause of action was based on conclusory pleading and was simply duplicative of one of plaintiff's other causes of action).)

 

As such, the demurrer to the Petition of Writ of Mandate is SUSTAINED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Respondent’s demurrer to the Petition of Writ of Mandate is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.
