DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV34229    Hearing Date: May 24, 2024    Dept: 73

05/24/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

ANIMAL PROTECTION AND RESCUE LEAGUE, INC., et al. v. GINOR, et al. (20STCV34229)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Bryan Pease (Pease Law, APC); David Tenenbaum (Law Offices of G. David Tenenbaum); John Maher (Law Offices of John Maher)

 

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Michael Tenenbaum (The Office of Michael Tenenbaum); Thomas Rickeman (Brown Neri Smith & Khan LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’s Motion to amend complaint (filed 03/28/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion to amend Complaint is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 4, 2020, Plaintiffs Animal Protection and Rescue League, Inc. (“APRL”), and Dina Kourda (“Kourda”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint.  The operative pleading is the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) which alleges causes of action for (1) Consumer Legal Remedies Act, (2) unfair business practices, and (3) declaratory relief against Defendants Michael Ginor, Izzy Yanay, HVFG LLC, D’Artagnan, Inc., D’Artagnan, LLC, Ariane Daguin, Andy Wertheim, Bella Bella Gourmet Foods LLC, La Belle Farm, Inc., Herman Lee, French Laundry Partners, L.P., Thomas Keller, Michael’s Gourmet Pantry, Inc.,  MGP Specialty Foods, J. Michael Stamm, and Cathy Stamm, Tyler Charman, Brian Redzikowski, Matthew Spencer, Kettner House LP, SDCM Inc., and Petrossian, Inc. 

 

Plaintiffs filed the instant lawsuit to enjoin the sale of foie gras, i.e., fattened duck liver, which is obtained from forced fed duck and geese, and which violates Health & Safety Code section 25982.  Plaintiffs also seek to enjoin false and misleading statements made by Defendants regarding the foie gras.  

 

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff APRL filed a Motion to Amend the Complaint, arguing:

·       APRL moves for an order amending the operative complaint to substitute successor-in-interest Laurie Ginor for deceased Defendant Michael Ginor pursuant to CRC Rule 3.1324(d).

·       Laurie Ginor is successor in interest to Michael Ginor, her deceased spouse. (Declaration of Attorney Bryan Pease (“Pease Decl.”) ¶¶ 2-3.)

·       There has been an ongoing course of conduct which after the death of Michael Ginor in 2022 was carried on by Laurie Ginor. (Pease Decl., ¶4.) This is the conduct that Plaintiff seeks to enjoin and the owners of HVFG, LLC must continue to be named for complete relief to be granted. If the Court were only to grant relief as to HVFG, LLC, relief against that entity would not prevent Laurie Ginor from simply forming a new entity and carrying on the same unfair business practices. (Ibid.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that:

·       APRL provides no authority for its proclamation that a claim for injunctive relief somehow survives the death of a defendant. A deceased party obviously cannot engage in conduct that is “ongoing or likely to recur,” and thus the claim cannot survive against him.

·       APRL has provided no explanation as to how Laurie Ginor is Mr. Ginor’s “successor-in-interest” with respect to APRL’s claim in this action. This is not an action for damages. Ms. Ginor cannot be a successor-in-interest to the conduct of her late husband.

·       APRL has no evidence or even proposed allegations that Ms. Ginor could be liable under the UCL as the “successor-in-interest” to Mr. Ginor.

·       APRL also fails to explain why it waited nearly 17 months to seek to amend its already twice-amended complaint.

In reply, APRL argues that:

·       The amendment is necessary for complete relief.

·       Laurie Ginor is the successor in interest to Michael Ginor and is continuing the business practices at issue.

·       Plaintiff does not seek to file a whole new amended complaint. Plaintiff merely seeks to amend the current operative complaint by adding the words “LAURIE STRAW GINOR, successor in interest to” before the words “MICHAEL GINOR” where it appears in just two places. This would substitute Laurie Ginor for deceased Michael Ginor and ensure that any relief ordered by the Court applies to both current owners of HVFG, LLC (the other owner being coDefendant Izzy Yanay.) Plaintiff asserts that the individual Defendants are directly and personally responsible for engaging in the unfair business practices alleged and must be enjoined.

·       Defendants argue Plaintiff’s motion should have been brought sooner. Had Plaintiff done so, Defendants surely would have argued it was unseemly at such a time and still accuse Plaintiff of “harassing the Ginor family,” this time while they were grieving.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

A court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)  Such amendment may occur “at any time before or after commencement of trial.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.)  The court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendments of the pleadings.  (Nestle v. City of Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.)¿ “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)¿ The declaration must also specify the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)¿ “An amendment to a pleading must not be made by alterations on the face of a pleading except by permission of the court. All alterations must be initialed by the court or the clerk.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(d).)

¿ 

If the party seeking the amendment has needlessly delayed, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the court has the discretion to deny leave to amend.¿ (See Hirsa v. Super. Ct. (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)¿ Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.¿ (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿¿ ¿¿ 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Here, APRL moves to amend the Second Amended Complaint to substitute the successor in interest to Defendant Michael Ginor, who is now deceased.

 

In support, APRL submits the declaration of its attorney, Bryan Pease, who states “Defendant Michael Ginor was the spouse of Laurie Ginor” and that Ms. Ginor’s Facebook page admitted “that she is the co-owner of Hudson Valley Foie Gras, which is the trade name of Defendant HVFG, LLC.” (Pease Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.) However, the Court notes that APRL provides no explanation as to how Laurie Ginor is Mr. Ginor’s “successor-in-interest” with respect to APRL’s claim in this action.

 

In addition, APRL also fails to comply with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b) which requires the declaration to provide when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered and why the request was not made earlier. APRL does not explain why it waited nearly 17 months to seek to amend its Complaint, after the close of discovery and three months before trial.

Further, APRL does not cite to any authority to support its contention that a claim for injunctive relief survives the death of a defendant. APRL cites to Herring v. Peterson (1981) 116 Cal. App. 3d 608, 611-612, in support of the statement that “[w]here a defendant dies and no personal representative is substituted, any judgment rendered is in excess of the court's jurisdiction to try the case.” However, in Herring, the court discussed that a cause of action for personal injuries survives the death of the defendant. Here, the claim is for injunctive relief and APRL does not provide any support that such a claim could survive against a successor-in-interest. Thus, the Court does not find APRL has established that amending the Second Amended Complaint is warranted.

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s motion to amend the second amended complaint is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s motion to amend the second amended complaint is DENIED.

THE MACHINE GROUP, INC. v. NC DYNAMICS LLC (21STCV29644)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant:  Albro Lundy III (Baker, Burton & Lundy PC)

 

Counsel for Defendant/Cross-Complainant:  Jeffrey Goodfried (Saul Ewin LLP)

 

(1)   DEFENDANT/CROSS-COMPLAINANT’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE SECOND AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT (filed 02/29/2024)

 

(2)   PLAINTIFF’s Motion to COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION SET 4 (filed 04/02/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s motion for leave to file the Second Amended Cross-Complaint is GRANTED. Defendant/Cross-Complainant is ordered to file the proposed Second Amended Cross-Complaint in ten (10) days. 

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s motion to compel is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 11, 2021, Plaintiff The Machine Group (“TMG”) filed this action against Defendant NC Dynamics.

 

This case concerns a contract dispute for the sale and underperformance of specialized machinery.  NC Dynamics, the Defendant/Cross-Complainant, is a full-service manufacturing facility that manufactures products for aerospace companies.  (FACC ¶ 5.)  According to NC Dynamics, NC Dynamics took steps to upgrade the manufacturing process in regard to an existing contract it had with Bombardier Aerostructures (“Bombardier”).  (FACC ¶ 8.)  As part of that process, NC Dynamics requested bids from three companies to sell manufacturing equipment to NC Dynamics.  (FACC ¶ 9.)  NC Dynamics also requested the companies to conduct time studies to establish the running time for the construction of specific titanium parts for the Bombardier contract.  (FACC ¶ 10.)   

 

 TMG, the Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant in this case, was one of the three companies to submit a bid, but the only company that offered to sell an upcoming machine from another company called OKK.  (FACC ¶ 9.)  OKK conducted the time study. (FACC ¶ 11, 14.)  NC Dynamics contend that TMG ultimately won the bid based on a guarantee made by TMG President Richard Wackeen (“Wackeen”) that the parts would be manufactured in ten hours with the OKK machine (FACC ¶ 11) and also upon the misrepresentation that OKK was aware that Wackeen was guaranteeing a ten hour running time (FACC ¶¶ 12, 16).   

 

NC Dynamics and TMG entered into two contracts for the purchase of two OKK machines.  (FACC ¶¶ 17, 18.)  The machines failed to construct the parts in the contracted ten-hour running time, and multiple attempts were made to reduce the running time.  (FACC ¶¶ 20-23.)  Because of the slow running time, NC Dynamics contend that they were forced to purchase a third machine from TMG to meet its production needs for the Bombardier contract.  (FACC ¶ 26.)  NC Dynamics further allege that TMG continued to guarantee that the OKK machines would achieve the ten-hour running time despite knowing the OKK machine could do so.  (FACC ¶¶ 29-30.)  NC Dynamics later learned that six weeks before the parties entered into the third contract, a TMG manager named Jim McCulloch (“McCulloch”) admitted in an email that the OKK machines would never achieve a running time.  (FACC ¶ 29.)  McCulloch was present to witness the final run of the OKK machines, which at the lowest point exceeded 24 hours.  (FACC ¶ 32.)  As a result of the running time, NC Dynamics contend that they lost Bombardier, and others, as a current and future customer.  (FACC ¶ 31.) 

 

On August 1, 2022, NC Dynamics alleged the following causes of action in its First Amended Cross Complaint: (1) breach of contract; (2) fraud in the inducement; (3) negligence; (4) intentional interference with contractual relations; (5) intentional interference with prospective economic relations; (6) negligent interference with prospective economic relations; and (7) breach of warranty of fitness for particular purpose.

 

On February 2, 2024, NC Dynamics filed a Motion for leave to file second amended Cross-Complaint, arguing:

·       Recently, in January 2024, NC Dynamics conducted the deposition of the US subsidiary of the manufacturer, Nidec OKK USA Corporation (“OKK”). OKK’s recent deposition revealed that contrary to NC Dynamics’ initial understanding of the facts, OKK became aware that its dealer, TMG, guaranteed the 10 hour run time under the Guaranteed Time Provision, but knew that the guaranteed time could never be achieved because TMG and its agent, 5th Axis, committed a crucial error in setting up and programming the OKK Machine.

·       OKK fraudulently concealed TMG’s gross negligence from NC Dynamics and instead allowed NC Dynamics to purchase the Third OKK Machine and proceed down the doomed path of expending more time, money, and labor in attempting to make the vertical design work, all the while further damaging NC Dynamics’ contractual relationship with its customer, Bombardier Belfast. Beyond that, OKK conspired with TMG to attempt to blame NC Dynamics for the OKK Machines’ failure to meet the guaranteed run time, all of which was done to force NC Dynamics to pay the full price without giving NC Dynamics any of the promised discounts.

·       NC Dynamics is seeking leave of Court to file its Second Amended Cross-Complaint, which asserts claims against OKK in addition to TMG.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

On April 2, 2024, TMG filed a motion to compel further discovery responses, arguing:

·       The discovery at issue seeks programming, correspondence, and related items regarding the use of the HMX-8000s by NC Dynamics, regarding the programming of part 7108, programming of other parts on the machines, regarding plans to purchase hard-metal manufacturing machines, regarding the capabilities of the machines, and regarding fixturing of the machines at issue.

·       Initial responses by the Defendant were boilerplate objections to each of 19 Requests for Production.

·       Further Responses were eventually provided, with a production on February 15, which limited the scope of the production to documents that NC Dynamics believes are relevant to Bombardier Belfast and to making part 7108 on the HMX-8000s. NC Dynamics now refuses to provide documents concerning certain issues.

·       The discovery is critical to learn relevant information concerning the Defendant’s statements about the machines at issue, as well as how they manufactured other parts for other customers on those machines.

·       TMG requests sanctions.

 

In opposition, NC Dynamics argues that:

·       TMG’s motion is time barred. The Court lacks jurisdiction to consider TMG’s late-filed motion.

·       TMG’s motion should also be denied for failing to meet and confer regarding all disputes raised in its motion.

·       Further, the documents sought by TMG are irrelevant or have already been produced

In reply, TMG argues that:

·       The timing of a motion regarding deficient document production should be calculated from the date of the deficient document production.  

·       TMG had satisfied its meet and confer obligations.

·       TMG maintains the requested documents are relevant.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     NC Dynamic’s Motion to Amend

 

a.     Discussion

 

“A court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)  Such amendment may occur “at any time before or after commencement of trial.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.)  The court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendments of the pleadings.  (Nestle v. City of Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.)¿ “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)¿ The declaration must also specify the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)¿ 

 

If the party seeking the amendment has needlessly delayed, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the court has the discretion to deny leave to amend.¿ (See Hirsa v. Super. Ct. (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)¿ Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.¿ (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿¿ ¿¿ 

 

Here, NC Dynamics seeks to amend its First Amended Cross-Complaint to asserts claims against OKK in addition to TMG.

 

Preliminarily, the Court finds NC Dynamics has sufficiently complied with the various procedural requirements outlined within California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324 (“Rule 3.1324”).  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324.)  Initially, as enumerated within subdivision (a) of Rule 3.1324, NC Dynamics has properly included a copy of the proposed Second Amended Cross-Complaint with the present Motion, and has specified the allegations which NC Dynamics proposes to be added to the previous Complaint.  (Id., Rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(1), (a)(2).)  Further, as required within subdivision (b) of Rule 3.1324, NC Dynamics has filed a Supporting Declaration.

 

In NC Dyamics’ declaration, it asserts that in January 2024, NC Dynamics conducted the deposition of the US subsidiary of OKK. OKK’s recent deposition revealed that contrary to NC Dynamics’ initial understanding of the facts, OKK in fact had actual knowledge that TMG agreed to the Guaranteed Time Provision and guaranteed that the OKK Machines could machine the Specific Part within the 10 hour run time. (Goodfried Decl., ¶ 9.) However, instead of promptly warning NC Dynamics of the gross negligence being committed by TMG, OKK allowed NC Dynamics to purchase the Third Machine causing NC Dynamics to suffer years of delay, incur hundreds of thousands of dollars in employee time and costs, and ultimately proximately causing NC Dynamics to lose its contract with Bombardier Belfast. (Goodfried Decl., ¶ 12.) Given these recent revelations, NC Dynamics seeks to assert claims against OKK for conspiring with TMG to defraud NC Dynamics, wrongfully depriving NC Dynamics of its contractual discounts, and tortiously interfering with its business relationship with Bombardier Belfast. The Court finds NC Dynamics has sufficiently specified the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and the reason why amendment was not requested earlier. 

 

No opposition has been filed. The trial date in this matter is set for February 10, 2025. The Court does not find the proposed amendment would delay trial or prejudice the opposing party.

 

For the foregoing reasons, NC Dynamic’s motion for leave to file the Second Amended Cross-Complaint is GRANTED.

 

B.     TMG’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

A party may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (a).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).) 

¿¿¿ 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)

 

b.     Timeliness

 

As an initial matter, NC Dynamics argues that TMG’s Motion to Compel is time-barred and the Court lacks jurisdiction to consider TMG’s late filed motion.

 

Notice of a motion to compel further responses must be served within 45 days after verified responses were served (extended per the method of service), unless the parties agree in writing to extend the time. (Code Civ. Proc. §§¿2030.300(c), 2031.310(c).) Noncompliance with these requirements waives the right to compel further responses. (Ibid.) The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional, and the court has no authority to grant a late-filed motion. (Sexton v. Superior Ct. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1409–10.)

 

Here, NC Dynamics served its Verified Supplemental Responses to TMG’s 4th Set of Document Requests on January 31, 2024. Thus, NC Dynamics asserts that TMG had 45 days after to file its Motion to Compel, by March 19, 2024. However, TMG did not file its motion until April 2, 2024. Thus, TMG’s motion is untimely.

In opposition, TMG argues that the 45-day deadline should run from the date of production, as opposed to the date of the verified written supplemental responses. Here, NC Dynamics served its further production on February 15, 2024. Therefore, TMG argues it had until April 2, 2024 to file its motion.

However, the Court does not find TMG’s argument persuasive. Not only does TMG fail to cite to any authority or case law in support of its contention, but California Civil Procedure Code § 2031.310(c) specifies that motions to compel further responses must be served within 45 days after verified responses were served. Here, verified responses were served on January 31, 2024. Further, it is undisputed that the parties did not extend the motion to compel further deadline. Thus, the Court finds TMG’s motion is untimely. Since the Court finds TMG’s motion is time-barred, the Court does not discuss the other arguments raised in the motion.

Accordingly, the Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s motion for leave to file the Second Amended Cross-Complaint is GRANTED. Defendant/Cross-Complainant is ordered to file the proposed Second Amended Cross-Complaint in ten (10) days. 

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s motion to compel is DENIED.

NEUMAN, et al. v. RYLAND (22STCV08019)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Fred Szkolnik (Law Offices of Fred Szkolnik); Vadim Frish (Frish Law Group, APLC)

 

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Michael Tenenbaum (The Office of Michael Tenenbaum); Thomas Rickeman (Brown Neri Smith & Khan LLP)

 

Motion to COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH SUBPOENA FOR RECORDS OF THIRD PARTY WITNESS (filed 12/21/2023)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Motion to Compel Compliance with Subpoena for Records of Third Party Witness is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This case arises out of a complaint for elder abuse filed by Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Claudia Neuman against Defendant and Cross-Complainant Evie Ryland (“Ryland”). In the Complaint,   Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Claudia Neuman alleges, among other things, that Ryland exerted undue influence on Marian Neuman (“Decedent”), Claudia Neuman’s stepmother, by secretly videotaping Decedent while intoxicated in an attempt to record the Decedent stating she would leave Ryland in Decedent’s will as a beneficiary.  

 

On April 15, 2022, Ryland filed a Cross-Complaint against Claudia Neuman, Victoria Talbot Neuman (collectively, the “Neumans”), and Roes 1 to 10, alleging that she had served without pay as a caregiver to Decedent for over four years based on an agreement that the Decedent would provide for Ryland in Decedent’s will.  The Cross-Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) Services Rendered; (2) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (3) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress.¿ 

 

On December 12, 2023, the Neumans filed a Motion to Compel Compliance with Subpoena for Records to Third Party Witness, arguing:

·       Ryland claims emotional distress damages, and so the Neumans subpoenaed records from the Kohan Foundation who allegedly provided psychological care.

·       Ryland failed to use proper outlets to oppose the subpoena, but during an informal meet and confer process, the Neumans’ attorneys agreed to allow Ryland’s counsel to have a first look at the subpoenaed records and to redact information which, in good faith, is deemed to be subject to protection, constituting psychotherapy notes or other statements that are not discoverable or relevant to the subject matter of the action.

·       The Neumans argue that Ryland abused the process by making pervasive redactions which prevent the use of the records at trial. Because Ryland and her attorneys have acted in bad faith in this matter, the Neumans have no choice but to seek to enforce their duly issued and duly served subpoena against the Kohan Foundation itself.

 

In opposition, Ryland argues that:

·       Cross-Defendants' motion for sanctions and compliance should be denied it is entirely on the grounds that it is in conflict with the informal discovery conference agreement on January 17, 2024 where the parties agreed to permit counsel for a first look at the notes submitted by the Kohan Foundation. (Wolfe Decl., 2.)

·       Kohan Foundation complied with the subpoena. As agreed by the parties, Ryland counsel informed the Cross-Defendants that said records were subject to substantial redaction and forwarded those redacted documents to opposing counsel on January 29, 2024. (Wolfe Decl., 4, Exhibit "C".) All of the redactions pertained to either information protected by individuals' privacy rights or unrelated to the claims in the litigation.

In reply, Neumans argue that:

·       The Neumans recognize that the Kohan Foundation did provide records pursuant to an agreement to Ryland’s attorneys. The Neumans therefore withdraw their sanctions request against the Kohan Foundation. The Neumans still maintain their efforts to enforce the subpoena.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

“[D]iscovery from a nonparty may be obtained only by ‘deposition subpoena’ (§ 2020.010, subd. (b)).” (Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 123, 130, italics removed.)  

 

“If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd.  (a).)  “This motion shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (b); Board of Registered Nursing v. Superior Court of Orange County (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1032 [explaining that objections by a nonparty constitute “the record of the deposition” and, therefore, a party has 60 days after receiving the objections to file a motion to compel].) 

 

“A business records subpoena directs the nonparty’s custodian of records (or other qualified person) to deliver the requested documents (in person, by messenger, or by mail) to the ‘deposition officer’ specified in the subpoena.  (§ 2020.430, subd. (a).)”  (Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader, supra, 156 Cal.App.4th at p. 131.)   

 

“A provider of health care, health care service plan, or contractor shall not disclose medical information regarding a patient of the provider of health care or an enrollee or subscriber of a health care service plan without first obtaining an authorization, except as provided in subdivision (b) or (c).”  (Civ. Code, § 56.10, subd.  (a).)  A provider of health care, a health care service plan, or a contractor shall disclose medical information if the disclosure is compelled “by a party to a proceeding before a court … pursuant to a … subpoena duces tecum, notice to appear served pursuant to Section 1987 of the Code of Civil Procedure, or any provision authorizing discovery in a proceeding before a court ….” (Civ. Code, § 56, subd.  (b)(1).) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, subdivision (a), provides that if a subpoena requires the production of documents, the Court may, upon motion reasonably made by a party, make an order “directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare.”  (See also Civ. Code Proc., § 2025.480, subd.  (a) [providing that if a deponent fails to produce any document “that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that… production”].)  

 

“A deposition subpoena that commands only the production of business records for copying shall designate the business records to be produced either by specifically describing each individual item or by reasonably particularizing each category of item, and shall specify the form in which any electronically stored information is to be produced, if a particular form is desired.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.410, subd.  (a).) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer

 

A motion compelling compliance with a deposition subpoena “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd.  (b).) 

 

Here, counsel for the Neumans submitted a meet and confer declaration stating that counsel met and conferred about the Kohan Foundation’s compliance with the subpoena, before the Neumans received the redacted records. (Ames Decl., ¶¶ 6-16.) Thus, it does not appear the parties met and conferred to solve the issues regarding the redacted records before filing the motion as required. The Court does not find the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations. However, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

 

The Neumans request the Court to compel nonparty Kohar Foundation (“Kohar”) to comply with the Neumans’ subpoena demanding the production of Ryland’s records.  

 

The Neumans submit their counsel’s declaration in support of their motion attesting to the following facts. On October 17, 2023, a process server personally served the subpoena on the Kohan Foundation with a production date of November 3, 2023. (Ames Decl., ¶ 5.) The same day, the parties agreed that any records produced pursuant to the subpoenas be subject to a stipulated protective order. (Ames Decl., ¶ 6.) On November 21, 2023, Ryland’s attorneys sent a letter to the Kohan Foundation asking for voluntary compliance with the subpoena. (Ames Decl., ¶ 8.) The parties further met and conferred and agreed to limit the records to September 3, 2018. (Ames Decl., ¶ 13.) On December 19, 2023, Ryland’s attorney stated in an email that he left a message with the Kohan Foundation directing the release of the records pursuant to the agreed upon limitation. (Ames Decl., ¶ 14.) On December 20, 2023, the Neumans’ attorney called the Kohan Foundation to request compliance with the subpoena, however, someone who claimed to be the director of Kohar stated they would not comply with the subpoena. (Ames Decl., ¶ 17.)

 

In opposition, Ryland contends that Kohan already complied with the subpoena. Ryland argues that the Neuman’s motion should be denied because it is in conflict with the parties’ agreement. Ryland’s counsel submits a declaration stating that on January 17, 2024, counsel attended an informal hearing with the court where it was agreed by counsel to permit Ryland’s counsel to have a first look at the notes of Kohan Foundation for the purpose of possible redaction. (Wolfe Decl., ¶ 2.) On January 25, 2024, Ryland's counsel received the treatment records from the Kohan Foundation. (Wolfe Decl., ¶ 3.) On January 29, 2024, said redacted treatment records were transmitted to the Neumans’ counsel. (Wolfe Decl., ¶ 4.) Although the Neumans argue that the redactions on the records were pervasive, Ryland asserts that all of the redactions pertained to either information protected by individuals' privacy rights or were unrelated to the claims in the litigation.

 

Thus, the Court finds that Kohar properly complied with the Neumans’ subpoena and the requested records were sent to the Neumans with redactions pursuant to the parties agreement.

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Neumans’ motion to compel is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Motion to Compel Compliance with Subpoena for Records of Third Party Witness is DENIED.

HOMELAND INSURANCE COMPANY OF NEW YORK v. WESCO INSURANCE COMPANY and

WESTCHESTER SURPLUS LINES INSURANCE COMPANY (22STCV16106)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant/opposing party:  Christian Jones, Sara Menton (Intact US Coverage Litigation Group); David Boren (Ritholz Levy Fields LLP)

 

Counsel for Defendant/Cross-Complainant Wesco/moving party:  Christopher Hicks (Hirsch Closson APLC)

 

(1)   PLAINTIFF/CROSS-DEFENDANT HOMELAND’S Motion to amend complaint (filed 04/25/2024)

(2)   DEFENDANT/CROSS-COMPLAINANT WESCO’S MOTION TO STAY (filed 05/01/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Homeland’s motion to amend the first amended complaint is GRANTED. Homeland is ordered to file the proposed First Amended Complaint in ten (10) days. 

 

Wesco’s motion to stay is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This is an insurance coverage dispute between insurance carriers which issued commercial general liability policies to the same insured, Permacity Construction Corp. (“Permacity”), during different time periods between January 7, 2013, and June 19, 2017. A lawsuit was filed on January 26, 2018, Sherlock, et al. v. Permacity Construction Corp., et al., Case No. BC 692058 pending in Los Angeles Superior Court (the “Sherlock Lawsuit”), against Permacity and other contractors for purported construction defects and resulting damages to the claimant’s property.

 

On May 13, 2022, Plaintiff Homeland Insurance Company of New York (“Homeland”) filed this action against Defendants Wesco and Westchester Surplus Lines (“Westchester”) seeking declaratory relief requiring them to defend Permacity in the underlying lawsuit and to contribute equally to Permacity’s defense.

 

On July 25, 2022, Wesco filed a cross-complaint against Homeland for declaratory relief and alleging that Homeland is not entitled to recover any amounts from Wesco to be paid towards the defense or indemnity of Permacity under Homeland’s insurance policy.

 

On April 24, 2024, Homeland filed a Motion to Amend the Complaint, arguing:

·       The Court has confirmed that Wesco is obligated to share equally in the cost of defending Permacity. However, in its Order dated March 19, 2024, the Court ruled that it could not grant monetary relief in favor of Homeland as the case was currently pled due to the absence of a cause of action for contribution.

·       Homeland now seeks to amend its Complaint to include a contribution cause of action as well as an equitable indemnity claim so that the Court can adjudicate fully the rights and obligations of the parties in this lawsuit without need for additional, duplicative litigation.

·       Homeland’s proposed First Amended Complaint also deletes references to, and claims previously asserted against, former Defendant Westchester Surplus Lines Insurance Company (“Westchester”), who was dismissed from this lawsuit as the result of a settlement

·       Wesco will not be prejudiced by the proposed amendment because it eventually will be required to face an adjudication regarding its financial obligations, and judicial economy favors that the adjudication occur in this lawsuit rather than in a separate, duplicative action. See Lincoln Property Co., 137 Cal.App.4th at 916 (a court’s “discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit” (quoting Kittredge Sports Co., 213 Cal.App.3d at 1047)

 

In opposition, Wesco argues that:

·       Homeland unjustifiably delayed in seeking leave to amend. Homeland waited three months to file this motion after Wesco notified Homeland that it must plead a cause of action for contribution to recover a specific sum in this action. Homeland fails to specify why it didn’t seek amendment earlier.

·       Leave to amend may be denied if there is prejudice to the opposing party, such as delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation. Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 390. Here, it is the significant increase in the costs of preparation based on the new issues that will prejudice Wesco if the FAC is permitted.

·       If the FAC is filed, Homeland will need to reopen discovery to allow Wesco to retain an expert witness, propound discovery and take depositions stemming from the claimed damages.

·       Homeland should file a new properly-pled lawsuit to seek contribution when the claims are fully ripe, at the proper time.

In reply, Homeland argues that:

·       Homeland did not unreasonably delay in seeking to amend its Complaint once the Court ruled on March 19, 2024, that it agreed with Wesco’s position that an equitable contribution claim should be pled expressly. The day after that ruling, Homeland reserved the earliest available hearing date on the Court’s calendar for its Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint, which was May 21, 2024.2 Given that the parties litigated this matter and conducted discovery throughout the life of this case as if the issue of defense cost allocation would be decided, there was no reason for Homeland to seek an earlier amendment

 

On May 1, 2024, Wesco filed a motion to stay, arguing:

·       The remaining claim for declaratory relief concerning the duty to indemnify is not ripe. Since the Court cannot rule further until the Underlying Action is fully resolved, a stay of this case is warranted and necessary to promote judicial economy and avoid duplicative litigation. the Court will not need to use its valuable time and resources conducting periodic status conferences or hearings in this action and can instead focus its attention on cases that are ready for trial.

·       Even if Homeland’s motion to amend is granted, a stay is warranted.

o   Homeland has filed a motion for leave to file an amended complaint in this action to include claims for contribution and equitable indemnity. If a stay is not imposed, any monetary judgment Homeland might obtain cannot possibly address the full extent of any claimed damages given the ongoing nature of the Underlying Action—i.e., there is no possibility for this Court to award complete relief until and unless the Underlying Action is fully and finally resolved.

 

In opposition, Homeland argues that:

·       A stay of this lawsuit would only be appropriate if Wesco could show that deciding the amount Homeland is entitled to recover from Wesco could involve making fact determinations that might conflict with fact issues that must be decided in the Sherlock Lawsuit

·       Wesco’s position that Homeland must wait for the conclusion of the Sherlock Lawsuit, and only then can it pursue a full adjudication of Wesco’s defense cost obligations, ignores that the very purpose of declaratory judgment actions is to allow courts to efficiently disputes that involve ongoing wrongs.

In reply, Wesco argues that:

·       The parties agree that Wesco has a duty to defend Permacity in the Underlying Action

·       In order for Homeland to recover a specific amount from Wesco relative to the defense obligation, Homeland will have the burden of proving that the amounts it has incurred to defend Permacity are both reasonable and necessary. (Aerojet-General Corp. v. Transport Indem. Co. (1997) 17 Ca1.4th 38, 64.) Thus, the Court cannot make a determination regarding the "reasonable and necessary" issue with respect to those costs which have not yet been incurred

·       Even if Homeland is permitted to belatedly amend its complaint to add the missing claims for contribution and equitable indemnity, such claims would similarly not be ripe until and unless the Underlying Action is fully and finally resolved because the amount of defense costs at issue continues to accrue

·       If the parties were to proceed to trial to adjudicate Homeland's presently unpled cause of action for contribution before the Underlying Action is final, any monetary judgment Homeland might obtain could not possibly address the full extent of any claimed damages given the ongoing nature of the Underlying Action. The need for a stay of this action is evident

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Homeland’s Motion to Amend

 

a.     Request for Judicial Notice

 

Homeland requests that the court take judicial notice of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Wesco Insurance Company’s Memorandum in Opposition to Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Homeland Insurance Company of New York’s Motion for Summary Adjudication.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)¿¿ The Court may take judicial notice of its own file. (See Stepan v. Garcia (1974) 43 Cal.App.3d 497, 500.)

 

Homeland’s Request for Judicial Notice is GRANTED.  

 

b.     Discussion

 

“A court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)  Such amendment may occur “at any time before or after commencement of trial.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.)  The court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendments of the pleadings.  (Nestle v. City of Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.)¿ “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)¿ The declaration must also specify the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)¿ 

 

If the party seeking the amendment has needlessly delayed, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the court has the discretion to deny leave to amend.¿ (See Hirsa v. Super. Ct. (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)¿ Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.¿ (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿¿ ¿¿ 

 

Here, Homeland seeks to amend its Complaint to include a contribution cause of action as well as an equitable indemnity claim. Homeland also seeks to delete references to, and claims previously asserted against, former Defendant Westchester who was dismissed from this lawsuit as the result of a settlement.

 

Preliminarily, the Court finds Homeland has sufficiently complied with the various procedural requirements outlined within California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324 (“Rule 3.1324”).  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324.)  Initially, as enumerated within subdivision (a) of Rule 3.1324, Homeland has properly included a copy of the proposed First Amended Complaint with the present Motion, and has specified the allegations which Homeland proposes to be added to the previous Complaint.  (Id., Rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(1), (a)(2).)  Further, as required within subdivision (b) of Rule 3.1324, Homeland has filed a Supporting Declaration which specifies the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper and the reason why amendment was not requested earlier. 

 

Wesco argues that Homeland unduly delayed in seeking to amend the Complaint and that Wesco will be prejudiced by the amendment. However, the Court disagrees. Homeland asserts that the day after the Court’s March 19, 2024 ruling stating that Homeland’s equitable contribution claim should be clearly pled as a cause of action, Homeland reserved the earliest available hearing date on the Court’s calendar for its Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint. Wesco also argues it will be prejudiced by the increase in the costs of preparation of the new claims. However as Homeland contends, even though the contribution cause of action was not expressly pled, the parties had already engaged in discovery as to the defense costs they had incurred in defending the underlying case. Further, Wesco will eventually be required to reach an adjudication as to its financial obligations and judicial economy favors that the adjudication occur in this lawsuit rather than in a separate, duplicative action. See Lincoln Property Co., 137 Cal.App.4th at 916 (a court’s “discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit” (quoting Kittredge Sports Co., 213 Cal.App.3d at 1047)

 

For the foregoing reasons, Homeland’s motion to amend the first amended complaint is GRANTED.

 

B.     Wesco’s Motion to Stay

 

a.     Request for Judicial Notice

 

Wesco requests that the court take judicial notice of the March 20, 2024 Notice of Minute Order from March 12, 2024 Status Conference including Minute Order.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)¿¿ The Court may take judicial notice of its own file. (See Stepan v. Garcia (1974) 43 Cal.App.3d 497, 500.)

 

Wesco’s Request for Judicial Notice is GRANTED.  

 

b.     Discussion

 

Courts have broad discretion to stay cases in the interest of efficiency and justice. Every court has the “inherent power, in its discretion, to stay proceedings when such a stay will accommodate the ends of justice.” (See St. Paul Fire and Marine Insurance Company v. AmerisourceBergen Corp. (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 1, 6-7.)  The Code of Civil Procedure further empowers every court to “provide for the orderly conduct of proceedings before it.” (C.C.P. §128(a)(3); see C.C.P. §187 [conferring on courts and judicial officers “all the means necessary” to carry out their jurisdiction].) 

 

Wesco requests the Court stay this action until the underlying case is fully resolved. On March 19, 2024, the Court stated in its Minute Order “[b]oth parties agree that indemnification cannot yet be adjudicated because the underlying case is not final.” (Court’s 03/19/24 Minute Order.) Further, the Court stated that “without the underlying case final and cause(s) of action for equitable contribution, the court cannot grant further relief in this action.” (Ibid.)

 

Homeland argues that a stay of this lawsuit would only be appropriate if Wesco could show that deciding the amount Homeland is entitled to recover from Wesco could involve making fact determinations that might conflict with fact issues that must be decided in the underlying case. However, defense costs continue to accrue in the underlying case. Therefore, the Court cannot award a specific monetary sum until the underlying case is resolved. Since Homeland’s claims for and equitable indemnity and contribution are not yet ripe, a stay of the action is warranted in the interests of justice and judicial economy and efficiency

Wesco’s motion to stay is GRANTED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Homeland’s motion to amend the first amended complaint is GRANTED. Homeland is ordered to file the proposed First Amended Complaint in ten (10) days. 

 

Wesco’s motion to stay is GRANTED.
