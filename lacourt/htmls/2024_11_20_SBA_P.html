DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV01757    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

 

Moving Parties: Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc.                    

Responding Party: Plaintiff Taiwo Alabi

 

RULING

 

Having considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply, the motion to compel binding arbitration is GRANTED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiff Taiwo Alabi (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. (“Defendant”) and DOES 1-10, inclusive alleging five causes of action for violations of Statutory Obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act Civ. Code sections 1790-1795.8.

 

On July 5, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion. On November 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. Defendant filed a reply on November 13, 2024.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

On September 19, 2023, Plaintiff purchased a Tesla Model Y (the “Vehicle”) manufactured by Defendant. (Complaint, ¶ 5.)

 

Plaintiff received an express written warranty which stated that if a defect developed during the warranty period, Plaintiff could deliver the Vehicle to a repair shop to make repairs. (Complaint, ¶ 9.)

 

During the warranty period, several defects developed. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) However, Defendant failed to repair the Vehicle after several attempts. (Complaint, ¶ 14.)

           

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) California law favors enforcement of valid arbitration agreements. (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 97.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-357.)       

 

The Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) applies to any contract evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce which contains an arbitration clause. (Wolls v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) Section 2 of the FAA provides that arbitration provisions shall be enforced, save upon grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. (Ibid.) A state court may refuse to enforce an arbitration clause on the basis of generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability. (Ibid.) A state court, however, may not defeat an arbitration clause by applying state laws applicable only to arbitration provisions. (Ibid.)  

 

For the FAA to apply, a contract must involve interstate commerce. (Ibid.) When it applies, the FAA preempts any state law rule that stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of the FAA’s objectives. (Carbajal v. CWPSC, Inc. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 227, 238.) A party asserting FAA preemption bears the burden to present evidence establishing a contract with the arbitration provision affects interstate commerce, and the failure to do so renders the FAA inapplicable. (Ibid.) Evidence must be presented, in the form of declarations or other evidence, that establishes that the contract affects interstate commerce.  (Ibid.) “[S]ince arbitration is a matter of contract, the FAA also applies if it is so stated in the agreement.” (Davis v. Shiekh Shoes, LLC (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 956, 963.)          

 

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b).) Thus, in assessing a motion to compel arbitration, the moving party “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the [motion] bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284, citation omitted.)        

“If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests judicial notice of the complaint filed by Plaintiff. The court may take notice of its own file, pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (d).

 

Defendant’s request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff consented to the terms of an online Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“Order Agreement”) and a Retail Installment Sales Contract (“Sale Contract”), both of which including arbitration provisions. Both the FAA and California law require Plaintiff to arbitrate this dispute.

 

Opposing Party’s Argument

 

The Order Agreement has an opt-out clause, and it is the controlling document. Further, Plaintiff argues that the arbitration clauses are unconscionable and unenforceable.

 

Reply Argument

 

The arbitration agreement should be enforced under the terms of the Order Agreement, as it is not unconscionable. Further, Plaintiff’s discussions of a manufacturer’s ability to enforce an arbitration provision with a third-party buyer is irrelevant as Defendant deals with the customer directly, not through dealerships.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

In support of the motion, Defendant submits the Declaration of Raymond Kim (“Kim”). Kim declares that when Plaintiff purchased the Vehicle, he signed two agreements containing arbitration provisions. (Kim Decl., Exh. 1 and 2.) The Order Agreement contains an arbitration provision on page 3, which provides Plaintiff with the opportunity to opt out, but Plaintiff did not exercise that option. (Kim Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff also signed the Sale Contract, which has an arbitration on page 5. (Kim Decl., ¶¶ 8, 12.)

 

The Order Agreement’s Arbitration provision provides that the parties agree that if a dispute arises between the parties and it is not resolved within 60 days, the parties agree to an arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association. Any fees incurred are to be paid by Tesla Motors, Inc. (Kim Decl., ¶ 6.) The Sale Contract’s arbitration provision provides that any dispute arising from the sale and purchase of the Vehicle; Plaintiff’s credit application; or the condition of the Vehicle is to be resolved in binding arbitration. (Kim Decl., ¶ 12.) The provision also states that the arbitration agreement is governed by the FAA.

 

The court notes that the Order Agreement only shows that Plaintiff was the customer and does not contain his signature. The Sale Contract, however, does show that it was signed by the Plaintiff. The court cannot bind the Plaintiff to an arbitration provision that he did not sign. Accordingly, the court will determine whether the parties have entered a binding arbitration agreement under the Sale Contract alone.

 

The court finds that with the Sale Contract, Defendant has met its burden of proving that an arbitration agreement exists between the parties. Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to establish any facts necessary to show that the agreement should not be enforced.

 

B.  Are the Express Warranties Separate from the Order Agreement and Sales Contract

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant cannot enforce the arbitration provisions, as this conflict arises out of a warranty, which is separate from the two agreements. Plaintiff cites to various cases in support, but as Defendant notes, these cases deal with a manufacturer trying to enforce an arbitration agreement between the purchaser and dealer. Defendant sells its cars directly to customers, and any agreements are signed directly between Defendant and the buyer.

 

Accordingly, the cases cited by Plaintiff are not persuasive or instructive in the instant matter.

 

C.  Grounds for Compelling Arbitration

 

The court finds that the language of the Sale Contract’s arbitration provision covers this dispute, as it arises from the condition of the vehicle. Plaintiff argues that it is procedurally unconscionable to compel arbitration because the Order Agreement was presented on a take-it-or-leave it basis, and Plaintiff had no opportunity to renegotiate the terms as it was a form contract. Plaintiff also asserts that the Order Agreement is substantively unconscionable because it requires the parties to exclusively submit to arbitration through the American Arbitration Association (the “AAA”).

 

In opposition, Defendant argues that the Order Agreement contained an opt-out provision, which Plaintiff did not exercise. Further, California Courts have held that form contracts are not inherently procedurally unconscionable, absent evidence that the plaintiff was unable to obtain a car elsewhere. (Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 931.) Here, Plaintiff is unable to present evidence that he would have been unable to obtain a comparable car that was not subject to such an arbitration clause. The Order Agreement also lacks substantive unconscionability because its terms provide for a selection of a neutral arbitrator through the AAA. California courts have already held that such a provision is not substantively unconscionable. (Chavarria v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (9th Cir. 2013) 733 F.3d 916, 923.)

 

First, the court notes that both parties address the Order Agreement rather than the controlling Sale Contract. However, the two contain similar arbitration provisions, with the main difference being the lack of an opt-out clause in the Sale Agreement. Thus, the parties’ arguments largely stand, and the court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden of showing that it would be unconscionable to enforce the arbitration clause found in the Sale Agreement.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration.

 

D.  Stay of Proceedings

 

Code Civ. Proc. section 1281.4 provides that if the court has ordered the arbitration of a controversy, it “shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.”  Pursuant to Section 1281.4, therefore, the Court stays this action pending conclusion of the arbitration proceedings. 

 

CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration is GRANTED. The court STAYS this action pending conclusion of the arbitration process.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 24TRCV02200    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint

 

Moving Party: Defendant Scott Murray        

Responding Party: None

 

RULING

 

Having considered the moving papers, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff American Premier Holding Group, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Scott Murray (“Defendant”) alleging a cause of action for (1) Breach of Commercial Lease Agreement.

 

On October 7, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion. On November 8, 2024, Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition. As of November 18, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

On November 27, 2013, Defendant made, executed, and delivered to Plaintiff a written Commercial Single-Tenant Lease Agreement (the “Lease”). (Complaint, ¶ 4.) Defendant agreed to pay $4,500.00 each month. (Complaint, ¶ 4.)

 

On May 16, 2019, Plaintiff raised the monthly rent to $8,700.00. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) On November 15, 2023, Defendant breached the Lease by failing to render payment. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) From November 15, 2023 to February 15, 2024, when Plaintiff received full possession of the property, Defendant failed to make monthly payments. (Complaint, ¶ 8.)

 

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.(Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.) 

 

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests judicial notice of search results from the California Secretary of State’s Business Entity Search portal. Pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (c), the court may take notice of the official acts of legislative, executive, and judicial departments of any state.


Defendant’s request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

Defendant demurs to the sole cause of action on the grounds that it is uncertain, as it does not contain the whole Lease. Further, Plaintiff is not a business entity in good standing and cannot bring an action.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Meet and Confer

 

Defense counsel declares that the parties have not been able to meaningfully meet and confer. Accordingly, the court finds that the parties have not satisfied the meet and confer requirements. However, the court will still discuss the merits of the demurrer.

 

B.  Demurrer for Uncertainty

 

A demurrer to a pleading lies where the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10, subd. (f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616, disapproved on other grounds in Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guar. Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 46 [holding claims for unfair business practices need not be pled specifically, impliedly disapproving Khoury].) As a result, a special demurrer for uncertainty is not intended to reach failure to incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading but is directed only at uncertainty existing in the allegations already made. (People v. Taliaferro (1957) 149 Cal.App.2d 822, 825, disapproved on other grounds in Jefferson v. J.E. French Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 717, 719-720 [statute of limitations question].) 

 

Where complaint is sufficient to state a cause of action and to apprise defendant of issues he is to meet, it is not properly subject to a special demurrer for uncertainty. (See ibid.; see also Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643 [“A special demurrer [for uncertainty] should be overruled where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet”].) 

 

Defendant demurs on the basis of uncertainty because the attached Lease does not contain all seventeen pages. Further, the attached pages do not contain a signature page. Thus, because an incomplete Lease is attached, the statement “A true and correct copy of the Commerical Lease Agreement…” is false and uncertain.

 

On demurrer, the court may only consider defects that appear on the face of the complaint, including attached exhibits. (Panterra GP, Inc. v. Superior Court of Kern County (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 697, 708.) Here, the attached Lease does lack a signature page. However, the first paragraph shows that the Plaintiff is the lessor and Defendant is the lessee. Further, the Lease indicates that Defendant is obligated to make a monthly payment of $4,500 for the term of the Lease.

 

Taken as a whole, Plaintiff’s complaint is not so uncertain that Defendant cannot be reasonably expected to respond to it.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer for uncertainty is OVERRULED.

 

C.  Failure to State a Cause of Action

 

Defendant demurs to the sole cause of action on the grounds that Plaintiff has not stated a cause of action because the complaint does not allege that both parties entered into the Lease. The complaint merely states that Defendant made, executed, and delivered the Lease to Plaintiff. Defendant also argues that the attached pages of the Lease do not contain the essential terms of a valid lease agreement, and thus it cannot be enforceable. In support, Defendant cites to Levin v. Saroff (1921) 54 Cal.App. 285, 289, which held that a valid lease agreement must contain a definite term; description of the boundary of the leased property; and agreed upon price of rental, and time and manner of payment.

 

As noted above, the Lease shows that Plaintiff is the lessor and Defendant is the lessee. The Lease identifies the subject premises, the term, base rent, and acceptable methods of payment. Thus, the Lease contains the essential terms of a valid lease agreement. The complaint further alleges that Defendant breached the Lease by failing to make the required monthly payments in November 2023.

 

At this stage of the proceedings, Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to state a cause of action for breach of contract.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

D.  Statute of Limitations

 

Defendant also raises the defense of statute of limitations, arguing that Plaintiff’s action should have been brought no later than December 31, 2021.

 

On demurrer, the court accepts all well-pleaded facts as true and liberally construes the complaint to consider all reasonable inferences. (Wilner v. Sunset Life Ins. Co. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 952, 958.)

 

Here, the Lease does show that its term ended on December 31, 2017. While the Lease appears to contain an addendum providing the parties with an option to renew the Lease, it is incomplete and contains only a portion of the written contract. (Complaint, Exh. 1, p. 2, ¶ 1.11.) Exhibit 2 of the complaint shows correspondence between the parties, discussing an increase in rent, effective March 16, 2020 and the complaint also alleges that a breach did not occur until November 15, 2023.

 

Plaintiff does not allege that the parties exercised an option to renew the terms of the Lease through November 2023 or whether a subsequent, superseding lease was signed. Thus, on its face Plaintiff’s complaint was not timely filed.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer on the basis of statute of limitations is SUSTAINED.

 

E.  Lack of Capacity to Sue

 

Defendant also demurs to the complaint because a suspended corporation has no right to prosecute or defend an action. In support, Defendant provides screenshots from a search conducted on the California Secretary of State’s Business Entity Search. The search results indicate that there is no active business registered in the state containing the phrase “American Premier Holding.”

 

A demurrer is properly sustained when a plaintiff lacks capacity to sue, and a suspended lacks capacity to sue. (Friends of Shingle Springs Interchange, Inc. v. County of El Dorado (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1470, 1482.) Plaintiff does not appear to be an LLC in good standing, and thus, it cannot maintain an action.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED.

 

F.  Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”] The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

Here, the attachments to the complaint itself are facially incomplete.  Therefore, the court grants leave to amend the complaint to allege compliance with the statute of limitations.

 

A suspended corporation may not prosecute or defend an action. (Timberline, Inc. v Jaisinghani (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 1361, 1365.) “However, if the corporation’s status only comes to light during litigation, the normal practice is for the trial court to permit a short continuance to enable the suspended corporation to effect reinstatement… to defend itself in court.” (Id. at p. 1366.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff may effect reinstatement and properly prosecute this action. Thus, leave to amend is GRANTED.

 

CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, the court SUSTAINS the demurrer with leave to amend.

 

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling. 

Case Number: 24TRCV02568    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint and Motion to Strike

 

Moving Party: Defendant Jae Ho Yoon        

Responding Party: Plaintiff JY and JA Investment, LLC

 

RULING

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply. The court SUSTAINS the demurrer with leave to amend.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff JY and JA Investment, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Jae Ho Yoon (“Defendant”) alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Fraud; and (4) Professional Negligence.

 

On September 23, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion. Plaintiff filed an opposition on November 6, 2024. Defendant filed a reply on November 13, 2024.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

From August 27, 2014 to April 5 2022, Plaintiff employed Defendant, a certified public accountant (“CPA”) to prepare its business taxes. (Complaint, ¶ 6.)

 

On April 30, 2021, the parties entered into an agreement whereby Defendant was to prepare Plaintiff’s application for the SBA Restaurant Revitalization Fund and submit the application on May 3, 2021, right when the SBA award portal opened. (Complaint, ¶ 7.) Defendant told Plaintiff that the application was submitted on the agreed upon date, and Plaintiff tendered $800.00 to Defendant as payment. (Complaint, ¶¶ 11-12.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant created an email account on behalf of Plaintiff to submit the application, but Plaintiff does not have access to it. (Complaint, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant fraudulently concealed and misrepresented the fact that he had not submitted the application on May 3, 2021. (Complaint, ¶¶ 14-16.) Plaintiff did not become suspicious until February 6, 2024, after Plaintiff demanded a receipt to prove submission, and Defendant refused to provide it. (Complaint, ¶ 17.)

 

Defendant now demurs to all four causes of action for failure to state sufficient facts. Defendant also seeks to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages.

 

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.) 

 

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.) 

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff has merely recited the elements of each cause of action without providing factual allegations in support. Further, the professional negligence and breach of fiduciary claims are duplicative. As Plaintiff fails to plead fraud with particularity, its request for punitive damages must be stricken.

 

Opposing Party’s Argument

 

Plaintiff contends that it has pleaded sufficient facts. Any uncertainty or ambiguity can be resolved through discovery.

 

Reply Argument

 

Plaintiff improperly raises new allegations that are not present in the complaint, and Plaintiff relies on uncited cases that hold no precedential value.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) A motion to strike also requires a meet and confer process, which is procedurally identical to the meet and confer process prior to filing a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.)

 

Defense counsel declares that the parties met telephonically to discuss issues with the complaint but were unable to reach an agreement. The court finds that the parties have satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

 

B.  First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) 

 

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.) 

 

As Defendant notes, a demurrer is properly sustained if “[i]n an action founded upon a contact, it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10, subd. (g); Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458-459.) Here, Plaintiff merely alleges that the parties entered into a contract without indicating whether the agreement is oral or written.

 

The failure to provide the definite terms of the contract and its form is also significant here where Plaintiff also asserts that the failure to comply with the contract terms is a breach of fiduciary duty and alleged to be a source of professional negligence.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

C.  Second Cause of Action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.)

 

To plead a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, a plaintiff must allege facts showing the existence of a fiduciary duty owed to that plaintiff, a breach of that duty and resulting damage. (Pellegrini v. Weiss (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 515, 524.) A fiduciary duty is founded upon a special relationship imposed by law or under circumstances in which “confidence is reposed by persons in the integrity of others” who voluntarily accept the confidence. (Tri-Growth Centre City, Ltd. v. Silldorf, Burdman, Duignan & Eisenberg (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1139, 1150; see CACI 4100, et seq.)

 

Defendant demurs to this cause of action on the grounds that it is duplicative of the professional negligence claim and Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts. Specifically, Plaintiff fails to allege that Defendant’s conduct is the “but for” cause of Plaintiff’s injury. Further, Plaintiff fails to allege what damages it actually suffered. Plaintiff argues in opposition that, at this stage of the proceedings, it does not need to describe its damages with more particularity. Further, professional negligence and breach of fiduciary duty are separate torts with different standards of care. Defendant argues in reply that Plaintiff’s cited cases are not instructive. Moreover, the underlying conduct for this cause of action is the exact same conduct underlying the professional negligence claim.

 

Plaintiff correctly notes that professional negligence and breach of fiduciary duty are two distinct causes of action. (Stanley v. Richmond (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1070, 1086.) However, as Defendant notes, California courts typically uphold them as separate causes of action when they are based on distinct allegations. (See Charnay v. Cobert, (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 176-177; Stanley v. Richmond, (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1070, 1087-1092.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that the same conduct underlies each cause of action: Defendant’s failure to submit the application on the agreed upon date. Thus, the court finds that they are duplicative.

 

Further, Plaintiff fails to plead facts showing damage. As discussed below, a bare statement that one suffered damage is a conclusion of law, not a factual allegation.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

D.  Third Cause of Action for Fraudulent Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.) 

 

Defendant demurs to this cause of action on the basis that Plaintiff fails to plead fraud with particularity and makes only conclusory allegations. Plaintiff argues that it is allowed flexibility in its pleading where the facts related to Defendant’s state of mind are not readily accessible to Plaintiff.

 

The court agrees that Plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts to state a fraudulent misrepresentation claim. First, Plaintiff fails to plead justifiable reliance. Plaintiff does not describe any actions it took or refrained from taking due to Defendant’s alleged misrepresentation. Plaintiff merely alleges that “Defendant’s actions were deceptive, intending to mislead Plaintiff into thinking the application was submitted on May 3, 2021, when it was not.” (Complaint, ¶ 35.) Plaintiff does not allege that it relied on this misrepresentation to its detriment. Further, Plaintiff makes only a conclusory allegation that it suffered damages. As discussed below, a statement that one suffered damage, without supporting factual allegations, is not sufficient to survive a demurrer.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

E.  Fourth Cause of Action for Professional Negligence

 

“The elements of a cause of action for professional negligence are (1) the existence of the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) breach of that duty; (3) a causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional negligence.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman, supra, 51 Cal.4th 811 at p. 821.) 

 

Defendant demurs to this cause of action because Plaintiff fails to make any factual allegation of damage. Instead, Plaintiff makes only conclusory allegations that it suffered damages because of Defendant’s actions. Plaintiff argues in its opposition that a loss of opportunity is a recoverable injury. Defendant argues in reply that an allegation raised only in an opposition cannot survive a demurrer. The allegation must appear in the face of the complaint.

 

In the context of professional negligence, “’[t]he mere breach of a professional duty, causing only nominal harm, speculative harm, or the threat of future harm – not yet realized – does not suffice to create a cause of action for negligence.’” (International Engine Parts, Inc. v. Feddersen & Co. (1995) 9 Cal.4th 606, 614.) Further, “[a]llegations of damages without allegations of fact to support them are but conclusions of law, which are not admitted by demurrer.” (Zumbrun v. University of Southern California (1972) 25 Cal.App.3d 1, 12.)

 

Here, the complaint merely states that Plaintiff suffered damages to be proven at trial. Plaintiff does not provide supporting factual allegations to show how Defendant’s purported failure to submit the application caused injury. Thus, Plaintiff has failed to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action for professional negligence.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

F.  Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., §436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

Defendant seeks to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages. Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation. Thus, Plaintiff has not alleged any conduct justifying the award of punitive damages.


Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED.

 

G.  Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”] The burden is on the complainant to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.) 

 

Plaintiff, in opposition, mentions that it suffered damages because it lost the opportunity to secure SBA funds. Thus, the court finds that there is some possibility of successful amendment.

 

Accordingly, leave to amend is GRANTED.

 

CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED in its entirety and the motion to strike is GRANTED. Leave to amend is GRANTED.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
