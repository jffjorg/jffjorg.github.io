     CASE NO.: 23VECV03053

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (SET TWO)

 

 

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This case arises out of a personal injury matter. Plaintiffs Alan and Paula Harris (collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege Defendants Debby and David Joshua Newman (collectively “Defendants”) were negligent when operating a motor vehicle and were the cause of injuries and damages to Plaintiffs on March 18, 2022. (See Complaint at 4-5.)

Defendants now seek to compel Plaintiffs’ responses to their request for production of documents, set two (“RFPs”).

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On July 14, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging two causes of action: (1) Motor Vehicle and (2) General Negligence.

On January 12, 2024, Defendants filed their Answer.

On August 15, 2024, Defendants filed their Motion to Compel Alan Harris’ responses to RFPs (“Motion RFPs”). Plaintiffs’ Opposition was due on November 4, 2024, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1005(b).[1] The Motion RFPs included a Declaration of Defendants’ attorney, Mark P. Lascola.

On November 7, 2024, Defendants’ filed a Notice of Non-Opposition to the present Motion (“Non-Opposition Motion”).

On November 7, 2024, Plaintiffs also filed their Opposition to Defendants’ Motion (“Opposition”), including a Declaration of Plaintiffs’ attorney, Daryl G. Crouse.[2]

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Request for Production

A party upon whom a demand for the production of documents has been made has 30 days after service of the demand to respond. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).) The propounding and responding parties may agree to extend the time for response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.270(a).) When a party fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a motion to compel responses. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b).) A responding party that does not provide responses waives the right to object to the demand, including objections based on privilege or work product. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).) However, the court may relieve the waiver if it determines the party subsequently served a substantially code compliant response and the party’s failure to serve a timely response was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a)(1-2).) 

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.       Defendants’ Motion RFPs is Granted.

Defendants’ contend Plaintiffs failed to serve timely responses to the requested discovery, and therefore should be ordered to provide code compliant responses without objection. (See Motion RFPs at 2: 7-12.); (See Non-Opposition Motion at 2: 3-7.) Plaintiffs argue the work product doctrine absolutely protects the requested documents because they were “created by or at the direction of Plaintiff’s counsel in anticipation of litigation and reflect counsel’s impressions, conclusions, or opinions.” (Opposition at 4: 7-15.)

On April 29, 2024, Defendants served their RFPs on Plaintiffs. (See Motion RFPs at 9-12, Exhibit A.) Plaintiffs do not deny they received Defendants’ RFPs, but rather note, in Plaintiffs’ attorney’s declaration, they did not serve timely responses or objections due to an inadvertent oversight. (See Opposition at 5, ¶¶ 2-3.) Defendants’ counsel attempted to reach Plaintiffs’ counsel to follow up on the April 29, 2024, RFPs, but no responses had been provided at the time of filing for the present Motion despite multiple extensions from Defendants. (See Motion RFPs at 2: 20-8.); (See also id. at 9-18, Exhibits A-C.) Plaintiffs failed to note they were provided any extension or further explain why no timely responses were provided beyond the inadvertence noted by Plaintiffs’ counsel. (See Opposition at 5, ¶ 3.)

Here, the Court finds Plaintiffs failed to provide timely responses to Defendants’ RFPs. By failing to provide responses to the initial RFPs, Plaintiffs waived their right to object to the demand for documents pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a). This Court can relieve the waiver if the Court finds the party subsequently served a substantially code compliant response and demonstrates the party’s failure to serve a timely response was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a)(1-2).) Both elements are required for the waiver to be relieved.

Here, Plaintiffs failed to demonstrate sufficient inadvertence for purposes of waiver relief. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a)(2).); (See also Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404 (noting the court may relieve the party of its waiver, but the party must first demonstrate each requirement under section 2031.300(a)(1-2)).) In Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal. App. 4th 1568, 1571, the Court of Appeal concluded a party’s failure to respond to RFPs in a timely manner resulted from its attorney’s excusable neglect. The Pelton-Shepherd Court held waiver relief should have been granted because Defendant’s attorney stated he inadvertently failed to open an envelope containing the demands from Plaintiff’s counsel but was finally informed after he received the document in the form of additional envelopes from Plaintiff’s counsel once the responses were overdue. (See id. at 1577.)

The instant matter is distinguishable from Pelton-Shepherd. There, defendant’s counsel only inadvertently failed to open a single envelope and only discovered it once Plaintiff’s counsel notified them after the responses were overdue. (See id.) Here, Defendants’ counsel notified Plaintiffs’ counsel multiple times, both before and after the responses were due, and provided multiple extensions for the responses, yet Plaintiffs never responded. Additionally, Plaintiffs’ counsel failed to explain its inadvertence further, only noting the inadvertence Plaintiffs’ attorney’s declaration. In Pelton-Shepherd, counsel placed his inadvertence on simply failing to open the envelope. (See id.) By contrast, Plaintiffs’ counsel here simply states, “[d]ue to an inadvertent oversight, my office did not serve timely responses or objections to the Requests.” (Opposition at 5, ¶ 3.)

In addition, Plaintiffs’ counsel has failed to meet his burden as to the other element of relief from waiver by still failing to respond to the RFPs even after Defendants’ Plaintiffs’ failure to timely respond to the present Motion RFPs, as Plaintiffs filed their Opposition days after it was due. Asserting certain objections in an untimely opposition is no substitute for code-compliant substantive responses to the RFPs.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion RFPs. Plaintiffs are ordered to serve Defendants code compliant responses without objection on or before December 9, 2024.

B.      Sanctions

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction¿. . .¿against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel¿. . .” responses to interrogatories or production demands, absent substantial justification or other circumstances that make imposing the sanctions unjust.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(c).) The court shall also impose a “monetary sanction…on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).) Similarly,¿“[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying¿a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)

For their Motion RFPs, Defendants seek discovery sanctions in the amount of $685.00 against Plaintiff and counsel of record, jointly and severally, for failing to provide any responses, which accounts for Defendants’ counsel’s filing fee, preparation of the present Motion (1.5 hours), and remote appearance for the hearing on the present Motion RFPs (1.0 hours anticipated). (See Non-Opposition Motion at 2: 3-9.); (See Motion RFPs at 8(Attorney Declaration pg. 2), ¶¶9-10.) The requested amount is based on Defendants’ counsel’s billable rate of $250.00 per hour. (See Motion RFPs at 8(Attorney Declaration pg. 2), ¶¶9-10.)

The Court finds Defendants’ counsel’s hourly rate of $250.00 reasonable given the prevailing rates in the Greater Los Angeles Area for attorneys with the listed attorneys’ experience. Likewise, the Court finds the time requested reasonable as well.  Accordingly, the Court awards Defendants’ counsel discovery sanctions for the requested amount of $685.00. This amount reflects Defendants’ counsel’s reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, associated with bringing the Motion RFPs.

Plaintiffs’ counsel shall pay Defendants’ counsel discovery sanctions in the amount of $685.00 on or before December 31, 2024.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion RFPs. Plaintiffs are ordered to serve Defendants with code compliant responses to Defendants’ Requests for Production without objection on or before December 9, 2024.

The Court also GRANTS Defendants’ request for discovery sanctions in the amount of $685.00. Defendant’s counsel shall pay Plaintiff’s counsel on or before December 31, 2024.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

I.               BACKGROUND 

This case arises out of an unpaid wages dispute between Plaintiffs Alma Ramirez, Jessica Jimenez, and Alexandra Grady (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) against Defendants Reliant Life Shares, LLC (“Reliant”), Scott Grady, and Longevity Asset Advisors, LLC (collectively, “Defendants”). Plaintiffs allege Defendants failed to pay them any compensation for work performed for Defendants from approximately June 15, 2023, through August 30, 2023, among other wage-related violations. (See Complaint, ¶ 40.)

Plaintiffs now seek to compel Reliant’s responses to their request for production of documents after Court order.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Labor Code §1197 and IWC Wage Orders; (2) Violation of Labor Code §204, §210 &        §218; (3) Violation of Labor Code §1198 and §510; (4) Violation of Labor Code          §§201, 202 & 203; (5) Violation of Labor Code §226; and (6) Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200, et seq.

On May 7, 2024, Reliant filed its Answer.  

On August 15, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiffs’ Motions to Compel Reliant’s responses to their request for production of documents (“RFPs”) and form and special interrogatories. The Court also granted Plaintiffs’ request for an order deeming the truth of the matters admitted in Plaintiffs’ requests for admissions.

On October 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion to Compel responses to their RFPs (“Motion”) per this Court’s August 15, 2024, Order (“Order”).

On October 8, 2024, Reliant filed its Notice of Pending Bankruptcy and Automatic Stay of Proceeding (“Notice”).

On November 5, 2024, Reliant filed its Opposition.

On November 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Reply.

///

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Request for Production

 A party upon whom a demand for the production of documents has been made has 30 days after service of the demand to respond. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).)[1] The propounding and responding parties may agree to extend the time for response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.270(a).) When a party fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a motion to compel response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b).) A responding party that does not provide responses waives the right to object to the demand, including objections based on privilege or work product. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).)     

“If a party then fails to obey the order compelling a response, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c).)

IV.          ANALYSIS

Plaintiffs argue Reliant has failed to comply with the Court’s Order by failing to provide response to Plaintiffs’ RFPs, despite Plaintiffs’ multiple attempts to follow-up. (See Motion, pg. 1.) In their Reply, Plaintiffs acknowledge the “bankruptcy proceeding likely stays further discovery from Reliant” but request “$4,640.00 as the amount of reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees and costs, incurred in bringing this motion plus a statutory sanction.” (Reply, pp. 8-9.)

This case was automatically stayed when Reliant filed for bankruptcy. (See 11 U.S.C. § 362.) “The stay's scope is quite broad and applies to almost any type of formal or informal action against the debtor or property of the estate.” (In re Swintek (9th Cir. 2018) 906 F.3d 1100, 1103.) Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion is DENIED and the Court declines to issue Plaintiffs’ requested sanctions.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production of Documents After Court Order.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

ORDER SUSTAINING IN PART AND OVERRULING IN PART DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER WITH AND WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

 

 

 

 

I.        INTRODUCTION

This case arises out of alleged violations of the Song-Beverly Act. Plaintiff Anthony Joseph Nanula (“Plaintiff”) alleges he purchased a defective 2018 Ford Transit Connect (“Subject Vehicle”) on May 7, 2018, from Defendant Ford Motor Company (“Ford”) that Defendant DCH Ford of Thousand Oaks, erroneously sued as DCH Ford Thousand Oaks (“DCH”)(collectively, “Defendants”) failed to repair and that Ford subsequently failed to repurchase.  

Defendants now demurrer to all Plaintiff’s causes of action.

///

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(d); (2) Violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(b); (3) Violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(a)(3); (4) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civ. Code, § 1791.1; § 1794; § 1795.5); and (5) Negligent Repair.

On October 14, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Demurrer.

On November 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Opposition.

On November 8, 2024, Defendants filed their Reply.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)[1] It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (See Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed. [Citation.]” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007; Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).) 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.”  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.” (Id.) In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (See SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) Accordingly, “[w]hether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer.” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609-10.) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (See Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245). 

IV.     MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a), (emphasis added).) A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(3).)

          Ford and DCH’s counsel declares they met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel on September 17, 2024, but the parties were unable to reach an agreement. (See Declaration of Chen Fei Liu filed October 14, 2024, ¶ 2.) Ford and DCH’s counsel does not detail how the parties met and conferred, however, and this Court is thus unable to evaluate whether they fulfilled their meet and confer requirements. The Court will rule on the Demurrer but reminds counsel to specify the method of communication in future meet and confer declarations.

V.       ANALYSIS

A.       Plaintiff’s First through Fourth Causes of Action are Time Barred as Currently Pled.

The four-year statute of limitations of Uniform Commercial Code (“UCC”) § 2725 applies to claims brought under the Song-Beverly Act whether for express or implied warranty. (See Krieger v. Nick Alexander Imports, Inc. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3rd 205, 213-14 (1991); see Mexia v. Rinker Boat Co., Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1297, 1306.)

Breach of express warranty causes of action (Plaintiff’s first through third causes of action), accrue “when tender of delivery is made, except that where a warranty explicitly extends to future performance of the goods and discovery of the breach must await the time of such performance the cause of action accrues when the breach is or should have been discovered.” (U. Com. Code § 2725(2)(emphasis added).) Accrual of breach of express warranty causes of action can therefore be postponed until discovery of the breach—that is, in the Song-Beverly express warranty context, after a plaintiff has unsuccessfully attempted to repair their vehicle. (See Krieger, 234 Cal.App.3rd at 217-219 (“If we adopted the position urged by respondent and held that a cause of action for breach of express warranty accrues on tender of delivery, before the seller has an opportunity to repair any defects, we would undermine the legislative purpose that the parties attempt to resolve any deficiencies in performance before resorting to the remedies provided in the Act.”).)

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s first, second, third, and fourth causes of action are barred by the Song-Beverly Act’s four-year statute of limitations for breach of express and implied warranty. (See Demurrer, pp. 6-7.) 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues “the statute of limitations defense is not clearly and affirmatively apparent from the allegations in the Complaint and Ford’s argument cannot be maintained on demurrer.” (Opposition, pg. 4.)

Plaintiff states, under the header “TOLLING OF THE STATUTES OF LIMITATION,” “Plaintiff discovered Defendant's wrongful conduct alleged herein shortly before the filing of the complaint, as the Vehicle continued to exhibit symptoms of defects following FMC's unsuccessful attempts to repair them.” (Complaint, ¶ 24 (emphasis added).) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded (i.e., all ultimate facts alleged, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law). (See Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Here, Plaintiff’s threadbare, copy and paste attempt to plead around a potential statute of limitation defense is conclusory. Plaintiff does not state the time and manner of the discovery; there are no allegations to suggest when Plaintiff brought the subject vehicle to repair the alleged “defects and nonconformities” and for how many times. Plaintiff cannot defeat Defendants’ statute of limitations argument by making his Complaint so general and devoid of facts so as to be immune from demurrer—Plaintiff must allege material facts.

Plaintiff’s allegations are also insufficient to state a cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of merchantability. It is the general rule that implied warranties do not cover future performance. (See Cardinal Health 301, Inc. v. Tyco Electronics Corp. (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 116, 134.) “Because an implied warranty is one that arises by operation of law rather than by an express agreement of the parties, courts have consistently held it is not a warranty that ‘explicitly extends to future performance of the goods ....’.” (Id. (citations omitted); see also Carrau v. Marvin Lumber & Cedar Co. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 281, 291 (concluding the future performance exception applies only when “a seller expressly warrants a product for a specified number of years.”).) Thus, per UCC § 2725(2), Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action accrued “when tender of delivery [was] made.” Here, Plaintiff alleges he entered into a warranty contract with Ford “[o]n or about May 7, 2018,” over six years before he filed the instant action. (Complaint, ¶ 7.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is clearly barred by the statute of limitations.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first through third causes of action WITH LEAVE TO AMEND and SUSTAINS the Demurrer WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action.

B.      Plaintiff Sufficiently Pleads his Fifth Cause of Action for Negligent Repair.  

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for negligent repair is barred by the economic loss rule as the claim arises from (and is not independent of) the warranty contract and Plaintiff is only seeking damages for the cost of the repair to the Subject Vehicle. (See Demurrer, pp. 7-10.) Defendants also argue Plaintiff fails to plead the essential element of damages. (See id. at p. 10.)

The necessary elements for negligence are: (1) the existence of a legal duty of care that the defendant owed to the plaintiff; (2) breach; (3) causation; and (4) damages. (See County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318; see also CACI No. 1220.)

Under the economic loss rule, a plaintiff is precluded from recovery in tort where her damages consist solely of an economic loss. (See Seely v. White Motor Co. (1965) 63 Cal.2d 9, 17-18.) “California Courts define economic loss as ‘damages for inadequate value, costs of repair and replacement of the defective product or consequent loss of profits—without any claim of personal injury or damages to other property.’” (Department of Water & Power of City of Los Angeles v. ABB Power T & D Co. (C.D. Cal. 1995) 902 F.Supp. 1178, 1186, fn. 4.) The economic loss rule requires a purchaser to recover in contract for purely economic loss due to disappointed expectations unless she can demonstrate harm above and beyond a broken contractual promise. (See Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 988, 993.)

There exist four (4) exceptions to the economic loss rule barring recovery in tort in a breach of contract action: (1) where the breach of duty directly caused physical injury; (2) where there was a breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing in insurance contracts; (3) for wrongful discharge in violation of fundamental public policy; or (4) where the contract was fraudulently induced. (See Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 551-552; see Robinson, 34 Cal.4th at 989-990.)

          Here, Plaintiff’s negligent repair cause of action is alleged only against DCH and is sparse in its factual support. Plaintiff alleges he presented the Subject Vehicle to DCH for repair, that DCH owed and breached their duty to repair the Subject Vehicle, and DCH’s negligence “was a proximate cause of Plaintiff's damages.” (Complaint, ¶¶ 54-58.)

As sparse as these allegations are, they appear to have arisen independently of Plaintiff’s warranty with Ford and therefore circumvent the economic loss rule. Plaintiff has not alleged a warranty or other contractual relationship with DCH; rather, Plaintiff’s allegations against DCH concern DCH’s duty to Plaintiff to use ordinary care and skill to repair the Subject Vehicle. If DCH was obligated to make the repair, it was because of its relationship with Ford, not with Plaintiff. Presumably, Ford reimburses or contracts with DCH to pay for each warranted repair that DCH performs. There is no promise in the warranty that DCH will successfully make those repairs. Instead, DCH’s duty to Plaintiff—to reasonably perform the repair—arises from tort, and the general duty of reasonable care that each person owes each other. Therefore, Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is distinct from a failure to perform under the warranty. The warranty was between Ford and Plaintiff, and, even though DCH was not successful at repairing the Vehicle, the failure to perform the warranty was Ford’s.

The Court also finds Plaintiff’s damages allegations sufficient at the pleading stage. Though general, Plaintiff does plead he was damaged by DCH’s breach. (See Complaint, ¶ 58.) Because of this, it is not yet clear that the only potential damages were the cost of the repairs, and Plaintiff never actually states these were his only damages. There is case law holding that “the general measure of damages where injury to property is capable of being repaired is the reasonable cost of repair together with the value of lost use during the period of injury." (Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 555.) Therefore, even if Plaintiff did not pay for the repairs, he could still have been damaged in time lost.

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for negligent repair.

VI.     CONCLUSION

          Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first through third causes of action WITH LEAVE TO AMEND and SUSTAINS the Demurrer WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action.

          The Court also OVERRULES Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for negligent repair.

          Plaintiff has until December 13, 2024, to amend his Complaint.

          IT IS SO ORDERED.
