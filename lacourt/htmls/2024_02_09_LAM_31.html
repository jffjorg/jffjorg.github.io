DEPARTMENT 31 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling by emailing Dept. 31 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. The email address is smcdept31@lacourt.org. Please do not call the court to submit on the tentative. Please do not submit to the tentative ruling on behalf of the opposing party. Please do not e-mail the Court if you plan to appear and argue.

In deciding whether to submit on the tentative ruling or attend the hearing and present oral argument, please keep the following in mind:

The tentative rulings authored by this court reflect that the court has read and considered all pleadings and evidence timely submitted to the court in connection with the motion, opposition, and reply (if any). Because the pleadings were filed, they are part of the public record.

Oral argument is not an opportunity to simply regurgitate that which a party set forth in its pleadings. Nor, is oral argument an opportunity to "make a record" when there is no court reporter present and the statements and arguments of counsel are already part of the record because they were set forth in the pleadings. Finally, simply because a party or attorney disagrees with the court's analysis and ruling or is not satisfied with it does not necessarily warrant oral argument when no new arguments will be articulated.

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify all other parties email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. If all parties to the motion submit, this tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date and it will be memorialized in a minute order. This tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such document will be considered by the Court.

**Tentative rulings on Motions for Summary Judgment will only be available for review in the courtroom on the day of the hearing.


Case Number: 22STCV25084    Hearing Date: February 9, 2024    Dept: 31

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Kerry Bensinger, Department 31

 

 

HEARING DATE:      February 9, 2024                                         TRIAL DATE:   Not set

                                                          

CASE:                         Fred Goldstein v. Joshua Driskell, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV25084

 

 

DEMURRER WITHOUT MOTION TO STRIKE

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Joshua Driskell

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Fred Goldstein

 

 

I.          FACTUAL AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

            This is a legal malpractice action.  Plaintiff, Fred Goldstein, a self-represented litigant, alleges that he retained Defendants, Joshua Driskell, Primuth Driskell & Terzian LLP, and Lagerlof LLP, in November 2018 to represent Plaintiff in the underlying probate action filed as 18STPB11264 in the County of Los Angeles. 

 

            On August 3, 2022, Plaintiff filed a Complaint in pro per against Defendants.  Thereafter, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint on November 4, 2022, and the Second Amended Complaint (SAC) on December 8, 2022.  The SAC alleged causes of action for (1) Legal Malpractice/Negligence, (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duties, (3) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, and (4) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.  Defendants demurred to the Second Amended Complaint. 

 

On June 26, 2023, the court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to all four causes of action in the SAC.  Plaintiff was granted leave to amend. 

 

On July 26, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (TAC) alleging the same four causes of action.  As alleged in the TAC, Plaintiff and his brother, Roy Goldstein (Roy), were appointed as co-executors of their mother’s estate in the underlying action.  Because Roy refused to cooperate or respond in a timely fashion towards commencing probate proceedings, Plaintiff retained Defendants to be appointed as the sole executor.  However, during the representation, Defendants refused to provide Roy with financial information of the estate assets which caused Roy to suspect Plaintiff of hiding estate assets; failed to locate or even search for the original will, which led the probate court to declare in April 2019 that the matter concerned a contested will; failed to timely file a creditor’s claim based on a misunderstanding of the law; failed to timely notify Plaintiff of discovery requests propounded by Roy’s counsel and submitted Plaintiff’s incomplete and unsigned discovery responses without his authorization; responded and filed a motion to withdraw as Plaintiff’s counsel based on the falsehood that Plaintiff was uncooperative; and was slow to respond to Plaintiff’s communications.  As a result, Plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress.

 

On August 25, 2023, Defendants filed this demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED) in the TAC.  

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition.  Defendants replied.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD FOR DEMURRER  

 

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings and will be sustained only where the pleading is defective on its face.  (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.)  “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.  [Citation.]”  (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007; Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)  Allegations are to be liberally construed.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  In construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations.  (Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 764, 769.)  

 

A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)  “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.”  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)   

Where the complaint contains substantial factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty will be overruled or plaintiff will be given leave to amend.  (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)  Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully.  (Ibid.)  

 

III.      DISCUSSION

 

Meet and Confer

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  “The parties shall meet and confer at least 5 days before the date the responsive pleading is due.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).)  “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).) 

 

Plaintiff argues the demurrer should be overruled because Defendants did not meet and confer 5 days prior to filing the demurrer.  Plaintiff is correct that the parties did not meet and confer at least 5 days before filing this demurrer.  (See Declaration of Nina Niedbalski, ¶ 2.)  However, Plaintiff makes no argument as to the insufficiency of the meet and confer.  Moreover, the court cannot overrule or sustain a demurrer based on the failure to comply with the meet and confer requirement.  (Code. Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).)  Accordingly, the court exercises its discretion to consider Defendants’ demurrer.

 

Analysis

 

The elements of an IIED cause of action are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) intention to cause or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) severe emotional suffering; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress. (See Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 780; Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009.) To satisfy the element of extreme and outrageous conduct, defendant’s conduct “‘must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’” (Moncada, 221 Cal.App.4th at p. 780, quoting Tererice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883.)¿¿ 

 

In sustaining Defendants’ demurrer to the SAC, the court concluded, in relevant part, that the IIED claim in the SAC contained conclusory allegations which did not specify what emotional distress Plaintiff suffered as a result of Defendants’ conduct nor sufficiently explain why withdrawal from representing Plaintiff was intentional and maliciously done to harm Plaintiff.  The court further stated that the litigation privilege bars tort claims premised on communications that bear some relation to judicial proceedings, such as filing and submitting discovery responses.  As such, Plaintiff could not rely on the allegations for deficient discovery responses, notice of withdrawal in the underlying probate action and the deficient discovery responses to support his IIED claim. 

 

Defendants now argue the IIED claim is still deficient because the TAC does not adequately plead intentional conduct or conduct that rises to the level of malice or conscious disregard. 

 

Given that the court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the IIED claim in the SAC, the court compares the allegations in the SAC to the operative complaint.  Upon review, the court finds that the factual allegations in the TAC remain largely unchanged from the SAC.  The IIED claim is still premised upon the same alleged conduct: Defendants refused to provide Roy with financial information of the estate assets; failed to locate or even search for the original will, which led the probate court to declare in April 2019 that the matter concerned a contested will; failed to timely file a creditor’s claim based on a misunderstanding of the law; failed to timely notify Plaintiff of discovery requests propounded by Roy’s counsel and submitting Plaintiff’s incomplete and unsigned discovery responses without his authorization; filed a motion to withdraw as Plaintiff’s counsel based on the falsehood that Plaintiff was uncooperative; and was slow to respond to Plaintiff’s communications.  (Compare SAC, ¶¶ 18-44 with TAC, ¶¶ 61-98.) 

 

Plaintiff does not add any new factual allegations showing Defendants’ conduct was carried out with the intent to cause Plaintiff emotional distress or that Defendants conduct rises to the level of malice or conscious disregard for the purposes of an IIED claim. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff attempts to draw the court’s attention to the numerous instances that the terms “emotional distress” and “intent” appear throughout the TAC.  However, the frequency with which these terms are alleged does not overcome the lack of new, factual allegations in the TAC to sustain his IIED claim.  The Fourth Cause of Action is deficient.

 

Leave to Amend

 

Plaintiff argues leave to amend should be granted.  He states that the complaint “can be amended by, for example, removing virtually all the email excerpts and replacing some of them as quotes within references as factual support for each allegation of fact.”  (Opp., p. 23:24-25.)  However, Plaintiff does not show what new or different allegations can be added to the complaint to state a claim for IIED.  Further, given that the court granted leave to amend once before and Plaintiff did not assert any new facts to support his IIED claim, Plaintiff does not demonstrate that the complaint can be amended to state an IIED claim.

 

 IV.       CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action for IIED in the TAC is SUSTAINED.  Leave to amend is DENIED.
