DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court may change tentative rulings at any time. Therefore, attorneys are advised to check this website to determine if any changes or updates have been made to the tentative ruling.

Counsel may submit on the tentative rulings by calling the clerk in Dept. O at 909-802-1126 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. Submission on the tentative does not bind the court to adopt the tentative ruling at the hearing should the opposing party appear and convince the court of further modification during oral argument.

The Tentative Ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such filing will be considered by the Court in the absence of permission first obtained following ex-parte application therefore.



Case Number: 23PSCV02887    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

(1)   GENERAL MOTORS LLC’S AMENDED NOTICE OF DEMURRER AND DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

(2)   GENERAL MOTORS LLC’S AMENDED NOTICE OF MOTION; MOTION TO STRIKE PUNITIVE DAMAGES FROM PLAINTIFF’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT is MOOT.

 

Background

 

This is a lemon law case arising from Plaintiff Mario Cerritos’ purchase of a 2022 Chevrolet Silverado Ltd that experienced “transmission defects, engine defects, infotainment defects, suspension defects; among other defects and non-conformities.” (FAC p. 2.) More specifically, the defect at issue is with the vehicle’s 8-speed transmission that is susceptible to sudden and premature failure. (FAC p. 11.)

 

On September 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed suit.

 

On November 27, 2023, Defendant filed a demurrer.

 

On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (FAC), rendering the 11/27/23 demurrer moot.

 

On March 28, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike (MTS).

 

On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition.

 

On May 13, 2024, Defendant filed its reply.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant demurs to the 5th COA for fraudulent inducement—concealment.

 

Specifically, Defendant argues that “Plaintiff failed to allege (i) the identity of the individuals at GM who purportedly concealed material facts or made untrue representations about the Silverado, (ii) their authority to speak and act on behalf of GM, (iii) GM’s knowledge about alleged defects in Plaintiff’s Silverado at the time of purchase, (iv) any interactions with GM before or during the purchase of the Silverado, or (v) GM’s intent to induce reliance by Plaintiff to purchase the specific Silverado at issue.” (Demurrer p. 8, emphasis original.)

 

Here, the court agrees that the FAC fails to plead specific facts as to Defendant’s knowledge of the defect (element 3). While the FAC may provide various allegations to support its COA, the allegations are conclusory nor specifically identify this vehicle. (Demurrer p. 5.)[1]

 

For example, the FAC references “shift quality issues” with the 8-speed transmission, but the FAC does not allege that the issues pertain to the 2022 Chevrolet Silverado. (FAC P68.) Similarly, the FAC references service bulletins issued by GM about transmission problems, but it is not alleged that said service bulletins from 2014 to 2019 pertain to the 2022 Chevrolet Silverado. (FAC P69.) And an example of conclusory pleading includes the following: “Plaintiff alleges that prior to the sale of the Vehicle to Plaintiff, Defendant GM knew that the Vehicle and its 8-speed transmission suffered from an inherent defect, was defective, would fail prematurely, and was not suitable for its intended use.” (FAC P72.) Absent references that the defects with the 8-speed transmission are flawed in the 2022 Chevrolet Silverado Ltd, the pleading fails to state sufficient facts to support a fraud COA.

 

To the extent that in opposition Plaintiff contends such allegations are sufficient by pointing to Dhital v. Nissan N. Am., Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, 844, that does not address the sufficiency of this pleading (i.e., cannot compare the complaint in Dhital to compare to complaint in the instant case). Moreover, the California Supreme Court has granted the petition for review of the case.

 

To the extent that Plaintiff argues it need not pled intent with particularity when the defendant holds the information, that may be true, but, per Plaintiff’s own citation, the “nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts must at the very least be pled.” (Opp. p. 2, citing Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216-217.) Here, however, the mere fact that the transmission may have had problems does not equate to a sufficient allegation that Defendant knew of a definitive problem and decided intentionally to withhold/conceal said information. After all, “[f]raud is never presumed” (Mason v. Drug Inc. (1939) 31 Cal.App.2d 697, 703), but Plaintiff’s allegations seek the court to make such a presumption.

 

 

 

 

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer is sustained with leave to amend. Consequently, the MTS to strike punitive damages based upon the 5th COA for fraudulent inducement—concealment is moot.

Case Number: 23PSCV03505    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

U-HAUL CO. OF ARIZONA’S NOTICE OF DEMURRER AND DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT is MOOT because Defendant has been dismissed from the action.

 

Background

 

This is a negligence case arising from a motor vehicle accident.

 

On November 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed suit against Defendants U-Haul Co. of Southern Arizona and U-Haul Co. of California (collectively “U-Haul Defendants”); Eddie Chongham; Glenn Ray Johnson and Ernestina Johnson (collectively, “Johnson Defendants”) for (1) motor vehicle and (2) negligence.

 

On April 24, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed U-Haul Defendants.

 

On April 25, 2024, Johnson Defendants filed their answer. That same day, U-Haul of Arizona filed the instant demurrer.

 

On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Chongham.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant demurs on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain as it lumps all Defendants making it unclear as to what U-Haul specifically did. Though the court would sustain the demurrer on said grounds, the demurrer is MOOT because Plaintiff has dismissed U-Haul Arizona.

 

 

 

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, as moving Defendant is no longer a named party in the action, the demurrer is MOOT.
