DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV19138    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DEFAULT JUDGMENT

Hearing Date: 10/17/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 19STCV19138 VANOOHI DAVOODIAN vs DOLLAR KING, INC.

Moving Party: Defendant Dollar King

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling:     MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DEFAULT JUDGMENT

IS GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

Section 473, subdivision (b) states in pertinent part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.... Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Italics added.) 

               Section 473, subdivision (b), provides for two distinct types of relief.  “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,” the court has discretion to allow relief from a “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against” a party or his or her attorney. Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,” the court shall vacate any “resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.”  The range of attorney conduct for which relief can be granted in the mandatory provision is broader than that in the discretionary provision and includes inexcusable neglect.  But the range of adverse litigation results from which relief can be granted is narrower. Mandatory relief only extends to vacating a default which will result in the entry of a default judgment, a default judgment, or an entered dismissal.”  (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 615–616.)     

To gain the statutory benefits, a party or legal representative must seek such relief “within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); see Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 980 (Rappleyea).

Discussion

On January 11, 2024, Plaintiff obtained a default judgment against Defendant Dollar Kings, Inc. Defendant now seeks relief from the default judgment under the mandatory relief pursuant to CCP 473(b).

The statutory requirements for relief have been satisfied. First, the default judgment was entered on January 11, 2024, and this motion was filed on July 2, 2024, which is within the six-month statutory deadline. Second, the motion addresses a dismissal, which falls under the scope of the mandatory relief provision. Third, Defendant’s counsel submitted a declaration stating that the default occurred due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. Fourth, while the parties were in communication before the default judgment, a family death caused Defendant’s counsel to take time off, and he failed to instruct office staff to properly calendar Defendant’s response. As a result, no responsive pleading was filed, leading to the entry of default judgment. The Court finds that the default judgment was entered a result of counsel’s neglect. Fifth, Defendant has attached a copy of the proposed answer to the motion.

Since all statutory requirements have been met and no opposition was filed, Defendant’s motion for relief is granted.

Case Number: 21STCV26067    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTIONS TO PRECLUDE EXPERT TESTIMONY AT TRIAL

Hearing Date: 10/17/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV26067 BRITHNEY GONZALEZ vs BRENDA JIMENEZ et al.

Moving Party: Defendant BRENDA JIMENEZ

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

 

Ruling: MOTIONS TO PRECLUDE EXPERT TESTIMONY AT TRIAL ARE GRANTED.

 

Background

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff designated Dr. Alen Nourian and Andrew Morris as retained experts, and Dr. Andrew Choi as one of several non-retained experts. Defendant noticed the depositions of these three experts, but none of them appeared for their depositions or produced their expert files. Expert discovery is now closed, and Defendant moves the Court to exclude all three experts from testifying at trial.

Facts

Dr. Nourian and Dr. Morris

·        On June 18, 2024, Defendant noticed the depositions of Plaintiff’s retained experts, Dr. Alen Nourian and Dr. Andrew Morris, both scheduled for July 15, 2024.

·        On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff served an untimely objection to the depositions, stating that 'Plaintiff’s counsel is unavailable on July 15, 2024.'

·        On August 7, 2024, Defendant re-noticed the depositions of Dr. Nourian and Dr. Morris to take place on August 26, 2024.

·        On August 20, 2024, Plaintiff again served objections, citing counsel’s unavailability. On the same day, Defense counsel advised Plaintiff’s counsel that the depositions would remain on calendar unless alternate dates were provided.

·        On August 22, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel responded, stating that they would be available only after Defense counsel worked out a deposition date directly with Plaintiff’s experts.

·        On August 23, 2024, Defense counsel’s paralegal, Julie Probst, spoke with Dr. Alen Nourian, who stated that he was unaware of the scheduled deposition for August 26th, as Plaintiff’s counsel had not contacted him. Dr. Nourian also confirmed that his expert file had not been requested for production.

·        On the same day, Julie Probst also contacted Dr. Andrew Morris’s office, and that office also confirmed that Dr. Morris was unaware of the August 26th deposition, as Plaintiff’s counsel had not communicated with him. Dr. Morris’s office further advised that they had not received a signed retainer agreement or the full required retainer fee. Dr. Morris confirmed that his expert file had not been requested for production.

·        Defense counsel’s office informed the experts and Plaintiff’s counsel that the depositions would proceed as scheduled, as no alternative dates were provided, and it is not defense counsel’s responsibility to arrange and schedule Plaintiff’s experts’ depositions beyond providing notice.

·        On August 26, 2024, neither Plaintiff’s counsel, Dr. Nourian, nor Dr. Morris appeared for the scheduled expert depositions, and Certificates of Non-Appearance were taken.

Dr. Andrew Choi

·        On July 8, 2024, Defendant served a notice of deposition for Plaintiff’s non retained expert, Dr. Andrew Choi, scheduled for July 23, 2024.

·        The deposition was then continued to August 26, 2024.

·        On August 26, 2024, Dr. Choi’s office informed Defendant that he was unavailable on that date and offered an alternative date of September 12, 2024, which was accepted, and the deposition was rescheduled accordingly.

·        Despite repeated requests for copies of Dr. Choi’s CV, fee schedule, and expert file, he failed and refused to produce the requested documents.

·        Dr. Choi did not comply with Defendant’s document request or appear for his deposition, and a Certificate of Non-Appearance was taken on September 12, 2024.

The current trial date is November 18, 2024. The trial was continued from October 9, 2024, to November 18, 2024, but the minute order continuing the trial did not specify that expert discovery or other case-related deadlines would follow the new trial date. As a result, expert discovery is now closed.

Legal Standard

Under California Code of Civil Procedure § 2034.300(d), the trial court shall exclude from evidence the expert opinion of any witness if a party has unreasonably failed to make that expert available for deposition, provided the moving party made a complete and timely expert witness exchange. Furthermore, the court has inherent authority to exclude evidence when a party has acted unfairly. This means that even if the mandatory provision under CCP § 2034.300(d) does not strictly apply due to the defendant’s incomplete or late disclosure, the court still has discretion to exclude expert testimony at trial. (Cottini v. Enloe Medical Center (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 401, 428.)

Discussion

Here, Defendant’s counsel has demonstrated that all parties, including Defendant, complied with the statutory requirements for timely expert designations under CCP 2034.260. (Declaration of Dwayne S. Beck ¶3.) Second, the Court finds that Plaintiff unreasonably failed to make her experts available; specifically, both Dr. Morris and Dr. Nourian confirmed that they had not been contacted by Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the deposition dates, nor were they asked to provide their expert files. In the case of Dr. Morris, it was revealed that a retainer agreement was not even signed, and his office had not received the full required retainer fee.  And, most significantly, the experts failed to appear on their noticed deposition date.

Plaintiff’s non-retained expert, Dr. Andrew Choi, also failed to comply with the deposition process. Despite confirming his availability for the September 12, 2024 deposition date, Dr. Choi did not appear. Furthermore, despite repeated requests, Dr. Choi failed and refused to provide expert documents such as his CV, fee schedule, and expert file.

Time for expert discovery is limited. When expert depositions are continued, as they were in this case, and the experts still fail to appear or provide necessary documents, the opportunity to conduct meaningful discovery is lost. The depositions of Dr. Morris, Dr. Nourian, and Dr. Choi were scheduled and continued multiple times, yet none of the experts appeared for their depositions, and expert files were not produced.

Plaintiff opposes Defendant’s motion, arguing that the exclusion of expert testimony is overly harsh, that the testimony is critical to the case, and that any prejudice suffered by Defendant can be mitigated. However, exclusion is mandatory under CCP § 2034.300(d), and the Court finds that Plaintiff acted unreasonably and that Defendant made a timely expert disclosure under CCP § 2034.260. Expert discovery is not meant to be a cat and mouse game, but that is exactly what Plaintiff engaged in.  Simply, a party is to make its experts available for deposition.  Plaintiff did not do so. The consequence for that is clearly set forth in the Code of Civil Procedure.

Therefore, the motion is granted. Dr. Morris and Dr. Nourian will be excluded from testifying at trial, and Dr. Choi, Plaintiff’s non-retained expert, is excluded from providing expert testimony at trial, though he can testify as a percipient witness.

MOTION TO CONTINUE

Hearing Date: 10/17/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV15440 CHRISTIE JOHN vs 855 N. FAIRFAX AVENUE, LLC
Moving Party: Defendant West Hollywood Healthcare and Wellness Center

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: MOTION TO CONTINUE WILL BE HEARD

 

On May 9, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present case. On May 5, 2023, Defendant West Hollywood Healthcare and Wellness Center filed its answer. The trial is currently set for November 4, 2024. Defendant West Hollywood now moves the Court to continue the trial to February 28, 2025.

On September 12, 2024, Defendant filed an ex parte application for a stipulated continuance of the trial, arguing that a continuance should be granted to allow the parties to attend mediation on February 5, 2025, and to provide more time to conduct discovery, citing the various factors under California Rule of Court 3.1332. The Court rendered its ruling on Defendant’s ex parte application, stating: “The bases for a continuance do not constitute good cause. The parties knew of the trial date and should have planned for mediation prior to that date. The failure to complete discovery is likewise not, in and of itself, a good cause basis for a continuance.”

On September 20, 2024, Defendant filed the present motion to continue trial, raising the same arguments that the continuance is stipulated and necessary for mediation. However, this had already been presented in the ex parte application and denied by the Court. Defendant raises no new rationale for the continuance, and the Court perceives of no reason to deviate from its prior ruling. Nonetheless, the Court will hear from the parties to provide them an opportunity to persuade the Court to grant their joint request.

 

 


Case Number: 22STCV21888    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 27

 

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT

Hearing Date: 10/17/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV21888 DANIEL ORTIZ vs RENO KESHISHIAN, AN INDIVIDUAL, et al. 

Moving Party: Defendants Keshishian and Legacy Global Private Security

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT IS GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

 

CCP section 877.6(a)(1) provides that “[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff . . . and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors . . . .” (Code. Civ. Proc., § 877.6(a)(1).) “A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.”¿(Id., § 877.6(c).)¿Although a determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, “it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated” by the settlement.¿(Id., § 877(a).) 

Factors to consider in determining if a settlement was made in good faith include “a rough approximation of plaintiffs’ total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial.”¿(Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499.)¿“Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of nonsettling defendants.”¿(Id.)

 

The evaluation of whether a settlement was made in good faith is required to “be made on the basis of information available at the time of settlement.” (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499.) “[A] court not only looks at the alleged tortfeasor’s potential liability to the plaintiff, but it must also consider the culpability of the tortfeasor vis-à-vis other parties alleged to be responsible for the same injury.” (TSI Seismic Tenant Space, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 159, 166.)  “Potential liability for indemnity to a nonsettling defendant is an important consideration for the trial court in determining whether to approve a settlement by an alleged tortfeasor.” (Id.) 

 

“The party asserting the lack of good faith shall have the burden of proof on that issue.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6(d).)¿The party asserting the lack of good faith can establish that the proposed settlement was not a settlement made in good faith by showing the settlement is so far “out of the ballpark” in relation to the Tech-Bilt factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute.¿(Tech-Bilt, Inc., supra, 38 Cal.3d at 499-500.)

 

Unopposed Motion

 

An unopposed motion for determination of good faith of settlement need not contain a full and complete discussion of the Tech-Bilt factors (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488) by declaration or affidavit; rather, a bare bones motion setting forth the grounds of good faith and a declaration containing a brief background of the case is sufficient. (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.)¿  

  

Analysis and Conclusion

 

This motion concerns the settlement agreement entered into between Defendants Keshishian and Legacy Global Private Security (Settling Defendants) and Plaintiff. The Settling Defendants now move the Court for a determination of good faith settlement. The present motion is unopposed, and there are no filings objecting to the settlement. The motion demonstrates that the settlement agreement was reached in good faith and provides a brief background of the case. Although not required, the Settling Defendants addressed all relevant Tech-Bilt factors. The moving party has made a sufficient showing. Thus, the present motion is GRANTED.
