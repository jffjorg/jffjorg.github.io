Case Number: 20STCV25778    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: B

JARAD BRYAN vs jarelle kate sisayan cenidoza

 

Application for Pro Hac Vice

 

Date of Hearing:          November 7, 2024                  Trial Date:       December 2, 2024

Department:               B                                              Case No.:         20STCV25778

 

Moving Party:             Defendants Sunrise Senior Living, LLC and Sunrise Senior Living                              Management, Inc.

Responding Party:       None

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from a motor vehicle accident. Plaintiff Jarad Bryan (“Plaintiff”) alleges that, on July 26, 2018, Defendants Sunrise Senior Living, LLC and Sunrise Senior Living Management, Inc. (“Defendants”) negligently entrusted a motor vehicle to Jarelle Kate Sisayan Cinidoza (“Cinidoza”), causing Plaintiff to sustain injuries when Cinidoza struck Plaintiff, a pedestrian.

 

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On October 1, 2024, Robert Wright, Esq. (“Counsel”) filed the instant unopposed application to be appear pro hac vice on behalf of Defendants Sunrise Senior Living, LLC and Sunrise Senior Living Management, Inc.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Robert Wright’s Application to Appear Pro Hac Vice is DENIED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Cal. Rules of Court Rule 9.40 provides that an attorney in good standing in another jurisdiction may apply to appear pro hac vice in this State by way of written application upon due notice to all interested parties, as well as service on the State Bar in San Francisco with payment of a $50.00 fee, so long as that attorney is not a resident of California, does not work in California, and does not perform regular or substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State. (CRC Rule 9.40.) 

 

The written application must provide the following information: (1) applicant attorney’s residence and office addresses; (2) the courts to which the applicant attorney has been admitted and dates of admission; (3) representation that the attorney applicant is a member in good standing in the courts of admission and is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (4) the title of each court and action in which the applicant attorney has appeared pro hac vice in this State in the preceding two years, if any; and (5) the name, address, and telephone number of the active California State Bar member who is counsel of record in the local action. (CRC 9.40(d).)  

 

ANALYSIS

 

Counsel seeks to appear on behalf of Defendants Sunrise Senior Living, LLC and Sunrise Senior Living Management, Inc. pro hace vice in this action. Based on the application and the attached declaration: (1) Counsel is not employed or residing within California (Wright Decl. ¶¶ 1-4); (2) Counsel has not applied to any other California courts to appear pro hace vice within the past two years (Ibid. at ¶ 8); (3) the application has the proper admission information for other courts where the attorney is admitted (Ibid. at ¶¶ 5-6); (4) the attorney is in good standing (Ibid. at ¶ 7; and (5) the applicant is to be associated with local counsel, Devon B. Moore. (Ibid. at ¶ 9). Additionally, notice of hearing upon this application was served upon the State Bar of California. (See Application Proof of Service.)

 

However, the application contains the following deficiencies:

 

1.      Applicant has not paid the required $50.00 fee. (CRC 9.40(e).)

 

Accordingly, the application does not abide by CRC Rule 9.40 and is denied without prejudice.

Hearing:              Thursday, November 7, 2024

Re:                         Rigoberto Vidauri, Jr. vs Whole Foods California, Inc. (20STCV3313)

 

 

Plaintiff Rigoberto Vidauri, Jr.’s Motion to Name Successor in Interest

 

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff Rigoberto Vidauri, Jr.’s Motion to Name Successor in Interest is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a personal injury action arising from a September 19, 2018, slip and fall incident. Plaintiff Rigoberto Vidauri, Jr. (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendants Whole Foods California, Inc. (“Whole Foods”) negligently maintained their premises, causing Plaintiff to slip and fall on an unknown substance and sustain serious injuries.

 

On August 31, 2020, Plaintiff filed their complaint.

 

On June 6, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of the Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed their initial Motion to Name Successor in Interest.

 

On August 26, 2024, the Court continued the motion to November 7, 2024, noting that Plaintiff failed to file proof of service or a certified copy of Decedent’s death certificate.

 

On September 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant amended Motion to Name Successor in Interest. The motion remains unopposed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

For the following reasons, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Name Successor in Interest.

 

Legal Standard

 

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 377.31 provides that “[o]n motion after the death of a person who commenced an action or proceeding, the court shall allow a pending action or proceeding that does not abate to be continued by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.” (See CCP § 377.30; Adams v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 71, 78-79.) A successor in interest is “the beneficiary of the decedent's estate or other successor in interest who succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of a cause of action.” (CCP § 377.11.) CCP § 377.33 provides that “[t]he court in which an action is commenced or continued under this article may make any order concerning parties that is appropriate to ensure proper administration of justice in the case, including appointment of the decedent's successor in interest as a special administrator or guardian ad litem.”    

 

Pursuant to CCP § 377.32, a person who seeks to commence such an action as the decedent’s successor in interest must file an affidavit or declaration providing the following: (1) decedent’s name, (2) date and place of decedent’s death, (3) language stating “[n]o proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent's estate,” (4) a final order showing distribution of decedent’s cause of action to successor if decedent’s estate was administered, (5) language stating “[t]he affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding” or “[t]he affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding,” (6) language stating “[n]o other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding”, (7) language stating “[t]he affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct,” and (8) a certified copy of decedent’s death certificate.

 

Discussion

 

Here, Rigoberto Vidauri, Sr. (“Senior”) seeks to serve as Rigoberto Vidauri, Jr. (“Junior”)’s successor in interest in this action following Junior’s death. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities p. 3:5-9.) In support of this motion, Senior has filed a declaration that meets all the requirements of CCP § 377.32. Accordingly, Senior’s motion is GRANTED.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Rigoberto Vidauri, Sr.’s motion to be appointed as Rigoberto Vidauri, Jr.’s successor in interest is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

 

On June 16, 2022, Defendant 2MANGAS, INC. (“Defendant”) initially noticed Plaintiff Anton Davis (“Plaintiff”)’s deposition for July 27, 2022. (Stoker Decl. ¶ 4; Exh. A [notice of deposition].) On February 27, 2023, the deposition was continued and a notice was served upon Plaintiff rescheduling for April 5, 2023. (Ibid. at ¶ 5; Exh. B [amended notice].) On December 8, 2023, the deposition was further continued and Defendant noticed Plaintiff’s deposition for January 23, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 6; Exh. C [amended notice].) On January 22, 2024, this deposition was canceled at the request of Plaintiff. No alternate dates were provided. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.) On January 25, 2024, defense counsel sent opposing party a meet and confer letter requesting Plaintiff’s availability for a rescheduled deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 8; Exh. D [meet and confer letter].) On February 22, 2024, Defendant requested Plaintiff to confirm his appearance at his February 23, 2024, deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 10; Exh. F [February 22, 2024 communication].) Opposing counsel stated they still needed to confirm with Plaintiff. (Ibid.) Plaintiff failed to appear at the February 23, 2024, deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 11.) On April 11, 2024, defense counsel sent a final notice of continuance, rescheduling Plaintiff’s deposition for May 8, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 13.) To date, Plaintiff has failed to appear for a deposition or provide available dates. (Ibid. at ¶ 15.) Defendant now moves the Court to compel Plaintiff’s deposition.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450 provides in pertinent part the following:

 

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

  

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040 or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

 

Where a motion to compel a party’s appearance and testimony at deposition is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent, unless the court finds the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)  On motion of a party who, in person or by attorney, attended at the time and place specified in the deposition notice in the expectation that the deponent’s testimony would be taken, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of that party and against the deponent.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(2).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants have met all requirements to compel deposition: the parties sufficiently met and conferred, the deposition was properly noticed for a date agreed upon by all parties, no objections were served, and Plaintiff failed to appear or reschedule a deposition. Plaintiff is ordered to sit for his deposition within 20 days of this hearing.

Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $1,135.00, consisting of a $60.00 filing fee and five hours of work at a rate of $215.00 per hour. Sanctions are mandatory and the Court fails to find Plaintiff acted with substantial justification. Plaintiff continuously failed to appear for or reschedule his deposition, thereby causing Defendant to incur fees associated with filing the present motion. The Court reduces sanctions to the amount of $705.00, consisting of a $60.00 filing fee and three hours of work at an hourly rate of $215.00. Therefore, the Court orders against Plaintiff Anton Davis and his attorney Ronda R. Dixon, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $705.00, payable to defense counsel within 30 days of this hearing.

I.       BACKGROUND  

            On June 15, 2022, Plaintiffs Gayane Avdalyan (“Gayane”), Kamo Avdalyan (“Kamo”), Khachatur Avdalyan (“Khachatur”), and Anjella Stepanyan (“Anjella”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint in the instant action against Defendant Young Men’s Christian Association of the Foothills (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10 alleging three causes of action: (1) negligence; (2) wrongful death; and (3) negligent infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiffs allege that, on or about April 6, 2022, Decedent Geghetsik Avdalyan (“Decedent”) drowned at a Young Men’s Christian Association (“YMCA”) facility owned by Defendant. Plaintiff's further allege that Decedent’s death was caused by Defendant’s negligent ownership, maintenance, and operation of the subject pool, in addition to Defendant’s negligent hiring, supervising, and retainment of lifeguards.

            On April 24, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiffs’ complaint.

            On June 5, 2024, the matter was assigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

            On October 24, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their opposition.

            On October 31, 2024, Defendant filed their reply.

 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

            Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiffs’ complaint. Defendant alleges that Plaintiffs’ claims are barred by Decedent’s waiver agreement and assumption of risk. (See Motion p. 6:19-20.)

 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

            In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue. (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).)

            A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The defendant must present evidence… showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.)

            Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence.

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)    

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections

Defendant’s October 31, 2024, evidentiary objections to portions of James McNamara’s and Gerald Dworkin’s declarations are sustained as to No. 1 and overruled as to Nos. 2 and 3.

 

Defendant’s Burden

            Defendant meets their initial burden. Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ claims are barred by Decedent’s waiver agreement and the assumption of risk doctrine.

            The primary assumption of risk doctrine was developed in recognition that some activities are inherently dangerous such that the defendant has no duty to protect the plaintiff from those inherent dangers.  (Nalwa v. Cedar Fair, L.P. (2012) 55 Cal.4th 1148, 1154; Knight v. Jewett (1992) 3 Cal.4th 296, 308.)  “The primary assumption of risk doctrine rests on a straightforward policy foundation: the need to avoid chilling vigorous participation in or sponsorship of recreational activities by imposing a tort duty to eliminate or reduce the risks of harm inherent in those activities.”  (Nalwa, at p. 1156.)  “It operates on the premise that imposing such a legal duty ‘would work a basic alteration—or cause abandonment’ of the activity.”  (Ibid. (quoting Kahn v. East Side Union High School Dist. (2003) 31 Cal.4th 990, 1003).)  “Where the doctrine applies to a recreational activity, operators, instructors and participants in the activity owe other participants only the duty not to act so as to increase the risk of injury over that inherent in the activity.”  (Ibid. at 1154 (emphasis in original).)  “When the doctrine applies, the plaintiff’s assumption of the risk acts as a complete bar to liability.”  (Moser v. Ratinoff (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 1211, 1219.)  “Determining whether the primary assumption of risk doctrine applies is a legal question to be decided by the court.”  (Ibid. at 1217.) 

            “[T]he primary assumption of risk doctrine is not limited to activities classified as sports, but applies as well to other recreational activities ‘involving an inherent risk of injury to voluntary participants . . . where the risk cannot be eliminated without altering the fundamental nature of the activity.’”  (Nalwa, supra, 55 Cal.4th at p. 1156 (quoting Beninati v. Black Rock City, LLC (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 650, 658).)  “Judges deciding inherent risk questions under Knight may consider not only their own or common experience with the recreational activity involved but may also consult case law, other published materials, and documentary evidence introduced by the parties on a motion for summary judgment.”  (Ibid. at 1158.) 

            The primary assumption of risk doctrine applies to Decedent’s swimming. Defendant argues that Plaintiffs cannot provide evidence suggesting that Defendant increased the risk of swimming over its already inherent dangers. (Kahn, supra, 31 Cal. 4th at 1154.) Instead, Defendant sets forth facts suggesting that they took all reasonable steps to mitigate the inherent dangers of swimming. It is undisputed that Kerilynn Eroh (“Eroh”) was a lifeguard on duty at the time of the underlying incident. (Plaintiffs’ Response to Defendant’s Separate Statement of Facts “PR-DSSF” Nos. 10 and 15.) It is undisputed that Eroh held the required certifications to be a lifeguard. (Ibid.) Further, it is undisputed that, at the time of the incident, Eroh had at least seven years of experience lifeguarding at the subject YMCA. (Ibid. at No. 11.) It is undisputed that, when Decedent began to drown, Decedent was in the shallow end of the pool (approximately three and ½ feet deep.) (Ibid. at No. 12.) Finally, both parties agree that Decedent was given CPR and oxygen after being removed from the pool. (Ibid. at No. 17.)

            Defendant sets forth evidence that they took reasonable steps as to not increase swimming’s inherent risk. Additionally, Defendant argues that their membership waiver, which Decedent signed, bars Plaintiffs’ claims. “In the recreational context, while a waiver of liability and an assumption of risk can serve as a bar to liability based on negligence, it cannot serve as a bar to liability based on gross negligence.” (Brown v. El Dorado Union High School Dist. (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 1003, 1024.) Once again, here, Defendants set forth facts which fail to suggest any engagement in gross negligence. Additionally, it’s undisputed that Decedent signed the membership agreement and waiver of liability when they first became a member of YMCA in 2015. (PR-DSSF No. 1; Exh. E [membership agreement].) The waiver states, in plain and conspicuous terms, that signatories “ASSUME FULL REPONSIBILITY FOR AND RISK OF BODILY INJURY, DEATH OR PROPERTY DAMAGE to the undersigned or such children due to negligence of releasee or otherwise while in, about or upon the premises of the YMCA and/or while using the premises or any facilities or equipment thereon or participating in any program affiliated with the YMCA.” (PR-DSSF No. 3; Exh. E [membership agreement].) Therefore, absent gross negligence, Defendant has provided evidence that Decedent assumed full responsibility for and risk of bodily injury, death, or property damage.

 

Plaintiffs’ Burden

            The burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to show a triable issue of fact as to Defendant’s gross negligence or conduct increasing the risk of dangers inherent in swimming.

            Plaintiffs claim that Defendant knew Decedent was a “high-risk swimmer.” (PR-DSSF No. 5.) It's undisputed that the YMCA’s Lifeguard and Procedures Manual require lifeguards to survey for high-risk swimmers. (Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement of Facts “P-SSF” No. 7; Exh. C [manual] p. 6.) Plaintiffs also allege that Eroh was unqualified and had not received proper training. (P-SSF Nos. 19-20; Exh. H [lifeguard training documents, 2021-2022].) Eroh had received verbal warnings for missing mandatory trainings prior to decedent drowning. (Ibid. at 19.) Facility-specific lifeguard training sessions may have only occurred two to three times a year. (Ibid. at No. 20; Exh. H.) Kristina Garcia, the aquatics department head at the time of Decedent’s drowning, stated that the subject lifeguarding program was “too relaxed” and there was no disciplinary action for lifeguards who missed trainings. (P-SSF No. 25; Exh. C [Garcia deposition].) It is undisputed that Eroh was engaged in conversation while Decedent entered a state of distress. (P-SSF No. 29; Exh. A [Gayane deposition].) Decedent was cyanotic when they were pulled from the pool, indicating several minutes of hypoxia prior to being removed from the water. (P-SSF No. 42; Exh. F [Los Angeles Police Department bodycam footage].) Additionally, Kristina Garcia attempted to employ the automated external defibrillator (“AED”), but the device failed because Eroh and YMCA employee Annie Azizian failed to “stand clear” or go “hands off.” (P-SSF No. 47; Exh. J [Azizian deposition].)

Finally, Plaintiffs provide evidence that YMCA had notice of its lifeguard training deficiencies. A YMCA employee, Colin Jacobs, drowned at Defendant’s Crescenta Valley location in 2020. (P-SSF No. 50; Exh. J [Azizian deposition].) Like Decedent’s drowning, only one lifeguard was on duty and CPR was delayed. (Ibid.) Despite this, Defendant did not make improvements in their training. (Ibid. at No. 52; Exh. H [lifeguard training documents 2021-2022].)

“The issue we must determine here is whether, with all facts and inferences construed in plaintiffs’ favor, the conduct shown by plaintiffs’ evidence could be found to constitute gross negligence. If so, then it is a question of fact for the jury . . . As [the California Supreme Court] has noted, whether conduct constitutes gross negligence is generally a question of fact, depending on the nature of the act and the surrounding circumstances shown by the evidence. (City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court (2007) 41 Cal.4th 767, 781. [reasoning that whether the evidence showed lack of care sufficient to constitute gross negligence was a triable issue of fact in that case].)” (Jimenez v. 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc. (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 546, 555.)

Here, Plaintiffs provided specific facts showing a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant was grossly negligent. Accordingly, Plaintiffs meet their burden.

 

V.       CONCLUSION

            A triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant was grossly negligent in their training or response to Decedent’s drowning. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.
