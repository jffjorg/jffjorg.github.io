LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT – SOUTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

 

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka                                                                                Wednesday, January 31, 2024

Department M                                                                                                                             Calendar No. 7

 

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

Blake Williams v. City of Torrance, et al.   

21TRCV00034

1.      City of Torrance, et al.’s Motion for Use of Peace Officer Personnel Records (Pitchess)

2.      City of Torrance, et al.’s Motion to File Peace Officer Records Under Seal

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            City of Torrance, et al.’s Motion for Use of Peace Officer Personnel Records (Pitchess) is granted.

 

             City of Torrance, et al.’s Motion to File Peace Officer Records Under Seal is granted.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on January 15, 2020. The Second Amended Complaint was filed on September 10, 2021. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff was employed as a police officer with the City of Torrance. Plaintiff was investigated for an arrest he made. Thereafter, he was improperly investigated in contravention of his rights under the Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights Act. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: 1. Violation of the Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights Act, Gov. Code Sections 3303, et seq.; 2. Declaratory Relief; 3. Labor Code 1102.5.

 

Pitchess Motion

 

Pursuant to Pitchess v. Superior Court (1974) 11 Cal.3d 531, a criminal defendant “could ‘compel discovery’ of certain relevant information in the personnel files of police officers by making ‘general allegations which establish some cause for discovery’ of that information and by showing how it would support a defense to the charge against him.  In 1978, the California Legislature codified the holding of Pitchess by enacting Penal Code Sections 832.7 and 832.8, as well as Evidence Code Sections 1043 through 1045.  . . . To initiate discovery, the defendant must file a motion supported by affidavits showing ‘good cause for the discovery,’ first by demonstrating the materiality of the information to the pending litigation, and second by ‘stating upon reasonable belief’ that the police agency has the records or information at issue.  This two-part showing of good cause is a ‘relatively low threshold for discovery.’”  Warrick v. Superior Court (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1011, 1018-19 (internal citations omitted).  A Pitchess motion can also be made in a civil action.  Haggerty v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1085.

 

Thus, peace officer records may be discoverable in civil actions under the mechanisms provided for in Evidence Code Sections 1043 through 1046.

 

Evid. Code § 1043(b) states: “The motion shall include all of the following: 

(1) Identification of the proceeding in which discovery or disclosure is sought, the party seeking discovery or disclosure, the peace or custodial officer whose records are sought, the governmental agency which has custody and control of the records, and the time and place at which the motion for discovery or disclosure shall be heard.

 (2) A description of the type of records or information sought.

 (3) Affidavits showing good cause for the discovery or disclosure sought, setting forth the materiality thereof to the subject matter involved in the pending litigation and stating upon reasonable belief that the governmental agency identified has the records or information from the records.” 

           

            A declaration by counsel, on information and belief, may be sufficient.   People v. Oppel (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1146, 1153, n.6.  Generally, counsel would have to provide specific facts that the officer committed misconduct. Warrick, 35 Cal.4th at 1026; People v. Hill (2005) 131 Cal.App. 4th 1089, 1098 overruled on other grounds in People v. French (2008) 43 Cal.4th 36, 48, fn. 5.  Here, however, in the context of this civil action, counsel would have to provide, generally, the facts of the instant action and why the materials sought would be material to the underlying litigation.  Haggerty v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1087. Evidence of habit, custom and practice may be admissible to prove that the conduct of an officer on a specified occasion conformed with her habit and custom.  People v. Gill (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 743, 749.  Further, the scope of disclosure is not necessarily limited to mere witness contact information.  Evid. Code § 1045(a); Haggerty v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1087.

 

            If the court orders an in camera inspection, the court inspects the records to determine the relevance of the materials to the instant action. People v. Gill (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 743, 749.  The scope of the in camera review is governed by Sections 1045(b) and (c).

 

Evid. Code Sections 1045(b) and (c) state:

“(b) In determining relevance, the court shall examine the information in chambers in conformity with Section 915, and shall exclude from disclosure:

(1) Information consisting of complaints concerning conduct occurring more than five years before the event or transaction that is the subject of the litigation in aid of which discovery or disclosure is sought.

(2) In any criminal proceeding the conclusions of any officer investigating a complaint filed pursuant to Section 832.5 of the Penal Code.

(3) Facts sought to be disclosed that are so remote as to make disclosure of little or no practical benefit.

(c) In determining relevance where the issue in litigation concerns the policies or pattern of conduct of the employing agency, the court shall consider whether the information sought may be obtained from other records maintained by the employing agency in the regular course of agency business which would not necessitate the disclosure of individual personnel records.”

Evid. Code § 1045(a) provides broad discovery rights of information relating to an incident that directly involves the peace officer and is the subject of the litigation.  Haggerty v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1087.  However, Evidence Code § 1045(b)(1) specifically requires the court to exclude from disclosure information consisting of complaints concerning conduct occurring more than five years before the event.

 

Defendants “move pursuant to Evidence Code section 1043 for an order permitting the City and Department to use and disclose, the following documents from the custodian of records for the Department in this matter[…]” (Notice of Motion, page 2, lines 8-10). The documents sought to be used and disclosed are Exhibits 14, 18, 21, 30, and 31 of Defendants’ motion for summary judgment which are emails that are contained in Plaintiff’s personnel file dating from July 2019 to June 2020.

 

The Court finds that Defendants have met the requirements set forth in Evid. Code § 1043(b) to compel disclosure. Defendants have identified the proceeding, the parties seeking disclosure, the records sought, the governmental agency with custody and control, and the time and place where the motion will be heard.  (Notice of Motion, page 2; Decl., Nicholas M. Grether.)   Plaintiffs have described the types of records or information sought.  (Id.)

 

Defendants submitted an affidavit showing good cause for the discovery or disclosure sought. Defendants’ counsel set forth the materiality thereof to the subject matter involved in the pending litigation and stated upon reasonable belief that the governmental agency identified has the records or information from the records. (Decl., Nicolas M. Grether, ¶¶ 5-7.) Defendants’ counsel has set forth a description of the records sought and made a sufficient showing of good cause. (Id.) The declaration by Defendants’ counsel, made on information and belief, is sufficient to establish good cause. People v. Oppel (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1146, 1153, n.6.

 

Therefore, the court finds that Defendants’ counsel has provided, generally, the facts of the instant action and why the materials sought would be material to the underlying litigation. Defendants have established that good cause exists for disclosure of the emails related to Plaintiff’s personnel file, internal affairs files, and disciplinary notices because Plaintiff placed the information at issue by filing this action.

 

Thus, Defendants’ motion for disclosure of peace officer personnel records is granted.

 

The Court will conduct an in camera review of the requested records on _______________________, 2024.

 

Motion to Seal Records

 

“A record must not be filed under seal without a court order. The court must not permit a record to be filed under seal based solely on the agreement or stipulation of the parties.”  Cal Rules of Court, Rule 2.551(a).

 

“A party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record. The motion or application must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.”  California Rules of Court, Rule 2.551(b)(1).  California Rules of Court, Rule 2.551(b)(4) also provides that the party requesting that a record be filed under seal must lodge it with the court when the motion or application is made and, pending the determination of the motion or application, the lodged record will be conditionally under seal. 

 

“The court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it expressly finds facts that establish:

(1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record;

(2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record;

(3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed;

(4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and

(5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.”

Cal Rules of Court, Rule 2.550(d).

 

Defendants move for an order sealing the following documents which were filed in connection with Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication: Exhibits B to E; Exhibits 1, 12 to 33. The motion is supported by a memorandum of points and authorities and evidence submitted by Defendants. Defendants identify the following overriding interest that supports sealing and overcomes the public right of access: the protection from disclosure of peace officers’ personnel records pursuant to Penal Code § 832.7 et seq.

 

The Court finds that Defendants made a competent showing to support a finding that the overriding interest identified by Defendants overcomes the public right of access to the identified documents and that the overriding interest supports sealing the documents. Defendants have adequately shown that there is a probability that the parties’ interests will be prejudiced if the documents are made public. The Court also finds that the proposed sealing order is narrowly tailored. Defendants have proposed sealing only the specific documents noted above in support of the motion for summary judgment and/or summary adjudication. The Court finds that there are no less restrictive means to achieve the identified overriding interest. 

 

Therefore, for the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ Motion to File Peace Officer Personnel Records Under Seal is granted.

Motion for A Right to Attach Order and Issuance of Writ of Attachment

           

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED in the amount of $93,031.75, and conditioned upon plaintiff’s payment of an undertaking in the amount of $10,000 as to each defendant and submission of a proposed right to attach order and order for issuance of writ of attachment after hearing (Form AT-120).

BACKGROUND

            On September 28, 2022, plaintiff Philadelphia Indemnity Insurance Company filed a complaint against Kaladar Co., Inc. dba Cal Auto Net and Mehdi Omrani for breach of contract.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“Upon the filing of the complaint or at any time thereafter, the plaintiff may apply pursuant to this article for a right to attach order and a writ of attachment by filing an application for the order and writ with the court in which the action is sought.”  CCP § 484.010.

The application shall be executed under oath and must include: (1) a statement showing that the attachment is sought to secure the recovery on a claim upon which an attachment may be issued; (2) a statement of the amount to be secured by the attachment; (3) a statement that the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based; (4) a statement that the applicant has no information or belief that the claim is discharged or that the prosecution of the action is stayed in a proceeding under the Bankruptcy Act (11 U.S.C. section 101 et seq.); and (5) a description of the property to be attached under the writ of attachment and a statement that the plaintiff is informed and believes that such property is subject to attachment.  CCP § 484.020.

“The application [for a writ of attachment] shall be supported by an affidavit showing that the plaintiff on the facts presented would be entitled to a judgment on the claim upon which the attachment is based.”  CCP § 484.030.

The court shall issue a right to attach order if the court finds all of the following:

(1)   The claim upon which the attachment is based is one upon which an attachment may be issued.

(2)   The plaintiff has established the probable validity of the claim upon which the attachment is based.

(3)   The attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based.

(4)   The amount to be secured by the attachment is greater than zero.

CCP § 484.090.

            “A claim has ‘probable validity’ where it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim.”  CCP § 481.190.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Philadelphia Indemnity Insurance Company seeks a right to attach order and an order for issuance of writ of attachment against defendants Kaladar Co., Inc. dba Cal Auto Net and Mehdi Omrani in the total amount of $93,031.75, which includes $13,856.96 in attorney’s fees and costs paid to defense counsel, together with interest in the sum of $11,174.79 from November 2021, and $15,006 (20%) in attorney’s fees “as required to pay contingency attorney’s fees” to plaintiff’s counsel under the indemnity agreement, and $3000 in costs.

            The complaint alleges that on May 6, 2013, defendants executed a Motor Vehicle Dealer Bond Application and Indemnity Agreement in favor of plaintiff.  On April 25, 2016, plaintiff made, executed, and delivered a dealer surety bond in the sum of $50,000 to the California Department of Motor Vehicles under Vehicle Code §11710.  On June 2021 and November 2021, defendants, as indemnitors, were required to hold plaintiff harmless and indemnify plaintiff against any claim made against the bond but defendants did not do so.  Multiple claims were filed against plaintiff by alleged victims under the bond arising from alleged misconduct by defendants concerning the sale of motor vehicles.  Plaintiff gave defendants notice of each claim and demanded that defendants furnish facts to support a defense, to defend or pay the claims and hold plaintiff harmless.  Plaintiff remitted payment in the penal sum of the bond as follows:  $40,000 to Vonda Morris on June 4, 2021 and $10,000 to Noe Flores Gutierrez on November 17, 2021.  Plaintiff remitted attorney’s fees and costs in the sum of $13,856.96 for defense of the bond.  Plaintiff suffered damages for breach of the agreement in the sum of $63,856.96 with interest plus $14,367.81 in collection agency fees.

                        Probable Validity of Claim

            This motion is based on a claim for breach of a Motor Vehicle Dealer Bond Application and Indemnity Agreement.  To establish a claim for breach of contract, a plaintiff must prove: (1) existence of a contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach of the contract, and (4) damages incurred by plaintiff as a result of the breach.  Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal. App. 4th 1350, 1367.

            Plaintiff argues that a surety is entitled to indemnification for all losses, including defense costs and fees, following the principal’s default.  “If the surety is compelled to make payment for damages caused by the principal, it has the right to seek reimbursement from the principal.  The . . . financial responsibility bond . . . expressly provides for reimbursement by the principal.”  Schmitt v. Insurance Co. of North America (1991) 230 Cal. App. 3d 245, 256-57 (citations omitted).  Plaintiff submitted a declaration from Robin Wilcox, Claim Examiner, Surety Division and custodian of records, which states that plaintiff is an admitted surety insurer and furnishes bonds and undertakings to parties who have executed an express indemnity agreement.  Among the bonds typically furnished by plaintiff are license bonds including bonds in the penal sum of $50,000 required by auto dealers under Vehicle Code §11710.  The purpose of said bonds is to protect victims of misconduct committed by auto dealers.  Robin Wilcox decl., ¶1.  On April 14, 206, defendants executed an Indemnity Agreement in favor of plaintiff.  The general indemnity agreement (Complaint, Exh. A) required that defendants indemnify and hold plaintiff harmless against any claim brought against plaintiff by an alleged victim of misconduct committed by Kaladar Co., Inc. dba Cal Auto Net.  The indemnity agreement covers all losses incurred by plaintiff.  The agreement authorized plaintiff to decide whether to pay, compromise, or appeal any claim against a bond.  Para. 6 of the indemnity agreement states:  “Principal agrees that Philadelphia have the exclusive right to decide whether to pay, compromise, or appeal any claim against a Bond.”  Id., ¶2, Exh. A.  On April 25, 2016, plaintiff made, executed and delivered a dealer surety bond in the sum of $50,000 to the DMV.  Id., ¶3.  Thereafter, defendants obtained a DMV license and commenced doing business as a motor vehicle dealer in Fullerton, California.  Id., ¶4. 

            Plaintiff also presents evidence that it received three claims against the bond from consumers who purchased allegedly defective used vehicles from Kalador.  The claimants, Vonda Morris, Jorge A. Corcio, and Noe Flores Gutierrez, filed civil actions against plaintiff.  In response to providing defendants with notice of the first claim (Corcio), the attorney for Kaladar responded to plaintiff.  When the second claim came in (Morris), plaintiff sent email notice to Kaladar’s attorney and also mailed notice to defendants because plaintiff had no email address for defendants.  In response to that notice, Kaladar’s attorney responded to plaintiff.  The Gutierrez claim was the third claim and was received after the Morris claim was settled and closed.  Id., ¶5.  Kaladar’s attorney asserted to plaintiff that defendants had defenses to the claims including the contention that the vehicles were sold “as is” and that the sales agreements were subject to binding arbitration.  The sale of vehicles “as is,” however, cannot be asserted to avoid a dealer’s liability to buyers of used vehicles that have defects which have not been expressly disclosed.  Also, an arbitration clause in a motor vehicle contract cannot be asserted by a license bond surety to compel a consumer to arbitrate his/her claim against plaintiff.  Plaintiff was willing to permit defendants to assert their alleged defenses as counsel for plaintiff in the Vonda Morris action.  Id., ¶6.   Plaintiff tendered defense of the Vonda Morris action to defendants by email on March 2, 2020, demanding that the tender of defense agreement be signed and returned by March 7, 2020.  Defendants signed and returned the tender of defense on March 13, 2020.  The tender acceptance was late and plaintiff had retained its own counsel to defend the Morris action.  Plaintiff is unaware of any steps taken by defendants to resolve the Corcio action or the Gutierrez action.  Id., ¶7.  Plaintiff’s attorney obtained dismissal of the Corcio action.  Id., ¶8.  Plaintiff’s counsel evaluated and determined the Vonda Morris claim was valid and recommended to plaintiff to pay $40,000 to settle the action.  Plaintiff remitted $40,000 to Morris on June 4, 2021.  Id., ¶9.  The Gutierrez claim substantially exceeded $10,000.  Plaintiff’s attorney evaluated and determined the Gutierrez claim was valid and recommended to plaintiff to pay $10,000.  Plaintiff remitted $10,000 to Gutierrez on November 17, 2021.  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff received fee statements and remitted to plaintiff’s attorney’s fees and costs in the sum of $13, 856.96 for defense of the bond in the civil actions.  Id., ¶11.  Pursuant to the terms of the indemnity agreement, there is now due and owing the sum of $63,856.96 together with interest in the sum of $11,174.79 from November 2021.  The indemnity agreement provides for recovery of attorney’s fees.  Plaintiff is required to pay contingency attorney’s fees to plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $15,006 (20%).  Plaintiff estimates that it will incur costs in the sum of $3000, including bond fees, reporter fees, and witness fees.  Id., ¶12. 

            In opposition, defendants contend that plaintiff refused to let defendant retain its own counsel because defendant was “not timely” responsive to an artificial deadline plaintiff set for defendant to respond at the very outset of Covid-19 on March 12, 2020.  Defendants also argue that plaintiff “sabotaged the defense of these underlying claims” in that plaintiff did not obtain any documents from defendants to help in resolving the underlying claims, it never sought witness statements from defendants, or attempted to enforce arbitration of the claims.  See Brian McMahon decl. and plaintiff’s responses to discovery.  Defendants also request an exemption for “vehicles and trade materials, tangible assets in defendants’ possession.”

            In reply, plaintiff argues that defendants ignore the express terms of the indemnity agreement and that plaintiff had absolute discretion to settle claims.  In any event, plaintiff retained counsel, conducted discovery, investigated the claims, and counsel recommended settling the claims based on the absence of a defense and the likelihood the claims would succeed at trial.  Also, plaintiff contends, defendants were furnished with the opportunity to defend plaintiff in the Vonda Morris action but did not do so, and the deadline was based on the statutory deadline to file a responsive pleading.  Also, plaintiff argues, it was not required to defend the civil actions or go to trial as a condition for recovering indemnity.  Plaintiff also points out that defendants have not provided any evidence that the vehicles were not defective, and that plaintiff should not have paid the claims.  Plaintiff further contends that no assets of a corporation are exempt.  

The court finds that plaintiff’s evidence is sufficient to support all elements of a breach of contract claim against defendants and establish the probable validity of its breach of lease agreement claim.

                        Basis of Attachment

            “[A]n attachment may be issued only in an action on a claim or claims for money, each of which is based upon a contract, express or implied, where the total amount of the claim or claims is a fixed or readily ascertainable amount not less than five hundred dollars ($500) exclusive of costs, interest, and attorney’s fees.”  CCP § 483.010(a).  “An attachment may not be issued on a claim which is secured by any interest in real property arising from agreement . . . .”  CCP § 483.010(b).

            “If the action is against a defendant who is a natural person, an attachment may be issued only on a claim which arises out of the conduct by the defendant of a trade, business, or profession.”  CCP § 483.010(c).  “An attachment may not be issued on a claim against a defendant who is a natural person if the claim is based on the sale or lease of property, a license to use property, the furnishing of services, or the loan of money where the property sold or leased, or licensed for use, the services furnished, or the money loaned was used by the defendant primarily for personal, family, or household purposes.”  Id.

            In their opposition, defendants argue that plaintiff has not submitted sufficient admissible evidence of a readily ascertainable amount of damages.

            In reply, plaintiff argues that the evidence is sufficient to support the amount in damages, which is calculated based upon settlement payments made in the penal sum of the $50,000 DMV bond and attorney’s fees paid to defense counsel.

The court finds that plaintiff’s motion for issuance of a writ of attachment is based on an express contract—i.e., the motor vehicle dealer bond application, dealer surety bond, and indemnity agreement.  The total amount allegedly due on the agreement is readily ascertainable and more than $500.  Plaintiff has also demonstrated that the claim arises out of defendants’ business.

                        Purpose and Amount of Attachment

            CCP § 484.090 states that the court shall issue a right to attach order if “the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based . . . [and] the amount to be secured by the attachment is greater than zero.”  CCP § 484.090.

            Plaintiff declares that the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than recovery on its claim.  Application, ¶ 4.  The amount to be secured is greater than zero.

                        Subject Property

            Plaintiff requests attachment against a natural person.

            CCP § 487.010 provides that where the defendant is a natural person, all of the following property is subject to attachment:

(1)               Interests in real property except leasehold estates with unexpired terms of less than one year.

(2)               Accounts receivable, chattel paper, and general intangibles arising out of the conduct by the defendant of a trade, business, or profession, except any such individual claim with a principal balance of less than one hundred fifty dollars ($150).

(3)               Equipment.

(4)               Farm products.

(5)               Inventory.

(6)               Final money judgments arising out of the conduct by the defendant of a trade, business, or profession.

(7)               Money on the premises where a trade, business, or profession is conducted by the defendant and, except for the first one thousand dollars ($1,000), money located elsewhere than on such premises and deposit accounts, but, if the defendant has more than one deposit account or has at least one deposit account and money located elsewhere than on the premises where a trade, business, or profession is conducted by the defendant, the court, upon application of the plaintiff, may order that the writ of attachment be levied so that an aggregate amount of one thousand dollars ($1,000) in the form of such money and in such accounts remains free of levy.

(8)               Negotiable documents of title.

(9)               Instruments.

(10)           Securities.

(11)           Minerals or the like (including oil and gas) to be extracted.

CCP § 487.010(c).

            As to individual Mehdi Omrani, plaintiff seeks attachment of residential dwelling at 5514 Sylvia Avenue, Tarzana, CA 91356, APN 2163-017-019.

 The request for attachment of this property is appropriate under CCP §487.010(c).

                        Reduction of Amount to be Secured

            CCP § 483.015(b) provides the amount to be secured by the attachment shall be reduced by:

(1)   The amount of any money judgment in favor of the defendant and against the plaintiff that remains unsatisfied and is enforceable

(2)   The amount of any indebtedness of the plaintiff that the defendant has claimed in a cross-complaint filed in the action if the defendant’s claim is one upon which an attachment could be issued

(3)   The amount of any claim of the defendant asserted as a defense in answer pursuant to Section 431.70 if the defendant’s claim is one upon which an attachment could be issued had an action been brought on the claim when it was not barred by the statute of limitations

(4)   The value of any security interest in the property of the defendant held by the plaintiff to secure the defendant’s indebtedness claimed by the plaintiff, together with the amount by which the value of the security interest has decreased due to the act of the plaintiff or a prior holder of the security interest

CCP § 483.015(b).

            “[T]o sustain reduction in a writ amount, most courts require that the defendant provide enough evidence about its counterclaims and/or defenses to prove a prima facie case [for attachment against Plaintiff].”  Ahart, California Practice Guide: Enforcing Judgments and Debts, ¶ 4:64 (1998 rev.).

            Defendants have not asserted any reductions pursuant to CCP § 483.015(b) and thus have not shown that the amount of attachment should be further reduced.

                        Undertaking

            CCP § 489.210 requires the plaintiff to file an undertaking before issuance of a writ of attachment.  Pursuant to CCP § 489.220, the amount of the undertaking will be $10,000 absent an objection.  The failure to post an undertaking prior to the issuance of the writ of attachment renders the writ of attachment void ab initio.  Vershbow v. Reiner (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 879, 883.

            Neither party has addressed nor argued for a different amount of undertaking.

                        Costs

            CCP § 482.110 provides the court with discretion to include an estimated amount for costs and allowable attorney’s fees in the amount to be secured by the attachment.  CCP § 482.110(a).

            Plaintiff seeks attorney’s fees in the amount of $15,000 and costs in the amount of $3000, which is supported and reasonable.  

Based on the foregoing, the motion for a right to attach order and issuance of writ of attachment is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

The motion for a right to attach order and issuance of writ of attachment is GRANTED in the amount of $93,031.75.

            The granting of the motion is conditioned upon plaintiff posting an undertaking in the amount of $10,000.00 as to each defendant and submission of a proposed right to attach order and order for issuance of writ of attachment after hearing (Form AT-120).

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

Motion to Compel the Depositions of Defendant ERC Trade, LP’s Employees/Agents

 

            The court considered the moving and opposition papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On February 22, 2023, plaintiff Chris M. Oba filed a complaint against Martinez Transport LLC, Leonel Esqueda Zavala, ERC Trade, LP, HPL-Apollo, LLC, and Willy Valenzuela for negligence involving a forklift.

On May 22, 2023, the court denied defendants HPL-Apollo, LLC and Willy Valenzuela’s motion to compel arbitration.

On September 18, 2023, ERC Trade, LP filed a cross-complaint against Martinez Transport LLC, Leonel Esqueda Zavala, HPL-Apollo, LLC and Willy Valenzuela for equitable indemnity, allocation and contribution, and declaratory relief.

On December 16, 2023, the court denied ERC Trade’s motion contesting good faith settlement determination.  The court granted HPL-Apollo, LLC and Willy Valenzuela’s application for good faith settlement.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §2025.450(a) provides:  “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”

CCP §2025.450(b) provides, “A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1)        The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2)        The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”

CCP §2025.230 states:  “If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Chris M. Oba requests an order compelling defendant ERC Trade, LP’s “employees/agents” Giselle Carrasco, Nadia Bojorques, Suzanna Cervantes, and Enrique Carrasco Sr. to appear and testify at deposition.

            Plaintiff asserts that on August 10, 2023, plaintiff noticed the depositions of various employees/agents of ERC Trade, LP that were mentioned in the deposition of Jose Carrasco, a part owner of ERC Trade.  On September 13, 2023, defendant objected to the depositions on the ground that they were “not ERC Trade’s officers, directors, managing agents, or employees.” 

            Plaintiff argues that the persons are employees, agents, directors, and officers of ERC Trade, LP and that defendant is “attempting to shield their depositions by saying these individuals work for different companies, though it is clear these are simply shell companies.”

            As to the corporate structure of defendant ERC Trade, LP and “related companies,” plaintiff contends that defendant ERC Trade, LP takes fresh produce into consignment and sells it to a list of customers and takes a commission out of the sale.  Plaintiff asserts that ERC Trade, LP is a limited partnership, owned by ERC Produce, LLC as well as Jose Carrasco, Giselle Carrasco, and Enrique Carrasco, Sr., and others.  Plaintiff argues that ERC Produce is “just a shell company” based on the deposition testimony of Jose Carrasco.  The “only member of ERC Produce, LLC is a Mexican company Agricola Hermanos Carrasco” and that Empaque Rio Colorado is a sister company that is a family business and a grower that consigns products to ERC Trade and is owned by nearly the same owners of ERC Produce—Jose Carrasco, Giselle Carrasco, Enrique Carrasco, Sr., and Nadia Bojorques.  See James Trotter decl., and attachments.

                        Giselle Carrasco

            Plaintiff contends that Giselle Carrasco is an employee of ERC Trade because she is the sister of Jose Carrasco and works out of the ERC Trade, LP office in San Luis, Arizona, and works in the accounts receivable and accounts payable departments of ERC Trade, LP.

            In opposition, defendant contends that there was no deposition notice of Giselle Carrasco.

The court finds that the amended deposition notice attached to attorney Trotter’s declaration does not include her name and thus she was not served with a deposition notice.

                        Nadia Bojorques

Plaintiff asserts that Nadia Bojorques is an employee of ERC Trade because she works in the accounting departments of ERC Trade, LP and “she would [be] the individual who could run a report showing how much moneys ultimately were paid to Martinez Transport by ERC Trade, LP over the years.” 

In opposition, defendant argues that Bojorques is not an ERC Trade officer, director, managing agent, or employee.  See Jose Enrique Carrasco Encinas decl.

The court finds that the evidence is insufficient that Bojorques falls under “officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party.”  Defendant’s objections have merit.

Suzanna Cervantes

Plaintiff asserts that Suzanna Cervantes is an “agent of ERC Trade” because she is the head of the department of logistics and customs at Empaque Rio Colorado and that she was the main contact with individuals from Martinez Transport, who identified her as the dispatcher for ERC Trade.  Plaintiff contends that she “is authorized by ERC Trade to arrange Martinez Transport to work, has been authorized during the entire term of the relationship between ERC Trade and Martinez Transport, and authorized the work order underlying the subject incident.”  Plaintiff further contends that concerning the garlic that was being transported, Cervantes contacted Martinez Transport on behalf of ERC Trade to arrange for the delivery and that Ms. Martinez was asked by ERC Trade to help when they are looking for freight companies, since she has contacts. 

In opposition, defendant argues that Cervantes is not an ERC Trade officer, director, managing agent, or employee.  See Jose Enrique Carrasco Encinas decl.  Defendant asserts that it does not dispute that it authorized Cervantes to contact Martinez Transport on its behalf but that she is not a “managing agent” of defendant.

The court finds that the evidence is insufficient that Bojorques falls under “officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party.”  Defendant’s objections have merit.

            Enrique Carrasco Sr.

Plaintiff asserts that Carrasco Sr. should be considered an “officer or director” of ERC Trade because he is one of the owners of ERC Trade, ERC Produce, and Empaque Rio Colorado and that all three operate under the same trade name, ERC.

In opposition, defendant argues that Carrasco Sr. is not an ERC Trade officer, director, managing agent, or employee.  See Jose Enrique Carrasco Encinas decl.  Defendant also asserts that based on the evidence submitted by plaintiff, he has only a .7% ownership interest in ERC Trade and that plaintiff has not provided any authority to compel a shareholder who does not hold any positions with the party to appear for deposition.

The court finds that the evidence is insufficient that Carrasco Sr. falls under “officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party” and whether he is a part owner is of no import under CCP §2025.450.  Defendant’s objections have merit.

            The motion is thus DENIED.

            Sanctions

If a motion to compel deposition is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. CCP §2025.450(g)(1).

Under CCP §2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP §2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

            The court finds that sanctions are not warranted against either party.

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

            Defendant ERC Trade is ordered to give notice of ruling.

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The preliminary injunction issued on September 15, 2023 is set aside, the TRO remains in place, and the court sets an OSC hearing re preliminary injunction for [parties to address].

BACKGROUND

On April 19, 2023, plaintiff Martin Hutto filed a complaint against City of Torrance and Torrance Police Department for (1) violation of POBRA, (2) declaratory relief, and (3) Labor Code §1102.5.

On April 25, 2023, the court (Judge Frank) issued a TRO on April 25, 2023.

On September 15, 2023, the court issued a preliminary injunction.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Question:  When notice of a hearing incorrectly lists the time of the hearing (11:00) so that an attorney appearing at the noticed time will be a couple hours late (8:30), may that “late” party seek to have the ruling vacated and a new hearing take place?”  Plaintiff says “NO.”  Is a request to vacate under these conditions a disguised “reconsideration governed by Code of Civil Procedure § 1008?

“The court may, upon terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . .”  CCP §473.

            “[T]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).

            Under CCP §533, “In any action, the court may on notice modify or dissolve an injunction or temporary restraining order upon a showing that there has been a material change in the facts upon which the injunction or temporary restraining order was granted, that the law upon which the injunction or temporary restraining order was granted has changed, or that the ends of justice would be served by the modification or dissolution of the injunction or temporary restraining order.”

DISCUSSION

            Pursuant to CCP §473(b), defendants City of Torrance and Torrance Police Department request that the court set aside the preliminary injunction issued by the court on September 15, 2023, based on defense counsel’s failure to appear at the OSC hearing on September 15.

Defendants argue that the failure to appear was based on attorney mistake, inadvertence, surprise, and/or neglect, and requests that the court reset the hearing to allow it to have an opportunity to present oral argument.

            As background, on April 25, 2023, the court granted a TRO enjoining defendants from subjecting plaintiff to any punitive action based on Torrance PD Internal Affairs Case PD2022-005 and/or the March 31, 2023 Letter of Intent to Discipline, and, specifically, not to terminate plaintiff’s employment pending the May 15, 2023 hearing.  Defendants were ordered to produce by May 8, 2023 documents that plaintiff had requested and “are relevant to the disciplinary action proposed against Plaintiff.”  The OSC re preliminary injunction was set for May 16, 2023.

            Defendants filed opposition to the preliminary injunction for hearing on May 16.  The hearing was continued to June 23, 2023 as the court was unavailable.  For the hearing on May 16, the court reviewed and considered the ex parte application for the TRO as well as the evidence and points and authorities and documents attached thereto.  The court reviewed and considered the declarations and ruled on the objections.  At the hearing, plaintiff’s counsel contended that plaintiff had yet to receive the metadata on the jail video, which was the subject of the push and the allegation of excessive force.  The court indicated that such information is informative as to arguments relative to the running of the statute of limitations.  Thus, the hearing was continued to July 28, 2023.  The parties were to meet and confer and the court disallowed any further filings of points and authorities but allowed supplemental evidence to be filed on any evidence that defendants did not produce to plaintiff prior to May 9.

            At the hearing on July 28, 2023, the court and counsel conferred re verified declarations and responses required.  The parties were to discuss timeline for defendants to provide declarations to plaintiff.  The minute order states that the OSC re preliminary injunction was continued to September 11, 2023, at 8:30 a.m. 

Plaintiff served a notice of ruling re OSC stating that the hearing was continued to September 11, at 11:00 a.m.

            On September 7, 2023, defense counsel filed a Notice of Remote Appearance for the OSC hearing on September 11, at 11:00 a.m.

            On September 11, 2023, at 8:30 a.m., the matter was called for hearing.  The court found that there was no appearance by the City of Torrance, acknowledging that defendants had filed a Notice of Remote Appearance.  The minute order reflects that plaintiff’s counsel acknowledged receipt of further documents that were not produced to the court and that it is unclear to the court the extent to which there have been supplemental verifications of information given.  Plaintiff’s counsel represented that there have been more than several hundred pages and attestations that certain information relative to log either never existed or have been mechanically overwritten due to the passage of time.  The court indicated that it would sign on the basis of plaintiff’s counsel’s arguments at the hearing that there was sufficient basis to support the proposed preliminary injunction and to find that there is a probability of prevailing. 

            On September 15, 2023, the court issued a preliminary injunction.

            As to the motion, defense counsel Alex Wong explains in this declaration that at the hearing on July 28, he wrote in his notes that the hearing was continued to September 11, at 11:00 a.m., and that consistent with that belief, immediately following the hearing he documented the continued hearing date and 11:00 a.m. starting time.  He also notes that plaintiff’s counsel served a notice of ruling also indicating a hearing time of 11:00 a.m.  He states that he first learned about the “calendaring error” on September 11, at approximately 10:00 a.m. when his assistant attempted to set up the remote appearance but was unable to do so and was informed that the hearing had been scheduled for 8:30 a.m. and had taken place.  He states that he immediately went to Dept. M and was informed that the matter was calendared for 8:30 a.m.

            Defendants further argue that no party will be prejudiced in allowing the matter to be fully heard on the merits and that the City will be irreparably harmed if the City is deprived of the ability to argue its position, respond to any questions from the court, or rebut plaintiff’s arguments in court.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that the motion is an improper motion for reconsideration, the preliminary injunction was not granted due to a default, and defendants have not made any new argument that it would have made.  Plaintiff contends that the court’s ruling was based on the evidentiary record before the court and not because defense counsel failed to appear.  Plaintiff further contends that defendants have not proven harm or prejudice.  Plaintiff acknowledges that both counsel initially believed that the matter was set for 11:00 a.m.  Plaintiff requests attorney’s fees, which the court finds are not warranted.

            In reply, defendants reiterate that plaintiff’s counsel served notice of the hearing for 11:00 a.m.  Further, defendants argue that they must be afforded an opportunity to participate in the hearing, at a minimum, so that it can ascertain the factual and legal basis for the preliminary injunction so that it may seek to have the injunction modified or dissolved in the future pursuant to CCP §533.

The Court rejects the suggestion that this must be viewed as a reconsideration motion and that it must meet the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure § 1008.  That statute presupposes that a properly noticed hearing has been afforded to the parties.  Where, as here, the hearing itself took place prior to the hour shown on the notice, there is more than “excusable neglect” or “mistake” on the part of the missing attorney.  The underlying hearing process was compromised.

            The court finds that it appears that at the hearing on July 28, the September 11 hearing was set for 11:00 a.m. but through inadvertence and/or mistake it was calendared for 8:30 a.m.  The court also notes that plaintiff’s counsel sent notice of the September 11 hearing for 11:00 a.m.  Further, the minute order reflects that the court considered plaintiff’s arguments at the hearing.  Thus, in the interests of justice and in the court’s discretion, the preliminary injunction will be set aside, the TRO remains in place, and an OSC hearing re preliminary injunction will be reset to allow for the court to hear and consider oral argument from both parties.

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED. 

The preliminary injunction issued on September 15, 2023 is set aside, the TRO remains in place, and the court sets an OSC hearing re preliminary injunction for [parties to confer with the Court to select a new date.]

            Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.
