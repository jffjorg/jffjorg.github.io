DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV47554    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

WAVE INVESTMENT, LLC,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

SECURCAPITAL CORP., et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

21STCV47554

Hearing Date:

January 9, 2025

Hearing Time:    2:00 p.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: 

 

MOTION BY DEFENDANTS STEPHEN J. RUSSELL AND SECURCAPITAL CORP. FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT AND/OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Wave Investment, LLC (“Wave”) filed this action on December 30, 2021 against Defendants SecurCapital Corp. (“SecurCapital”) and Stephen J. Russell (“Russell”) (jointly, “Defendants”).

Wave filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) on November 15, 2023, alleging causes of action for (1) breach of Non-Circumvention Agreement, (2) declaratory relief, (3) breach of partnership or joint venture agreement, (4) breach of fiduciary duty, (5) conversion,

(6) fraudulent concealment, (7) fraudulent misrepresentation, (8) negligent misrepresentation,

(9) unjust enrichment, (10) accounting, and (11) constructive trust.

            On February 20, 2024, SecurCapital filed a Cross-Complaint against Wave and Mark Boyce, alleging causes of action for (1) fraudulent concealment, (2) fraudulent misrepresentation, (3) fraudulent inducement, (4) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and (5) declaratory relief.  

Russell now moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to each cause of action asserted against Russell in TAC., i.e., Wave’s first, fourth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and eleventh causes of action against Russell. In addition, SecurCapital moves for summary adjudication as to the second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action asserted against SecurCapital in Wave’s TAC. Wave opposes.

Request for Judicial Notice

The Court grants Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

Legal Standard

“[A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” ((Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs.” ((Id., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Ibid.)  

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. ((Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) If the moving party carries this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. ((Ibid.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” ((Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) When a defendant seeks summary judgment or summary adjudication, he/she must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established; or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., section 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

Discussion

A.    Allegations of the TAC

In the TAC, Wave alleges that in 2015, Carl Fairbank (“Fairbank”) founded Breakout Capital (“Breakout”), a small business lender that provides lending solutions to small businesses, along with loan collections and servicing. (TAC, ¶ 17.)  

In or around late 2018, Breakout began facing financial issues and began seeking purchasers or investors to stabilize its operations. (TAC, ¶ 18.) At Breakout’s request, from January through April 2019, Wave communicated with a number of private investors, financial institutions, and companies to gauge interest in a potential investment in Breakout and/or an acquisition of Breakout. (TAC, ¶ 19.)  

In or around early April 2019, “Boyce” of Wave met Russell, the CEO of SecurCapital. (TAC, ¶ 20.) Shortly after this meeting, Wave/Boyce and SecurCapital/Russell entered into a written Non-Circumvention Agreement. (TAC, ¶ 21.) In the Non-Circumvention Agreement, Defendants agreed “not to undertake any transaction or a series of transactions of any kind with [Breakout] or to collect any fees in connection with [Breakout] without the express prior written agreement of [Wave], which agreement may be withheld in [Wave]’s sole discretion.” (TAC, ¶ 23.) Over the following weeks, Wave, SecurCapital, and Breakout negotiated the outlines of an investment transaction whereby Wave and SecurCapital, as co-investors, would invest approximately $3,000,000 as operating capital of a newly-formed company (“Newco”), which would purchase the assets of Breakout. (TAC, ¶ 26.)   

In late April and early May 2019, the parties’ negotiations culminated in multiple written agreements related to the contemplated transaction, including: (1) an April 26, 2019 term sheet between Wave, SecurCapital, and Breakout (the “Term Sheet”); (2) an April 29, 2019 side letter agreement between Wave and SecurCapital; and (3) a May 1, 2019 standstill agreement between Wave, SecurCapital, Breakout, and Breakout Capital Holdings, Inc. (“BHI”). (TAC, ¶ 32.) The Term Sheet provided, among other things, that BHI would be formed as the contemplated NewCo. (TAC, ¶ 33.) Pursuant to the terms of the parties’ agreements, on or about May 1, 2019, Wave wired $100,000 to SecurCapital to fund Wave’s portion of the first tranche of the partnership’s investment in Breakout through BHI. (TAC, ¶ 36.)  

On or about June 29, 2019, Fairbank (Breakout’s founder) sent an email to a group of people, including others on the Breakout team and Russell, without copying Boyce or any other representative of Wave, stating that he was ready to sign the deal, and that he was “happy to wait till the 8th” as he had discussed with Russell. (TAC, ¶ 40.) When Boyce received Fairbank’s email later that day, he emailed Russell expressing concern that the deal would “close without his consent” on July 8 as suggested in Fairbank’s email, and further noting that Fairbank had not copied him on the June 29 email. (TAC, ¶ 40.) Russell responded to Boyce by denying that he had a “secret deal with Carl to close on the 8th.” (TAC, ¶ 41.)  

Wave alleges that on or about July 8, 2019, Defendants unilaterally closed a deal to purchase the assets of Breakout through BHI. (TAC, ¶ 43.) Defendants did so without the involvement or prior consent of Wave, and Wave did not receive notice of the transaction until after it had closed. (TAC, ¶ 43.) Defendants never returned the $100,000 previously paid by Wave for the first tranche of its investment in Breakout and BHI. (TAC, ¶ 44.) Wave is informed and believes that Defendants instead used that $100,000 to complete its own transaction with Breakout and BHI. (TAC, ¶ 44.)  

B.    First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract as to Russell

Russell moves for summary adjudication as to Wave’s first cause of action for breach of Non-Circumvention Agreement. In the first cause of action, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “[o]n or about April 8, 2019, Defendants entered into the Non-Circumvention Agreement with Wave...” (TAC, ¶ 48.) Wave alleges that “Defendants materially breached the Non-Circumvention Agreement by the following wrongful acts: a. Defendants engaged in direct, secret contacts with Breakout without disclosing such contacts to or obtaining approval from Wave; and b. Defendants entered into transactions to acquire Breakout without Wave’s participation or prior written approval. c. SecurCapital obtained an equity stake in BHI and Russell obtained a seat on its Board of Directors, without Wave’s participation or prior written approval.” (TAC, ¶ 51.) Wave alleges that “Defendants had neither the right nor the ability to purchase Breakout or obtain a Board seat: the Non-Circumvention Agreement expressly prohibited it.” (TAC, ¶ 52.)

Russell first asserts that the breach of contract cause of action has no merit because Russell is not a party to the Non-Circumvention Agreement.[1] Russell attaches as Exhibit 1 to his supporting declaration a copy of the “Non-Circumvention Agreement” (the “NCA”). (Russell Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1.) Russell notes that the NCA’s signature blocks provide, inter alia, as follows:

 

“By: [Signature]

Stephen, J. Russell, CEO

SecurCapital Corp

15233 Ventura Blvd., Suite 740

Sherman Oaks, CA 91403

srussell@securecapital.com.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1, p. 3.)

Russell argues that “[t]he signature block’s use of the term ‘By’ establishes that Russell executed the NCA in his representative capacity as SecurCapital’s CEO and not in any individual capacity.” (Mot. at p. 8:23-24 [emphasis omitted].) Russell cites to Carlesimo v. Schwebel (1948) 87 Cal.App.2d 482, 487, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[i]n the present case, had Schwebel appended the preposition ‘by’ immediately before his signature, there would be no doubt at all that the contract would have disclosed, on its face, not only that appellant was dealing with the corporation, but that Schwebel was signing as an agent and not as a principal. Where that appears on the face of the contract the corporation is liable and the agent is not. But, says appellant, the word ‘by’ does not appear in this signature, and therefore Schwebel must be conclusively presumed to have signed in his individual capacity, even though the evidence is susceptible of the interpretation that Schwebel signed as an agent.” (Internal citations omitted.)

However, as noted by Wave, the NCA provides that “[t]his Non-Circumvention Agreement (this ‘Agreement’) is entered into as of this_3rd day of_ March, 2019, and is by and between Stephen J. Russell, CEO SecurCapital including officers, directors, shareholders, agents, employees, consultants, attorneys and affiliates (all known as Presentee) and Mark Boyce, Wave Investments (all known as Presentor).” (Russell Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1, p. 1, emphasis added.) As Wave notes, Russell references the signature block but has “not identified any case where the preamble specifically names the signatory as a party to the Agreement…like the NCA.” (Opp’n at p. 13:5-6.) Russell contends that the language “CEO SecurCapital including officers, directors, shareholders” shows that Russell “executed the NCA on SecurCapital’s behalf and not on behalf of himself.” (Mot. at pp. 8:28-9:1, emphasis omitted.) But Russell does not appear to acknowledge the language that the NCA is “by and between Stephen J. Russell, CEO SecurCapital… and Mark Boyce, Wave Investments…” (Russell Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1, p. 1, emphasis added.)

 Russell also points to Article II, Section 2 of the NCA, which provides that “[e]ach of Presentor and Presentee hereby represents that it has full right, power and authority to execute this Agreement and to perform the actions contemplated hereby. Upon execution of this Agreement, each of Presentor and Presentee hereby binds its representatives and heirs and all subsidiaries and firms affiliated with Presentor or Presentee, as the case may be, under the terms of this Agreement.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1, p. 2, emphasis added.) Russell contends that “[h]ad the parties intended to bind Russell individually, ‘it has’ and ‘its’ would have stated ‘he has’ and ‘his,’ further showing that Russell executed the NCA only in a representative capacity.” (Mot. at p. 9:11-13.) But as noted by Wave, “[t]he NCA defines all SecurCapital parties as ‘Presentee’ and all Wave parties as ‘Presentor.’” (Opp’n at p. 13:17-18.) As set forth above, the NCA provides that “[t]his Non-Circumvention Agreement (this ‘Agreement’) is entered into as of this_3rd day of_ March, 2019, and is by and between Stephen J. Russell, CEO SecurCapital including officers, directors, shareholders, agents, employees, consultants, attorneys and affiliates (all known as Presentee) and Mark Boyce, Wave Investments (all known as Presentor).” (Russell Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1, p. 1, emphasis added.)

Russell also notes that “Boyce likewise signed [the NCA] as ‘By:’ ‘Mark Boyce, Wave Investments.’” (Mot at p. 9:15-16; Russell Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 1, p. 3.) Russell contends that “Wave brought the Breach of NCA Cause of Action alone – Boyce is not a party – demonstrating that the parties understood that Boyce and Russell each executed the NCA only in their representative capacities.” (Mot. at p. 9:16-18, emphasis omitted.) The Court is not persuaded by this argument. Russell does not provide evidence demonstrating that Wave purportedly understood that Mr. Boyce and Russell “executed the NCA only in their representative capacities.”

Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find that Russell has shown that he is not a party to the NCA.  

Next, Russell argues that he did not breach the NCA. First, Russell cites to Shoemaker v. Myers (1990) 52 Cal.3d 1, 24, where the California Supreme Court noted that “corporate agents and employees acting for and on behalf of a corporation cannot be held liable for inducing a breach of the corporation’s contract.” Wave counters that “Shoemaker is no help to Russell because he is a party to the NCA…” (Opp’n at p. 14:19-20.) Indeed, as set forth above, the Court does not find that Russell has shown that he is not a party to the NCA.

Russell also contends that he did not breach the NCA because “Russell’s discussions with Breakout were not ‘secret,’” because “Russell did not acquire Breakout or Breakout’s assets; BCH did,” and because “the NCA did not prohibit Russell from obtaining a seat on BCH’s board.” (Mot. at p. 10:2-4, emphasis omitted.)

As to the assertion that “Russell’s discussions with Breakout were not secret,” Russell points to paragraph 26 of his declaration, which provides that “[o]n or around June 26, 2019, SecurCapital’s outside counsel, who was involved in the negotiation of the Breakout acquisition, sent an email to Breakout’s counsel, copying me and Boyce, stating that ‘[a]attached you’ll find the following draft Asset Purchase and Equity Financing documents’ and that ‘[w]e consider all of the attached to be really close to their final forms.’” (Russell Decl., ¶ 26.) In addition, Russell indicates that June 29, 2019, Mr. Boyce sent Russell an email indicating, inter alia, “So I won’t be overly concerned about this, when Carl says he discussed waiting until the 8th as he discussed with you, and also he didn’t copy me on this. Should there be any concern that this would close without my consent on Monday the 8th?” (Russell Decl., ¶ 27, Ex. 10.)[2] On the same day, Russell sent an email to Mr. Boyce stating, inter alia, “[a]re you serious, this is getting idiotic, no I don’t have a secret deal with Carl to close on the 8th…I’ve told EVERYONE clearly our target is to close the WEEK of the 8th of July (not the 8th)…” (Ibid.)

Wave counters that “Russell says these discussions ‘were not ‘secret’…but Wave did not know about them.” (Opp’n at p. 15:10-11.) Wave cites to, inter alia, the supporting declaration of Mark Boyce, the founder and sole member of Wave. (Boyce Decl., ¶ 1.) Mr. Boyce states that “Wave did not give written consent for SecurCapital to close the Breakout deal. I was not aware of the agreement to close the Breakout deal on July 11, 2019 until Russell e-mailed me after the closing had occurred. Needless to say, Wave did not participate in the deal…Wave never gave consent for Russell to contact attorneys, officers, and/or employees of Breakout in June/July 2019 in connection with BCH’s acquisition of Breakout without Wave, of which I was unaware.” (Boyce Decl., ¶¶ 24-25.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Wave has raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether “Russell’s discussions with Breakout were…secret.” (Mot. at p. 10:2.) Accordingly, the Court need not address the merits of Russell’s remaining arguments that “Russell did not acquire Breakout or Breakout’s assets; BCH did,” and “the NCA did not prohibit Russell from obtaining a seat on BCH’s board.” (Mot. at p. 10:3-4, emphasis omitted.) The Court notes that “[a] motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 437c, subd. (f)(1).) In the breach of contract cause of action, Wave alleges that Defendants materially breached the Non-Circumvention Agreement” by certain “wrongful acts,” including, inter alia, that “Defendants engaged in direct, secret contacts with Breakout without disclosing such contacts to or obtaining approval from Wave…” (TAC, ¶ 51(a).) As discussed, the Court finds that Wave has raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether Russell’s discussions with Breakout were secret.

In light of the foregoing, the Court denies Russell’s motion for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action.

C.    Second and Third Causes of Action

SecurCapital moves for summary adjudication as to Wave’s second cause of action for declaratory relief and third cause of action for breach of partnership or joint venture agreement.

In the second cause of action for declaratory relief, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “[a]n actual controversy has arisen and now exists between Wave and SecurCapital. Pursuant to the parties’ statements, written agreements, and actions between April 2019 and July 2019, Wave and SecurCapital intended to and did form a partnership, or in the alternative, enter into a joint venture, for the purpose of engaging in an investment or acquisition transaction with Breakout, as well as related transactions in the logistics industry. SecurCapital subsequently has—through its statements and actions—denied the existence of a partnership or a joint venture with Wave, including by unilaterally treating Wave’s $100,000 investment in the partnership or joint venture business as an investment in SecurCapital itself.” (TAC, ¶ 57.) Wave alleges that “[a] judicial declaration as to the existence of a partnership, or in the alternative, a joint venture between Wave and SecurCapital is necessary and appropriate at this time, as SecurCapital continues to operate by itself what was supposed to be the business of the partnership or joint venture between Wave and SecurCapital, and recently has presented Wave with documents purporting to memorialize an investment by Wave in SecurCapital, rather than a co-investment by Wave and SecurCapital as a partnership or joint venture in Breakout and BHI.” (TAC, ¶ 58.)

In the third cause of action for breach of partnership or joint venture agreement, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “[p]ursuant to the parties’ statements, written agreements, and actions between April 2019 and July 2019, Wave and SecurCapital intended to and did form a partnership or joint venture for the purpose of engaging in an investment or acquisition transaction with Breakout and BHI, as well as related transactions in the logistics industry.” (TAC, ¶ 61.) Wave alleges that “SecurCapital breached the partnership or joint venture agreement by repudiating the existence of the partnership or joint venture, denying Wave’s interest in the partnership, and using the money Wave contributed for its own acquisition of the assets of Breakout.” (TAC, ¶ 63.)

SecurCapital argues that “Wave’s second and third causes of action have no merit because the parties did not enter into a ‘partnership or joint venture’ under the undisputed facts.” (Mot. at p. 10:6-7, emphasis omitted.) SecurCapital cites to Bustamante v. Intuit, Inc. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 199, 215, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he failure to reach a meeting of the minds on all material points prevents the formation of a contract even though the parties have orally agreed upon some of the terms, or have taken some action related to the contract.” (Internal emphasis omitted.) SecurCapital asserts that here, “the parties never had any ‘meeting of the minds’ as to that alleged agreement’s essential terms.” (Mot. at p. 11:9-10.)

SecurCapital first asserts that “[t]he NCA…does not provide the essential terms of any ‘partnership or joint venture.’” (Mot. at p. 11:11-12, emphasis omitted.) SecurCapital cites to the following testimony from Mr. Boyce’s deposition: “Q. Did you consider the non-circumvention agreement to be a partnership agreement? MR. MILLER: Object. Legal conclusion. Go ahead. THE WITNESS: I don’t know how to -- I don’t know how to answer that question. I considered it to be a non-circumvention agreement just like what it says. I mean, going beyond what the agreement is, I don’t see anything in the document on that. So whatever the agreement says is what I considered it to be.” (Lode Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. 14 (Boyce Depo.) at p. 74:12-22.)

SecurCapital also argues that “the ‘Side Lettter’ is non-binding and expressly requires a further ‘binding document’.” (Mot. at p. 11:15, emphasis omitted.) In the TAC, Wave alleges that “[i]n late April and early May 2019, the parties’ negotiations culminated in multiple written agreements related to the contemplated transaction, including…an April 29, 2019 side letter agreement between Wave and SecurCapital…” (TAC, ¶ 32.) Exhibit 3 to Russell’s declaration is an April 29, 2019 letter titled “Re: Side Letter related to Wave Investments & SecurCapital Transaction with Breakout Capital.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. 3.) SecurCapital notes that this April 29, 2019 letter provides, inter alia, that “[t]he parties expect to incorporate these terms into a final binding document in connection with the Acquisition, but unless and until they do so, these terms are nonbinding.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. 3.) Russell states that “SecurCapital and Wave never entered into any ‘final binding document’ that incorporated the terms of the Side Letter.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 12.) SecurCapital asserts that accordingly, “the Side Letter also cannot provide the essential terms.” (Mot. at p. 11:27.)

In addition, SecurCapital argues that “the parties never reached any agreement as to specific contributions for the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture.’” (Mot. at p. 12:8-9.) In his supporting declaration, Russell asserts that “SecurCapital and Wave never reached or entered into any agreement – written, oral, or otherwise – on the terms of any alleged ‘partnership or joint venture.’ Among other things, SecurCapital and Wave never reached any agreement on the exact contributions that supposedly would be made by SecurCapital and Wave (e.g., 51/49 or 80/20) to the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture;’ whether the Finder’s Fee referenced in the Side Letter would be included in the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture;’[3] how the Finder’s Fee would be paid; whether SecurCapital was obligated to bring or present to the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture’ all opportunities SecurCapital had after 2019; the duration of the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture;’ whether SecurCapital could terminate the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture;’ and, if so, how.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 40.)

            In the opposition, Wave asserts that “[t]here are triable issues as to whether the essential element of a partnership—an ‘intent to carry on a business for profit’—exists.” (Opp’n at p. 16:18-19.) Wave cites to Greene v. Brooks (1965) 235 Cal.App.2d 161, 165-166, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[a] partnership is an association of two or more persons to carry on a business for profit as coowners. The sharing of profits of a business is prima facie evidence that a partnership exists except where such profits are received as debts, wages or rent, interest on a loan or consideration for the sale of good will of a business or other property. The ultimate test of the existence of a partnership is the intention of the parties to carry on a definite business as coowners. Such intention may be determined from the terms of the parties’ agreement or from the surrounding circumstances. A partnership need not be evidenced by writing. It is immaterial that the parties do not designate the relationship as a partnership or realize that they are partners, for the intent may be implied from their acts.” (Internal citations omitted.)[4]

            In his supporting declaration, Mr. Boyce states that “Russell and I decided to form a partnership through our respective entities, SecurCapital and Wave, in order to work together to pursue Breakout and other acquisition opportunities that could strengthen our business,” and that “[o]nce Russell confirmed that he and SecurCapital were interested, I put together a Non-Circumvention Agreement (‘NCA’).” (Boyce Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.)

            Mr. Boyce further states that “[o]n April 16, 2019, Wave and Defendants attended an introductory meeting with Breakout, during which we learned that Breakout needed money by May 1, 2019 or it would be unable to fund payroll and operating expenses. To save our deal, we doubled down and entered into an April 29, 2019 Side Letter…The Side Letter set forth the intentions of our partnership as well as specifics for how the parties would invest in BCH as partners. It confirmed that SecurCapital and Wave (or an affiliated entity or persons) would form Newco, which both SecurCapital and Wave would contribute money to. It memorialized the expected allocation of assets between SecurCapital and Wave which was ultimately agreed would be 51/49. It also explains that they would be joint investors and that they would acquire the assets of Breakout in tranches of $500,000 each, to be split between Wave and SecurCapital.” (Boyce Decl., ¶¶ 15-16.) Mr. Boyce states, “[f]or the first ‘tranche,’ the Side Letter provides that SecurCapital would contribute $400,000 in cash and Wave would contribute $100,000 in cash. Essentially, this tranche represented a short-term loan to keep Breakout afloat pending the acquisition, to be repaid to Wave and SecurCapital in the form of equity in BCH at the time of closing. I wired the $100,000 to SecurCapital on behalf of Wave, which I always intended its portion of this tranche to be used for the partnership’s purchase of Breakout…Russell confirmed to me that SecurCapital had sent the parties’ combined $500,000 to Breakout.” (Boyce Decl., ¶¶ 17, 18.)

            In addition, Mr. Boyce states that “[i]n late April and May 2019, we drafted and executed a number of documents with the contemplated terms for the transaction in addition to the Side Letter, including various term sheets and a Standstill Agreement. Russell signed every document on behalf of SecurCapital.” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 19.) Mr. Boyce states that “[w]e shared business plans and addressed every single element that contributes to the health of a business. We shared an attorney for purposes of due diligence and negotiations with Breakout.” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 14.)

            In addition, Wave cites to the following testimony from Russell’s deposition: “Q. When you signed the non-circumvention agreement with Mr. Boyce and Wave, did you think that Breakout Finance, the newco opportunity, was outside the scope of the non-circumvention agreement? A. No. I think that the Breakout, the non-circumvent itself was part of that agreement with Boyce that we signed that day. What I’m saying is that the introduction of Breakout Capital by Mark was secondary and third stage from when we first started talking to Breakout Capital. So the non-circumvent -- if he’s the middleman and he’s put this in the bundle, we’re going to go as partners and close this deal. That’s the non-circumvent I was signing. So Breakout was part of that.” (Brody Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. O (Russell Depo.) at p. 205:1-14, emphasis added.)

            Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Wave has raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether Wave and SecurCapital formed a “partnership” or “joint venture.” As discussed, “[t]he ultimate test of the existence of a partnership is the intention of the parties to carry on a definite business as coowners. Such intention may be determined from the terms of the parties’ agreement or from the surrounding circumstances…A partnership need not be evidenced by writing…” (Greene v. Brooks, supra, 235 Cal.App.2d at pp. 165-166.)

            SecurCapital also argues in the motion that “there is no rational basis for the assessment of any damages.” (Mot. at p. 13:10.) SecurCapital notes the Bustamante Court found that “[t]o be enforceable, a promise must be definite enough that a court can determine the scope of the duty[,] and the limits of performance must be sufficiently defined to provide a rational basis for the assessment of damages.” (Bustamante v. Intuit, Inc., supra, 141 Cal.App.4th at page 209 (internal quotations and citation omitted).)

            SecurCapital cites to a copy of excerpts from Wave’s First Amended Responses to Defendants’ Form Interrogatories (Set One),   dated August 8, 2023. (Lode Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 16.) SecurCapital appears to note that Wave’s amended response to Form Interrogatory No. 9.1 provides, inter alia, that “the categories of damages that Responding Party seeks are,” inter alia, “Responding Party’s losses that otherwise resulted from Propounding Party’s breach of the Non-Circumvention Agreement, including the lost opportunity to participate in further investments and acquisitions, including but not limited to the acquisitions of Business Intelligence Group, LLC (d/b/a B.I.G. Logistics), Xcell Logistics Services Inc. (d/b/a Xcell Logistics), and Xcell Logistics de Mexico, S.C. (d/b/a Xcell Logistics).” (Lode Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 16, p. 8.) SecurCapital asserts that “Boyce was unable to testify whether the alleged partnership even existed at the time of these transactions.” (Mot. at p. 13:18-19, emphasis omitted.) But SecurCapital does not appear to provide evidence of when the asserted “B.I.G. Logistics” and “Xcell Logistics” transactions took place.

            In addition, Wave asserts that “[a]s an initial matter, Defendants do not argue that Wave’s damages from being cut out of the Breakout deal are uncertain, which requires summary judgment to be denied.” (Opp’n at p. 20:5-6.) In the third cause of action for breach of partnership or joint venture agreement, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of SecurCapital’s breaches of the partnership or joint venture agreement, Wave has suffered damages in the sum of $100,000 based upon its contribution to the partnership business or joint venture, plus the significant lost profits that Wave would have realized had SecurCapital not breached the partnership or joint venture agreement.” (TAC, ¶ 64.)[5] SecurCapital’s arguments concerning the purported lack of a “rational basis for the assessment of any damages” (Mot at p. 13:10) do not appear to address Wave’s allegation that it suffered damages in the sum of “$100,000 based upon its contribution to the partnership business or joint venture…” (TAC, ¶ 64.) As discussed, “[a] motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Id., section 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

            In light of the foregoing, the Court denies SecurCapital’s motion for summary adjudication as to the second and third causes of action.

D.    Fourth Cause of Action

Russell and SecurCapital move for summary adjudication as to Wave’s fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.

In the fourth cause of action, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “[a]s a result of the partnership or joint venture between Wave and SecurCapital, as well as SecurCapital’s leading role in negotiating the proposed transaction with Breakout and handling the funds to be invested by the partnership or joint venture in Breakout and BHI, SecurCapital owed fiduciary duties of care, loyalty, and good faith to Wave.” (TAC, ¶ 67.) Wave further alleges that “Russell, acting individually and as the principal of SecurCapital, caused Wave to repose trust and confidence in SecurCapital in connection with the partnership or joint venture’s investment in Breakout and BHI. Defendants voluntarily accepted a fiduciary role with respect to Wave, including the duty to act with the utmost good faith, loyalty, and to avoid prioritizing their own interests over the interests of the partnership or joint venture and Wave.” (TAC, ¶ 68.)

Wave alleges that “Defendants breached their fiduciary duties to Wave by: (a) unilaterally closing the transaction with Breakout and BHI without Wave’s consent or knowledge; (b) keeping Wave in the dark as to the true state of the negotiations and the other parties’ plans for closing; (c) blocking Wave out of the partnership or joint venture’s transaction with Breakout and BHI; and (d) failing to treat Wave as a partner and co-investor alongside SecurCapital in BHI, despite Wave having contributed $100,000 in partial performance of the contemplated investments by the partnership or joint venture in Breakout and BHI.” (TAC, ¶ 69.)

In the motion, Defendants assert that “[t]he TAC does not allege that Russell himself was one of the ‘partners’ or ‘joint venturers.’…Wave brings the Second and Third Causes of Action against only SecurCapital. Thus, the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture’ cannot form the basis of Russell’s alleged ‘fiduciary duty.’” (Mot. at p. 15:7-10, emphasis omitted.)

In the opposition, Wave notes that “‘[f]iduciary’ and ‘confidential’ relationships are relationships existing between parties to a transaction wherein one party is duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other. Such a relationship ordinarily arises when one party reposes a confidence in the integrity of the other, and the other voluntarily accepts that confidence.” (Brown v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 938, 959-960.) Wave asserts that “the evidence shows that Russell, acting individually and as the principal of SecurCapital, caused Wave to repose trust and confidence in SecurCapital in connection with the partnership or joint venture’s investment in Breakout and Newco. Russell voluntarily accepted Wave’s confidence—the Breakout opportunity and special knowledge about the company— pursuant to the NCA…He was the person who signed the NCA, term sheets, the Side Letter, and the Standstill Agreement.” (Opp’n at p. 21:6-11.) In his supporting declaration, Mr. Boyce states that “[c]onsistent with the NCA, we immediately began to negotiate what our partnership would look like. We agreed to set up a new company to hold Breakout and any other companies Russell and I acquired. It is the entity now called Breakout Capital Holdings, Inc. (‘BCH’), which we also referred to as ‘Newco.’…With respect to Breakout, we agreed that BCH would acquire the assets in an attractive manner, comprising Breakout’s lending business…” (Boyce Decl., ¶¶ 13-14.) Mr. Boyce also states that “[i]n late April and May 2019, we drafted and executed a number of documents with the contemplated terms for the transaction in addition to the Side Letter, including various term sheets and a Standstill Agreement. Russell signed every document on behalf of SecurCapital.” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 19.) The Court finds that Wave has raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether Russell owed fiduciary duties to Wave.

Defendants also assert that “SecurCapital and Wave never entered into the alleged ‘partnership or joint venture’ under the undisputed facts…This non-existent agreement therefore cannot establish a fiduciary relationship between SecurCapital and Wave.” (Mot. at p. 15:12-15, emphasis omitted.) But as set forth above, the Court finds that Wave has raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether Wave and SecurCapital formed a “partnership” or “joint venture.” In Hasso v. Hapke (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 107, 140, cited by Wave, the Court of Appeal noted that “[f]iduciary duties are imposed by law in certain technical, legal relationships such as those between partners or joint venturers…trustees and beneficiaries, principals and agents, and attorneys and clients…” Thus, the Court finds that Wave has also raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether SecurCapital owed fiduciary duties to Wave.

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth cause of action.

E.     Fifth Cause of Action

SecurCapital moves for summary adjudication as to Wave’s fifth cause of action for conversion. In the fifth cause of action, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “[o]n or about May 1, 2019, Wave contributed $100,000 to the partnership or joint venture for the purpose of the partnership or joint venture’s investment or acquisition transaction with Breakout. As a result, the $100,000 became an asset of the partnership or joint venture at the time of transfer…On or about July 8, 2019, SecurCapital unilaterally closed a transaction with Breakout instead of closing that transaction on behalf of the partnership or joint venture, thereby converting Wave’s $100,000 partnership or joint venture contribution to SecurCapital’s own private use.” (TAC, ¶¶ 74-75.)

SecurCapital first asserts that “[b]ecause the partnership or joint venture claim fails, the conversion claim necessarily fails as well, as there was no ‘partnership or community’ property to ‘convert.’” (Mot. at p. 16:3-4.) But as discussed, the Court finds that Wave has raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether Wave and SecurCapital formed a “partnership” or “joint venture.”

SecurCapital also argues that the conversion cause of action fails because “SecurCapital did not take any action to exercise dominion over [the] funds.” (Mot. at p. 16:5-6, emphasis omitted.) “The elements of a claim for conversion are (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property, (2) the defendant’s wrongful act or disposition of the property that interferes with the plaintiff’s possession, and (3) damages. Conversion requires the defendant to take some affirmative action to exercise dominion over or deprive a plaintiff of his or her property.” (Archer v. Coinbase, Inc. (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 266, 276 (internal citations omitted).)

In his supporting declaration, Russell indicates that “[o]n or around May 2, 2019, Boyce wired $100,000 to SecurCapital’s account. Prior to the transfer, on or around April 30, 2019, Boyce copied me on an email in which he represented that he was ‘fine with the $100kto [sic] SC.’” (Russell Decl., ¶ 17.) Russell also states that “[o]n July 10, 2019, I spoke with Boyce again by phone. I told Boyce that the deal was moving forward, as planned, and Boyce confirmed that he and Wave were not going to participate. I told Boyce that SecurCapital would wire back to the Wave [sic] the $100,000 that Boyce transferred to SecurCapital on or around May 2, 2019…Following that conversation, I emailed Boyce on July 10, 2019 that, ‘per our conversation today, SecurCapital Corp is wiring you the $100,000 from our AP ledger today.’ Boyce replied, ‘[t]o be clear, I have not asked for any money to be wired back.’” (Russell Decl., ¶¶ 30-31.) Russell states that “[o]n or around August 2, 2020, SecurCapital treated the $100,000 that Wave and/or Boyce wired to SecurCapital on or around May 2, 2019 as a SecurCapital note with interest as of May 2, 2019 and issued Boyce a SecurCapital stock certificate.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 37.) SecurCapital asserts that accordingly, “the undisputed facts show that SecurCapital did not take any affirmative action to exercise dominion over, or deprive Wave of, the $100,000…” (Mot. at p. 16:27-28, emphasis omitted.)

In the opposition, Wave asserts that “whether Defendants converted Wave’s money is a disputed fact.” (Opp’n at p. 22:15.) In his supporting declaration, Mr. Boyce states that “[f]or the first ‘tranche,’ the Side Letter provides that SecurCapital would contribute $400,000 in cash and Wave would contribute $100,000 in cash. Essentially, this tranche represented a short-term loan to keep Breakout afloat pending the acquisition, to be repaid to Wave and SecurCapital in the form of equity in BCH at the time of closing. I wired the $100,000 to SecurCapital on behalf of Wave, which I always intended its portion of this tranche to be used for the partnership’s purchase of Breakout.” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 17.) Mr. Boyce states that “SecurCapital never gave me my portion of the BCH stock representing my $100,000 investment, nor did SecurCapital ever pay me my portion of interest on the loan to Breakout or reimburse me for my expenses incurred in obtaining a suitable acquirer for Breakout…I did not consent for SecurCapital to treat Wave’s $100,000 investment in Breakout as an investment in SecurCapital, or to issue Wave securities in SecurCapital.” (Boyce Decl., ¶¶ 26-27.)[6] The Court finds that this evidence raises a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendants took “some affirmative action to exercise dominion over or deprive [Wave] of [its] property.” (Archer v. Coinbase, Inc., supra, 53 Cal.App.5th at p. 276.)

SecurCapital also asserts that “SecurCapital…did not take [the] money or engage in any wrongful act as to it. Where, as here, property was lawfully conveyed to a defendant, there is no conversion. (Mot. at p. 16:19-20, emphasis omitted.) SecurCapital cites to Gardena Valley Airport, Inc. v. All American Sports Enterprises, Inc. (1964) 230 Cal.App.2d 478, 482, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[w]here the original possession is lawfully obtained, or there is an acknowledgment of the mortgagor’s interest, the dominion exercised by the mortgagee is not unlawful and thus not a conversion.” Wave counters that “SecurCapital can be liable for conversion for using Wave’s funds in a manner not expressly authorized by Wave.” (Opp’n at pp. 23:26-24:1.) In SP Inv. Fund I LLC v. Cattell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 898, 907, cited by Wave, the Court of Appeal noted that “[i]n this case, SP alleges that Cattell received monetary distributions from the Partnership that, under the Agreement, he held in trust for SP’s benefit and he has refused to turn those funds over to SP. Its conversion claim, therefore, is not a generalized claim for money but rather a claim for a specific identifiable sum of money received by Cattell for SP’s benefit. Thus, SP adequately stated a cause of action for conversion.” Similarly here, Wave alleges that it “contributed $100,000 to the partnership or joint venture for the purpose of the partnership or joint venture’s investment or acquisition transaction with Breakout,” and that “SecurCapital unilaterally closed a transaction with Breakout instead of closing that transaction on behalf of the partnership or joint venture, thereby converting Wave’s $100,000 partnership or joint venture contribution to SecurCapital’s own private use.” (TAC, ¶¶ 74-75.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies SecurCapital’s motion for summary adjudication as to the fifth cause of action.

F.     Sixth, Seventh, and Eighth Causes of Action

Russell and SecurCapital move for summary adjudication as to Wave’s sixth cause of action for fraudulent concealment, seventh cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation, and eighth cause of action for negligent misrepresentation.

In the sixth cause of action for fraudulent concealment, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “Defendants fraudulently and despicably concealed material information regarding its secret negotiations with Breakout to oust Wave from the very business opportunity that Wave introduced them to.” (TAC, ¶ 79.) Wave alleges that “Defendants concealed the fact that, contrary to their assurance to Wave that they had no ‘secret deal with Carl to close on the 8th,’ they were doing exactly that: Defendants negotiated a deal with Breakout to close on July 8, 2019 that did not involve Wave. The transaction between Defendants and Breakout did in fact close on July 8, 2019.” (TAC, ¶ 80.)

In the seventh cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “[n]ot only did Defendants fraudulently and maliciously conceal their contacts and negotiations with Breakout to the exclusion of Wave, they also blatantly lied to Wave about not having a deal to close on July 8th when confronted about it specifically.” (TAC, ¶ 86.) In the eighth cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, Wave alleges, inter alia, that “when confronted by Wave on June 29, 2019, Defendants misrepresented to Wave that they did not have a secret deal with Breakout to close on July 8, 2019. Defendants knew, or should have known, their assurance to Wave was false. Defendants made this misrepresentation and omission of material facts to induce Wave’s reliance.” (TAC, ¶ 99.)

Defendants assert that the sixth through eighth causes of action have no merit because “Defendants did not conceal or misrepresent material facts.” (Mot. at p. 17:2-3.) “The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or scienter); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638 (internal quotations omitted).) “The tort of negligent misrepresentation, a species of the tort of deceit, does not require intent to defraud but only the assertion, as a fact, of that which is not true, by one who has no reasonable ground for believing it to be true.” (Conroy v. Regents of University of California (2009) 45 Cal.4th 1244, 1255 (internal citation omitted).)

As discussed above, Defendants indicate that on June 29, 2019, Mr. Boyce sent Russell an email indicating, inter alia, “So I won’t be overly concerned about this, when Carl says he discussed waiting until the 8th as he discussed with you, and also he didn’t copy me on this. Should there be any concern that this would close without my consent on Monday the 8th?” (Russell Decl., ¶ 27, Ex. 10.) On the same day, Russell sent an email to Mr. Boyce stating, inter alia, “[a]re you serious, this is getting idiotic, no I don’t have a secret deal with Carl to close on the 8th…I’ve told EVERYONE clearly our target is to close the WEEK of the 8th of July (not the 8th)…” (Ibid.) Defendants assert that “[t]he undisputed evidence shows that the transaction did, in fact, close on July 11, 2019 – i.e., the week of the 8th, not ‘Monday the 8th.’…As there thus was no ‘concealment’ or ‘false representation,’ Wave cannot establish the first element of any of its three claims for fraud and deceit.” (Mot. at p. 18:7-10, emphasis omitted.) In his declaration, Russell states that “[t]he Breakout acquisition at issue in this lawsuit closed on July 11, 2019, which resulted in BCH acquiring certain of Breakout’s assets.” (Russell Decl., ¶ 32.)

But as noted by Wave, the sixth through eighth causes of action are not based on whether the transaction closed on July 11, 2019, rather than July 8, 2019. As set forth above, the alleged misrepresentation is that Defendants lied to Wave about not having a deal to close with Breakout when confronted about it. (TAC, ¶¶ 86, 99.) As discussed, Wave alleges that “Defendants concealed the fact that, contrary to their assurance to Wave that they had no ‘secret deal with Carl to close on the 8th,’ they were doing exactly that…” (TAC, ¶ 80.)

Defendants also assert that “Wave’s alleged reliance on Russell’s statement about the closing of the Breakout transaction was not justifiable in any event.” (Mot. at p. 18:15-16.) In the fraudulent concealment cause of action, Wave alleges that “Wave was completely unaware of Defendants’ true intentions and plan. If Wave had known, it would have exercised its right and discretion under the Non-Circumvention Agreement to block Defendants’ deal with Breakout and would not have allowed the deal to close. Wave would not have relied on Defendants’ representation about not having a secret deal with Breakout and would have informed Breakout of its rights under the Non-Circumvention Agreement to block the deal.” (TAC, ¶ 81.)

In the fraudulent misrepresentation cause of action, Wave alleges that “Wave changed its

position and relied on Defendants’ false representation described herein. Had Wave known Defendants’ true intent, it would have exercised its right and discretion under the Non-Circumvention Agreement to block Defendants’ deal with Breakout and would not have allowed the deal to close. Wave would not have relied on Defendants’ representation about not having a secret deal with Breakout and would have informed Breakout of its rights under the Non-Circumvention Agreement to block the deal.” (TAC, ¶ 89.)

In the negligent misrepresentation cause of action, Wave alleges that “Wave justifiably relied on Defendants’ misrepresentation to their detriment. Defendants’ material misrepresentation caused Wave to, among other things: (1) believe that Defendants were still acting as its partner and for their joint interest; (2) believe that Defendants have not gone around behind Wave’s back to engage in secret negotiations with Breakout directly, to the exclusion of Wave; (3) believe that there was no need to inform Breakout about the Non-Circumvention Agreement and Wave’s right and discretion to halt any transaction between Defendants and Breakout; and (4) not exercise its right and discretion pursuant to the Non-Circumvention to preclude Defendants’ transaction with Breakout…Had Defendants not misrepresented to Wave that they had no secret deal with Breakout, Wave would have exercised its right and discretion under the Non-Circumvention Agreement to block the deal and would not have allowed the deal to close.” (TAC, ¶¶ 100-101.)

Defendants assert that “Russell’s email expressly stated that ‘I’ve told EVERYONE clearly our target is to close the WEEK of the 8th of July (not the 8th).’…Because the email clearly stated that the target was to close the transaction ‘the WEEK of the 8th,’ which is in fact what happened…Wave could not have justifiably relied on any imagined representation that the transaction would not close that week.” (Mot. at p. 18:18-22, emphasis omitted.)

In the opposition, Wave argues that whether its reliance was justifiable is a disputed fact.

In his supporting declaration, Mr. Boyce states that “[i]f I had known there was a deal to close without Wave’s consent, I would have reminded Breakout of Wave’s rights under the NCA to block the deal…However, the lawyers for Breakout were leading the preparation of the draft deal documents, and because Russell told me there was no ‘secret deal,’ and assured me he would not close without Wave’s consent and participation, I did nothing.” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 22.) Mr. Boyce states that “Wave did not give written consent for SecurCapital to close the Breakout deal. I was not aware of the agreement to close the Breakout deal on July 11, 2019 until Russell e-mailed me after the closing had occurred. Needless to say, Wave did not participate in the deal.” (Boyce Decl., ¶ 24.) The Court finds that Wave has raised a triable issue of material fact as to Wave’s “justifiable reliance” on Russell’s June 29, 2019 statements concerning the subject deal.

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.

G.    Ninth Cause of Action

Russell and SecurCapital move for summary adjudication as to Wave’s ninth cause of action for unjust enrichment. In the ninth cause of action, Wave alleges that “Defendants took the $100,000 payment Wave submitted for the first tranche of Wave and SecurCapital’s joint investment or acquisition transaction with Breakout, and instead of using it for that purpose, converted the $100,000 for their own private use…As a result of their actions, Defendants have been enriched by taking the full value of Wave and SecurCapital’s joint investment in Breakout and BHI for themselves, rather than sharing it as agreed with Wave.” (TAC, ¶¶ 107-108.)

Wave further alleges that “[o]n or about July 8, 2019, Defendants unilaterally and without Wave’s permission closed a transaction with Breakout instead of closing that transaction on behalf of the partnership or joint venture formed between Wave and SecurCapital. Defendants thereby violated their agreement not to close any transaction with Breakout without Wave’s express prior consent, as well as their agreement to engage in a joint transaction whereby Wave and SecurCapital would be co-investors in Breakout and BHI.” (TAC, ¶ 106.)

Defendants assert that the unjust enrichment cause of action has no merit “because Wave alleges contracts between the parties.” (Mot. at p. 19:2-3.) Defendants cite to California Medical Assn. v. Aetna U.S. Healthcare of California, Inc. (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 151, 172, where the Court of Appeal found that “as a matter of law, a quasi-contract action for unjust enrichment does not lie where, as here, express binding agreements exist and define the parties’ rights.” In addition, in Lance Camper Manufacturing Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 203, cited by Defendants, the Court of Appeal noted that “the Insured has alleged the existence and validity of an enforceable written contract between the parties in its first two causes of action. The Insured then realleges the existence of the written contract in its claim of a quasi-contract. This is internally inconsistent. The Insured must allege that the express contract is void or was rescinded in order to proceed with its quasi-contract claim.”

In the opposition, Wave asserts that “[t]his is not a case where there are undisputed express written agreements governing the entire subject matter. The NCA does not encompass Wave’s $100,000 loan, for instance.” (Opp’n at p. 26:10-12.) But in the third cause of action for breach of partnership or joint venture agreement, Wave alleges that it “performed all the terms and conditions required by the parties’ partnership or joint venture agreement to be performed by Wave, including by contributing $100,000 to the partnership on or about May 1, 2019.” (TAC, ¶ 62, emphasis added.) Wave alleges that “[p]ursuant to the parties’ statements, written agreements, and actions between April 2019 and July 2019, Wave and SecurCapital intended to and did form a partnership or joint venture…” (TAC, ¶ 61.) Defendants note that “[a]n express contract is one, the terms of which are stated in words.” (Civ. Code, § 1620.) As discussed, “a quasi-contract action for unjust enrichment does not lie where…express binding agreements exist and define the parties’ rights.” (California Medical Assn. v. Aetna U.S. Healthcare of California, Inc., supra, at p. 172.) The Court does not find that Wave has shown that express binding agreements do not exist in this action that pertain to Wave’s $100,000 loan.

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the ninth cause of action.

H.    Tenth and Eleventh Causes of Action

SecurCapital moves for summary adjudication as to Wave’s tenth cause of action for accounting. Russell and SecurCapital move for summary adjudication as to Wave’s eleventh cause of action for constructive trust. Defendants argue that “Wave’s tenth and eleventh causes of action have no merit because they are wholly derivative of Wave’s failed breach of fiduciary duty claim.” (Mot. at p. 19:21-22.) As set forth above, the Court finds that Wave has raised triable issues of material fact as to whether SecurCapital and Russell owed fiduciary duties to Wave. As discussed, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty. Accordingly, the Court also denies SecurCapital’s motion for summary adjudication as to the tenth cause of action, as well as Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the eleventh cause of action.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Russell’s motion for summary judgment. The Court grants Russell’s motion for summary adjudication as to the ninth cause of action of the TAC. Russell’s motion for summary adjudication is otherwise denied.

The Court grants SecurCapital’s motion for summary adjudication as to the ninth cause of action of the TAC. SecurCapital’s motion for summary adjudication is otherwise denied.

Discussion

A.    Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application to Advance the Hearing on Plaintiff’s “Motion for Order”

As set forth above, Plaintiff moves ex parte “to advance the hearing on [Plaintiff’s] Motion for Order to Deem the Real Property Commonly Known as 5930 Beeman Avenue, Valley Village, California 91607 Community Property and for an Order that Creditor’s Judgment Lien Attach to the Property (the ‘Motion for Order’), which was filed on November 27, 2024, and is currently set for hearing on May 1, 2025, to January 19, 2025, or alternatively, to the first available date on the Court’s calendar in the next sixty (60) days.” (Ex Parte Application at p. 2:5-11.)

Plaintiff cites to California Rules of Court, rule 1.10, which provides that “[u]nless otherwise provided by law, the court may extend or shorten the time within which a party must perform any act under the rules.” Plaintiff also cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 128, subdivision (a)(3), which provides that “[e]very court shall have the power to do all of the following:…(3) To provide for the orderly conduct of proceedings before it, or its officers.”

Plaintiff argues that “[g]ood cause exists for the relief requested herein because Debtor and Harrosh have demonstrated calculated efforts to divert income and assets in order to avoid paying [Plaintiff] the amount due pursuant to the judgment.” (Ex Parte Application at p. 6:13-15, emphasis omitted.) Plaintiff notes that on September 13, 2024, the Court entered an “Order on Motion to Garnish Wages of Spouse of Judgment Debtor and for Assignment Order Against Spouse” in this action. Judgment was entered on October 25, 2018, in the principal amount of $190,000.00. Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[o]n October 4, 2024, less than three (3) weeks after the Order, Debtor and Harrosh encumbered [5930 Beeman Avenue, Valley Village, California 91607 (the ‘Property’)] by taking a second deed of trust in the amount of $285,127.00, secured by the Property (the ‘2nd DOT’) recorded with the Los Angeles County Recorder’s office as instrument number 20240682403.” (Medioni Decl., ¶ 11.) B.     Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application for a Temporary Restraining Order

A temporary restraining order may issue when “[i]t appears from facts shown by affidavit or by the verified complaint that great or irreparable injury will result to the applicant before the matter can be heard on notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 527, subd. (c)(1).) “[T]rial courts should evaluate two interrelated factors when deciding whether or not to issue [a restraining order]. The first is the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits at trial. The second is the interim harm that the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the [restraining order] were denied as compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the [order] were issued.” (Church of Christ in Hollywood v. Superior Court (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1244, 1251.)

 

“The trial court’s determination must be guided by a mix of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff’s showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support [a restraining order]…Of course, [t]he scope of available preliminary relief is necessarily limited by the scope of the relief likely to be obtained at trial on the merits…A trial court may not grant a [restraining order], regardless of the balance of interim harm, unless there is some possibility that the plaintiff would ultimately prevail on the merits of the claim.”(Church of Christ in Hollywood v. Superior Court, supra, 99 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1251-1252 [internal quotations omitted].)

            Furthermore, “an injunctive order should be limited in scope to the subject of the litigation.” (City of Redlands v. County of San Bernardino (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 398, 415.)

Here, Plaintiff asserts that he has a “reasonable probability of success on the merits.” (Ex Parte Application at p. 8:2.) Plaintiff cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 708.240, subdivision (b)[1], which provides that “[t]he judgment creditor may apply to the court in which an action under this article is pending for either or both of the following:…(b) A temporary restraining order or a preliminary injunction or both, restraining the third person from transferring to any person or otherwise disposing of the property in which the judgment debtor is claimed to have an interest, pursuant to Chapter 3 (commencing with Section 525) of Title 7, and the court may make, dissolve, and modify such orders as provided therein.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.240, subd. (b).)

As set forth above, the Court entered a Judgment in this action on October 25, 2018 indicating that “judgment is entered in favor of Plaintiff Daniel Perez (“Plaintiff”) and against defendants Joseph Safran, an individual (“Safran”) and One Capital Group, Inc., a California Corporation (“One Capital”) jointly and severally, in the sum of $190,000.00.” (Emphasis in original.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[Safran] has made absolutely zero payments towards the Judgment.” (Medioni Decl., ¶ 15, emphasis omitted.)

Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[o]n August 7, 2020, Harrosh purchased the real property commonly known as 5930 Beeman Avenue, Valley Village, California 91607 (the ‘Property’) for $1,200,000.00.” (Medioni Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. 3.) As set forth above, on September 13, 2024, the Court issued an Order in this action providing, inter alia, as follows:

 

“That the following right to payment due, or to become due, including, but not limited to rent, referral fees, fees for services, contingency fees, royalties, commissions, payments in general, payments from sales, payments from leases, payments from refinances, payment from loan originations, payments from relocations, payments from a patent or copyright, advances, accounts receivable, general intangibles (as defined by Commercial Code Section 9102(a)(42)), judgments, instruments (as defined by Code of Civil Procedure Section 708.510(a) and the comment to Section 708.510), etc. (the Funds) from all third parties, including, but not limited to, Sapir Realty Corporation, a California corporation and doing business as Weichert, Realtors – All Stars, and the tenant(s) occupying the real property commonly known as 5930 Beeman Avenue, Valley Village, California 91607, be, and hereby are, assigned to Creditor’s counsel c/o Daniel Medioni, Esq. of Wolf, Rifkin, Shapiro, Schulman & Rabkin, LLP, 11400 West Olympic Boulevard, 9th Floor, Los Angeles, California 90064, to be applied to the judgment herein until such judgment is fully satisfied, including interest thereon. Judgment was entered on October 25, 2018, in the principal amount of $190,000.00. The balance on the judgment is $262,597.70 as of September 23, 2022 (the date the last writ of execution was issued) and is accruing interest at the rate of $52.05 per day from and after September 24, 2022…

 

Harrosh and any representative, agent, employee or attorney of Harrosh and any person(s) in active concert and participating with Harrosh are hereby restrained from encumbering, assigning, disposing of or spending the Funds and/or all rights to payment thereunder…”

Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[o]n October 4, 2024, less than three (3) weeks after the Order, Debtor and Harrosh encumbered the Property by taking a second deed of trust in the amount of $285,127.00, secured by the Property (the ‘2nd DOT’) recorded with the Los Angeles County Recorder’s office as instrument number 20240682403.” (Medioni Decl., ¶ 11.) Plaintiff’s counsel asserts that “[t]he 2nd DOT has reduced the amount of equity in the Property, which is Debtor and Harrosh’s only known asset.” (Meidioni Decl., ¶ 12.)

As discussed, on November 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for an Order to Deem the Real Property Commonly Known as 5930 Beeman Avenue, Valley Village, CA 91607 Community Property and for an Order that Plaintiff’s Judgment Lien Attach to the Property. In the November 27, 2024 motion, Plaintiff asserts that “[j]udgment debtor [Safran] has been married to Karen Harrosh…since 2001,” and that “[5930 Beeman Avenue, Valley Village, California 91607] is a community asset that is subject to enforcement of the Judgment.” (Plaintiff’s November 27, 2024 Motion at p. 1:3-4; 7:12.) Per Code of Civil Procedure section 708.240, subdivision (b), Plaintiff, as the judgment creditor, may apply for a temporary restraining order restraining Harrosh “from transferring to any person or otherwise disposing of the property in which [Safran] is claimed to have an interest…”

Plaintiff asserts that he will suffer irreparable injury if a temporary restraining order is not issued. Plaintiff’s counsel asserts that “[Safran] and Harrosh encumbered the Property with the 2nd DOT for the express purpose of thwarting [Plaintiff’s] ability to enforce the Judgment against the Property,” and that “[Safran] has made absolutely zero payments towards the Judgment.” (Medioni Decl., ¶¶ 13, 15, emphasis omitted.) Plaintiff argues that “[Safran] and Harrosh will not suffer any damage whatsoever by the imposition of the injunction. Debtor is required by law to comply with the judgement ordered by the Court…” (Ex Parte Application at p. 9:11-13.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has shown a likelihood of prevailing on the merits (as discussed, Judgment has been entered in favor of Plaintiff). The Court finds that Plaintiff has shown that he is likely to sustain interim harm if the temporary restraining order is denied, as compared to any purported harm that Safran is likely to suffer if the order were issued. In addition, as discussed, the ex parte application is unopposed. Thus, Safran does not dispute that the requested injunctive relief is warranted here.
