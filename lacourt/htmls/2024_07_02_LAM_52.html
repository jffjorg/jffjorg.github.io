Case Number: 23STCV23403    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendants’ Motion to Stay During Pendency of Appeal

Defendants California Clinic Management, LLC dba Acuity Eye Group, Acuity Holdings, LLC, Trilogy Eye Medical Group, Inc., Tom Chang, M.D., and Michael Samuel, M.D., move to stay this action pending their appeal of the court’s order denying their motion to compel arbitration.   

Defendants argue the court must stay the action pending appeal because the parties agreed to adopt the procedures of the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA).  The FAA permits an interlocutory appeal of an order denying a motion to compel arbitration.  (9 U.S.C. § 16(a)(1)(B).)  In federal court, “[t]he district court must stay its proceedings” pending such an interlocutory appeal.  (Coinbase, Inc. v. Bielski (2023) 599 U.S. 736, 738 (Coinbase).)  The California Arbitration Act (CAA) also permits appeal of an order denying a motion to compel arbitration.  (CCP § 1294(a).)  But such an appeal “shall not automatically say any proceedings in the trial court during the pendency of the appeal.”  (Ibid.)  Defendants also argue that, despite Code of Civil Procedure section 1294, staying the action is mandatory under California law for other reasons.

The court does not reach the issue of whether staying the action pending appeal is mandatory.  The court will instead exercise its discretion to stay the action.  “Even when the statutes do not call for an automatic stay on appeal, the trial and appellate courts both have the power to issue discretionary stays.”  (Daly v. San Bernardino County Bd. of Supervisors (2021) 11 Cal.5th 1030, 1039 (Daly).)  “ ‘[A] court ordinarily has inherent power, in its discretion, to stay proceedings when such a stay will accommodate the ends of justice.’ ”  (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 141.)  “ ‘[T]he power to stay proceedings is incidental to the power inherent in every court to control the disposition of the causes on its docket with economy of time and effort for itself, for counsel, and for litigants.’ ”  (Ibid.)

Plaintiff’s opposition argues the court should consider the following four factors: “ ‘(1) whether the stay applicant has made a strong showing that he is likely to succeed on the merits; (2) whether the applicant will be irreparably injured absent a stay; (3) whether issuance of the stay will substantially injure the other parties interested in the proceeding; and (4) where the public interest lies.’ ”  (Daly, supra, 11 Cal.4th at p. 1054, fn. 13 [citing federal authority].)  California courts have not adopted these factors.  The court has considered these factors and other relevant factors in deciding whether to grant a stay.

 

1. Likelihood of Success on Appeal

Defendants make a moderate showing of likelihood of success on appeal.  The court denied their motion to compel arbitration for two reasons.  First, the court ruled plaintiff was entitled to invalidate the agreement (at least in part) under the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act (EFAA), which Congress enacted in 2022.  The court’s ruling relied on a recent decision interpreting the EFAA.  (Kader v. Southern California Medical Center, Inc. (2024) 99 Cal.App.5th 214.)  This area of law is still evolving.  Defendants may be able to succeed on their appeal as to this issue.       

Second, the court found the arbitration agreement was permeated by unconscionability.  Defendants’ papers in support of this motion do not address this basis for the court’s order.  Nonetheless, defendants will likely be able to make reasonable arguments on appeal regarding unconscionability. 

2. Irreparable Injury to Defendants

            Permitting this action to continue pending appeal could irreparably harm defendants.  In addition to the four factors used by federal courts, Daly noted the rule in the State of Washington: “ ‘[W]hether a stay pending appeal should be granted depends on (1) whether the issue presented by the appeal is debatable, and (2) whether a stay is necessary to preserve for the movant the fruits of a successful appeal, considering the equities of the situation.’ ”  (Daly, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 1054.) 

Not staying the action could completely deprive defendants of the fruits of a successful appeal.  In discussing an automatic stay pending appeal in federal court, the United States Supreme Court stated: “The common practice… is for a district court to stay its proceedings while the interlocutory appeal on arbitrability is ongoing.  That common practice reflects common sense.  Absent an automatic stay of district court proceedings, Congress’s decision … to afford a right to an interlocutory appeal would be largely nullified.  If the district court could move forward with pre-trial and trial proceedings while the appeal on arbitrability was ongoing, then many of the asserted benefits of arbitration (efficiency, less expense, less intrusive discovery, and the like) would be irretrievably lost—even if the court of appeals later concluded that the case actually had belonged in arbitration all along.  Absent a stay, parties also could be forced to settle to avoid the district court proceedings (including discovery and trial) that they contracted to avoid through arbitration.”  (Coinbase supra, 599 U.S. at pp. 742–743.)

The Court of Appeal has similarly noted, “In the absence of a stay, the continuation of the proceedings in the trial court disrupts the arbitration proceedings and can render them ineffective.”  (Federal Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (Mackey) (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1370, 1375.) 

The same reasoning applies here.  If defendants succeed on appeal, they would be entitled to enforce the arbitration agreement.  This case is set for trial on June 4, 2025.  There is a realistic chance that, without a stay, this action may be fully adjudicated before the Court of Appeal issues a decision.  In that scenario, defendants will have already tried the action in court.  They would get no benefit from their right to arbitrate.  Even if the action is not fully adjudicated before the Court of Appeal issues an opinion, defendants will be required to engage in substantial litigation they otherwise might have avoided.   

3. Injury to Plaintiff

Staying the action is not likely to significantly injure plaintiff.  Plaintiff relies on the adage, “[O]ne of the basic principles of our legal system” is that “justice delayed is justice denied.”  (Dietrich v. Boeing Company (9th Cir. 2021) 14 F.4th 1089, 1095.)  Staying the action would delay plaintiff’s efforts to obtain relief for her causes of action against defendants.  But plaintiff identifies no specific injury or prejudice.  She makes the following generic argument: “Delaying this case any longer would stand in the way of justice, as key witnesses may move away, witness memories will inevitably fade, and documentary evidence could be lost.”  (Opp., p. 14.)  She provides no details about these potential injuries.  Moreover, plaintiff alleges defendants fired her on February 23, 2022.  (Comp., ¶ 123.)  She filed this action 17 months later on September 27, 2023. 

The civil legal system is rarely speedy.  Without a stay, the trial would take place over three years after plaintiff’s termination.  Plaintiff does not show that additional delay is likely to result in losing evidence or other prejudice.  While staying the action will further delay proceedings, it will not be a serious impediment to justice.             

4. Public Interest

            The public interest moderately favors plaintiff.  On the one hand, there is “a ‘ “strong public policy in favor of arbitration as a speedy and relatively inexpensive means of dispute resolution.” ’ ”  (Wagner Construction Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 25.) 

On the other hand, Congress enacted the EFAA to ensure any “person alleging conduct constituting a sexual harassment dispute or sexual assault dispute” can litigate in court, despite their prior agreements waiving that right.  (9 U.S.C. § 402(a).)  And the purpose of the Legislature’s recent amendment to Code of Civil Procedure section 1294(a) is to “give[] courts the discretion to prevent corporations from using a common delay tactic against workers and consumers.”  (Assem. Com. on Appr., Analysis of Sen. Bill No. 365 (2023-2024 Reg. Sess.) July 12, 2023, p. 1.)  This interest outweighs the competing interest in favor of arbitration.

The court notes, however, that defendants’ appeal is not just a delay tactic.  They made colorable arguments in support of their motion to compel arbitration.  Their appeal is a legitimate effort to enforce an arbitration contract.

After considering the totality of the circumstances, the court finds staying this action pending appeal serves the interests of justice and judicial economy.    

Disposition

            Defendants California Clinic Management, LLC dba Acuity Eye Group, Acuity Holdings, LLC, Trilogy Eye Medical Group, Inc., Tom Chang, M.D., and Michael Samuel, M.D.’s motion to stay the action pending appeal is granted.  The court hereby stays the entire action pending defendants’ appeal.
