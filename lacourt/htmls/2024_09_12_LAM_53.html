Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

The court grants defendant HDI Global Insurance Company’s request for judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (c).)

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS 

The court rules on defendant HDBI Global Insurance Company’s evidentiary objections, filed on August 23, 2024, as follows:

The court sustains Objections Nos. 1-2.

The court overrules Objections Nos. 3-5.

LEGAL STANDARD

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant or cross-defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “Once the defendant or cross-defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)  “When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Id. at p. 467; Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

DISCUSSION

Defendant HDI Global Insurance Company (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order granting its motion for summary judgment on the Complaint filed by plaintiffs J Cellars Investment LLC, d/b/a Joseph Cellars Winery, and Joseph Bartholomew (“Plaintiffs”) or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on the second cause of action for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing and Plaintiffs’ claim for punitive damages.

1.     First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

“‘A cause of action for breach of contract requires proof of the following elements: (1) existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) damages to plaintiff as a result of the breach.’”  (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402.)

Defendant issued the Commercial Lines Policy, number GK28X000385-01, for the period of May 16, 2020 to May 16, 2021 (the “Policy”) to plaintiff Joseph Cellars Winery, LLC, located at 4455 St. Helena Highway, Calistoga, California 94515 (the “Insured Property”).  (Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) No. 1.)  On September 28, 2020, the City of Calistoga issued a mandatory evacuation order for the city due to the Glass Fire, which lasted until October 7, 2020.  (UMF No. 10; RJN Ex. A, p. 1 [excerpt of City of Calistoga Mandatory Evacuation from September 28, 2020 to October 7, 2020].)  Plaintiffs submitted a claim to Defendant for losses caused by the Glass Fire on October 19, 2020.  (Def. Compendium of Evidence (“Def. Ex.”), Ex. 3, Property Loss Notice, p. 00068 [describing loss and damage to be the “sustained Business Income loss due to mandatory evacuation orders from the Glass Fire”].)  Defendant paid a total of $159,588 to Plaintiffs on June 21, 2021, consisting of $147,708 paid under the “Loss of Income – Civil Authority” provision, and $11,800 paid under the “Extra Expense” provision to rent a generator.  (UMF No. 37; Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 5; Morris Decl., ¶¶ 10-12.) 

Plaintiffs allege that Defendant breached the Policy “by failing to pay [P]laintiffs all the sums due and owing under the insurance contract.”  (Compl., ¶ 20.)   In its motion, Defendant contends that (1) it paid all benefits required for (i) covered equipment damage and (ii) lost business income for Plaintiffs’ custom crush and tasting room revenues, and (2) there is no coverage for damage caused to the air conditioning unit and the lost business income related to the 2020 Estate Cabernet Sauvignon wine (the “2020 Estate Wine”). 

 

a.      Covered Equipment Damage

The court finds that Defendant has not met its burden of showing that the first cause of action for breach of contract has no merit because Defendant has not shown that the element of breach of the Policy cannot be established as to Plaintiffs’ claim for losses relating to the damaged generator.

The Policy states that Defendant “will pay for direct physical loss of or damage to ‘buildings’, ‘business personal property’ or ‘stock’ caused by or resulting from a peril not otherwise excluded, not to exceed the applicable Blanket Limit of Insurance for ‘buildings’, ‘business personal property’ and/or ‘stock’ described in the Declarations caused by or resulting from any Covered Cause of Loss.”  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 20, § I, subd. (A); UMF No. 2.)  Excluded from the Policy are losses caused by or resulting from, inter alia, wear and tear, rust, corrosion, deterioration, or faulty, inadequate, or defective design, workmanship, repair, or maintenance.  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 22, § II, subds. (B)(3), (B)(4), (D)(3)(b), (D)(3)(d); UMF No. 8.)  Here, Plaintiffs submitted claims for losses relating to (1) an air conditioning unit, and (2) damage to a generator.  (UMF No. 29; LeRose Decl., ¶¶ 6, 9.) 

The court acknowledges that Defendant has presented evidence showing that (1) the broken air conditioner unit was determined to have stopped working due to the loss of refrigeration, caused by a pinhole in a copper tube rubbing against another copper tube hard enough to puncture a hole through it, such that (2) it was not a covered loss since the loss of refrigeration was caused by wear and tear and deterioration.  (LeRose Decl., ¶ 8; Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 7; Def. Ex. 4, p. 00856 [letter denying claim for the damage to the HVAC system]; Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 22, § II, subd. (B)(3), (B)(4) [excluding from coverage damage caused by wear and tear and deterioration].)  However, Defendant did not present evidence showing that it did not breach the Policy as a result of its failure to cover the loss associated with the damaged generator.

Defendant asserts that, although it initially denied coverage for the replacement of the generator, Defendant has since “paid the full policy sublimit of $25,000.”  (Mot., p. 20:16-21.)  However, Defendant did not present evidence showing that it has actually paid that amount to Plaintiffs.  The evidence cited in support of this assertion consists of (1) a letter to counsel for Plaintiffs, in which Defendant stated that, based on new information, it had reevaluated coverage for the generator and “will cover the replacement value of the generator up to the $25,000 sublimit for that coverage provided by the Utility Services – Direct Damage coverage” provision, and (2) the declaration of Inez Corenevsky, an Assistant Vice President of Claims for Defendant, in which Corenevsky stated the same, including that Defendant “will cover” the value of the generator up to the $25,000 sublimit.  (Def. Material Fact No. 48 [citing Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 10 and Ex. 7]; Def. Ex. 7, pp. 1, 2 [internal emphasis omitted]; Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 10.)  However, this evidence does not show that Defendant has paid to Plaintiffs that amount.  (Ibid.)  At most, it reflects a future intent to pay this amount.

Thus, the court finds that Defendant has not shown that it has not breached the Policy because the evidence submitted by Defendant shows that (1) the damaged generator is a covered loss pursuant to the provisions of the Policy, and (2) Defendant did not cover that loss by paying the $25,000 sublimit to Plaintiffs.  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 35, § III, ¶ C, subd. 21 [providing coverage for loss of or damage to business personal property caused by an interruption in utility services to the Insured Premises].)  

b.     Lost Business Income for Custom Crush and Tasting Room Revenues

The court finds that Defendant has met its burden of showing that the first cause of action for breach of contract has no merit because Defendant has shown that an element of the cause of action (Defendant’s breach of the Policy as to Plaintiffs’ claims relating to their lost revenues for custom crushing services and their tasting room) cannot be established.

The Civil Authority Endorsement provision of the Policy provides that Defendant will pay for the actual loss of business income sustained by the insured and the necessary extra expense “caused by action of civil authority that prohibits access to the described premises due to a direct physical loss of or damage to property, other than at the described premises, caused by or resulting from a Covered Cause of Loss.”  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 69 [modifying Civil Authority provision].)  “Business Income” is defined to mean (1) the net income (i.e., the net profit or loss before income taxes) that would have been earned or incurred, and (2) continuing normal operating expenses incurred, including ordinary payroll.  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 46, § VIII, ¶ D; UMF No. 6.)  Civil authority coverage for lost business income “begin[s] immediately after the time of the first action of civil authority that prohibits access to the described premise” and applies “for a period of up to three (3) consecutive weeks from the date on which such coverage began.”  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 69 [modifying Civil Authority provision].)

Defendant has submitted evidence showing that it paid to Plaintiffs $147,708 for lost business income under the Civil Authority Coverage provision of the Policy, calculated by an accountant at the accounting firm Matson, Driscoll & Damico LLP (“MDD”), which was hired by Defendant’s third-party claim administrator.  (UMF No. 28; Morris Decl., ¶¶ 1-2, 10-12.)  Accountant Lisa Morris (“Morris”) determined that (1) Plaintiffs sustained $169,939 in lost revenue for red wines, no loss for white wines, and $22,639 in lost tasting room revenue, for a net lost income amount of $192,578, (2) Plaintiffs would have incurred $23,417 in costs of goods sold, and (3) Plaintiffs would have incurred $21,453 in non-continuing expenses.  (Morris Decl., ¶¶ 8 [lost custom crushing revenue], 9 [lost tasting room revenue], 10 [net lost income], 11-12 [non-continuing expenses]; UMF No. 22 [Plaintiffs did not sustain loss of business income related to white wine].)  Thus, Defendant has shown that Plaintiffs sustained, and Defendant paid, a total net loss of income of $147,708 (($169,939 + $22,639) – ($23,417 + $21,453)).  (UMF No. 37.)

The court finds that Plaintiffs have met their burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the element of Defendant’s breach of the Policy by showing that there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant paid to Plaintiffs all amounts owed for the lost revenue related to Plaintiffs’ custom crushing services.

First, the court notes that Plaintiffs have argued that Defendant failed to pay the “actual loss of business income due to the shutdown of its tasting room, which was $12,943.27.”  (Opp., pp. 5:10-12, 7:1-2.)  However, Defendant has presented evidence showing that, of the $147,708 paid for lost revenue, $22,639 was allocated to compensate for the lost tasting room revenue for the covered period.  (Morris Decl., ¶ 9.)  Thus, Plaintiffs have not shown that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant has breached the Policy by failing to pay Plaintiffs all amounts due and owing for lost tasting room revenues.

Second, Plaintiffs assert that Defendant owes additional amounts in lost revenue related to their custom crush services on the ground that Defendant deducted certain costs of goods sold and non-recurring expenses that would not have been incurred had Plaintiffs been permitted to operate their business.  (Opp., pp. 5:20-23, 7:3-5.) 

Plaintiff Bartholomew has asserted, in his declaration, that “[n]one of [Plaintiffs’] custom crush clients pay their tonnage fees by credit card[,]” and “[t]he same is true for Freight / Shipping / Postage and retail merchandise.”  (Bartholomew Decl., ¶ 23.)  However, Defendant deducted from its payment $5,161 in bank and credit card fees and $9,398 in freight, shipping, and postage fees.  (Morris Decl., ¶ 11.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiffs have met their burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant properly deducted those expenses from Plaintiffs’ net lost business income, such that Plaintiffs have shown that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant breached the Policy by failing to pay to Plaintiffs all amounts due and owing thereunder.

Third, Plaintiffs contend that Defendant failed to pay them $78,061 (representing a total amount of 80 tons of lost grapes) for lost business income from custom crush clients.  (Bartholomew Decl., ¶ 25; Opp., p. 7:6-8.) 

The parties agree that Plaintiffs provided a spreadsheet reflecting that 80 tons of red wine grapes were not received from three wineries due to the evacuation order.  (UMF No. 18; Morris Decl., ¶ 5; Def. Ex. 18, p. 00334 [chart setting forth 80 total tons not received for the timeframe of September 28 to October 11].)  Defendant submitted evidence, in support of its moving papers, showing that (1) Morris requested from Plaintiffs documents “that demonstrated that 80 tons were actually crushed, but not sold,” but that Plaintiffs did not provide those documents, such that (2) Morris—not having received actual loss data for 2020—relied on the California Department of Food and Agriculture 2020 Grape Crush Final Report dated March 10, 2021 in calculating Plaintiffs’ loss to be approximately 55 tons. (Morris Decl., ¶¶ 5 [emphasis in original omitted], 6.) 

In opposition, Plaintiffs have submitted the declaration of individual plaintiff Bartholomew, in which Bartholomew states that Plaintiffs “provided to the company [i.e., the accounting firm MDD] information about the 80 contracted for tons of red grapes that we did not receive because of the fires[,]” but that Defendant accepted MDD’s suggestion to reduce the tonnage amount from 80 tons to 55 tons.  (Bartholomew Decl., ¶ 26.)  It is unclear whether Plaintiffs are referring to the spreadsheet transmitted to MDD, which may have been unsupported by documentation.  (Def. Ex. 18, p. 3.)  However, liberally construing this evidence in favor of Plaintiffs, the court finds that Plaintiffs have submitted evidence showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether (1) Plaintiffs presented evidence to Defendant establishing that they lost 80, rather than 55 tons, of red wine grapes, such that (2) Defendant failed to pay to Plaintiffs lost revenue in the amount of $78,061 for the additional loss of 25 tons of red grapes, such that Defendant breached the Policy by failing to pay Plaintiffs all amounts due and owing thereunder.  (Bartholomew Decl., ¶ 26; Morris Decl., ¶ 8 [calculating $169,939 in lost revenue for red wine based on 54.819 lost tons at the rate of $3,100].)

c.      Lost Business Income for 2020 Estate Wine

The court finds that Defendant has not met its burden of showing that the first cause of action for breach of contract has no merit because Defendant has not shown that the element of breach of the Policy cannot be established as to Plaintiffs’ claim for lost business income relating to the 2020 Estate Wine.

By letter dated September 7, 2022, Plaintiffs asserted that Defendant failed to pay $1,666,350 for their loss of business income pursuant to the Civil Authority Endorsement provision of the Policy on the ground that the civil authorities prevented Plaintiffs from accessing the vineyard and harvesting 22.14 tons of Cabernet Sauvignon, which subsequently caused smoke damage to the grapes, and therefore prevented Plaintiffs from producing the 2020 Estate Wine.  (Def. Ex. 5, p. 01386.)  Defendant denied this claim for coverage on the ground that Plaintiffs did not suffer actual loss during the evacuation period because the grapes were still on the vine during the Glass Fire.  (Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 9.)

As set forth above, the Policy provides that Defendant will pay for the actual loss of business income (defined to mean the net income that would have been earned and continuing normal operating expenses incurred) sustained by Plaintiffs and the necessary extra expenses “caused by action of civil authority that prohibits access to the described premises due to direct physical loss of or damage to property, other than at the described premises, caused by or resulting from any Covered Cause of Loss.”  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 69; Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 46, § VIII, ¶ D; UMF No. 6.)  The coverage for business income “begin[s] immediately after the time of the first action of civil authority that prohibits access to the described premises and will apply for a period of up to three (3) consecutive weeks from the date on which such coverage began.”  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 69.)

In submitting their claim to Defendant, Plaintiffs stated that, “when the Glass fire broke out, the 2020 Estate Vineyard Cabernet Sauvignon did not have smoke damage, and it was ready to be harvested and processed into [Plaintiffs’] flagship wine.  [Citation.]  However, because of the fire damage at neighboring properties, and from 9/28/20 through the early afternoon of 10/07/20, the civil authorities prevented [Plaintiffs] from accessing its Estate vineyard and harvesting 22.14 tons of Cabernet Sauvignon in it.  Because that 2,881 gallons of Cabernet Sauvignon could not be moved to the safety of the caves and the fermenting process, it became smoke damaged.”  (Def. Ex. 5, p. 01386.)  Plaintiffs further advised Defendant that they were able to produce a 2020 Cabernet Sauvignon that could be sold at $35 per bottle, rather than the $150 per bottle price for the 2020 Estate Wine.  (Id. at pp. 01386, 01403 [showing 2020 Estate Wine to be priced at $150 per bottle].)  Thus, Plaintiffs asserted their lost revenue associated with the 2020 Estate Wine to be $1,666,350.  (Ibid.)  

First, Defendant asserts that this loss is not covered by the Policy because Plaintiffs did not sustain “actual loss of ‘Business Income’” as required by the Civil Authority Endorsement provision since (1) an insured “sustain[s] its burden of proof of the loss of net profits ordinarily [by] determining (a) the total cost, including depreciation, of manufacturing the merchandise[,] the production of which is prevented during the period of the use and occupancy coverage . . . and, (b) the price at which the product would have been sold in that period, either by prior sales agreement or the current market price in the absence of such commitments[,]” and (2) the grapes were on the vine during the Glass Fire, such that no wine could have been processed or sold during the evacuation period.  (General Ins. Co. of America v. Pathfinder Petroleum Co. (9th Cir. 1944) 145 F.2d 368, 369 [internal footnote omitted]; Mot., pp. 22:24-23:1.)  However, Defendant did not submit evidence showing that the wine could not have been processed or sold during the evacuation period.

            The court acknowledges, and the parties appear to agree, that the subject grapes were on the vine at the time of the Glass Fire.  (Def. Ex. 5, p. 01386.)  Defendant appears to contend that, because the grapes were on the vine, and eventually suffered smoke damage, “no wine could have been processed or sold during the evacuation period.”  (Mot., pp. 22:24-23:1.)  But Plaintiffs did not assert their claim for coverage on the ground that the grapes were damaged by smoke during the relevant period.  Instead, Plaintiffs asserted that they could not produce the 2020 Estate Wine because Plaintiffs could not access its vineyard, move the Cabernet Sauvignon to the caves, and produce the 2020 Estate Wine.  (Def. Ex. 5, p. 01386.)  Had Plaintiffs not been prevented from accessing the Insured Property, Plaintiffs would have been able to harvest the grapes and produce their wine during the evacuation period.

            Thus, even though the grapes were on the vine at the time of the evacuation period, Defendant has not shown that “no wine could have been processed or sold during” that period.  (Mot., pp. 22:24-23:1.)  Pursuant to the authority cited by Defendant, the loss of net profits would therefore be calculated by determining (1) the total cost of manufacturing the 2020 Estate Wine which was prevented during the evacuation order, and (2) the price at which the 2020 Estate Wine would have been sold in the covered period (i.e., three consecutive weeks from September 28, 2020 (the date on which coverage began)).  (General Ins. Co. of America, supra, 145 F.2d at p. 369.)  Defendant did not provide coverage to Plaintiffs pursuant to this provision.  (Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 9 [“On March 14, 2023, I sent a letter . . . denying coverage for the 2020 Estate Claim”].)

            Second, Defendant asserts that there is no coverage for smoke damage to the unharvested grapes under the Policy, such that the smoke damage to Plaintiffs’ grapes was not covered. 

            It is undisputed that the Policy (1) provides coverage for direct physical loss or damage to trellis or grape vines at the Insured Property if it is caused by the perils delineated in the Policy, but (2) excludes from its coverage “grapes growing on grape vines . . . .”  (Def. Ex. 2, Policy, p. 25, § III, ¶ A, subd. (1)(a); UMF No. 4.)  However, as set forth above, Plaintiffs have not asserted a claim seeking coverage for the smoke damage caused to the grapes, but rather the lost income sustained by Plaintiffs due to their inability to, pursuant to the evacuation order, harvest their grapes and produce the 2020 Estate Wine. 

            Thus, the court finds that Defendant has not met its burden to show that it did not breach the Policy (i.e., the Civil Authority Endorsement provision) by denying Plaintiffs’ claim for lost income related to the 2020 Estate Wine.

d.      Conclusion

For the reasons set forth above, the court finds that (1) Defendant has not met its burden to show that it did not breach the Policy as to Plaintiffs’ claims for (i) the damaged generator, and (ii) lost business income relating to the 2020 Estate Wine, and (2) although Defendant has met its burden to show that it did not breach the Policy as to Plaintiffs’ claims relating to the lost revenues for custom crush and tasting room, Plaintiffs have shown that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant paid to Plaintiffs all amounts owed for the loss of custom crushing services.

The court therefore denies Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action for breach of contract.

2.     Second Cause of Action for Tortious Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

“[I]n a claim against an insurance carrier, ‘there are at least two separate requirements to establish breach of the implied covenant: (1) benefits due under the policy must have been withheld; and (2) the reason for withholding benefits must have been unreasonable or without proper cause.’”  (Grebow v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 564, 581.)  “‘In a bad faith case, the “primary test is whether the insurer withheld payment of an insured’s claim unreasonably and in bad faith.  [Citation.]  Where benefits are withheld for proper cause, there is no breach of the implied covenant.”’”  (Janney v. CSAA Ins. Exchange (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 374, 399.)

The court finds that Defendant has not met its burden of showing that the second cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing has no merit because Defendant has not shown that the element of Defendant’s unreasonable withholding of benefits cannot be established.  

First, the court finds that Defendant has shown that it did not act unreasonably or without proper cause regarding its payment of claims for Plaintiffs’ lost custom crush and tasting room revenues because it relied on the expert evaluation of Morris, the accountant retained by Defendant’s third-party administrator, as to the calculation of those lost revenues.  (Morris Decl., ¶¶ 8-12; Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 5; LeRose Decl., ¶¶ 2-3; McCoy v. Progressive West Ins. Co. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 785, 793 [“‘The “genuine dispute” doctrine may be applied where the insurer denies a claim based on the opinions of experts’” unless the insurer dishonestly selected the experts, the experts were unreasonable, or the insurer did not conduct a thorough investigation]; Paslay v. State Farm General Ins. Co. (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 639, 653.)

Second, the court finds that Defendant has shown that it did not act unreasonably or without proper cause in investigating Plaintiffs’ claim for 2020 Estate Wine coverage because Defendant has submitted evidence showing that, notwithstanding its position that Plaintiffs are not entitled to coverage under the Civil Authority Endorsement provision, it also denied Plaintiffs’ claim because it received detailed information about this claim for the first time on September 7, 2022, two years after the loss, and therefore Defendant was prevented from investigating the claim.  (Corenvsky Decl., ¶¶ 8-9; Def. Ex. 5 [Sept. 7, 2022 letter from counsel for Plaintiffs to Defendant raising coverage for the lost revenues related to the 2020 Estate Wine]; Paslay, supra, 248 Cal.App.4th at p. 655 [finding that there were no triable issues regarding the adequacy of the insurer’s investigation since the plaintiff-insureds removed damaged property before the insurer had an opportunity to conduct a full assessment].)

Third, the court finds that Defendant has not shown that (1) it did not act unreasonably or without proper cause in initially denying Plaintiffs’ claim for loss relating to the damaged generator, and (2) it thoroughly and fairly investigated this claim. 

The court acknowledges, as set forth above, that Defendant asserts that it initially denied coverage for this claim under the exclusion for wear and tear of property upon receiving a report from Leete Generators, in which Leete Generators discovered metal particles in the engine port, which indicated internal engine damage.  (Def. Ex. 10, p. 0494; LeRose Decl., ¶ 7.)  The court further acknowledges that reliance on an expert to estimate repair benefits owed may show that an insurer’s position is maintained in good faith and on reasonable grounds.  (Paslay, supra, 248 Cal.App.4th at p. 653.)  However, the report from Leete Generators did not state that the internal engine damage was caused by normal wear and tear, and instead indicated only that there was internal engine damage.  Defendant did not argue, or present evidence showing, that Defendant, or its claims adjuster, conducted further investigation to identify the cause of the internal damage.  Instead, Defendant merely determined that the damage was the result of wear and tear.  (LeRose Decl., ¶ 7.)

Moreover, Defendant has since conceded that the generator’s engine was damaged because there was nobody available to check the engine oil levels during the evacuation period, such that it is a covered loss.  (Corenevsky Decl., ¶ 10.)  Defendant did not present evidence or argument establishing that its delay in investigating this claim and discovering this information was in good faith or reasonable.  (Paslay, supra, 248 Cal.App.4th at p. 653 [“The genuine dispute doctrine ‘does not relieve an insurer of its obligation to thoroughly and fairly investigate, process, and evaluate the insured’s claim’”].)  Further, as set forth above, Defendant has not submitted evidence showing that it has paid Plaintiffs this amount.

Thus, the court finds that Defendant has not shown that its initial denial of coverage as to the generator was not unreasonable or without proper cause, or that it thoroughly investigated this claim.[1]

Further, even if Defendant had met its burden to show that all of its actions were not unreasonable or without proper cause, the court finds that Plaintiffs have met their burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant’s denial of their claim for the damaged generator was unreasonable or without proper cause.  Plaintiffs’ General Manager has attested, in her declaration, that she informed Daniel LeRose, a claim adjuster with North American Risk Services (i.e., the third-party administrator acting on behalf of Defendant), that Leete Generators had determined that “either the generator or its engine needed to be replaced because that engine had worked so hard during the time the winery was evacuated that it had ran [sic] out of oil, [and] continued to run without lubrication until it shut down,” thereby damaging the engine.  (LeRose Decl., ¶ 1; Blumberg Decl., ¶ 7.)  Thus, Plaintiffs have submitted evidence showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant knew that the generator was damaged as a result of the evacuation order (i.e., because the evacuation order prevented Plaintiffs from checking the engine oil levels) and therefore constituted a covered loss.

The court therefore denies Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the second cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

3.     Claim for Punitive Damages

“In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “The standard for a motion for summary adjudication on a claim for punitive damages is whether clear and convincing evidence exists to support that claim.  [Citations.]  While a plaintiff need not prove his or her case for punitive damages to defeat summary adjudication, [i]n ruling on a motion for summary judgment or adjudication motion, the judge must view the evidence presented through the prism of the substantive [clear and convincing] evidentiary burden . . . .”  (Szarowicz v. Birenbaum (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 146, 171 [internal citations and quotation marks omitted].)

The court finds that Defendant has not met its burden of showing that Plaintiffs’ claim for punitive damages has no merit because Defendant has not shown that the element of malicious conduct cannot be established.  As set forth above, the court has concluded that, based on Defendant’s evidence, Defendant (1) unreasonably and without proper cause initially denied Plaintiffs’ claim relating to the damaged generator, and (2) failed to thoroughly investigate this claim.  The court finds that this evidence shows that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendant’s conduct was despicable and carried on by Defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights of Plaintiffs, such that there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant is guilty of malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subds. (a), (c)(1).)

The court therefore denies Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to Plaintiffs’ claim for punitive damages.

ORDER

            The court denies defendant HDI Global Insurance Company’s motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Italia Arteaga (“Arteaga”) and Henry Ron (“Ron”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against defendants Adventist Health White Memorial, Cinna Toy Wohlmuth, M.D. (“Dr. Wohlmuth”), and Sarah Simko, M.D. (“Dr. Simko”) on March 28, 2023, alleging two causes of action for (1) wrongful death, and (2) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

Now pending before the court are two motions for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication.  First, Dr. Wohlmuth and Dr. Simko move for summary judgment on Plaintiffs’ Complaint or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on various issues.  Second, defendant White Memorial Medical Center d/b/a Adventist Health White Memorial (“Adventist”) moves for summary judgment on Plaintiffs’ Complaint or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on each cause of action and on the issue of agency.

LEGAL STANDARD

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant or cross-defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “Once the defendant or cross-defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)  “When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Id. at p. 467; Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT FILED BY DEFENDANTS DR. WOHLMUTH AND DR. SIMKO

Defendants Dr. Wohlmuth and Dr. Simko (collectively, “Physician Defendants”) move the court for an order granting summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication in their favor and against Plaintiffs. 

1.     Plaintiffs’ First Cause of Action for Wrongful Death

To prevail on a cause of action for wrongful death, “plaintiffs must prove ‘(1) a “wrongful act or neglect” on the part of one or more persons [(that is, negligence)] that (2) “causes” (3) the “death of [another] person.” ’”  (Musgrove v. Silver (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 694, 705.)   Plaintiffs have alleged, in connection with this cause of action, that Physician Defendants negligently, carelessly, and unskillfully treated, triaged, cared for, diagnosed, operated on, and otherwise handled Plaintiffs’ daughter, decedent Alaia Valentina Ron (“Decedent”), such that Decedent died on April 20, 2022.  (Compl., ¶ 13.)  Thus, the wrongful act underlying this cause of action is the alleged medical malpractice of Physician Defendants.  

The court finds that Physician Defendants have met their burden of showing that the first cause of action for wrongful death has no merit because Physician Defendants have shown that an element of the cause of action (a wrongful act or neglect on the part of Physician Defendants) cannot be established since Physician Defendants have shown that they (1) did not breach their duties to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of their profession commonly possess and exercise, and (2) did not cause the death of Decedent.  (San Antonio Regional Hospital v. Superior Court of San Bernardino County (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 346, 351 [“‘The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage’”].)

Physician Defendants have submitted evidence establishing their involvement in the labor and delivery of Decedent as follows.

On April 18, 2022, Dr. Wohlmuth admitted plaintiff Arteaga to the hospital because of her uterine contractions, leakage of fluid, and a non-reassuring fetal heart tracing.  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 10; Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 10.)  On April 19, 2022, Dr. Simko evaluated the fetal heart tracing of Decedent, verified that it showed a sinusoidal pattern, and called Dr. Wohlmuth to evaluate the tracing.  (Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 25; Dr. Ellio Decl., ¶ 25)  Thereafter, Physician Defendants performed an emergent low transverse cesarean section, following Dr. Simko’s discussion with plaintiff Arteaga regarding the risks, benefits, and alternatives of a cesarean section delivery for a Category 3 fetal heart tracing with a sinusoidal pattern.  (Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶¶ 27-28; Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶¶ 27-28.)  After delivery, Decedent suffered various issues and was determined to be in critical condition.  (Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶¶ 30-32; Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶¶ 30-32.)  Decedent passed away at 12:35 a.m. on April 20, 2022.  (Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 37; Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 37.)

Physician Defendants have submitted the expert declarations of (1) Dr. Elliot, a physician licensed to practice in the State of California, who has been practicing obstetrics and gynecology since 1974, and (2) Dr. Friedlich, a physician licensed to practice in the State of California, who has been practicing pediatrics and neonatal medicine since 1992.  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶¶ 1, 3-4; Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶¶ 1, 3-4.)  In reviewing the medical records of plaintiff Arteaga and Decedent, Dr. Elliot and Dr. Friedlich have set forth the following opinions.

First, Dr. Elliot asserts that the monitoring of plaintiff Arteaga’s labor progress was appropriate and within the standard of care because, inter alia, the physicians (i.e., Physician Defendants) “appropriately came to the bedside” of plaintiff Arteaga upon being informed by the nurses of their concerns regarding the fetal heart tracings.  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶¶ 38, 39 [“a physician came to the bedside” after the fetal heart tracing changed from Category 1 to Category 3, which “was appropriate and within the standard of care”].)

Second, Dr. Elliot asserts that, due to the sinusoidal pattern of the fetal heart tracing, a decision was reached to perform an emergency cesarean to deliver Decedent.  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 40.)  The timing of the decision to the procedure was “timely, appropriate, and within the standard of care.”  (Ibid.)  

Third, Dr. Elliot states that the sinusoidal heart rate pattern that Decedent developed was due to Decedent losing blood into the maternal circulation.  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 41.)  Dr. Friedlich asserts that Decedent was “losing blood into the maternal circulation for at least one week before the delivery.”  (Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 38.)  However, the fetal heart tracing did not indicate possible fetal anemia until the sinusoidal pattern began around 11:30 p.m., such that “this information was unknown to the obstetrical team and there was no way that the obstetricians could have determined this prospectively during the labor process.”  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 41; Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 38.)  Dr. Elliot further asserts that the cesarean section was appropriately and timely performed once the sinusoidal heart pattern appeared and became persistent.  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 41.)

Fourth, Dr. Elliot asserts that “the cord blood gases at birth did not reflect acidosis and demonstrate that there was no intrapartum hypoxic event during the delivery process[,]” such that “nothing that occurred during the labor and delivery process made [Decedent’s] pre-existing severe chronic anemia worse.”  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 42; Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 41.)  Decedent was determined to be profoundly anemic at 1:40 a.m., meaning that Decedent “was trying to produce red blood cells to replace the blood that was lost into the maternal circulation . . . .”  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 43.)  The Kleihauer Betke test results indicated that more than half of Decedent’s expected blood volume was found in the mother’s system (i.e., plaintiff Arteaga’s system), which “is evidence that [Decedent] had a chronic bleed in utero.”  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶ 44; Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 39.)  

Based on the opinions set forth above, Dr. Elliot concluded that (1) Physician Defendants complied with the standard of care during the labor of plaintiff Arteaga and delivery of Decedent, and (2) Physician Defendants did not cause or contribute to causing the death of Decedent.  (Dr. Elliot Decl., ¶¶ 45-46.)  Similarly, Dr. Friedlich concluded that (1) an earlier delivery of Decedent would not have changed the outcome, and (2) no act or omission of Physician Defendants caused or contributed to causing the death of Decedent.  (Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶¶ 43-44.)

            Thus, the court finds that Physician Defendants have met their burden to show that they did not, in handling the labor of plaintiff Arteaga and delivery and care of Decedent, breach the standard of care or cause the death of Decedent.  (San Antonio Regional Hospital, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th at p. 351 [elements of medical malpractice]; Ibid. [elements of standard of care and causation requires competent expert testimony].)  The court therefore finds that Physician Defendants have shown that Plaintiffs cannot establish the element of a wrongful act or neglect on Physician Defendants’ part that caused the death of Decedent.  (Musgrove, supra, 82 Cal.App.5th at p. 705.)

The court finds that Plaintiffs have not met their burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the element of a wrongful act or neglect on the part of Physician Defendants that caused the death of Decedent.  Plaintiffs did not file an opposition to this motion, and therefore have not presented argument or evidence showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Physician Defendants’ delivery, treatment, and care of Decedent breached the standard of care and caused Decedent’s death.

The court therefore grants Physician Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action for wrongful death.

2.     Plaintiff Arteaga’s Second Cause of Action for Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

The “‘ “negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort but the tort of negligence . . . .”  [Citation.]  “The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply.” ’”  (Eriksson v. Nunnink (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 708, 729 [internal emphasis omitted]; Romero v. Los Angeles Rams (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 562, 567 [“The elements of a negligence cause of action are 1) the existence of a duty, 2) a breach of that duty, 3) injury to the plaintiff caused by the defendant’s breach, and 4) actual damages”].) 

The court finds that Physician Defendants have met their burden of showing that the second cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress has no merit because Physician Defendants have shown that an element of the cause of action (Physician Defendants’ breach of their duty of care and causation) cannot be established.

Plaintiff Arteaga alleges, in connection with this cause of action, that Physician Defendants caused Arteaga severe emotional distress arising from the abnormal event of participating in a negligent delivery of Decedent.  (Compl., ¶ 23.)  Thus, this cause of action is based on the alleged medical malpractice of Physician Defendants.  As set forth above, Physician Defendants have met their burden to show that they did not, in handling the labor of plaintiff Arteaga and delivery and care of Decedent, breach the standard of care or cause the death of Decedent.  (Dr. Elliott Decl., ¶¶ 38-46; Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶¶ 38-44.)  The court therefore finds that Physician Defendants have shown that they did not (1) breach their duty to competently care for Decedent and, further, their duty to plaintiff Arteaga, or (2) cause plaintiff Arteaga emotional distress.

The court finds that Arteaga has not met her burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the elements of Physician Defendants’ breach of their duties and causation.  Arteaga did not file an opposition to this motion, and thus has not presented argument or evidence showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Physician Defendants negligently treated Decedent and therefore caused Arteaga’s emotional distress.

The court therefore grants Physician Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the second cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress.

3.     Conclusion

Because the court has granted summary adjudication in favor of Physician Defendants on all of the causes of action alleged in the Complaint, the court finds that all of the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, and that Physician Defendants are entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The court therefore grants Physician Defendants’ motion for summary judgment.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT FILED BY DEFENDANT ADVENTIST

Defendant Adventist moves the court for an order granting summary judgment in its favor and against Plaintiffs or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to each cause of action and the issue of agency between it and Physician Defendants.

1.     Plaintiffs’ First Cause of Action for Wrongful Death

To prevail on a cause of action for wrongful death, “plaintiffs must prove ‘(1) a “wrongful act or neglect” on the part of one or more persons [(that is, negligence)] that (2) “causes” (3) the “death of [another] person.” ’”  (Musgrove, supra, 82 Cal.App.5th at p. 705.) 

In connection with this cause of action, as alleged against Adventist, Plaintiffs have alleged that Adventist (1) negligently and carelessly treated, examined, operated on, and otherwise handled Decedent, and (2) negligently failed to (i) select a competent medical staff,  (ii) review the competency of its medical staff, and (iii) monitor its staff.  (Compl., ¶¶ 13-14.)  Thus, Plaintiffs have based this cause of action on the alleged medical malpractice of Adventist’s staff, and Adventist’s alleged negligence in failing to monitor the competence of its staff.

The court finds that Adventist has met its burden of showing that the first cause of action for wrongful death has no merit because Adventist has shown that an element of the cause of action (a wrongful act or neglect on the part of Adventist) cannot be established since Adventist has submitted evidence showing that its staff did not (1) breach the standard of care while treating Decedent and Arteaga during labor and delivery, or (2) cause the death of Decedent.  (San Antonio Regional Hospital, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th at p. 351 [elements of medical malpractice].)    

            First, Adventist has shown that it may not be held vicariously liable for the acts of Physician Defendants.

            “A hospital is liable for a physician’s malpractice when the physician is actually employed by or is the ostensible agent of the hospital.”  (Franklin v. Santa Barbara Cottage Hospital (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 395, 403 [internal quotations marks and citation omitted].) Adventist has submitted copies of the “Consent to Surgery or Special Procedure” and the “Conditions of Registration” initialed by plaintiff Arteaga on April 18, 2022, which state that physicians and surgeons “are NOT employees of the hospital and have been granted the privilege of using the hospital for the care and treatment of their patients.”  (Def. Ex. 2, Arteaga Med. Records, PDF p. 85, ¶ 3; Def. Ex. 6, Conditions of Registration, PDF p. 75, ¶ 3.)  Thus, Adventist has shown that (1) it did not employ Physician Defendants, such that Physician Defendants were not the agents of Adventist, and (2) Physician Defendants were not the ostensible agents of Adventist, because (i) Adventist gave Arteaga actual notice that they were not its employees, and (ii) there is no evidence suggesting that Arteaga was unable to understand that information.  (Franklin, supra, 82 Cal.App.5th at pp. 403 [“‘An agency is actual when the agent is really employed by the principal’”], 405 [“a hospital may be liable for their negligence on an ostensible agency theory, unless” it gives the patient actual notice that the physicians are not hospital employees and there is no reason to believe the patient was unable to understand or act on the information].)

            Second, as to its employees, Adventist has shown that its staff did not cause breach their duties of care and did not cause the death of Decedent.

Adventist has submitted the declarations of (1) Dr. Kessler, a physician licensed by the American Board of Obstetrics and Gynecologists since 1982, and (2) Dr. Friedlich, a licensed physician and current Co-Director of the Fetal and Neonatal Institute & Division Chief in the Division of Neonatology.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶¶ 2-3; Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 2.)  Both expert physicians have set forth the following history.

On April 18, 2022, plaintiff Arteaga presented to Adventist at 39.0 weeks pregnant.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (a); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (a).)  Induction of Arteaga was recommended for non-reassuring fetal heart tracings.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (b); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (b).)  From 12:00 p.m. to 5:00 p.m., the fetal heart tracings were noted as Category 1, with moderate variability and no accelerations or decelerations.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (c); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (c).)  At 8:30 p.m., the fetal heart monitor tracings were noted as Category 1.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (h); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (h).)  At 9:00 p.m., nurse Pena noted the fetal heart monitor tracings as Category 2.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (i); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (i).)  Arteaga was thereafter turned on her side.  (Ibid.)  The fetal heart monitor tracings were noted as Category 1 at approximately 10:00 p.m.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (k); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (k).)  At 10:30 p.m. and 11:00 p.m., nurse Pena noted the tracings as Category 1 with moderate variability and no accelerations or decelerations.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subds. (l), (m); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subds. (l), (m).)  However, at 11:30 p.m., nurse Pena noted the tracings as Category 3 and a sinusoidal pattern with no variability, accelerations, or decelerations.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (n); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (n).)  The nurse notified the physicians of the Category 3 tracings and the sinusoidal patterns.  (Ibid.)

At approximately 12:07 a.m., Dr. Liao was at Arteaga’s bedside.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (o); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (o).)  At approximately 12:20 a.m., the order was given for an emergency cesarean section, at which time Dr. Simko noted the indications, risks, and alternatives for the procedure with Arteaga.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (q); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (q).)  Physician Defendants performed the cesarean section on Arteaga at 12:32 a.m.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (r); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (r).)  Decedent was delivered at 12:33 a.m.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (s); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (s).)  Upon delivery, Decedent was noted to be inactive, limp, blue and pale in color, and was therefore transferred to the neonatal intensive care unit.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subds. (s), (t); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subds. (s), (t).)  An abdominal ultrasound was performed on Decedent.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (x); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (x).)  Thereafter, Decedent received hypothermic treatment for persistent moderate encephalopathy.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (y); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (y).)  “Despite numerous medications, transfusions of packed red blood cells and plasma, and therapeutic cooling measures, [Decedent] remained unstable” and her oxygen saturation and heart rate decreased.  (Ibid.)  Decedent passed away at 12:35 a.m.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 7, subd. (aa); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 6, subd. (aa).)

Dr. Kessler concluded, based on their review of the records and their experience, that   (1) the monitoring of Arteaga’s labor progress by Adventist’s nurses and employees was appropriate and within the standard of care because the nursing evaluations were completed approximately every hour; (2) when the fetal heart tracings showed abnormal rhythms, the nurses appropriately performed interventions, such as position changes; (3) the time between making the decision to perform the cesarean, performing the cesarean, and delivering Decedent were timely, appropriate, and within the standard of care; and (4) the acts of the nurses and physicians did not (i) cause or contribute to any delay in timely delivering Decedent, or (ii) cause or contribute to the death of Decedent.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 8, subds. (a)-(f), (k).)  Dr. Kessler further concluded that Decedent had a chronic bleed in utero, and that there “was nothing on the fetal heart rate tracing to indicate possible fetal anemia until the sinusoidal pattern started around 11:30 p.m.[,]” at which time the physicians appeared and subsequently performed the cesarean section.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 8, subds. (k), (g).)  

Dr. Friedlich similarly concluded that Adventist’s employees and nursing personnel did not cause or contribute to the death of Decedent, including because (1) the cord blood glasses at birth demonstrated that there was no intrapartum hypoxic event during the delivery process, such that nothing that occurred during labor and delivery made the baby’s preexisting chronic anemia worse, (2) the sinusoidal heart rate pattern that Decedent developed which led to the emergency cesarean was due to Decedent losing blood for at least one week before delivery, and (3) earlier delivery would not have changed the outcome, since Decedent was severely anemic at least 72 hours before birth.  (Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 8, subds. (a), (d), (g).)

Thus, the court finds that Adventist has met its burden to show that its staff did not, in handling the labor of plaintiff Arteaga and delivery and care of Decedent, breach the standard of care or cause the death of Decedent.  (San Antonio Regional Hospital, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th at p. 351 [elements of medical malpractice]; Ibid. [elements of standard of care and causation requires competent expert testimony]; Compl., ¶ 12)  Moreover, the court finds that this evidence shows that Adventist did not cause the death of Decedent by employing and failing to monitor incompetent staff.  (Compl., ¶ 14.)  The court therefore finds that Adventist has met its burden to submit evidence showing that it did not commit a wrongful or negligent act that caused the death of Decedent.  (Musgrove, supra, 82 Cal.App.5th at p. 705.)

The court finds that Plaintiffs have not met their burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the element of a wrongful or negligent act on the part of Adventist that caused the death of Decedent.  Plaintiffs did not file an opposition to this motion, and therefore have not presented argument or evidence showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether (1) Adventist’s staff’s delivery, treatment, and care of Decedent caused Decedent’s death, or (2) Adventist’s monitoring of incompetent and negligent staff caused Decedent’s death.

The court therefore grants Adventist’s motion for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action for wrongful death.

2.     Plaintiff Arteaga’s Second Cause of Action for Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

The “‘ “negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort but the tort of negligence . . . .”  [Citation.]  “The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply.” ’”  (Eriksson, supra, 233 Cal.App.4th at p. 729 [internal emphasis omitted]; Romero, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 567 [“The elements of a negligence cause of action are 1) the existence of a duty, 2) a breach of that duty, 3) injury to the plaintiff caused by the defendant’s breach, and 4) actual damages”].) 

The court finds that Adventist has met its burden of showing that the second cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress has no merit because Adventist has shown that an element of the cause of action (that Adventist breached its duty of care to Arteaga and caused her severe emotional distress) cannot be established. 

Plaintiff Arteaga’s second cause of action is based on the same facts alleged in connection with the first cause of action, i.e., that Adventist failed to provide the necessary medical treatment to Arteaga and Decedent, which caused Arteaga emotional distress “arising from the abnormal event of participating in a negligent delivery and labor” of Decedent.  (Compl., ¶ 23.)  As set forth above, the court finds that Adventist has met its burden to show that its staff did not breach the standard of care in delivering Decedent and did not cause the death of Decedent.  (Dr. Kessler Decl., ¶ 8, subds. (a)-(f), (k); Dr. Friedlich Decl., ¶ 8, subds. (a), (d), (g).) Based on this evidence, the court finds that Adventist has shown that it did not breach its duty to completely care for Decedent, and thus did not breach its duty of care to Arteaga, and did not cause plaintiff Arteaga emotional distress.

The court finds that Arteaga has not met her burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the elements of Adventist’s breach of its duty to plaintiff Arteaga and causation.  Arteaga did not file an opposition to this motion, and thus has not presented argument or evidence showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Adventist’s staff negligently cared for and delivered Decedent and therefore caused plaintiff Arteaga’s emotional distress.

The court therefore grants Adventist’s motion for summary adjudication as to the second cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress.

3.     Conclusion

Because the court has granted summary adjudication in favor of Adventist on each cause of action alleged in Plaintiffs’ Complaint, the court finds that all of the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, and that Adventist is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The court therefore grants Adventist’s motion for summary judgment.
