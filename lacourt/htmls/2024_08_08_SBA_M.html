Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.



Case Number: 23TRCV03096    Hearing Date: August 8, 2024    Dept: M

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT – SOUTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka                                                                      Thursday, August 8, 2024

Department M                                                                                                        Calendar No. 6

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

            Nira Schwartz v. Skyline Park Land, LLC, et al.

            23TRCV03096

1.      Skyline Park Land, LLC, et al.’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action    

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Skyline Park Land, LLC, et al.’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action is granted.     

 

            Background

 

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on September 19, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff Nira Schwartz owns a mobile home. She rents a space to place her mobile home in a mobile home park owned, controlled, and/or managed by Skyline Park Land, LLC and Saunders Property LLC. The manager of the mobile home park, Manuel Guzman, struck Plaintiff without any warning. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: 1. Negligence; 2. Assault and Battery; 3. Premises Liability; 4. Elder Abuse (Welfare and Institutions Code § 15600, et seq.).

 

            Objections

 

            Plaintiff’s objections 1 to 3 are overruled.

 

            Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

            “California law reflects a strong public policy in favor of arbitration as a relatively quick and inexpensive method for resolving disputes.  [Citation.]  To further that policy, [Code of Civil Procedure] section 1281.2 requires a trial court to enforce a written arbitration agreement unless one of three limited exceptions applies.  [Citation.]  Those statutory exceptions arise where (1) a party waives the right to arbitration; (2) grounds exist for revoking the arbitration agreement; and (3) pending litigation with a third party creates the possibility of conflicting rulings on common factual or legal issues.  (§ 1281.2, subds. (a)–(c).)”  Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 959, 967.

 

            “The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.”  Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.

 

            “The party opposing arbitration has the burden of demonstrating that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to require arbitration of the dispute.  Nonetheless, this policy does not override ordinary principles of contract interpretation.  [T]he contractual terms themselves must be carefully examined before the parties to the contract can be ordered to arbitration:  Although [t]he law favors contracts for arbitration of disputes between parties, there is no policy compelling persons to accept arbitration of controversies which they have not agreed to arbitrate.”  Rice v. Downs (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 1213, 1223 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

            In Rowe v. Exline (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1286, the Court of Appeal found that “a nonsignatory sued as an agent of a signatory may enforce an arbitration agreement.”  Id. at 1286.  In addition, “a nonsignatory who is the agent of a signatory can even be compelled to arbitrate claims against his will.”  Id. at 1285, citing Harris v. Superior Court (1986) 188 Cal.App.3d 475, 477–78.  Further, “in many cases, nonparties to arbitration agreements are allowed to enforce those agreements where there is sufficient identity of parties.”  Valley Casework, Inc. v. Comfort Construction, Inc. (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 1013, 1021.  This includes nonparties as agents of a party as well as “a third party beneficiary of an arbitration agreement.”  Ibid. 

 

            Defendant moves for an order compelling arbitration and a stay of proceedings. The motion is made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §1281 et seq., on the grounds Plaintiffs are bound by a written agreement to arbitrate the subject matter of the Complaint. Code Civ. Proc. §1281.2. Defendant argues that a valid arbitration agreement exists between the parties that requires arbitration of all disputes. Defendant also moves pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.4 to stay all proceedings. Defendant has attached the lease agreement entered into by the parties which sets forth an arbitration provision on paragraph 51.1 of the agreement. The provision states: “ANY DISPUTE BETWEEN HOMEOWNER AND PARK RELATING TO, CONCERNING OR CONNECTED WITH THIS AGREEMENT, RESIDENCY DOCUMENTS, THE INTERPRETATION OR ENFORCEMENT THEREOF, THE LEASEHOLD, THE HOMESITE, SERVICES, FACILITIES, OR MAINTENANCE IN OR ABOUT THE PARK, AND ANY DISPUTE RESPECTING THESE MATTERS BETWEEN HOMEOWNER AND OWNER SHALL BE RESOLVED SOLELY BY MEDIATION AND ARBITRATION IN ACCORDANCE WITH THE PROVISIONS SET FQRTH BELOW, INSTEAD OF IN COURT.” (Decl., Michael Misera, Ex. A, para. 51.1). In paragraph 2.2, the definition of “owner” is as follows: “"Owner" includes, but it is not limited to, the owners of the Park (including the owner’s partners, directors, representatives, officers, employees, and agents) and the management of the Park.”  (Id., at Ex. A, para. 2.2.)

 

            Plaintiff objected to the entirety of and portions of the declaration of Michael Misera. (See, ruling on objections above. The burden of production of evidence in the context of a motion to compel arbitration was cogently articulated by the following case. “First, the moving party bears the burden of producing prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy. The moving party can meet its initial burden by attaching to the [motion or] petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the [opposing party's] signature. Alternatively, the moving party can meet its burden by setting forth the agreement's provisions in the motion. For this step, it is not necessary to follow the normal procedures of document authentication. If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party does not dispute the existence of the arbitration agreement, then nothing more is required for the moving party to meet its burden of persuasion. [¶] If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party disputes the agreement, then in the second step, the opposing party bears the burden of producing evidence to challenge the authenticity of the agreement. The opposing party can do this in several ways. For example, the opposing party may testify under oath or declare under penalty of perjury that the party never saw or does not remember seeing the agreement, or that the party never signed or does not remember signing the agreement. [¶] If the opposing party meets its burden of producing evidence, then in the third step, the moving party must establish with admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties. The burden of proving the agreement by a preponderance of the evidence remains with the moving party.” Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165–166 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

            Defendant has met its initial burden of producing prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate pursuant to the declaration of Michael Misera. As noted above, at this stage, Defendant need only show prima facie evidence which it has properly done.

 

            Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to produce evidence to challenge the authenticity of the agreement, by, for example, challenging that she ever signed the agreement. The Court notes that Plaintiff produced no competent evidence to challenge the agreement. Instead, Plaintiff freely acknowledges signing the agreement. Thus, the burden does not shift to the third step where the moving party must establish with admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties.

 

            Defendant has met its initial burden to show the existence of an arbitration agreement. Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to show that the arbitration clause should not be enforced.  Rice, supra, 247 Cal.App.4th at 1223.  Plaintiff did not meet her burden to show that the arbitration agreement should not be enforced.

 

            First, Plaintiff argues that the agreement is void as against public policy. Residential lease agreements purporting to waive certain tenant procedural rights are deemed “void as contrary to public policy” by Civ. Code § 1953. Section 1953(a)(4) “precludes a residential lease agreement from including any modification or waiver of a tenant's ‘procedural rights in litigation in any action involving his rights and obligations as a tenant.’” Jaramillo v. JH Real Estate Partners, Inc. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 394, 403-04. Therefore, pursuant to CC § 1953(a)(4), public policy prohibits arbitration provisions which are contained within residential lease or rental agreements. See, also, Williams v. 3620 W. 102nd Street, Inc. (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 1087, 1089-92; See also Harris v. University Village Thousand Oaks, CCRC, LLC (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 847, 850-56. Section 1953 voids, as contrary to public policy, the waiver of procedural litigation rights in a residential lease agreement and inherent in an arbitration agreement is a waiver of any right to a jury trial. Id. Thus, pursuant to CC § 1953(a)(4), a residential tenant cannot validly agree to binding arbitration to resolve disputes regarding the tenant's rights and obligations as a tenant. Id. at 852.  A tenant may validly agree to arbitrate habitability issues through a separate agreement to arbitrate that is entirely independent of any lease agreement. See, Williams, supra, 53 Cal.App.5th at 1092-93; See, also, Jaramillo, supra, 111 Cal.App.4th at 404.

 

            Here, however, Plaintiff fails to provide competent authority to establish that the authority noted above applies to a lease agreement in which Plaintiff actually owns her own “residence” (mobile home), and merely rents the space or land to place the mobile home on Defendant’s premises. For example, Civ. Code § 1953, which is the statutory underpinning of the case authority noted above, states right from the outset: “Any provision of a lease or rental agreement of a dwelling by which the lessee agrees to modify or waive any of the following rights shall be void as contrary to public policy[.]” Civ. Code, § 1953(a)(emphasis added). Plaintiff did not lease or rent a “dwelling.” Similarly, Plaintiff’s numerous references to Civ. Code § 1941 et seq. are unavailing. Civ. Code § 1940(a) which provides definitions regarding the application of that chapter states: “Except as provided in subdivision (b), this chapter shall apply to all persons who hire dwelling units located within this state including tenants, lessees, boarders, lodgers, and others, however denominated.” Civ. Code, § 1940(a). Civ. Code § 1940(c) defines dwelling unit: “‘Dwelling unit’ means a structure or the part of a structure that is used as a home, residence, or sleeping place by one person who maintains a household or by two or more persons who maintain a common household.” Id. Plaintiff did not lease a dwelling unit.

 

            Plaintiff then, correctly, analyzes and presents arguments referring to the more applicable statutory provisions set forth in the Mobilehome Residency Law (“MRL”), codified by Civ. Proc. Code. § 798 et seq. Civ. Code, § 798.25.5 states: “Any rule or regulation of a mobilehome park that (a) is unilaterally adopted by the management, (b) is implemented without the consent of the homeowners, and (c) by its terms purports to deny homeowners their right to a trial by jury or which would mandate binding arbitration of any dispute between the management and homeowners shall be void and unenforceable.” Here, however, there is no showing that the arbitration agreement was “unilaterally adopted by” Defendant. Instead, as noted above, both Plaintiff and Defendant’s assignor signed the agreement. Plaintiff’s reference to the MRL (Civ. Code § 798.870) that deals with nuisance claims is unavailing as no nuisance cause of action was asserted. In addition, simply because that portion of the statute referred to remedy only by “civil action” does not render the arbitration agreement unenforceable.

 

            “Lionsgate's primary argument centers on the False Claims Act's references to a ‘civil action’ to recover penalties and damages, and to assessments and findings by the court. However, it is settled that statutory claims are not inarbitrable merely because the statute in question includes such provisions. Business and Professions Code section 17204 declares that “[a]ctions for relief pursuant to this chapter shall be prosecuted exclusively in a court of competent jurisdiction....” Nevertheless, our Supreme Court has held that claims for restitution and disgorgement under Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq. are arbitrable. Similarly, although Civil Code section 1780, subdivision (c) formerly specified that actions under the Consumers Legal Remedies Act (CLRA) must be filed in “any court ... having jurisdiction of the subject matter,” the high court ruled that actions for damages under that version of the CLRA could be arbitrated. It has also been established that antitrust claims under the Cartwright Act (Bus. & Prof.Code, § 16700 et seq.) are subject to arbitration, despite language in Business and Professions Code section 16750, subdivision (a) contemplating civil actions “in any court having jurisdiction....” County of Solano v. Lionsgate Corp. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 741, 747 (internal citations and quotations omitted).


            Plaintiff then attempts to argue the purported unfairness of the provision regarding costs and then argues the alleged the adhesive nature of the agreement. Thus, Plaintiff appears to begin arguing unconscionability by arguing substantive first and then procedural.

 

            A court can invalidate an arbitration agreement when it is unconscionable or against public policy.  See Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 99.  Unconscionability contains two elements:  procedural unconscionability and substantive unconscionability. 

 

            “[U]nconscionability has both a procedural and a substantive' element, the former focusing on ‘oppression' or 'surprise' due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on 'overly harsh' or 'one-sided' results.  The prevailing view is that [procedural and substantive unconscionability] must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability." But they need not be present in the same degree. Essentially a sliding scale is invoked which disregards the regularity of the procedural process of the contract formation, that creates the terms, in proportion to the greater harshness or unreasonableness of the substantive terms themselves.  In other words, the more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.”  See Armendariz, 24 Cal.4th at 114.     

 

            “The traditional standard of unconscionability . . . is that the inequality amounting to fraud must be so strong and manifest as to shock the conscience and confound the judgment of any man of common sense.   Subsequent decisions have defined an unconscionable contract in varying but similar terms, such as a contract that no man in his senses and not under delusion would make on the one hand, and as no honest and fair man would accept on the other.”  See California Grocers Assn. v. Bank of America (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 205, 214-15 (internal citations omitted).

 

            Procedural unconscionability may be established by showing oppression and surprise.  Oppression occurs where the parties have unequal bargaining power, and the contract is not the result of meaningful negotiations.  Surprise recognizes the extent to which the agreed upon terms were hidden.

 

            “In many cases of adhesion contracts, the weaker party lacks not only the opportunity to bargain but also any realistic opportunity to look elsewhere for a more favorable contract; he must either adhere to the standardized agreement or forego the needed service.”  Madden v. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (1976) 17 Cal.3d 699, 711. Simply because a contract may have elements of an adhesion contract does not render the agreement procedurally unconscionable.  “The ultimate issue in every case is whether the terms of the contract are sufficiently unfair, in view of all relevant circumstances, that a court should withhold enforcement.”  Sanchez v. Valencia Holding (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 912. 

 

            “[C]ourts, including ours, have used various nonexclusive formulations to capture the notion that unconscionability requires a substantial degree of unfairness beyond a simple old-fashioned bad bargain. This latter qualification is important. Commerce depends on the enforceability, in most instances, of a duly executed written contract. A party cannot avoid a contractual obligation merely by complaining that the deal, in retrospect, was unfair or a bad bargain. Not all one-sided contract provisions are unconscionable; hence the various intensifiers in our formulations: ‘overly harsh,’ ‘unduly oppressive,’ ‘unreasonably favorable.’ The ultimate issue in every case is whether the terms of the contract are sufficiently unfair, in view of all relevant circumstances, that a court should withhold enforcement.” Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1245 (internal citations and quotations omitted; emphasis in original).

 

            To support her procedural unconscionability argument, Plaintiff candidly acknowledges recognizing paragraphs 49 to 51 of the agreement and that she initialed these provisions. Plaintiff argues that she did not read or understand these provisions, and no one explained them to her.  (Decl., Nira Schwartz, ¶¶ 3-5.) 

 

            Defendant has adequately established that Plaintiff signed the arbitration agreement. It is not an adequate defense to enforcement that Plaintiff failed to read and understand the agreement before signing the agreement. Caballero v. Premier Care Simi Valley LLC (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 512, 519. In addition, Defendants have no obligation to highlight, explain, or discuss the terms of the arbitration agreement as any such requirement would be contrary to the FAA. Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 914-15.

 

            By signing the agreement, Plaintiff agreed to the following: “BY INITIALING IN THE SPACE BELOW YOU ARE AGREEING TO HAVE ANY DISPUTE ARISING OUT OF THE MATTERS INCLUDED IN THE "ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES" PROVISION DECIDED BY NEUTRAL ARBITRATION AS PROVIDED BY CALIFORNIA LAW AND YOU ARE GIVING UP ANY RIGHTS YOU MIGHT POSSESS TO HAVE THE DISPUTE LITIGATED IN A COURT OR JURY TRIAL. BY INITIALING IN THE SPACE BELOW, YOU ARE GIVING UP YOUR JUDICIAL RIGHTS TO DISCOVERY AND APPEAL, UNLESS SUCH RIGHTS ARE SPECIFICALLY INCLUDED IN THE "ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES" PROVISION. IF YOU REFUSE TO SUBMIT TO ARBITRATION AFTER AGREEING TO THIS PROVISION, YOU MAY BE COMPELLED TO ARBITRATE UNDER THE AUTHORITY OF THIS CALIFORNIA CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE. YOUR AGREEMENT TO THIS ARBITRATION PROVISION IS VOLUNTARY. WE HAVE READ AND UNDERSTAND THE FOREGOING AND AGREE TO SUBMIT DISPUTES ARISING OUT OF THE MATTERS INCLUDED IN THE "ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES" PROVISION TO THE NEUTRAL ARBITRATION.”  (Decl., Misera, Ex. A, Para. 51.6.) (Capitalization in original.)  The required level of oppression and surprise is lacking here. Further, the language of the agreement itself states in explicit terms that Plaintiff’s agreement was voluntary. Therefore, the Court finds that the arbitration was not procedurally unconscionable.

 

            As noted above, both procedural and substantive unconscionability must exist for the Court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce the arbitration provision. As noted, Plaintiffs submitted insufficient evidence to support the procedural unconscionability element. Thus, the Court need not even address the substantive unconscionability arguments.

 

            Therefore, the Motion to Compel Arbitration is granted. The Court action is ordered stayed. The Motion for Trial Preference, currently scheduled for August 19, 2024, is taken off the Court’s calendar.

Terracotta Credit Reit LLC v. Loren McBride Cohen, et al.

23TRCV03681

1.      Terracotta Credit Reit LLC’s Motion for Reference     

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Terracotta Credit Reit LLC’s Motion for Reference is granted.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint was filed on November 6, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. “Plaintiff and the Defendants entered into certain a series of written agreements related to certain real estate loans for property located in Washington State. Specifically, the agreements at issue are: (a) written Commercial Guaranty agreements with respect to six loans (Compl. Exs. D, F, H, K, M, O); and (b) written Hazardous Substances Certificate and Indemnity Agreements (“HSCI Agreements”) with respect to three of those six loans (Compl. Exs. E, G, I). By the terms of those agreements, the borrowers’ default on the corresponding loan agreements triggered the Defendants’ obligation under the six corresponding Commercial Guaranty agreements. Accordingly, TCCR has brought breach of contract claims against the Defendants who entered into the six Commercial Guaranty agreements to recover the amounts owed under those agreements.” (Plaintiff’s Case Management Statement, Attachment 4(b).)  

 

Motion for Reference

 

Plaintiff moves to compel a reference pursuant to the written agreement entered into between Plaintiff and Defendants.

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 638 states, in relevant part:

“A referee may be appointed upon the agreement of the parties filed with the clerk, or judge, or entered in the minutes, or upon the motion of a party to a written contract or lease that provides that any controversy arising therefrom shall be heard by a referee if the court finds a reference agreement exists between the parties: (a) To hear and determine any or all of the issues in an action or proceeding, whether of fact or of law, and to report a statement of decision.”

 

“The six guaranty agreements provide that: “If there is a lawsuit, Guarantors agree upon Lender's request to submit to the jurisdiction of the courts of Los Angeles County, State of California.” Compl. Ex. D at 3, F at 3, H at 3, K at 3, M at 4, O at 4.” (Memorandum of Points and Authorities, page 2, lines 16-19.)

 

The provision requiring judicial reference states:

““The parties agree that in any action, proceeding or counterclaim brought by any party against the other involving any controversy, dispute or claim arising out or relating to this Agreement or any of the Related Documents, including all issues therein, whether of fact and law, shall be heard and determined, at the option of either party, by a referee appointed by the court in accordance with the reference procedures in California Code of Civil Procedure Section 638 et seq.” (Complaint, Ex. D at 3; Ex. E at 3, Ex. F at 3, Ex. G at 3, Ex. H at 3, Ex. I at 3, Ex. K at 3, Ex. M at 3, Ex. O at 3.)  Plaintiff moves to compel a reference pursuant to the specific language of these sections.

 

Defendants oppose the motion. First, Defendants argue that Loren Cohen is not a party to the agreements that contain the judicial reference provisions. However, the provisions are specifically set forth in the guaranty agreements to which Cohen was a party as a principal, and not merely as an agent for any other parties or entities.

 

Defendants next argue that the first two causes of action for Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation are not encompassed within the reference provision. However, this argument is misplaced. The provision specifically involves “any controversy, dispute or claim arising out [of[ or relating to this Agreement.”  The causes of action for misrepresentation relate to a dispute arising out of the loan agreements.  Ericksen, Arbuthnot, McCarthy, Kearney & Walsh, Inc. v. 100 Oak Street (1983) 35 Cal.3d 312, 323 (holding that claims of fraudulent inducement of the contract fall within the scope of the contract’s broad-form arbitration agreement). Along these lines, Defendants also argue that reference should be denied as claims should not be “split” based on the potential for conflicting rulings on facts and law. This argument appears to be based on the presumption that the first two causes of action are not subject to reference. Because all claims are subject to reference, this concern is unavailing.

 

Defendants also argue that Plaintiff violates the “one-action rule.” Defendants contend that Plaintiff has filed multiple different actions on the same facts in Washington state. Defendants’ argument is based on the contention that Plaintiff violates the “one-action rule” pursuant to Washington law. The Court was prepared to provide an analysis of the one-action rule as set forth under California law pursuant to CCP § 726 and applicable case law interpreting this section. However, Defendants are attempting to analyze based on Washington law without any showing that Washington law applies to this California state court action. Defendants have not established through any procedural mechanism that Washington law must be applied to this case. Thus, this issue is not before this Court in the context of this motion.

 

As to the merits of the issues before this Court, Plaintiff has sufficiently met its burden to establish the existence of a reference provision encompassing the claims presented by this action, and that this controversy should be heard by a referee.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion for reference is granted.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Anthony Rodriguez, et al. v. LAX Auto Center, LLC, et al.

            24TRCV00265

1.      LAX Auto Center, LLC’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            LAX Auto Center, LLC’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action is granted.

 

Background

           

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on January 25, 2024.  Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff is a former employee of Defendant. Defendant failed to provide proper break and rest periods. Defendant failed to compensate Plaintiff at the required rate for all the hours worked. Defendant failed to provide accurate payroll records. Plaintiff alleges the following cause of action: 1. Violation of California Labor Code § 2698, et seq.

 

            Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

            “California law reflects a strong public policy in favor of arbitration as a relatively quick and inexpensive method for resolving disputes.  [Citation.]  To further that policy, [Code of Civil Procedure] section 1281.2 requires a trial court to enforce a written arbitration agreement unless one of three limited exceptions applies.  [Citation.]  Those statutory exceptions arise where (1) a party waives the right to arbitration; (2) grounds exist for revoking the arbitration agreement; and (3) pending litigation with a third party creates the possibility of conflicting rulings on common factual or legal issues.  (§ 1281.2, subds. (a)–(c).)”  Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 959, 967.

 

“The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.”  Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.

 

“The party opposing arbitration has the burden of demonstrating that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to require arbitration of the dispute.  Nonetheless, this policy does not override ordinary principles of contract interpretation.  [T]he contractual terms themselves must be carefully examined before the parties to the contract can be ordered to arbitration:  Although [t]he law favors contracts for arbitration of disputes between parties, there is no policy compelling persons to accept arbitration of controversies which they have not agreed to arbitrate.”  Rice v. Downs (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 1213, 1223 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

In Rowe v. Exline (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1286, the Court of Appeal found that “a nonsignatory sued as an agent of a signatory may enforce an arbitration agreement.”  Id. at 1286.  In addition, “a nonsignatory who is the agent of a signatory can even be compelled to arbitrate claims against his will.”  Id. at 1285, citing Harris v. Superior Court (1986) 188 Cal.App.3d 475, 477–78.  Further, “in many cases, nonparties to arbitration agreements are allowed to enforce those agreements where there is sufficient identity of parties.”  Valley Casework, Inc. v. Comfort Construction, Inc. (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 1013, 1021.  This includes nonparties as agents of a party as well as “a third party beneficiary of an arbitration agreement.”  Ibid. 

 

Defendant moves for an order compelling Plaintiff’s individual PAGA claims to arbitration and for an order to stay Plaintiff’s representative PAGA claims. The motion is made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1281 et seq. and the FAA, on the ground that Plaintiff is bound by a written agreement to arbitrate the subject matter of the Complaint. Defendant argues that a valid arbitration agreement exists between the parties that requires arbitration of the claims, including the individual PAGA action, and that authority compels a stay of Plaintiff’s representative PAGA claims.

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2 states, in relevant part: “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists[. . .]” “Generally, an arbitration agreement must be memorialized in writing. A party's acceptance of an agreement to arbitrate may be express, as where a party signs the agreement. A signed agreement is not necessary, however, and a party's acceptance may be implied in fact or be effectuated by delegated consent. An arbitration clause within a contract may be binding on a party even if the party never actually read the clause.” Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236 (internal citations omitted).

 

            The burden of production of evidence in the context of a motion to compel arbitration was cogently articulated by the following case. “First, the moving party bears the burden of producing prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy. The moving party can meet its initial burden by attaching to the [motion or] petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the [opposing party's] signature. Alternatively, the moving party can meet its burden by setting forth the agreement's provisions in the motion. For this step, it is not necessary to follow the normal procedures of document authentication. If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party does not dispute the existence of the arbitration agreement, then nothing more is required for the moving party to meet its burden of persuasion. [¶] If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party disputes the agreement, then in the second step, the opposing party bears the burden of producing evidence to challenge the authenticity of the agreement. The opposing party can do this in several ways. For example, the opposing party may testify under oath or declare under penalty of perjury that the party never saw or does not remember seeing the agreement, or that the party never signed or does not remember signing the agreement. [¶] If the opposing party meets its burden of producing evidence, then in the third step, the moving party must establish with admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties. The burden of proving the agreement by a preponderance of the evidence remains with the moving party.” Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165–166 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

Defendant has met its initial burden of producing prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate. (Decl., Mary Hicks, ¶¶ 3-15, Exs. A-C.) The arbitration agreement requires that the parties agree to submit any controversy arising out of Plaintiff’s employment with Defendant to binding arbitration.  As noted above, at this stage, Defendant need only show prima facie evidence of the existence of the agreement which it has properly done.

 

Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to produce evidence to challenge the authenticity of the agreement, by, for example, challenging that he ever signed the agreement. The Court notes that Plaintiff produced no competent evidence to challenge the agreements. Instead, Plaintiff acknowledges signing the agreements. Thus, the burden does not shift to the third step where the moving party must establish with admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties.

 

            Because the moving party met its initial burden to show the existence of a valid arbitration clause with respect to the claims of the Plaintiff, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to show that the arbitration clause should not be enforced as to these claims. Rice, supra, 247 Cal.App.4th at 1223.

 

            Plaintiff argues that the arbitration clause is both procedurally and substantively unconscionable.  A court can invalidate an arbitration agreement when it is unconscionable or against public policy.  See, Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 99.  Unconscionability contains two elements:  procedural unconscionability and substantive unconscionability. 

 

            “[U]nconscionability has both a procedural and a substantive' element, the former focusing on ‘oppression' or 'surprise' due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on 'overly harsh' or 'one-sided' results.  The prevailing view is that [procedural and substantive unconscionability] must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability." But they need not be present in the same degree. Essentially a sliding scale is invoked which disregards the regularity of the procedural process of the contract formation, that creates the terms, in proportion to the greater harshness or unreasonableness of the substantive terms themselves.  In other words, the more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.”  See Armendariz, 24 Cal.4th at 114.     

 

            “The traditional standard of unconscionability . . . is that the inequality amounting to fraud must be so strong and manifest as to shock the conscience and confound the judgment of any man of common sense.   Subsequent decisions have defined an unconscionable contract in varying but similar terms, such as a contract that no man in his senses and not under delusion would make on the one hand, and as no honest and fair man would accept on the other.”  See, California Grocers Assn. v. Bank of America (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 205, 214-15 (internal citations omitted).

 

            Procedural unconscionability may be established by showing oppression and surprise.  Oppression occurs where the parties have unequal bargaining power, and the contract is not the result of meaningful negotiations.  Surprise recognizes the extent to which the agreed upon terms were hidden.

 

            “In many cases of adhesion contracts, the weaker party lacks not only the opportunity to bargain but also any realistic opportunity to look elsewhere for a more favorable contract; he must either adhere to the standardized agreement or forego the needed service.”  Madden v. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (1976) 17 Cal.3d 699, 711. Simply because a contract may have elements of an adhesion contract does not render the agreement procedurally unconscionable.  “The ultimate issue in every case is whether the terms of the contract are sufficiently unfair, in view of all relevant circumstances, that a court should withhold enforcement.”  Sanchez v. Valencia Holding (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 912. 

 

            “[C]ourts, including ours, have used various nonexclusive formulations to capture the notion that unconscionability requires a substantial degree of unfairness beyond a simple old-fashioned bad bargain. This latter qualification is important. Commerce depends on the enforceability, in most instances, of a duly executed written contract. A party cannot avoid a contractual obligation merely by complaining that the deal, in retrospect, was unfair or a bad bargain. Not all one-sided contract provisions are unconscionable; hence the various intensifiers in our formulations: ‘overly harsh,’ ‘unduly oppressive,’ ‘unreasonably favorable.’ The ultimate issue in every case is whether the terms of the contract are sufficiently unfair, in view of all relevant circumstances, that a court should withhold enforcement.” Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1245 (internal citations and quotations omitted; emphasis in original).

 

            To support his procedural unconscionability argument, Plaintiff states that he was mandated to sign several documents without explanation, was not adequately explained what arbitration meant, was not explained his rights, was not given enough time to review, and did not understand he was waiving his right to a jury trial.  (Decl., Anthony Rodriguez, ¶¶ 3-6.) 

 

            As noted earlier, Defendant has adequately established that Plaintiff signed the arbitration agreement. It is not an adequate defense to enforcement that Plaintiff failed to read and understand the agreement before signing the agreement. Caballero v. Premier Care Simi Valley LLC (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 512, 519. In addition, Defendants have no obligation to highlight, explain, or discuss the terms of the arbitration agreement as any such requirement would be contrary to the FAA. Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 914-15.

 

            By signing the agreement, Plaintiff “ATTESTS TO THE FACT THAT I HAVE BEEN GIVEN AN OPPORTUNITY TO READ AND CONSIDER THIS AGREEMENT, AND THAT I HAVE READ, UNDERSTAND, AND AGREE TO BE LEGALLY BOUND BY ALL OF THE ABOVE TERMS. I AGREE THAT THE COMPANY IS BINDING ITSELF TO THIS AGREEMENT BY PRESENTING THIS AGREEMENT TO ME FOR SIGNATURE.” (Defendant’s Ex. A, p. 4). (Capitalization in original). In addition, Plaintiff signed an agreement that stated the following: “I UNDERSTAND THAT I AM GIVING UP THE RIGHT TO TRIAL BY JURY, BY A SITTING TRIAL COURT JUDGE AND/OR BY FINAL ADJUDICATION THROUGH AN ADMINISTRATIVE PROCESS BY AGREEING TO BINDING ARBITRATION.” (Id. at Ex. A, Para. 14.) (Capitalization in original). The required level of oppression and surprise is lacking here. Therefore, the Court finds that the arbitration was not procedurally unconscionable.

 

            As noted above, both procedural and substantive unconscionability must exist for the Court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce the arbitration provision. As noted, Plaintiff submitted insufficient evidence to support the procedural unconscionability element. Thus, the Court need not even address the substantive unconscionability arguments. The Court does note however that the portions of the agreement which Plaintiff contends is substantively unconscionable (fee sharing of certain claims and arbitration payment due date) could have potentially been carved out or severed had Plaintiff met his burden to show procedural unconscionability.

 

However, as to the former provision, the cost provision does specify that costs are to be allocated pursuant to controlling law set forth in the California Arbitration Act. A costs provision that complies with the law does not appear to be substantively unconscionable. As to the latter provision, the Court simply notes that if Defendant fails to meet the payment deadline Plaintiff could easily seek an order to vacate the order to compel arbitration on this basis alone. Doe v. Superior Court (2023) 95 Cal. App. 5th 346.

 

On June 15, 2022, the United States Supreme Court issued its opinion in Viking River Cruises, Inc, v. Moriana, 142 S.Ct. 1906 (2022). The Supreme Court held that employees who sign valid arbitration agreements are subject to arbitration of PAGA actions on an individual basis. Thus, Viking River Cruises overruled the lines of cases led by Iskanian v. CLS Transportation Los Angeles, LLC (2014) 59 Cal.4th 348.

 

An argument could be made that Viking River Cruises only invalidated Iskanian “insofar as it precludes division of PAGA actions into individual and non-individual claims through an agreement to arbitrate.” Viking River Cruises, Inc, v. Moriana, 142 S.Ct. 1906, 1924 (2022). Thus, Viking River Cruises left open the question of whether Plaintiff’s representative PAGA claim is subject to arbitration.

 

On July 17, 2023, the California Supreme Court issued its opinion in Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1104 resolving this question. In Adolph, the Supreme Court held that a plaintiff whose individual claims are compelled to arbitration retains standing to pursue representative PAGA claims in court. Id. at 1123-24. The Supreme Court did not mandate a stay of the civil action but stated that the Court has discretion to enter a stay. “When an action includes arbitrable and nonarbitrable components, the resulting bifurcated proceedings are not severed from one another; rather, the court may ‘stay the trial of the action until such arbitration has been had in accordance with the terms of the agreement.’ (9 U.S.C. § 3; see Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.) In McGill v Citibank, N.A. (2017) 2 Cal.5th 945, 966 [216 Cal. Rptr. 3d 627, 393 P.3d 85], we explained that this principle extends to “piecemeal litigation of ‘arbitrable and inarbitrable remedies derived from the same statutory claim.’” Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1104, 1125. Plaintiff contends that the Court should exercise its discretion here to not stay the instant action because Plaintiff, other aggrieved employees, and the State will be unduly prejudiced by a stay because of undue delay.

 

            The Court finds that Plaintiff has not articulated significant prejudice that would occur if the instant action were stayed. In addition, while Adolph does not necessarily mandate a stay, but instead only states that the Court has discretion to order a stay, the instant Court notes, however, that significant portions of the Adolph decision discuss a trial court’s discretion to stay the court action, thus, providing a strong suggestion that a stay in most situations would be the most efficient and reasonable outcome. The instant Court also finds that a stay of the instant action would be most appropriate in this instance.

 

            Finally, Plaintiff’s attempt to invoke the provisions of CCP § 1281.6 is not proper at this time and premature as that section specifically states that it must be presented pursuant to a noticed petition.

 

Therefore, the Motion to Compel Arbitration is granted. The individual PAGA claims are ordered to proceed through arbitration. The instant action, which would now only encompass representative PAGA claims, is ordered stayed.
