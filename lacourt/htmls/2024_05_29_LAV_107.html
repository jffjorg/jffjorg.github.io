DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV01477    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

EFRAIN HERNANDEZ GOMEZ, et al.,

Plaintiffs,

 

   v.

 

PARK RESEDA CONDOMINIUM ASSOCIATION INC., A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION, et al.

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   23VECV01477               107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Motion to Strike (not anti-SLAPP) - without Demurrer

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Granted as to first and second causes of action; denied as to fourth cause of action; granted in part as to prayer for

damages; leave to amend is granted.

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Efrain Hernandez Gomez (“Gomez”) and Norma Letisia Valencia (“Valencia” and, together with Gomez, “Plaintiffs”) are a married couple who live at Park Reseda Condominiums (the “Property”) with their children. On April 5, 2021, Plaintiffs were involved in a violent altercation with defendant Raymundo Reducindo Oliva (“Reducindo Oliva”) on the Property, at which Reducindo Oliva was a security guard employed by defendant GTO Security Corp. (“GTO Security” and, together with Plaintiffs, “Parties”). The remaining named defendants, Park Reseda Condominium Association Inc. (“Association”) and LB Property Management Inc. d/b/a LBPM (“LBPM” and, together with Reducindo Oliva, GTO Security, and Association, “Defendants”) are the owners, operators, or managers of the Property. 

 

On April 4, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendants alleging causes of action for (1) battery, (2) assault, (3) negligence, (4) negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of employee, and (5) loss of consortium. 

 

On December 18, 2023, this court granted GTO Security’s motion to strike punitive damages from the Complaint as to itself.

 

On January 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an amended complaint (the “FAC”), with the same causes of action and again seeking punitive damages against all Defendants. 

 

On February 28, 2024, this court granted GTO Security’s motion to strike punitive damages from the FAC as to itself.

 

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiffs served a second amended complaint (the “SAC”) on Defendants, although the SAC was, by accident, not filed with this court until May 15, 2024.

 

On April 9, 2024, GTO Security filed a motion (“the Motion”) to strike punitive damages from the SAC as to itself.

 

On April 19, 2024, pursuant to a stipulation between Plaintiffs, Association, and LBPM, this court struck punitive damages from the SAC, without prejudice, as to Association and LBPM.

 

On May 3, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition (“the Opposition”) to the Motion. GTO did not timely reply to the Opposition.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer in person or by telephone” in good faith before the filing of a motion to strike to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the motion to strike.

 

GTO Security submits with the Motion a declaration by its counsel stating that he sent an e-mail to Plaintiffs’ counsel describing GTO’s objections to the SAC and asking Plaintiffs’ counsel to “[p]lease let [him] know [Plaintiffs’] position in this regard.” (Snyder Decl. ¶ 1.) GTO Security’s counsel additionally states that he and Plaintiff’s counsel did not reach a resolution of the issues raised by the Motion before it was filed. (Id. at ¶ 2.)

 

Because the statute mandating that parties meet and confer before the filing of a motion to strike specifies that such meet and confer efforts be made “in person or by telephone,” an e-mail exchange is insufficient to meet this requirement. GTO’s counsel does not clarify in his declaration if any subsequent meet and confer efforts between himself and Plaintiff’s counsel occurred in person or by phone or if any subsequent efforts were made. We remind Parties of their obligation to meet and confer with each other in good faith.

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at §¿437.) 

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.) 

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice….”  (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).) “In order to justify an award of punitive¿damages, the defendant must be guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462 (internal citations omitted).) “[M]alice in fact is required rather than implied malice.¿However, malice does not require actual intent to harm.¿Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228 (internal citations omitted).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his¿conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)  

 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And, “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,” within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra at 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra at 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].) 

 

California Civil Code section 3294 provides that an employer shall be liable for punitive damages only if it “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,” and further, “[w]ith respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (b).) “In order to demonstrate that an employee is a true managing agent under¿section 3294, subdivision (b), a plaintiff seeking punitive damages would have to show that the employee exercised substantial discretionary authority over significant aspects of a corporation's business.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 577.) “The scope of a corporate employee's discretion and authority under our managing agent test is therefore a question of fact for decision on a case-by-case basis.” (Davis v. Kiewit Pacific Co. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 358, 366, emphasis original.) Moreover, “liability under¿Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (b)¿requires actual knowledge by an¿employer.” (Weeks v. Baker & McKenzie (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1158.)

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

GTO Security moves to strike from the SAC paragraph 34 and paragraph 46, both of which relate to GTO Security’s liability for punitive damages, the punitive damages aspects of the SAC’s fourth cause of action in paragraphs 53 through 60, and the SAC’s prayer for punitive damages. (Notice of Motion p. 2; Motion p. 7.)

 

  1. Paragraph 34 / First Cause of Action

 

Paragraph 34 is the last paragraph of the SAC’s first cause of action for battery and reads,

 

GTO [Security] is also liable for punitive damages because it ratified Reducindo [Oliva]’s misconduct by hiring him to be a security guard and giving him a gun knowing that he was neither licensed to be a security guard or carry a gun, failing to ensure that he became licensed, knowingly allowing him to drink on the job, and accepting his violence toward the public without providing him with anti-violence training or terminating him.

 

(SAC p. 6.)

 

The SAC’s first cause of action does not allege any acts of GTO Security that independently warrant punitive damages. Rather, Plaintiffs base GTO Security’s liability on a respondeat superior theory, alleging that “GTO [Security] is liable for Reducindo [Olivo]’s battery because it occurred within the course and scope of his employment by GTO [Security].” (SAC p. 5.)

 

Paragraph 34 of the SAC only alleges that GTO Security is liable for punitive damages because it ratified Reducindo Olivo’s actions. (SAC p. 6.) “‘Ratification’ of its [employee’s] acts by a corporation is the confirmation and acceptance of a previous act.” (Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 168.) The SAC does not allege any acts of GTO Security that confirmed or accepted Reducindo Oliva’s acts and that occurred after the violent incident between Plaintiffs and Reducindo Oliva that underlies this action. For example, GTO Security’s hiring of Reducindo Oliva and provision of a gun to Reducindo Olivo are necessarily acts that would have occurred before Reducindo Olivo’s violent incident with Plaintiffs and therefore cannot be said to indicate ratification by GTO Security of Reducindo Olivo’s acts. Thus, the SAC’s first cause of action fails to plead facts sufficient to show that Plaintiffs are entitled to punitive damages from GTO Security.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion as to the punitive damages allegations in paragraph 34 of the SAC.

 

  1. Paragraph 46 / Second Cause of Action

 

Paragraph 46 is the last paragraph of the SAC’s second cause of action for assault and reads,

 

GTO [Security] is also liable for punitive damages because it ratified Reducindo [Olivo]’s misconduct by hiring him to be a security guard and giving him a gun knowing that he was neither licensed to be a security guard or carry a gun, failing to ensure that he became licensed, knowingly allowing him to drink on the job, and accepting his violence toward the public without providing him with anti-violence training or terminating him.

 

(SAC p. 7.)

 

Like was the case in the SAC’s first cause of action discussed immediately above, Plaintiffs base GTO Security’s liability on a respondeat superior theory, alleging that “GTO [Security] is liable for Reducindo Olivo’s assault because it occurred within the course and scope of his employment by GTO [Security].” (SAC p. 6.) And, like was the case in the SAC’s first cause of action, the SAC’s second cause of action does not allege any acts of GTO Security that independently warrant punitive damages.

 

Paragraph 46 of the SAC only alleges that GTO Security is liable for punitive damages because it ratified Reducindo Olivo’s actions. (SAC p. 7.) For the same reason we found that punitive damages were not supported by paragraph 34’s allegations that GTO Security had ratified Reducindo Olivo’s actions, we find here that Plaintiffs’ claim for punitive damages is not supported by the allegations, which do not show any after-occurring acts by GTO Security that could be said to have ratified Reducindo Olivo’s actions.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion as to the punitive damages allegations in paragraph 46 of the SAC.

 

  1. Punitive Damages Elements of the Fourth Cause of Action

 

The fourth cause of action seeks punitive damages from GTO Security for the negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of an employee. (SAC p. 8.) Under California Civil Code section 3294, this cause of action “shall” be the basis of a claim for punitive damages if the employer “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”

 

Plaintiffs allege in support of the fourth cause of action four specific factual allegatins: (1) GTO Security “knew that Reducindo [Olivo] was not a licensed security guard and not authorized to carry a gun” and consequently, knew that “Reducindo [Olivo] was unfit to be a security guard when he was hired as one”; (2) GTO Security did not “cause or ensure that Reducindo [Olivo] became a licensed security guard” and consequently, “[GTO Security] retained Reducindo [Olivo] as a security guard despite [its] awareness that he was unfit be one”; (3) GTO Security “became aware that Reducindo [Olivo] drank alcohol on the job yet failed to supervise him to prevent it, failed to get him treatment, failed to train him to work without drinking alcohol, and failed to terminate him”; and (4) GTO Security knew, before the incident with Plaintiffs, “that Reducindo [Olivo] had committed violence upon members of the public while in the course and scope of his duties as a security guard, yet failed to supervise him to prevent it, failed to get him treatment, failed to train him to work with the public non-violently, and failed to terminate him.” (SAC p. 8.)

 

These factual allegations adequately plead that GTO Security had advance actual knowledge of Reducindo Olivo’s unfitness as a security guard (because he was not licensed, because he was not authorized to carry a gun, because he drank alcohol while on duty at work, and because had committed violence upon members of the public before while acting as a security guard) but nonetheless retained Reducindo Olivo as its employee as a security guard. With such knowledge of Reducindo Olivo’s unfitness for the job and past history of violent altercations with members of the public, GTO Security’s retention of Reducindo Olivo could be said to have been done with “a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”

 

Because GTO Security is a corporate employer, such “advance knowledge and conscious disregard” it demonstrated through its retention of Reducindo Olivo must have been “on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation” to support a claim for punitive damages. (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (b).) Although Plaintiffs do not name a particular officer, director, or managing agent of GTO Security’s who allegedly possessed such advance actual knowledge about Reducindo Olivo’s fitness as a security guard, we do not believe they are required to at this early stage before discovery as long as it is alleged of someone in such a position of authority within GTO Security. The allegations raised in the SAC’s fourth cause of action could only have been made by an agent of GTO Security with the authority to investigate, supervise, hire, and fire security guards and therefore someone with the discretionary authority to control employee retention and supervision, which could be a “significant aspect of [GTO Security’s] business.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc., supra, 21 Cal.4th 563 at 577.) We find that the current allegations of the SAC’s fourth cause of action, which are all phrased in terms of what the corporate entity GTO Security knew or did rather than what individual agents of GTO Security knew or did, are sufficient to put GTO on notice of the general nature of the claims being made against it. We also find that the SAC’s fourth cause of action adequately pleads that such knowledge and acts belonged to or were conducted by “an officer, director, or managing agent of [GTO Security]” so as to support a claim for punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion as to the punitive damages allegations in the fourth cause of action of the SAC.

 

  1. Prayer for Relief

 

GTO Security seeks to strike the third item of the SAC’s prayer for relief, which reads, “[p]unitive damages as to Plaintiffs’ First, Second, and Fourth causes of action.” (SAC p. 9.)

 

Because we agree with GTO Security that the first and second causes of action, as pled by the SAC, do not plead allegations sufficient to support claims of punitive damages, we agree that references to those causes of action may be struck from the SAC’s prayer for relief. However, because we find that the fourth cause of action pleads allegations sufficient to support a claim of punitive damages, we decline to strike the reference to the fourth cause of action or punitive damages entirely from the SAC’s prayer for relief.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion as to references to the first and second causes of action in the SAC’s prayer for relief.

 

  1. Leave to Amend

 

“Where the defect raised by a¿motion¿to¿strike¿or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave¿to¿amend¿is routinely and¿liberally¿granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) “[An order granting a motion to strike] is analogous to an order sustaining a demurrer.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.)

 

We see a possibility that the first and second causes of action could be amended to include additional factual allegations that support claims for punitive damages, if such factual allegations exist, such as allegations about any after-occurring acts by GTO Security that show its ratification of Reducindo Olivo’s actions. We also observe that although this is the second amended complaint, only the FAC was previously subject to a granted motion to strike as Plaintiffs amended the original Complaint voluntary.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS leave to amend the SAC.

III. LEGAL STANDARD

 

A motion to quash service of summons may be brought on the grounds that the court lacks personal jurisdiction over the defendant. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿418.10, subd. (a)(1).) “[W]here a defendant properly moves to¿quash¿service¿of¿summons¿the burden is on the plaintiff to prove facts requisite to an effective¿service.” (Sheard v. Superior Court (1974) 40 Cal.App.3d 207, 211.) 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure § 415.20, subdivision (a) provides for the substitute service of a summons and complaint, which “may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in [the defendant’s] office or, if no physical address is known, at his or her usual mailing address, other than a United States Postal Service post office box, with the person who is apparently in charge thereof….”

 

“Compliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction.” (Renoir v. Redstar Corp. (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1152.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 410.50, subdivision (a) provides that except as otherwise provided by statute, the court obtains ‘jurisdiction over a party from the time summons is served on him….’” (Ibid.) “Knowledge by a defendant of a plaintiff's action does not satisfy the requirement of adequate service of a summons and complaint.” (Ibid.) “[A] default judgment entered against a defendant who was not served with a summons in the manner prescribed by statute is void.” (Ellard v. Conway (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 540, 544.) “Void judgments are ineffective and unenforceable.” (Calvert v. Al Binali (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 954, 961.) However, “[a] general appearance by a party is equivalent to personal service of summons on such party” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 410.50, subd. (a).) “It makes up for defective service, or even a complete lack of service.” (Humphrey v. Bewley (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 571, 579.) And, “it is clear” that when it comes to service of process issues in California after the 1969 legislative revisions thereto, “the old rule of strict construction has been rejected and a new rule of liberal construction has been adopted.” (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 411.)

 

“As a general rule, the entry of a default terminates a defendant's rights to take any further affirmative steps in the litigation until the default is set aside. Any motion by a defaulted defendant, other than a motion to set aside the default, is unauthorized and void. It has no legal effect.” (Humphrey v. Bewley, supra, 69 Cal.App.5th 571 at 580, internal citations and quotation marks omitted.) However, “a demurrer filed by defendant after his default is taken constitutes a general appearance. This is true even though a demurrer or answer filed after entry of default is otherwise a ‘nullity.’” (Id. at 580–81, internal citations and quotation marks omitted.) But, “[a] general appearance does not retroactively validate a default entered earlier…. Code of Civil Procedure section 410.50, subdivision (a) merely provides that a general appearance is the equivalent of service of summons. Subdivision (b) then provides, ‘Jurisdiction of the court over the parties and the subject matter of an action continues throughout subsequent proceedings in the action.’” (Id. at 581, emphasis original.) Thus, subsequent service of summons or a general appearance made after the entry of default will not support the earlier entry of default. (Ibid.) In Humphrey v. Bewley, supra, 69 Cal.App.5th 571 at 581, “the trial court did not err by vacating the default” of a defendant who had filed an answer after the entry of the default, but “did err, however, by quashing service as to [the defendant].” The court observed that because the defendant had made a general appearance, he was subject to the court’s personal jurisdiction and should have been ordered to respond to the complaint. (Ibid.)

 

California Code of Civil Procedure § 473.5 (“Section 473.5”) provides that, upon the motion of a party against whom a default has been entered, a court “may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action” when certain conditions are met:

 

(a)   When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.

 

(b)   A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. The party shall serve and file with the notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action.

 

(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5.) Judges have discretion in granting relief pursuant to Section 473.5. (Olvera v. Olvera (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 32, 41.) “Section 473.5 requires that the motion to set aside the default judgment be accompanied by ‘an affidavit showing under oath that the party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect’ and ‘a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action.’” (Sakaguchi v. Sakaguchi (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 852, 861.)

 

“Whenever, under any law of this state or under any rule, regulation, order or requirement made pursuant to the law of this state, any matter is required or permitted to be supported, evidenced, established, or proved by the sworn statement, declaration, verification, certificate, oath, or affidavit, in writing of the person making the same (other than a deposition, or an oath of office, or an oath required to be taken before a specified official other than a notary public), such matter may with like force and effect be supported, evidenced, established or proved by the unsworn statement, declaration, verification, or certificate, in writing of such person which recites that it is certified or declared by him or her to be true under penalty of perjury, is subscribed by him or her, and (1), if executed within this state, states the date and place of execution, or (2), if executed at any place, within or without this state, states the date of execution and that it is so certified or declared under the laws of the State of California.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2015.5.) 

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken… No affidavit or declaration of merits shall be required of the moving party.”  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473, subd. (b).) The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief. (Cruz v.¿Fagor¿America, Inc.¿(2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.) The general¿underlying¿purpose¿of California Code of Civil Procedure § 473, subdivision (b) (“Section 473(b)”) is to promote the determination of actions on their merits.¿(Even Zohar Const. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC¿(2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 838–39.) Any doubts in applying Section¿473(b)¿must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief. (Rappleyea¿v. Campbell¿(1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 981.)¿ 

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

  1. Javier

 

In his Motion, Javier argues that because the Complaint was served by substituted service and not personal service at Ivy Green’s corporate offices, which Javier states is neither where he lives nor where he works, the service of summons was invalid. (Motion p. 42.) In a portion of the Motion labelled as a declaration, though not signed under penalty of perjury, Javier identifies the address of his workplace and distinguishes it from the address of Ivy Green’s office, where Plaintiff attempted to effect substitute service on him. (Motion p. 11.) Javier admits to having actual knowledge of the Complaint and the instant action by November 2023, a couple months after Plaintiff attempted to serve him. (Ibid.)

 

That Plaintiff attempted to effect service on Javier by substitute service and not by personal service is irrelevant, as both are acceptable means of serving summons and a complaint. However, her service of the Complaint on a person at an office that Javier does not work at defeats her efforts to effect substitute service. Javier declares that he works at a different office location than the one at which Plaintiff attempted to serve him, and substitute service on an employee at a different workplace is not calculated to give him actual notice of the action, which is emphasized by Javier’s statement that he indeed did not receive actual notice of the Complaint and this action for over a month after substitute service was effected.

 

Because service of the Complaint was defective, Javier’s actual knowledge of it is irrelevant, as proper service establishes personal jurisdiction over the defendant to an action and the defendant’s actual knowledge cannot cure improper service. (Renoir v. Redstar Corp., supra, 123 Cal.App.4th 1145 at 1152.) However, Javier did file an answer to the Complaint on December 19, 2023, eleven days after the clerk entered default as to him, and answers constitute general appearances, even if made after the entry of a default. (Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10.) Thus, Javier made a general appearance in this action and consented to personal jurisdiction on December 19, 2023. Thus, like the defaulted defendant in Humphrey v. Bewley, supra, 69 Cal.App.5th 571 at 580–81, it is appropriate to vacate Javier’s default because personal jurisdiction over him had not been established at the time default was entered, but it is not appropriate to grant his Motion to quash because he has subsequently generally appeared in this action.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS Javier’s motion to set aside/vacate default and DENIES Javier’s motion to quash service of summons.

 

  1. Byron

 

1.      Service of Summons and Complaint

 

Like Javier does in his Motion, Byron similarly argues in his Motion that Plaintiff’s service of the Complaint was invalid because the Complaint was served by substituted service and not personal service at Ivy Green’s corporate offices, which Byron states is neither where he lives nor where he works. (Motion pp. 42–43.) Byron explains that although the address at which Plaintiff attempted to effect substitute service is the address of Ivy Green’s offices and although he owns Ivy Green “as a Civil Engineer,” Plaintiff nonetheless
“never personal served me the documentation and served it to the wrong address” because [his] “employees delay to notify me about the paperwork because everything for the construction company is receiving in [a different] office.” (Id. at pp. 9, 11–12.) The address at which Plaintiff attempted to effect service on Byron is the address listed for Ivy Green in the second construction contract, which Byron signed in his capacity as founder and owner. (Id. at Exh. 2.) Byron denies having received actual notice of this action until “the end of November 2023.” (Id. at pp. 9, 43.)

 

California Code of Civil Procedure § 415.20, subdivision (a) does not require that the party being served receive actual notice of the summons and complaint for substituted service to be effective and “deemed complete.” Thus, so long as Plaintiff’s service of the Complaint complied with the requirements of Section 415.20, it is irrelevant that Byron did not receive actual notice of the Complaint until over a month after Plaintiff effected service on him. Although Byron insists in the Motion that the address at which Plaintiff attempted to effect service was the “wrong address” and that he was never served with the Complaint because “[he] did not work or live at the address [at which Plaintiff effected service]” (Motion pp. 42–43), these assertions are contradicted by other statements in and attachments to the Motion. For example, he admits elsewhere in the Motion that he is the owner of Ivy Green and works for Ivy Green, and that Ivy Green’s offices are located at the address at which Plaintiff attempted service. (Id. at pp. 9, 11–12.) It follows from these facts that Byron works at the office location at which Plaintiff effected service, even if he also works at other offices or locations. Thus, we find that Plaintiff effected substitute service on Byron at his office. There being no other grounds on which Byron challenges Plaintiff’s service of the Complaint, we find that Plaintiff properly served Byron by substitute service and that such substitute service was deemed complete on September 25, 2023.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Byron’s motion to quash service of summons.

 

2.      Section 473.5

 

Byron timely brings the Motion under Section 473.5. Notice of Byron’s Motion was filed on April 29, 2024, which was 143 days after notice of the default was served on Byron and therefore within the 180-day window permitted by subdivision (a) of Section 473.5.

 

Byron submits with the Motion a declaration, dated April 7, 2024, that states that his lack of actual notice was not the result of service evasion. (Motion p. 43.) He “contend[s] that [his] failure to respond was the result of inadvertence, surprise, mistake, or excusable neglect or lack of notice.” (Ibid.) And, while the declaration recites that Byron thereby “declare[s] under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing [declaration] is true and correct,” the signature block below this statement is unsigned . (Id. at p. 50.) To constitute an affidavit under California law, the declaration must be executed with the declarant’s signature. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2015.5.) This declaration attached to the Motion is therefore not an affidavit, and the Motion therefore does not include an affidavit “showing under oath that the party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect” as required by subdivision (b) of Section 473.5. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5, subd. (b).)

 

The Motion to set aside/vacate the default therefore cannot be granted under Section 473.5.

 

3.      Section 473(b)

 

Byron timely brings the Motion under Section 473(b). Six months after default was entered against Byron on December 8, 2023 will be June 8, 2024, and the Motion has been brought before that date. We find the time in which Byron moved for relief after discovering the default in late November 2023 to be reasonable.

 

Byron includes with the Motion substantial portions of a proposed responsive pleading to the Complaint. (Motion pp. 25–42.) Additionally, Byron already attempted to file an answer to the Complaint on December 21, 2024. Although because such answer had been filed after default had already been entered against him in this action, the answer as filed is “unauthorized and void” (Humphrey v. Bewley, supra, 69 Cal.App.5th 571 at 580), it may nonetheless serve as a proposed responsive pleading that Byron would re-serve and -file were his Motion to set aside/vacate the default to be granted. We find that one or either of these two proposed responsive pleadings Byron files with the Motion to satisfy this requirement of Section 473(b).

 

For relief under Section 473(b), Byron is not required to and he does not include with the Motion any affidavit or declaration of merits. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473, subd. (b).)

 

However, for relief under Section 473(b), Byron must show that the entry of default against him resulted from his “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473, subd. (b).) Although the declaration Byron includes with the Motion is unsigned, the Motion itself is signed and portions of the declaration appear above this signature. (Motion p. 48.) Byron declares that although the Complaint was served on an employee of his company, Ivy Green, at Ivy Green’s office in mid-September 2023 and subsequently mailed to Ivy Green’s office, he nonetheless did not learn about the Complaint until he received the served documents “in the end of November 2023.” (Id. at p. 43.) He contends that this service at the “wrong address” constitutes his “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Ibid.) Earlier in the Motion, he also explains the service at Ivy Green’s office “prevents me from being aware of the lawsuit and by not appearing in court I lose by default” because Ivy Green’s “employees delay to notify me about the paperwork because everything for the construction company is receiving in the [office at a different address].” (Id. at p. 9.) Thus, Byron explains that disorganization in paperwork and mail handling by the employees who had accepted substitute service for him at Ivy Green’s office prevented him from being notified about the Complaint until over a month after service had been deemed effected. Without actual notice of the Complaint, he was unable to appear in the instant action or prevent a default from being filed. We note that the default against Byron was entered in early December 2023, which is less than a month after late November 2023. Thus, because his actual notice of the Complaint was substantially accidentally delayed, Byron was effectively given less than a month to respond to the Complaint, and the entry of default against him cut short the time he would have had to respond had he been immediately notified of the service of the Complaint. Based on the foregoing, this court finds that Byron’s failure to answer the Complaint before default was entered against him resulted from  “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS Byron’s motion to set aside/vacate default.

 

  1. Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions

 

In each of her oppositions to the Motions, Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,950.00 for a total of $3,900.00. (Oppositions p. 7.) She brings her requests under California Code of Civil Procedure § 128.5, subdivision (b)(1). (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff contends that the Motions were frivolously brought and that Moving Parties “knowingly and intentionally submitted materially false factual information in [their] declaration[s].” (Oppositions p. 7.) She argues that “the [Moving Parties’] representation that he was not personally served with the summons and complaint is blatantly false” and that this “false representation” is the “entire basis of the [M]otion[s].” (Ibid.)

 

Each of the Motions repeats that the Moving Parties were never personally served with the Complaint, which we understand to mean that Plaintiff effected service of the Complaint on Moving Parties by substituted service rather than personal service, which we find above is true as to only Byron, and not that service was never effected, which we find above is true as to only Javier. We find that a reasonable dispute existed as to whether substituted service was properly effected on Moving Parties, and Moving Parties’ contentions that they were not served by personal service are true. Because we grant both Motions in part, we do not find them to have been frivolously brought. We therefore fail to find a proper basis on which Plaintiff may be awarded sanctions in relation to the Motions.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions.

I. BACKGROUND

On August 25, 2019, plaintiff Pejman Kohansedgh (“Plaintiff”), the landlord of a certain residential real property (the “Property”) entered a lease (the “Lease”) and a guaranty (the “Guaranty”) to the Lease with defendants Gonzalo Lizama (“Gonzalo”), Yasmina Monjaraz (“Yasmina”), Santiago Monjaraz (“Santiago”), and Yesenia Cayetano (“Yesenia” and, together with Santiago, “Moving Parties” and Moving Parties, together with Gonzalo and Yasmina, “Defendants”). (Opposition p. 2.) In June 2023, Defendants ceased rent payments but remained in possession of the Property. (Ibid.) 

 

On December 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed an unlawful detainer complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendants. On February 9, 2024, demurrers brought by Santiago and Yesenia were sustained with leave to amend. On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint (the “FAC”). On April 18, 2024, this court overruled Defendants’ demurrers to the FAC.

 

On May 6, 2024, Moving Parties filed the instant motion for summary judgment (the “Motion”), which Plaintiff opposed (by filing the “Opposition”) on May 24, 2024.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings

 

A motion for summary judgment “may be made at any time after 60 days have elapsed since the general appearance in the action or proceeding of each party against whom the motion is directed or at any earlier time after the general appearance that the court, with or without notice and upon good cause shown, may direct.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿437c, subd. (a).) “Notice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing” with additional days depending on how the notice is served. (Ibid.) “The motion shall be heard no later than 30 days before the date of trial, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise.” (Ibid.) “An opposition to the motion shall be served and filed not less than 14 days preceding the noticed or continued date of hearing, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise” and “[a] reply to the opposition shall be served and filed by the moving party not less than five days preceding the noticed or continued date of hearing, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise. (Id. at subd. (b).)¿Supporting documents to filings should be filed by the same deadlines. (See, e.g., id. at subd. (b)(2)–(b)(3).) 

¿ 

“A trial court has broad¿discretion¿under¿rule 3.1300(d) of the California Rules of Court¿to refuse to consider papers served and filed beyond the deadline without a prior court order finding good cause for late submission.” (Bozzi v. Nordstrom, Inc. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 755, 765.) Similarly, courts have wide latitude to grant continuances.¿ 

 

The Opposition, having been filed only five days before the Motion is set to be heard, is untimely.

 

  1. The Separate Statement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (b)(1) requires that a motion for summary judgment “be supported by affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice shall or may be taken. The supporting papers shall include a separate statement setting forth plainly and concisely all material facts that the moving party contends are undisputed. Each of the material facts stated shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿437c, subd. (b)(1).)

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (b)(3) requires that opposition papers to a motion for summary judgment “include a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating¿if¿the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed. The statement also shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts¿the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. Failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may constitute a sufficient ground, in the court's discretion, for granting the motion.” (§¿437c, subd. (b)(3).)

 

The separate statement “provides a convenient and expeditious vehicle permitting the trial court to hone in on the truly disputed facts.” (Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 251–252.) In United Community Church v. Garcin (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 327, 337, the court set forth the “Golden Rule” as follows: “all material facts must be set forth in the separate statement… if it is not set forth in the separate statement, it does not exist.”

 

Neither Moving Parties nor Plaintiff submit with the Motion or the Opposition, respectively, a separate statement setting out the undisputed and disputed material facts of the action. While we decline to either grant or deny the Motion on these grounds, we remind Moving Parties and Plaintiff of the importance of complying with this rule.

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A party may move for summary judgment in¿an¿action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿437c, subd. (a)(1).) The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure §¿437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿“A triable issue of material fact exists where ‘the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.’” (California Medical Assn. v. Aetna Health of California Inc. (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 660, 665, quoting Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra at 850.)  

¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the moving party must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) A cause of action “cannot be established” if the undisputed facts presented by defendant prove the contrary of plaintiff's allegations¿as a matter of law. (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1597.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the moving party has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿ 

 

“Unlawful¿detainer¿is a remedy available to a landlord against a tenant who breaches a lease and is intended and designed to provide an expeditious remedy for the recovery of¿possession¿of real property.” (Castaic Studios, LLC v. Wonderland Studios LLC (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 209, 215.) “The remedy is available in only three situations: to a lessor against a lessee for unlawfully holding over or for breach of a lease; to an owner against an employee, agent, or licensee whose relationship has terminated; and to a purchaser at an execution sale, a sale by foreclosure, or a sale under a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust against the former owner and possessor.” (Greene v. Municipal Court (1975) 51 Cal.App.3d 446, 450.) “[U]nlawful¿detainer¿actions are limited to right of¿possession¿and not other claims, even if related to the property.” (Castaic Studios, LLC v. Wonderland Studios LLC, supra at 216, describing Larson v. City and County of San¿Francisco¿(2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1263, 1297.) 

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

Moving Parties contend that they are not tenants in possession of the Property but are only guarantors of the Lease and parties to the Guaranty. (Motion pp. 3–4.) As such, they argue, the instant unlawful detainer action cannot be maintained against them since the plaintiff’s right to possession of real property is an essential element to an unlawful detainer action. (Id. at pp. 4–6.) In support of the Motion, Moving Parties submit a sworn and signed declaration (the “Santiago Declaration”) by Santiago stating that Moving Parties “do not have any claim of right to possession at this property. We have never paid rent to the Plaintiff, we are not in possession of the property, and we were certainly not in possession of the property when this action was filed.” (Santiago Decl. ¶ 5.)

 

Moving Parties made a similar argument in their demurrer to the FAC, which this court overruled on April 18, 2024. In ruling on Moving Parties’ demurrer, this court considered submitted copies of the Lease and the Guaranty and held, in relevant part,

 

In this case, it is an overstatement to say that there is a conflict between a complaint’s allegations and exhibits that is direct and indisputable. Instead, the court finds that the exhibit is ambiguous and can be construed in the manner stated in the complaint. For example, while it is true that on page one of the agreement, only Gonzalo Lima and Yasmina Monjaraz (misspelled as “Monjarz”) are listed on the “tenant” line, there are at least four other places in the Lease Agreement and the “Guarantor Form as Attachment to Lease” where it could be argued that Santiago Monjarez and Yesinia Cayetano agreed as tenants. Specifically, on line two of the “Guarantor Form as Attachment to Lease”, the names Santiago Monjarez and Yesinia Cayetano appear on the “tenant” line. Additionally, on page one of the lease, paragraph 1, subsection B., it states that “the premises are for the sole use as a personal residence by the following named persons only: Gonzalo Lizama, Santiago David Monjarez, Yesinia Cayetano only.” Furthermore, the exhibit suggests that both Santiago Monjarez and Yesinia Cayetano placed their respective initials in the bottom lower right hand corner of each page, for a total of five separate times, next to “Tenant Initials.” Lastly, the exhibit suggests that Santiago Monjarez and Yesinia Cayetano both signed and dated the lease agreement itself, separate and apart from the Guarantor Form as Attachment to the Lease.

 

(04/18/24 Minute Order pp. 4–5.)

 

In support of the Opposition, Plaintiff submits (1) a sworn and signed declaration by an employee of his attorneys’ law firm in this action, in which the declarant states that she has visited the Property multiple times during the pendency of this action and has seen Moving Parties and young children at the Property (Kohansedgh Decl. ¶¶ 2–5); (2) a similar sworn and signed declaration by another employee of his attorneys’ law firm in this action, in which the declarant also states that she has visited the Property and has seen Moving Parties and young children at the Property (Avatisyan Decl. ¶¶ 2–6); (3) a copy of the Lease (Opposition Exh. 1); (4) redacted copies of Moving Parties’ driver’s licenses (Id. at Exh. 2); and (5) a copy of a set of special interrogatories propounded on Defendants by Plaintiff and Santiago’s responses to those special demurrers (Id. at Exhs. 3–4). At least some of the visits to the Property made on behalf of Plaintiff were video recorded. (Kohansedgh Decl. ¶¶ 2–5; Avatisyan Decl. ¶¶ 2–6.)

 

Plaintiff contends that the copies of Moving Parties’ driver’s licenses were provided to Plaintiff by Moving Parties at the time they executed the Lease. (Opposition p. 2.) Plaintiff points out that in the Santiago Declaration Santiago declares that Moving Parties have three children. (Opposition p. 3; Santiago Decl. ¶ 2.) Plaintiff does not certify that the document copies submitted as exhibits to the Opposition are true and correct. However, the same copy of the Lease is attached to the FAC. (FAC Exh. 1.) The FAC describes the attached copy of the Lease as “[a] copy of the written agreement, including any addenda or attachments that form the basis of this [C]omplaint,” and Plaintiff signed the Complaint under a declaration “under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.” (FAC pp. 2, 4.) We are thus satisfied that Plaintiff has verified that the copy of the Lease we consider for purposes of ruling on the Motion is a true and correct copy of the Lease.

 

By submitting the Santiago Declaration, in which Santiago denies, under penalty of perjury, that Moving Parties live at the Property or have any claim to possession of the Property, Moving Parties satisfy their initial burden of negating an essential element of Plaintiff’s unlawful detainer action. The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the possession issue. By submitting a copy of the Lease, Plaintiff meets this burden. We make the same findings now that we made when confronted with the same question about whether Mowing Parties may be tenants under the Lease that we answered in ruling on their demurrer to the FAC. The executed copy of the Lease is ambiguous as to whether Moving Parties execute it as tenants residing at the Property or strictly as guarantors without a claim to possession of the Property, and it therefore give rise to a triable issue of material fact.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion.
