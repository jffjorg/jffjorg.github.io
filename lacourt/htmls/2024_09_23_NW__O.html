I.        INTRODUCTION

          This case arises out of Plaintiff Michael Malone’s (“Plaintiff” or “Cross-Defendant”) previous employment as the Chief Financial Officer (“CFO”) of Defendant SRAX, Inc., a California corporation (“Defendant” or “Cross-Complainant”). On August 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant, alleging breach of contract, multiple violations of Labor Code § 200, et seq., and violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq. [1] Plaintiff alleges he

 was terminated without cause on or about April 2023, and that, to date, Plaintiff has not been paid his remaining owed salary, benefits, or personally incurred business expenses. (See Complaint, ¶¶ 7-17.)         

          Defendant filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff on May 6, 2024, alleging Plaintiff failed to perform his duties as CFO and breached his confidentiality agreement, among other claims. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 21-22, 26-32.)

          Cross-Defendant now seeks to strike Cross-Complainant’s Cross-Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16.

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On August 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Complaint alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Violation of Labor Code §§ 201 and 202; (3) Violation of Labor Code §§ 2800 and 2802; (4) Violation of Labor Code § 204; (5) Violation of Labor Code § 226(A), and (6) Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq.

On October 9, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer and Cross-Complaint. The Court rejected the filing of the Cross-Complaint due to Defendant’s incorrect completion of the e-filing form. Defendant filed the Cross-Complaint correctly on October 25, 2023.

On November 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Answer to the Cross-Complaint but withdrew his Answer on November 13, 2023.

On May 1, 2024, this Court granted Defendant leave to file a Cross-Complaint.  

On May 6, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed a Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendant, alleging (1) Breach of Contract (Proprietary Information, Invention Assignment, and Confidentiality Agreement); (2) Breach of Contract (Employment Agreement); (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (4) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets.

On August 13, 2024, Cross-Defendant filed the instant Special Motion to Strike Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16 (“Motion”).[2]

On September 10, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed its Opposition.

On September 16, 2024, Cross-Defendant filed his Reply.   

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

“A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 (b)(1); see also Church of Scientology v. Wollersheim (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 628, 644-645 (observing the legislature enacted Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 to protect citizens exercising constitutional rights of petition and free speech from meritless lawsuits) (overruled in other part by Equilon Enters. v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 68, fn. 5.)

The trial court applies a burden shifting analysis when considering a special motion to strike brought under section 425.16. (See Soukun v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 278.) First, the defense must carry its burden of making a threshold showing that a challenged cause of action arises from “any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the [federal or state constitution] with a public issue.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 (b)(1); see also Soukun, 39 Cal.4th at 278.) Acts which are protected under section 425.16 include “any written or oral statement or writing made before a … judicial proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 (e)(1); see also Briggs v. Eden Council for Hope & Opportunity (1999) 19 Cal.4th 1106, 1116 (anti-SLAPP statute protects all petition-related activity before a governmental body, whether the statements involve a public issue or not). Second, only after the defendant has met its initial burden does the burden shift to the plaintiff to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the merits of the pleading or on part of the pleading at issue. (See Equilon, 29 Cal.4th at 67.)   

A plaintiff opposing a special motion to strike has the burden to “state [] and substantiate [] a legally sufficient claim.” (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 88 & 93.) “Put another way, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.” (Navellier, 20 Cal.4th at pp. 88–89 (cleaned up).)  To that end, the plaintiff must present competent evidence, “that would be admissible at trial.” (HMS Capital, Inc. v. Lawyers Title Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 204, 212.) “[D]eclarations may not be based upon ‘information and belief’” and documents submitted without the proper foundation will not be considered. (Id. (citation omitted).)  The complaint, even if verified, is insufficient to carry the plaintiff's shifted burden. (See Roberts v. Los Angeles County Bar Assn. (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 604, 614; see Karnazes v. Ares (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 344, 354 (stating “pleadings do not constitute evidence”).)

IV.     ANALYSIS

A.             Cross-Defendant Meets Its Burden as to the First Prong.

“A cause of action is subject to a special motion to strike if the defendant shows that the cause of action arises from an act in furtherance of the Defendants’ constitutional right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue and the plaintiff fails to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the claim.” (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 883 (citations omitted).)  

“An ‘act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue’ is defined by statute to include ‘(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.’ … If the defendant shows that the cause of action arises from a statement described in clause (1) or (2) of section 425.16, subdivision (e), the defendant is not required to separately demonstrate that the statement was made in connection with a ‘public issue.’” (Id. (citations omitted).) 

Cross-Defendant seeks to strike Cross-Complainant’s entire Cross-Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e)(4). (See Motion, pg. 1.) Cross-Defendant argues all four of Cross-Complainant’s causes of action concern Cross-Defendant’s “alleged sharing of ‘Proprietary and Confidential Information.’” (Motion, pg. 2.) In an attached declaration, Cross-Defendant does not deny he shared information with others, but asserts such communications were not privileged. (See Declaration of Michael Malone filed August 13, 2024 (“Malone Decl.”), ¶¶ 4-5.) Cross-Defendant declares he only communicated about Cross-Complainant’s acts he believed to be unlawful, including, but not limited to: Cross-Complainant’s misleading disclosures related to revenue and the valuation of subsidiaries; Cross-Complainant’s CEO Chris Miglino’s improper use of Cross-Complainant’s stock as collateral for a loan on a SoFi Stadium suite; and Cross-Complainant’s failure to properly disclose an increase in its security holdings. (See id.)

Cross-Defendant argues Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e)(4) applies to his conduct, the communication of Cross-Complainant’s allegedly unlawful acts, as subsection (e)(4) is substantially broader than the other subsections, encompassing “private activity and communications that concern an issue of public interest.” (Motion, pg. 5 (citations omitted).) Such communications, in turn, concern an issue of public interest because they are specifically prohibited by Government Code § 12964.5, which prohibits an employer, as a condition of employment or continued employment, from requiring an employee to sign a nondisclosure agreement or other document “‘to the extent it has the purpose or effect of denying the employee the right to disclose information about unlawful acts in the workplace.’” (Motion, pg. 5, quoting Gov. Code § 12964.5(a)(1)(B)(i).)

In sum, Cross-Defendant argues “[t]he entire Cross-complaint is grounded on the erroneous premise that SRAX can prevent Malone from disclosing what he believes are unlawful acts. Since Gov’t Code § 12964.5 makes it clear that these are protected activities, the first prong is met.” (Motion, pg. 6.)

In opposition, Cross-Complainant focuses exclusively on Cross-Defendant’s Government Code § 12964.5 argument, stating Cross-Defendant “cannot provide any legal support for his theory that a phrase in a section of the California statutes governing workplace harassment and discrimination somehow governs Malone’s alleged ‘communications’ about his former employer which have nothing to do with harassment or discrimination.” (Opposition, pg. 4.)

In reply, Cross-Defendant engages in a lengthy discussion of statutory interpretation, comparing Government Code § 12964.5 to Labor Code § 1102.5, and finally arriving at his ultimate point—that “[t]he public certainly has an interest in what the legislature has twice specifically declared is ‘public policy’ in this statute, and it is beyond reasonable debate that the public has an interest in allowing the discussion or disclosure of information about unlawful acts in the workplace.” (Reply, pg. 6.)

As a preliminary matter, the Court does not find either Cross-Defendant or Cross-Complainant’s argument on whether the communications here are covered by Government Code § 12964.5 to be particularly compelling or, frankly, all that relevant. Cross-Defendant is asking this Court to determine (1) his communications are protected under Government Code § 12964.5; (2) it is therefore “public policy” to protect such communications; and, as a result, (3) his communications concern an issue of public interest within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e)(4). The Court declines to make such leaps in its section 425.16 analysis.

          Instead, the Court will first analyze whether Cross-Defendant has met his burden in proving all four causes of action in the Cross-Complaint are based on what he argues is protected activity—his communications. The Court finds he has not. While the Court agrees the basis for Cross-Complainant’s first, third, and fourth causes of action are Cross-Defendant’s communications, the basis for the third cause of action is both Cross-Defendant’s communications and his alleged failure to adequately perform his duties as CFO. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 40-46.) Cross-Defendant does not address the latter basis at all, much less whether this basis also constitutes protected activity. (See Motion.) Cross-Defendant bears “the burden of showing that each allegation supporting [Cross-Complainant’s] claim of recovery is one that rests on protected activity…to the extent any acts are unprotected, the claims based on those acts will survive.” (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1012.) The Court will thus only analyze the claims that Cross-Defendant claims are protected—his communications—and will not strike or consider striking Cross-Complainant’s allegations concerning Cross-Defendant’s actions as CFO.

          The Court next addresses whether Cross-Defendant’s communications constitute protected activity. “In making its determination, the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(b)(2).) Cross-Complainant alleges Cross-Defendant: (1) likely disclosed non-public information about Cross-Complainant to an unnamed investor; and (2) Cross-Defendant and/or the unnamed investor disclosed the non-public information to a potential customer of Cross-Complainant. (See Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 28, 30.) Cross-Defendant does not refute these allegations but provides context; the communications were only about things Cross-Defendant believes were “unlawful act[s] in the workplace.” (Malone Decl., ¶ 5.)

          As an initial matter, Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e)(4) has no “public forum” requirement and is substantially broader, encompassing private activity and communications, such as Cross-Defendant’s communications here with an investor and potential customer, so long as they concern an issue of public interest. (See Briggs, 19 Cal.4th at 1116.)

          The heart of this Court’s inquiry, then, is whether such communications concern an issue of public interest. “The inquiry under the catchall provision instead calls for a two-part analysis rooted in the statute's purpose and internal logic. First, we ask what “public issue or … issue of public interest” the speech in question implicates—a question we answer by looking to the content of the speech. (§ 425.16, subd. (e)(4).) Second, we ask what functional relationship exists between the speech and the public conversation about some matter of public interest. It is at the latter stage that context proves useful.” (FilmOn.com Inc. v. DoubleVerify Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 133, 149–150 (“FilmOn”).)

          “FilmOn’s first step asks what issue or issues the challenged activity may reasonably be understood to implicate. On that question, the movant’s beliefs, motivations, or characterizations may be relevant and, if objectively reasonable, will inform the analysis. But they are not themselves dispositive and, if not objectively reasonable, will not carry weight. If a reasonable inference can be drawn that the challenged activity implicates a public issue then the analysis proceeds to FilmOn's second step.” (Geiser v. Kuhns (2022) 13 Cal.5th 1238, 1254.)

          Here, the Court finds Cross-Defendant’s communications implicate an issue of public interest. Cross-Defendant declares they concern the potentially unlawful operation of “a public…financial technology company that unlocks data and insights for publicly traded companies…[allowing companies to]…track their investors' behaviors and trends and use those insights to engage current and potential investors across marketing channels.” (Complaint, ¶ 8.) “[A] publicly traded company with many thousands of investors is of public interest because its successes or failures will affect not only individual investors, but in the case of large companies, potentially market sectors or the markets as a whole.” (Sugarman v. Benett (2021) 73 Cal.App.5th 165, 177 (citations omitted).) The Court therefore finds it is reasonable to infer Cross-Defendant’s communications concerning Cross-Complainant’s failures/unlawful acts implicate an issue of public interest.

          The Court then turns to FilmOn’s second step—what functional relationship exists between the communications and the public conversation about some matter of public interest? “[T]he inquiry of whether a statement contributes to the public debate is one a court can hardly undertake without incorporating considerations of context—including audience, speaker, and purpose.” (FilmOn, 7 Cal.5th at 151–152.) The Court finds the context surrounding Cross-Defendant’s communications furthered the discourse that makes the issue one of public interest—that is, they furthered the discourse surrounding Cross-Complainant’s alleged unlawful acts, which would naturally be of great interest to investors and potential customers alike. The communications were to an investor and potential customer. The purpose, according to Cross-Defendant and in the absence of any submitted evidence by Cross-Complainant, was to communicate about Cross-Complanaint’s unlawful acts. Finally, the result was to cause Cross-Complainant “significant financial and reputational damages.” (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 32.)

          The Court thus finds Cross-Defendant has met his burden and shown Cross-Complainant’s first, second (as discussed above), third, and fourth causes of action arose from “any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(b)(1).)

          The burden thus shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the merits.

B.      Cross-Complainant Hasn’t Demonstrated a Probability of Prevailing on the Merits.

As described above, Cross-Complainant does not submit any additional evidence in opposition or make any additional argument besides its argument concerning Government Code § 12964.5. (See Opposition.) Cross-Complainant’s only argument regarding its probability of prevailing on the merits is as follows: “Because Malone’s alleged communications are not protected by any public policy set forth in Gov't code Section 12964.5, Malone cannot prevail on his anti-SLAPP motion. Whether or not Malone had reasonable cause to believe he was communicating about an unlawful act in the workplace, he was not speaking about any behavior made illegal under the FEHA. With no showing that Malone's communications concern an issue of public interest, he cannot prevail on his anti-SLAPP motion.” (Opposition, pp. 4-5.)

Cross-Complainant thus has not met its burden of demonstrating a probability of prevailing on any of its causes of action.       

C.      Fees Shall Be Determined by Separately Noticed Motion.

Cross-Defendant requests attorney’s fees, which he intends to file as a separate noticed motion. The Court will consider the request at that time.

IV.     CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS IN PART Cross-Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16.

The Court orders Cross-Complainant to file an amended cross-complaint in conformity with this Court’s ruling on or before October 21, 2024.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S ATTORNEY STEPHEN L. COOPER'S MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

 

 

I.        INTRODUCTION

          Stephen L. Cooper (“Cooper”) moves to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff Aaron Smith (“Plaintiff”). This instant motion arises out of Cooper’s claim that there are irreconcilable differences between Plaintiff and Cooper, rendering it challenging to represent Plaintiff effectively pursuant to Model Rule 1.116(b)(7). (See Declaration of Stephen L. Cooper filed August 13, 2024 (“Cooper Decl.”) ¶ 2.)

///

///

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On October 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Complaint alleging one cause of action related to Medical Malpractice.

On August 13, 2024, Cooper filed his motion to be relieved as counsel of record and attached his supporting declaration (Form MC-052) and notice of motion (MC-051). Cooper also attached proofs of service to the moving papers indicating these forms were served on all parties to the action.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure[1] § 284(1) allows for a change or substitution of attorney “[u]pon the consent of both client and attorney, filed with the clerk, or entered upon the minutes.” If both parties do not consent to a substitution of attorney, Code of Civ. Proc. § 284(2) allows for a substitution “[u]pon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”

Under California Rules of Court Rule 3.1362, an attorney seeking to withdraw by motion rather than by consent of the client is required to make that motion using approved Judicial Council forms. (Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1362(a-e).) The motion requires an MC-051 notice of motion form to be filed with the court, accompanied by an MC-052 declaration form stating, “in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1).” (Id. at (c).) A proposed order prepared on form MC-053 must also be lodged with the court with the moving papers. (Id. at (e).)

The motion, notice of motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case. (Id. at (d).) If the notice is served by mail or e-mail, the notice must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts that show that either the service address is current or that “[t]he service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.” (Id.) 

IV.     ANALYSIS

A.             Cooper’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is Denied

          Section “3. Service” of the MC-052 form is incomplete, as Cooper failed to indicate how the motion was served on Plaintiff. Based on the response in section “3. Service,” subsection “b.” the Court assumes Cooper served the Plaintiff via mail at the Plaintiff’s last known address. However, Cooper failed to state how he confirmed Plaintiff’s last known address or that whether he even tried. Simply stating that Plaintiff was served notice by “mail and email” does not indicate that Cooper confirmed the Plaintiff’s last known address within the last 30 days as required. (See Declaration of Stephen L. Cooper filed August 13, 2024 (“Cooper Decl.”) ¶ 2.)

V.       CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court DENIES Cooper’s motion to be relieved as counsel.
