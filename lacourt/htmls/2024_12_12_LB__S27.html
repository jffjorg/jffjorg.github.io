DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21LBCV00329    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: S27

On 7/0824, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiffs and against Defendant.  On 7/16/24, Plaintiffs filed a memorandum of costs.  The sole item in the memorandum is $95,000 in attorneys’ fees.  On 7/31/24, Defendant filed a motion to strike the memorandum, correctly noting that the memorandum of costs permits a party to seek, by way of memorandum, “attorney fees (enter here if contractual or statutory fees are fixed without necessity of a court determination, otherwise a noticed motion is required), which is not the case here. 

 

Plaintiffs oppose the motion, but appear to misunderstand the purpose of the motion.  The purpose of the motion is not to adjudicate the right to recover fees (this will be done on 1/23/25, when Plaintiffs’ motion for attorneys’ fees is heard), but instead to adjudicate the right to recover fees by way of a memorandum, rather than a noticed motion. 

Plaintiffs are not entitled to recover their fees by way of memorandum.  The motion to strike the memorandum is therefore granted.  The ruling is without prejudice to Plaintiffs’ right to recover their fees by way of noticed motion, and the ruling on this motion has no bearing on the ultimate outcome of the 1/23/25 hearing. 

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. If any party does not submit on the tentative, the party should make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on this matter.  


Case Number: 22LBCV00206    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Gregg Kirkpatrick filed this action against Defendants, Paradise Gardens, L.P., Paradise Garden, LLC, James E. Wolf, and Daniel L. Wayne for breach of contract and related claims arising out of the parties’ ground lease, pursuant to which the entity defendants leased a building that Plaintiff owns.  The crux of the complaint is that Defendants failed to maintain the property in the condition required per the terms of the lease, leaving Plaintiff with $20 million in deferred maintenance and repairs that Defendants should have completed while they were leasing the property.   

 

The complaint includes causes of action for breach of contract and negligence against the entity defendants on a direct liability theory and against the individual defendants on an alter ego theory.  It also includes a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations against the individual defendants on a direct liability theory. 

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication by Entity Defendants

  1. Parties’ Positions

Defendants contend they are entitled to judgment as a matter of law because the parties’ 2017 settlement agreement bars all claims asserted against them.  Additionally, they contend the negligence cause of action fails because Defendants did not owe Plaintiff any duty sounding in tort, as opposed to contract.

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  He argues the release does not bar the claims because the claims accrued after the parties signed the release agreement.  He contends the claim for negligence is proper because Defendants’ work on the property caused additional damage to the property.

 

Defendants, in reply, contend Plaintiff’s supporting declarations are inadmissible.  They contend the opposing evidence fails to raise a triable issue to establish the release agreement is not a bar to the claims.  They contend Plaintiff has failed to show any duty running between the parties that sounds in tort.

 

  1. Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

  1. Settlement Agreement

In 2013, the parties litigated disputes relating to their ground lease and property.  Undisputed fact 8.  The litigation included two court cases (NC059205 and NC059655), as well as an arbitration (A210633).  Fact 9.  In 2017, the parties entered into a settlement agreement.  The agreement is attached as Exhibit H to the Wayne Declaration in support of the motion. 

 

The agreement lists four distinct groups of persons and entities that are settling disputes.  Those persons include (a) James E. Wolf, Daniel L. Wayne, and Wolf and Wayne dba Real Property Services; (b) Mark R. Sawusch and related entities; (c) Paradise Gardens, L.P., (d) LB Paradise Gardens, LLC, and (e) Gregg Kirkpatrick and related persons and entities.  The agreement defined Paradise Gardens, L.P. as “the partnership.”  The agreement required the partnership to pay to LB Paradise Gardens, LLC and the Kirkpatrick parties the amount of $525,000.  ¶3 of the settlement agreement includes a very broad release provision, which released claims known or unknown, suspected or unsuspected, disclosed or undisclosed, contingent or vested, matured or unmatured, so long as those claims were or could have been asserted in the first action, the second action, and/or the arbitration.  The only claims exempted from the release are claims for breach of the settlement agreement and claims “arising after the effective date of this settlement agreement.” 

 

Defendants contend all claims asserted in this action could have been asserted in connection with the prior litigation, and did not arise after the effective date of the settlement agreement.  Plaintiff, in opposition to the motion, argues the crux of this action is that Defendants did not surrender the premises in good order and condition at the conclusion of the parties’ lease, which was in 2020, and which necessarily did not occur and could not have occurred prior to 2017.  Additionally, Plaintiff contends new and worse conditions on the property came into existence after 2017, which were not known and could not have been known prior to the parties’ settlement agreement. 

 

The parties’ ground lease contains a provision that requires the lessors to surrender the property in good order and condition at the conclusion of the lease.  Plaintiff’s additional fact 36.  The parties’ lease expired on 12/31/20.  Fact 2.  Plaintiff contends that, pursuant to Avalon Pacific-Santa Ana, L.P. v. HD Supply Repair & Remodel (2011) 192 Cal.Ap.4th 1183, 1204-1205, this condition could not have possibly been satisfied until the lease concluded, and any claim for breach of this provision did not exist until the parties’ lease concluded. 

 

Defendants do not address Avalon in their reply papers, and do not meaningfully articulate how Plaintiff could have asserted a claim for failure to return the property in good order and conclusion of the lease, which occurred in 2020, prior to 2017.  At a minimum, there are triable issues of material fact concerning whether Plaintiff is asserting claims that existed prior to the parties’ 2017 settlement agreement.  The motion for summary judgment is therefore denied.

 

  1. Summary Adjudication

Defendants seek summary adjudication of two issues in connection with their motion.  First, they seek adjudication of the first cause of action on the ground that they were released from liability per the parties’ 2017 settlement agreement.  The alternative request for summary adjudication is denied for the reasons discussed above.

 

They also seek summary adjudication of the second cause of action on the ground that all allegations at issue in the lawsuit arise from obligations under the parties’ ground lease and therefore do not sound in tort.  Plaintiff’s second cause of action is for negligence.  Defendants argue a claim that sounds in contract cannot give rise to a negligence cause of action.  Plaintiff, in opposition, argues that Defendants’ negligent repair and maintenance of certain aspects of Plaintiff’s property caused damage to other elements, such that a claim for negligence is proper under Aas v. Superior Court (2000) 24 Cal.4th 627, 635. 

 

Plaintiff’s complaint is grounded in Defendant’s contractual obligation to return the property to Plaintiff in “good order and repair.”  Plaintiff is conflating construction law with the obligations of a tenant under a lease.  Construction law, which is detailed in Aas, explains that when a contractor undertakes to perform contractual duties in a home or other structure, and does so in a negligent manner that causes damage to other parts of the structure, the contractor exposes him or herself to tort liability.  By way of example, if the contractor is hired to repair the windows, but does so in a way that allows for leaking, which in turn damages the walls and floors, the contractor is not only liable for breach of contract (with resulting damages of fixing the windows), but also in tort, with resulting damages for the cost of repairing all subsequent damage.

 

Plaintiff is arguing that, in this case, Defendant’s failure to return the property in good order and condition caused damage to other parts of the property.  All of this, however, would be part of Defendant’s contractual obligation to return the property in good order and condition.  Plaintiff failed to show how any non-contractual obligation arises as a result of Defendants’ failure, and therefore the summary adjudication motion is granted. 

 

3.     Motion for Summary Judgment (Individual Defendants)

a.     Parties’ Positions

The individual defendants move for summary judgment on the complaint, contending all claims against them fail in light of the parties’ 2017 settlement agreement.  They also contend the first and second causes of action (breach of contract and negligence) fail against them because they are not PG LP’s alter egos.  They contend the negligence cause of action fails because any duties sound in contract and not in tort.  They contend the third cause of action (intentional interference with contractual relations) fails because they are the entity defendant’s agents and cannot be liable for inducing a breach of contract, they are immune from liability due to the manager’s privilege, and they are immune from liability due to the financial privilege. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  He argues the settlement agreement does not bar the claims because the claims arose after 2017.  He argues there are triable issues of material fact concerning whether the individual defendants are PG LP’s alter egos.  He contends the claims for negligence sound in tort because the individual defendants’ waste on the property caused additional damage to the property.  He contends ownership and control of an entity defendant does not relieve a defendant from liability for interference with the entity’s contracts, and any financial privilege is not absolute. 

 

Defendants, in reply, contend Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue of material fact and their motion must be granted.

  1. Alter Ego

Defendants contend the first (breach of contract) and second (negligence) causes of action asserted against them fail because Plaintiff cannot show the individual defendants are PG LP’s alter ego.

 

“In California, two conditions must be met before the alter ego doctrine will be invoked.  First, there must be such a unity of interest and ownership between the corporation and its equitable owner that the separate personalities of the corporation and the shareholder do not in reality exist.  Second, there must be an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.”  Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App 4th 523, 538.

 

Among the factors to be considered in applying the doctrine are commingling of funds and other assets of the two entities, the holding out by one entity that it is liable for the debts of the other, identical equitable ownership in the two entities, use of the same offices and employees, and use of one as a mere shell or conduit for the affairs of the other.  Id., at 538-539.  Other factors include inadequate capitalization, disregard of corporate formalities, lack of segregation of corporate records, and identical directors and officers.  Id., at 539.  No one characteristic governs.  The courts must look at the totality of the circumstances to determine whether the doctrine should be applied.  Alter ego is an extreme remedy which is sparingly used.  Id.

 

Defendants, in support of their motion, provide facts 27-35.  Facts 27-29 describe Plaintiff’s responses to Defendants’ discovery requests relating to alter ego allegations.  Fact 30 details Plaintiff’s alter ego allegations made in his operative complaint.  Specifically, fact 30 states, “Plaintiff alleges that Wolf and Wayne are the ‘only managers and only members’ of PG LLC, that PG LLC is the general partner of PG Partnership, and therefore Wolf and Wayne ‘are the sole beneficial owners of 100% of the PG Partnership, and have treated that entity as a mere extension of themselves, and have withdrawn money from the entity in order to render it insolvent and unable to perform the required maintenance and repair of the Property.’”

 

Fact 31 indicates PG Partnership was capitalized in 1998 when it was created.  Fact 32 explains part of the interest in PG Partnership was sold in 1998.  Fact 33 indicates PG Partnership has an EIN and filed state and federal taxes from 1999 to 2020.  Fact 34 states that PG Partnership has separate assets and ban accounts from the individual defendants. 

 

None of the facts in Defendants’ separate statement purport to establish that the individual defendants have not “withdrawn money from the entity in order to render it insolvent and unable to perform the required maintenance and repair of the Property,” as alleged in the complaint.  Defendants therefore failed to meet their moving burden to show they are not PG LP’s alter ego. 

 

Notably, Defendants, in their points and authorities, page 13, argue Plaintiff cannot show bad faith.  The only fact Defendants cite in support of their argument is fact 36, which states that Plaintiff admittedly includes the individual defendants as a means of enforcing the judgment.  Plaintiff’s decision to indicate the individual defendants as a means of enforcing the judgment does not preclude a showing of bad faith.  Defendants had the moving burden to show lack of bad faith, and they failed to include any facts to make such a showing. 

 

The Court finds Defendants failed to meet their burden to show Plaintiff cannot establish alter ego liability. 

 

  1. Additional Issues

Defendants also seek adjudication of the second (negligence) and/or third (interference) causes of action.  The motion for summary adjudication of the second cause of action is granted for the reasons discussed above in connection with the entity defendants’ motion. 

 

The final issue is whether the individual defendants can be held liable for intentional interference with contractual relations.  They argue they cannot, because (a) as agents, they cannot be held liable for inducing breach of the ground lease, (b) they are immune per the manager’s privilege, and (c) they are immune per financial privilege. 

 

i.              Agent Immunity

Defendants’ first argument is that they are immune from liability under the agent immunity rule.  Both parties agree that the basic rule is that an entity cannot be liable for inducing a breach of its own contract, and an entity’s agents also cannot be liable for inducing a breach of the entity’s contract.  See, for example, Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co. (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, 576. 

 

Defendants, in their moving papers, argue that there is no “individual advantage” exception to the rule, and any such exception has been abrogated.  Plaintiff, in opposition to the motion, argues the exception continues to be legally recognized.  The parties discuss the holding of Mintz v. Blue Cross of California (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1594 in this regard.  In Mintz, the plaintiff sued the administrator of his health insurance plan, alleging the administrator interfered with the contract between him (the insured) and his insurance company.  The trial court sustained the administrator’s demurrer to the intentional interference with contractual relations cause of action, holding the cause of action was barred by the agent immunity rule.  The court of appeals upheld.  Because the ruling is directly on point, the Court will quote it at length.  The Mintz Court held:

Mintz points out that he alleged Blue Cross was “acting for its own financial advantage” when it denied coverage for the RFA,4 and that the “agent's immunity rule” does not apply when the agents are acting “as individuals for their individual advantage.” (See Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at p. 512, fn. 4, 28 Cal.Rptr.2d 475, 869 P.2d 454;  Doctors' Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 49 Cal.3d 39, 47, 260 Cal.Rptr. 183, 775 P.2d 508 (Doctors' Co.) [the rule “does not preclude the subjection of agents to conspiracy liability for conduct which the agents carry out ‘as individuals for their individual advantage’ and not solely on behalf of the principal”];  1–800 Contacts, Inc. v. Steinberg (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 568, 592, 132 Cal.Rptr.2d 789 (1–800 Contacts ) [“[a]n exception to the agent's immunity, for conduct undertaken for personal advantage, is consistent with the immunity rule, because pursuit of a personal interest renders the actor more than merely the agent of another”].) While Mintz's statement is correct, the “individual advantage” exception to the agent's immunity rule is of no help to him.

First, the “agent's immunity rule” has no direct applicability to a claim for interference with contract rights. The rule is simply that “duly acting agents and employees cannot be held liable for conspiring with their own principals....” (Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at p. 512, 28 Cal.Rptr.2d 475, 869 P.2d 454.) While the agent's immunity rule “ ‘derives from the principle that ordinarily corporate agents and employees acting for or on behalf of the corporation cannot be held liable for inducing a breach of the corporation's contract’ ” (Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at p. 512, fn. 4, 28 Cal.Rptr.2d 475, 869 P.2d 454, emphasis added), the rule, on its face, applies only to claims of conspiracy to commit a tort or violate a statute. (See, e.g., Doctors' Co., supra, 49 Cal.3d at pp. 41, 45, 260 Cal.Rptr. 183, 775 P.2d 508 [because noninsurer defendants (insurer's retained attorney and expert witness) were not subject to insurer's statutory duty to attempt to effectuate a settlement, and were acting merely as agents of the insurer and not as individuals for their individual advantage, “ ‘they cannot be held accountable on a theory of conspiracy’ ”].) As the court stated in 1–800 Contacts, “the exception for conduct undertaken in pursuit of a personal interest or advantage applies only to the agent's immunity rule. It does not relax the requirement that to be liable for conspiracy to breach a duty, the defendant must be bound by that duty and capable of breaching it.” (1–800 Contacts, supra, 107 Cal.App.4th at p. 592, 132 Cal.Rptr.2d 789, emphasis added.) Mintz has not alleged that Blue Cross conspired with CalPERS to do anything, and hence neither the agent's immunity rule nor its exception *1606 has any literal application in this case. The only question is whether the representative of a contracting party may be held liable for the substantive tort of interfering with the contract. The cases answer that question in the negative. (See  Shoemaker, supra, 52 Cal.3d at pp. 24–25, 276 Cal.Rptr. 303, 801 P.2d 1054;  PM Group, supra, 154 Cal.App.4th at p. 65, 64 Cal.Rptr.3d 227.)

Second, the conclusion that there is no “financial advantage” exception to the rule that a corporate agent cannot be liable for interfering with its principal's contract makes good sense. Every agent, in one way or another, acts for its own financial advantage when it acts for its principal, because the agent is compensated by its principal, and conduct in furtherance of the principal's interest will necessarily serve the agent's interests as well. A “financial advantage” exception to the sound rule that the contracting party's agent, like the contracting party, cannot be liable for interference with the contract, would entirely swallow up the rule.

Third, even if a “financial advantage” exception were applicable to the rule that an agent cannot be liable for interfering with its principal's contract, the cases discussing the exception to the agent's immunity rule demonstrate that merely receiving monetary compensation for its services to the principal is not enough. As stated in  Berg & Berg Enterprises, LLC v. Sherwood Partners, Inc. (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 802, 834, 32 Cal.Rptr.3d 325 (Berg & Berg ), “[c]ases have interpreted the ‘financial advantage’ exception to the agent's immunity rule to mean a personal advantage or gain that is over and above ordinary professional fees earned as compensation for performance of the agency.” Berg & Berg involved a statutory provision with exceptions that allowed a conspiracy claim against an attorney; the exceptions mirrored those carved out from the agent's immunity rule. The court held the term “in furtherance of the attorney's financial gain” meant that “through the conspiracy, the attorney derived economic advantage **432 over and above monetary compensation received in exchange for professional services actually rendered on behalf of a client.” (Id. at pp. 824, 836, 32 Cal.Rptr.3d 325, emphasis added.) Even allegations of excessive billing for the services rendered by the attorney did not satisfy the financial gain requirement of the statute's exception. (Id. at pp. 835–836, 32 Cal.Rptr.3d 325.)

In short, the agent's immunity rule, with its exceptions, applies to civil conspiracy claims, which this is not. And, even if the “financial advantage” exception could be applied in the context of a claim for interference by an agent with its principal's contract, Mintz's allegations that Blue Cross was “acting for its own financial interests,” and “engaged in this conduct for the purpose of obtaining financial incentives available to it under the Plan for keeping plan costs down,” would be insufficient to state the necessary *1607 economic advantage “over and above” the compensation received in exchange for Blue Cross's services to CalPERS. (Berg & Berg, supra, 131 Cal.App.4th at p. 836, 32 Cal.Rptr.3d 325.)

 

Plaintiff’s cited cases either pre-date Mintz and/or concern conspiracy claims.  Plaintiff does not articulate any argument that would allow his claim for interference to stand against the individual defendants in the absence of conspiracy allegations between the entity defendants and the individual defendants.  The motion for summary adjudication of this cause of action is therefore granted.

Case Number: 23LBCV02211    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Anthony Craig, by and through his GAL, Joseph Craig filed this action against Defendant, Human Touch, LLC for strict products liability and negligence.  Plaintiff is an adult with Down Syndrome.  He alleges he was using Defendant’s massage chair in a foreseeable manner on 11/26/21, at which time he suffered severe injuries.  He alleges the massage chair contained defects that rendered it dangerous, and also that Defendant failed to warn of the defects, such that Defendant is liable in strict products liability.  He alleges Defendant negligently designed, manufactured, distributed, etc., the subject chair, such that it is also liable for negligence.      

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

a.     Summary Adjudication

Defendants seek summary adjudication of three issues.  Defendants, in their separate statement, list 39 facts that they contend entitle them to judgment as a matter of law on each of the three issues.

 

CRC 3.1350(d)(2) makes clear that the separate statement must include only facts pertinent to disposition of the motion.  The three issues subject to the motion for summary adjudication, however, to not rely on the same sets of facts.  The facts that would establish the chair is not defective as a matter of law are different from the facts that would establish failure to warn as a matter of law.   

 

The Court will not pick through the separate statement and determine which facts apply to which issues to be adjudicated.  The motion will therefore either be granted or denied in its entirety, and summary adjudication will not be entertained. 

 

b.     Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

 

c.     Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant, with its moving papers, seeks judicial notice of Plaintiff’s discovery responses.  Plaintiff opposes the RJN, contending his discovery responses are not the proper subject of judicial notice.  Regardless of whether judicial notice is the correct mechanism for presenting the discovery responses to the Court, it is clear that a plaintiff’s discovery responses can be used to meet the moving burden on a summary judgment motion.  See Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 749. 

 

The moving party, however, must authenticate the discovery responses in order to present them, whether via a request for judicial notice or not.  In California, a moving party, such as a defendant in a summary judgment motion, must authenticate documents like the plaintiff's discovery responses to meet its moving burden. Authentication requires presenting evidence sufficient to sustain a finding that the document is what the proponent claims it is (Midland Funding LLC v. Romero, 5 Cal.App.5th Supp. 1 (2016)). This can be done through declarations or other means provided by law (West's Ann.Cal.Evid.Code §1400).

 

Documents obtained in discovery can be used to support or oppose a motion for summary judgment, but they must be presented in admissible form, which includes proper identification and authentication (Serri v. Santa Clara University, 226 Cal.App.4th 830 (2014)). If the opposing party does not admit the genuineness of the document, the proponent must present declarations or other evidence to sustain a finding of authenticity (Serri v. Santa Clara University, 226 Cal.App.4th 830 (2014)), (Hooked Media Group, Inc. v. Apple Inc., 55 Cal.App.5th 323 (2020)).

 

For example, in the case of Serri v. Santa Clara University, the court emphasized that documents must be properly identified and authenticated unless the opposing party admits their genuineness (Serri v. Santa Clara University, 226 Cal.App.4th 830 (2014)). Similarly, in Hooked Media Group, Inc. v. Apple Inc., the court found that documents produced in discovery were sufficiently authenticated by their form and the attorney's statement that they were produced in discovery (Hooked Media Group, Inc. v. Apple Inc., 55 Cal.App.5th 323 (2020)).

 

Defendant did not authenticate Plaintiff’s discovery responses.  Defense Counsel’s declaration authenticated Plaintiff’s GAL’s deposition testimony, but not Plaintiff’s discovery responses.  The Court has therefore not considered the responses in ruling on the motion. 

d.     Evidentiary Objections

Defendant submitted objections with its reply papers.  A ruling on the objections would not alter the analysis on the motion, and therefore the Court declines to rule on the objections.  CCP §437c(q).

 

e.     Moving Burden

Many of the facts in Defendant’s separate statement are supported solely by Plaintiff’s discovery responses.  These facts include facts 6, 11, 21, 22, 37, and 38.  By placing these facts in the separate statement, Defendant concedes their materiality.  Because these facts are not established by competent, admissible evidence, the summary judgment motion is denied. 

 

f.      Conclusion

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is denied.  Its alternative motion for summary adjudication is also denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV02411    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Kirk Cully filed this action against Defendants, Matson Navigation Co., Sargeant Marine, Inc., Point Shipping Corp., Vulcan Carriers, Ltd., Sheridan Transportation, and Atlandic Tankships for Jones Act negligence, unseaworthiness, and maintenance cure and found.  Plaintiff named Moving Defendant Hess Corp. as a doe defendant in the action.

 

Plaintiff sues for exposure to benzene while working on container ships in various locations.

 

2.     Motion to Quash

  1. History of Motion

The Court was originally scheduled to hear this motion on 7/09/24.  At the conclusion of the hearing, the Court continued the hearing to permit Plaintiff to conduct jurisdictional discovery.  The Court will not re-state the entirety of its 7/09/24 ruling by reference in this order, but incorporates the prior ruling by reference.

 

  1. Supplemental Papers

Plaintiff filed supplemental opposition on 11/26/24.  Plaintiff argues (a) in Jones Act cases, the Court has jurisdiction in any state where the defendant is incorporated, licensed to do business, or doing business, (b) the Court has specific jurisdiction over Defendant, and (c) Plaintiff has attempted to conduct jurisdictional discovery but Defendant has objected, and this motion needs to be continued until after the Court rules on Plaintiff’s pending discovery motions.

 

Defendants filed supplemental reply papers on 12/05/24.  Defendants argue (a) a recent decision makes clear that the Court must rule in Defendants’ favor, (b) Plaintiff’s requested discovery is not necessary because it is not premised on the correct jurisdictional analysis, and (c) Plaintiff misstates the law regarding jurisdiction in Jones Act cases. 

 

  1. Jones Act

Plaintiff argues that the Court, in Jones Act cases, has personal jurisdiction in any state where the defendant is incorporated, licensed to do business, or doing business.  He argues that, because Defendants are registered to do business in California, imposition of personal jurisdiction is proper. 

 

Defendants correctly note in reply that this is a misstatement of the cases relied upon.  The cases relied upon are VENUE cases, not personal jurisdiction cases.  Merely being registered to do business in California and appointing an agent for service of process does not automatically subject a corporation to general jurisdiction for disputes unrelated to its activities in California (Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 783 (2016)). The defendant must have substantial, continuous, and systematic contacts with the state to be subject to general jurisdiction (In re Automobile Antitrust Cases I & II (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 100 (2005)). 

 

  1. Hardell v. Vanzyl (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 960

Defendants, in their supplemental reply, contend Hardell makes clear that imposition of personal jurisdiction in this case is not proper.  Defendants correctly note that the Hardell case, at page 973, makes clear that general jurisdiction can only be imposed based on acts that occur between the time of filing of the case and the time of service of the summons and complaint on the defendant.  Notably, not discussed by Defendants is the portion of Hardell holding that the Court can also use a look-back period prior to the filing of the suit for determining whether or not the defendant’s forum state contacts are continuous and systematic; this is not relevant, however, because Defendants established, in their moving papers, that they did not have systematic contacts with California for more than a decade before the suit was filed. 

 

Plaintiff, at Section C of his supplemental opposition brief, blurs the line between specific and general jurisdiction.  Plaintiff contends he can establish jurisdiction by showing Defendant, through a subsidiary, owned and operated natural gas plants in California in the 1990s, and products from those plants were sold in state or transported out of the state on ships like the one Plaintiff was on in 1993.  Plaintiff cites HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1168 to support his position that these types of contacts can give rise to general jurisdiction.  However, per the Hardell analysis, general jurisdiction may have existed in the 1990s, but cannot exist in 2024 based on acts that were occurring in the 1990s. 

 

Defendants contend Hardell also stands for the conclusion that specific jurisdiction can only be imposed based on acts that occurred at the time of the injury, and the acts that give rise to imposition of jurisdiction must be related to the injury itself.  Indeed, this is a basic statement of the law re: specific jurisdiction that was cited in connection with the Court’s original ruling.  Defendants conclude that, because the ship on which Plaintiff was stationed in 1993 did not sail to California, there are necessarily not sufficient contacts with California to support imposition of jurisdiction in California. 

 

Plaintiff’s theory is that Defendants were creating natural gas products in California in the 1990s and directing a subsidiary to ship those products out of California onto various ships, including Plaintiff’s ship, where he was exposed to benzene from the natural gas products.  Plaintiff has been attempting to conduct discovery in this regard, and Defendant has thwarted the discovery.  Plaintiff has discovery motions scheduled for hearing on 1/21/25 and 2/13/25 in this regard. 

 

Neither party cites authority directly on point concerning whether, if Defendants were engaging in this behavior, it would subject them to specific jurisdiction. 

 

The Court wishes to consider the discovery motions in connection with its ruling on this motion.  The hearing on this motion is continued to 1/21/25, to be heard concurrently with the scheduled discovery motion on that date.  Additionally, the hearing on 2/13/25 is rescheduled for 1/21/25, so all issues can be considered together. 

 

The Court asks the parties to focus their briefing on the issue of whether Defendants would be subject to specific jurisdiction if they were shipping natural gas, through their subsidiaries, from California onto ships at sea, including Plaintiff’s ship.

Case Number: 24LBCV001602    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Kareron Theus filed this action against Defendant, Hof’s Hut Restaurants, Inc. for negligence, negligent training, hiring, and retention, IIED, and respondent superior on 8/01/24.  Plaintiff alleges he was dining in Defendant’s restaurant and ordered a fish entree.  The dish was named, in large letters, as a fish entrée, but in smaller letters below a bacon sauce was described.  When the dish arrived, Plaintiff saw the bacon and explained to the server that he has a severe allergy to pork.  The server returned the dish to the kitchen and had a new one made; the server ensured Plaintiff that the new dish would not contain any pork.  The new dish contained the same sauce, but no bacon.  The sauce, however, had been simmered with the bacon, which caused Plaintiff to commence experiencing an allergic reaction. 

 

Plaintiff carries an epi-pen, but the pen was in his car.  He asked the server if the restaurant had an epi-pen, and the server indicated it did.  The server returned four minutes later and said that, unfortunately, the restaurant does not have an epi-pen.  At this point, his friends went to his car to get his, but his reaction had become severe, such that he was vomiting; he went to the emergency room as his symptoms, which included fatigue, slurring of speech, and ultimately loss of consciousness, worsened. 

 

2.     Motion to Strike

Plaintiff includes a prayer for punitive damages in connection with his negligent hiring, training, and retention cause of action, and also with his IIED cause of action.  Defendant moves to strike the prayer for punitive damages and related allegations.

 

As an initial note, Defendant’s moving papers, at pages 7-8, argue IIED is not adequately pled.  Defendant did not file a demurrer to the IIED cause of action.  While doing so would not have been proper, Defendant also did not move to strike the IIED cause of action.  The motion to strike is directed at the prayer for punitive damages and related allegations only.  The IIED cause of action, therefore, will remain in the complaint regardless of this ruling.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff has not pled facts that rise to the level of malice, fraud, or oppression as defined by Civil Code §3294.  Defendant’s server’s conduct, while clearly negligent, does not rise to the level of despicable contemplated by the Code.  The motion to strike is therefore granted.  Because it does not appear additional facts could be pled to support the prayer, leave to amend is denied.    

 

Defendant is ordered to file an answer to the complaint, with the prayer for punitive damages and related allegations deemed stricken, within ten days.

Case Number: 24LBCV00861    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: S27

This Court has, in the past year, seen a dramatic increase in motions to compel further responses and motions to compel PMK depositions in lemon law cases.  Similar issues are presented on all of the motions, and the attorneys for both parties fail and refuse to meet and confer in good faith in light of this Court’s prior rulings in order to resolve issues relating to each case.  The Court’s docket has been inundated with these motions, with discovery motions in lemon law cases often taking up more than five motion hearing slots in a week. 

 

The Court finds Counsel, with the assistance of this Court’s prior rulings, can easily resolve the issues on these discovery motions.  The Court has ruled on discovery motions relating to PMK depositions in the following lemon law cases (among others):

22LBCV00586

23LBCV00024

22LBCV00901

22LBCV00761

22LBCV00456

23LBCV00126

21LBCV00261

 

The Court has read and considered Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Court (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 331-334, wherein the Court of Appeals affirmed the trial court’s award of sanctions against BOTH attorneys on the case, which sanctions were ordered payable to the other party directly.  The Court finds the attorneys in these lemon law cases are acting in a way that does not benefit their clients, constantly incurring attorneys’ fees in connection with discovery disputes when they are already aware of how the Court will rule on the motions. 

 

The Court is continuing the hearing on this motion for one month, to Tuesday, 1/14/25 at 8:30 a.m. in Department S27 to require the parties to meet and confer in good faith in an attempt to resolve the issues posed by way of this motion.  Counsel must review the rulings in the above-listed cases in an attempt to resolve the issues.  Counsel must not use rulings issued by other trial courts to assist them in their meet and confer, as those rulings are not relevant to how this courtroom will rule on the motions.

 

If Counsel are unable to resolve all of their issues prior to the continued hearing on the motion, they must file a joint statement of items that remain in dispute.  The joint statement must list each discovery item in dispute, the most current response to that item, and each party’s statement of why a further response should or should not be ordered.  The joint statement must make arguments relevant only to the item in dispute, not to the other items, so the Court can easily and quickly rule on each item.

 

If the parties resolve their issues, they must take the motions off calendar using the online reservation system.  If they are unable to resolve their issues, they must also file short (no more than five pages) briefs and declarations detailing their efforts to meet and confer and making any argument against imposition of sanctions that they wish to articulate. 

 

The joint statement, briefs and declarations are due at least seven court days prior to the continued hearing date.
