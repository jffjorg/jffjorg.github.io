DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

BACKGROUND

This action arose from a dispute over the regulation by Respondent/Defendant City of Pomona (the “City”) of billboards.  Plaintiffs filed a complaint on February 14, 2019, essentially alleging that the City was helping Real Party-In-Interest Regency Outdoor Advertising, Inc. (“Regency”) flout the law by allowing Regency to operate and maintain billboards in violation of Proposition L, which imposed restrictions on the erection of new billboards in the City.

 

On November 17, 2022, the City filed its operative first amended cross-complaint (the “FACC”) against Regency, alleging: (1) implied equitable indemnity; (2) contribution; (3) declaratory relief; (4) express contractual indemnity; (5) breach of contract; (6) breach of contract; and (7) declaratory relief. 

 

On May 5, 2023, the City filed a Motion for Summary Adjudication as to its third and seventh causes of action for declaratory relief.  In its October 30, 2023 Minute Order, the Court adjudicated the City’s third cause of action as moot since the City cannot be found liable for Plaintiffs’ damages, and granted the City’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to its seventh cause of action. 

 

On March 19, 2024, the City filed a Request for Dismissal, voluntarily dismissing its first, second, third, and fourth causes of action in its FACC against Regency.  As a result, the only presently remaining causes of action under the City’s FACC are the fifth and sixth causes of action for breach of contract. 

 

REGENCY’S MSA

            On May 15, 2024, Regency filed the instant Motion for Summary Adjudication (the “MSA”) as to the City’s fifth cause of action in its FACC.  The City filed its opposition on July 31, 2024.  Regency filed a late reply on August 9, 2024.

 

The Court has considered the moving and opposition papers.  The Court has exercised its discretion in considering the late reply papers.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

             

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

            The City’s Objection Nos. 1, 4 and 5 are SUSTAINED.

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  The Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿¿ 

¿ 

As to each cause of action as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action ... cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2); see also Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿¿ 

¿ 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿¿ 

 

Fifth Cause of Action for Breach of Contract 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must plead: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (D’Arrigo Bros. of California v. United Farmworkers of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 790, 800.) 

 

            In its MSA, Regency does not dispute the existence of the contract at issue (the Development Agreement) nor deny its failure to remove the subject billboards. The City’s performance under the contract is also not an issue.

 

            Here, Regency’s main argument is that the City’s fifth cause of action for breach of contract fails because it is premised on an unenforceable contractual penalty provision.  More specifically, the City’s Fifth Cause of Action alleges that Regency breached the Development Agreement between the parties by failing to pay a fine set forth in Section IV.J thereof.  Section IV.J provides that, to the extent Regency does not remove certain billboards, Regency is required to pay a “fine” of either $500 per day (adjusted to the CPI formula) or the actual revenues that Regency receives from the billboards, whichever is greater.  (Declaration of Brian Kennedy in Support of Regency’s MSA (“Kennedy Decl.”), ¶ 8; Exh. A.)  Regency contends that Section IV.J represents an improper liquidated damages provision.  Therefore, if Regency establishes that the liquidated damages clause is an unenforceable penalty, it negates the essential element of damage.  

 

In support of its contention, Regency submits the declaration of Brian Kennedy (“Kennedy”), Regency’s owner and co-founder, who declared that he was personally involved with the negotiations of the Development Agreement between Regency and the City, and the amendments thereto.  (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 3.)  Kennedy declares that “[t]he fine set forth in Section IV.J of the Development Agreement was not based on a reasonable endeavor by the parties to estimate [sic] the losses the City might suffer from Regency's failure to remove the billboards.  At the time the Development Agreement was executed, the parties did not make any attempt to estimate the actual losses the City might suffer if the billboards were not timely removed.  The fine was not calculated based on any analysis by the parties with respect to the City's anticipated losses if the billboards were not timely removed.”  (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

In opposition, the City counters that Regency’s sole evidence to support its position in the form of Kennedy’s declaration is clearly refuted by the City’s June 24, 1992, Planning Commission meeting, and November 9, 1992, February 8, 1993, and May 24, 1993, City Council meetings.  (RJN, Exhs. 3-6.)  The City contends that before executing the Development Agreement, the City and Regency engaged in extensive discussions and negotiations regarding its terms.  (RJN, Exhs. 1-3.)  For example, the City points out that during such negotiations, Regency was represented by an attorney and had many opportunities to dispute or negotiate the Development Agreement’s various terms. (e.g., RJN, Exh. 3, p. 11.), and that the City provided Regency an opportunity to make revisions to the Development Agreement. (RJN, Exh. 2.) 

 

Liquidated Damages

Civil Code § 1671(b) provides that “a provision in a contract liquidating the damages for the breach of the contract is valid unless the party seeking to invalidate the provision establishes that the provision was unreasonable under the circumstances existing at the time the contract was made.”  Courts evaluate liquidated damages clauses by determining if the clause represents a “reasonable endeavor” to estimate the cost of a breach. (El Centro Mall, LLC v. Payless Shoe Source, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 58, 62-63.)  

 

Here, Regency argues that the liquidated damages provision “was not based on any reasonable endeavor by the parties to estimate the City’s potential losses if the billboards were not timely removed.”  Regency relies on Kennedy’s declaration, in which he testifies that the parties did not “make any attempt to estimate the City’s potential losses if the billboards were not timely removed” or “engage in any analysis of the City’s potential losses in connection setting the fine set forth” in the liquidated damages provision.

 

The City, however, presents evidence refuting Kennedy’s declaration – for example, the City’s June 24, 1992 Planning Commission meeting, and November 9, 1992, February 8, 1993, and May 24, 1993 City Council meetings. (RJN, Exhs. 3-6.)  The Court also notes that the City has not had the opportunity yet to depose Regency’s witnesses, particularly Kennedy, which will likely reveal facts surrounding the negotiations of the Development Agreement, and whether Regency accepted the liquidated damages provision at issue.  The Court also notes that to the extent that the fine is based at least in part on the actual revenues collected from the billboards in question, it has an actual relationship to the potential losses arising from Regency’s actions.

 

There is thus a triable issue on whether the terms were unreasonable under the circumstances existing at the time of the contract was entered into by the parties.  Under these circumstances, the Court cannot conclude as a matter of law that Section IV.J is an unenforceable penalty provision.  Thus, Regency has not satisfied its initial burden to show that no triable issue of material fact exists.

 

Accordingly, Regency’s MSA as to the City’s Fifth Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

THE CITY’S MOTION FOR CONTEMPT

            On May 20, 2024, the City filed the instant Motion for an Order to Show Cause re: Contempt (the “Motion for Contempt”) against Regency.  Regency filed its opposition on August 1, 2024, and the City filed a reply on August 7, 2024.

 

The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

Legal Standard

“Disobedience of any lawful judgment, order, or process of the court” is a contempt of the authority of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1209 (a)(5).)  “When the contempt is not committed in the immediate view and presence of the court … an affidavit shall be presented to the court or judge of the facts constituting the contempt.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1211 (a).)  The judge shall thereupon make a finding re: contempt, and the court may grant an order to show cause.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1212.)  If the court finds that the moving party has, by affidavit, alleged facts sufficiently showing the required bases for contempt jurisdiction, the court signs an order to show cause re: contempt, setting the date and time for a contempt hearing.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1212.)

 

            The trial court must have jurisdiction over an alleged contemnor in order to hold a contempt proceeding, for which it is required that the moving party show by affidavit: (1) an order was made; (2) the alleged contemnor’s knowledge of that order; (3) the alleged contemnor’s ability to render compliance; and (4) the alleged contemnor’s willful disobedience.  Anderson v. Superior Court (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1240, 1245.)  The moving party’s affidavit shall function like a complaint, and facts giving rise to the court’s jurisdiction to charge the alleged contemnor must be “well-pleaded” therein.  (People v. Hadley (1924) 66 Cal.App. 370, 379-380.)

Here, the City moves for an order requiring Regency to show cause why it should not be held in contempt of Court for failing to comply with this Court’s October 30, 2023 Order Re: City’s Motion for Summary Adjudication (“MSA Order”).  The City contends that by refusing to take down the billboards, Regency has willfully violated the terms of the MSA Order, and is therefore guilty of contempt.

 

The Court disagrees with the City’s contention.  The MSA Order granted summary adjudication on the City’s declaratory relief cause of action regarding a provision in the Development Agreement.  The Court, in so doing, declared the rights of the parties pursuant to the billboard removal provision of the Development Agreement.  The Court, did not, however expressly order Regency to remove the billboards.  Thus, while the Court, in the MSA Order, declared the City’s contractual rights under the Development Agreement, it did not order Regency to take any specific action, that is, to remove the billboards.

 

Contempt proceedings are criminal in nature, and the prescribed procedural safeguards must be accorded the alleged contemner.  (City of Culver City v. Superior Court, 38 Cal.2d 535, 541.)  The prime purpose of such proceedings is punishment for disobedience of a valid order directing performance of a specified act.  Liability for such drastic punishment ‘should not rest upon implication or conjecture’ but rather upon an order expressing in ‘clear, specific and unequivocal’ language the act required.  (Ransom v. Superior Ct., 262 Cal. App. 2d 271, 276.)

 

In the absence of specific language in the Court’s MSA Order requiring Regency to take a specific action, the Court, under the circumstances, cannot hold Regency to be in contempt.

 

Accordingly, the City’s Motion for Contempt is DENIED.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Defendant’s objections to the Declaration of Jason Peplinski Ed.D nos. 4 and 5 are SUSTAINED.  All other objections by LAUSD are OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 437c, subdivision (c) requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense.  (CCP § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)  Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)  Courts liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

A motion for summary adjudication may be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action. (CCP § 437 c(f).)

 

THE MOTION

            The Motion is based upon LAUSD’s position that:

 

LAUSD did not have any direct or indirect knowledge of Ayon’s alleged misconduct.  There was no suspicion of misconduct identified from any Beyond the Bell Staff member working with Ayon.  There were no prior complaints or concerns against Ayon.  Specifically, there were no complaints made by the plaintiffs, themselves, or their parents.  Ayon’s background check was cleared before and throughout his employment.  Further the plaintiffs cannot state any facts that hiring or having Ayon as an employee itself was a substantial factor in the plaintiffs’ injuries from Ayon’s abuse because it is not foreseeable that hiring Ayon could lead to the sexual abuse of the plaintiff. (Memorandum in Support of the Motion, p. 10, ll. 14-21.)

 

The District contends that the first notice received by LAUSD of Ayon’s alleged sexual misconduct with children occurred on Thursday, April 4, 2019, when Plaintiff BW ripped his pants in the morning while attending school. (Defendant’s Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) 1.) BW told his grandmother and parents later that day that Ayon had touched his “private area” when supposedly examining the rip in B.W.’s pants. (UMF 3.)  Plaintiff BW’s mother called the principal of the school the following day, and the principal called the Gardena Police Department and followed up with a Suspected Child Abuse Report (“SCAR”). (UMF 5.)  On Friday, April 5, 2019, Ayon’s supervisor was called and Ayon was removed from campus.  (UMF 6.)

 

The Motion relies upon this incident as being the first indication that Ayon was committing sexual abuse on the children in the Beyond the Bell program which he supervised, and that Ayon was immediately removed from the school to assert that it is entitled to summary judgment on all the claims alleged against it.  LAUSD contends that Plaintiffs’ alleged sexual assaults were not foreseeable to LAUSD and that therefore they cannot be held liable to Plaintiffs as a matter of law.  (UMF 1-40, inclusive.) 

 

Plaintiffs assert that the evidence shows that other incidents pre-dating the April 4, 2019 incident placed LAUSD on notice of Ayon’s activities and that therefore neither summary judgment nor summary adjudication should be granted.

 

The Evidence

LAUSD presents evidence in the form of deposition transcript excerpts and records from the personnel file of Ayon to support their contention that it did not receive notice of Ayon’s actions in sexually abusing Plaintiffs until April 5, 2019.  Specifically, the District presents evidence that the minor Plaintiffs testified in their depositions that they did not notify other staff members at the School of Ayon’s actions before the ripped-pants incident on April 4, 2019.

 

Plaintiffs present their own excerpts of the depositions to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether or not LAUSD had notice of these actions and therefore whether it was foreseeable that Ayon would sexually abuse children in the School. Plaintiffs also present the declaration of plaintiff A.C., which states that “[i]n the Fall of my kindergarten year” he informed “Miss Jasmine,” a staff member of the School, of Ayon’s abuse and that this abuse was not acted upon.  A.C.’s declaration states that Miss Jasmine [Jimenez] “told me to be quiet and sit down.  Mr. Danny touched my private area after that.”  (A.C. Decl., ¶2.)

 

It is undisputed that the Fall of AC’s kindergarten year was in 2018 and that the ripped-pants incident occurred in April of 2019.

 

Plaintiffs also present the deposition testimony of Plaintiff A.Z., who testified that “Coach Jasmine” walked in when A.Z. was alone with Ayon while Ayon was abusing A.Z.  It was against LAUSD’s policies for a teacher to be alone with a student.  (Additional Material Disputed Facts 17.)

 

The Authority

The parties agree that LAUSD, as a school district, is in a special relationship with its students.   “[B]ecause of the special relationship, imposing obligations beyond what each person generally owes others under Civil Code section 1714, the duty of care owed by school personnel includes the duty to use reasonable measures to protect students from foreseeable injury at the hands of third parties acting negligently or intentionally."  (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.App.4th 861, 870.) 

 

As the Court held in Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (“Lawndale”) (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 129: “To the extent Doe v. DCFS [(2019) 37 Cal.App.5th, 675] suggests that, in all cases where a defendant has a special relationship with a plaintiff, the defendant has a duty to protect the plaintiff from third party assault or abuse only if the defendant has actual knowledge of the third party’s propensity for assault or abuse, California law does not support such a proposition.” (Id. (emphasis added).) 

 

“Simply put, the school grounds provide a different setting than an adult’s home.  And there are differing public policy concerns relating to the responsibilities of school districts that provide mandatory education as compared to adults who invite children into their home on a voluntary basis.” (M.W. v. Panama Buena Vista Union School Dist. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 508, 524.)

 

As in Lawndale, LAUSD here contends it is not foreseeable that school employees, and specifically Ayon, will sexually abuse students. In Lawndale, although the school district contended that “sexual abuse is so unforeseeable that it is outside the course and scope of everyone’s employment as a matter of law,” the court recognized that: “a school district is not like every employer.  As the court recognized in [Doe v.] United States Youth Soccer [(2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1129], sexual abuse by members ‘of an organization that provide[s] activities exclusively for children’ – like an elementary school district – is reasonably foreseeable, even where the organization ‘had no knowledge that [the employee] had previously sexually or physically abused anyone or had a propensity to do so. [citations omitted]’” (Id. at p. 132.)

 

Once a court determines a defendant owes a duty to a plaintiff, the remaining liability questions, breach as well as factual and legal causation, are usually questions for the jury. (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 228.) “Breach, injury, and causation must be demonstrated on the basis of facts adduced at trial, and a jury’s determination of each must take into account the particular context in which any act or injury occurred.” (Vasilenko v. Grace Family Church (2017) 3 Cal.5th 1077, 1084.)  Nevertheless, “[e]ven if an organization is in a special relationship with the tortfeasor or plaintiff, ‘[t]he court may depart from the general rule of duty… if other policy considerations clearly require an exception.’”  (Doe v. The Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles (“Archbishop”) (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 657, 673.) 

 

The California Supreme Court has explained that once a special relationship has been found, as it has here, the trial court must consult the factors described in Rowland v. Christian (“Rowland”) (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108 to determine whether relevant policy considerations justify limiting that duty.[1]  The Rowland factors are: “the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, the closeness of the connection between the defendant’s conduct and the injury suffered, the moral blame attached to the defendant’s conduct, the policy of preventing future harm, the extent of the burden to the defendant and consequences to the community of imposing a duty to exercise care with resulting liability for breach, and the availability, cost and prevalence of insurance for the risk involved.” (See, Archbishop, supra, at pp. 673-674.)  “In considering [the Rowland factors], we determine, not whether they support an exception to the general duty of reasonable care on the facts of the particular case before us, but whether carving out an entire category of cases from that general duty rule is justified by clear considerations of policy.” (Regents of University of Cal. v. Superior Court (2018) 4 Cal.5th 607, 624.)

 

As in Lawndale, LAUSD in the instant case does not discuss the Rowland factors in an attempt to assert that “carving out an entire category of cases from that general duty rule is justified by clear considerations of policy.” (Lawndale at 130.)  Also as in Lawndale, “[a]s with all defendants moving for summary adjudication or summary judgment, the District had the burden to show [Plaintiffs] could not establish the element of duty because the Rowland factors supported an exception to the District’s general duty of care. (Id. at 130-131.)  As the Court held in Lawndale: “even if the District had raised the issue, the Rowland factors do not weigh in favor of limiting the District’s duty to protect students from sexual abuse by teachers and other school employees as [LAUSD] proposes: to cases where school administrators have actual knowledge of prior sexual misconduct by the teacher or employee and where sexual misconduct is not ‘ambiguous.’” (Id. at p. 131.)

 

The Court here therefore reviews the Rowland factors to determine whether a legal basis exists not to apply the general duty of care for special relationships to LAUSD’s actions here. 

 

1.     Foreseeability

As in Lawndale, LAUSD here argues that it had no duty to prevent injury to Plaintiffs by Ayon because there were no “red flags” in his conduct or his background to indicate that he would sexually assault them.  As the Court held in Lawndale, the issue is not whether it is foreseeable a particular perpetrator’s conduct would injure a plaintiff, but whether a particular plaintiff’s injury was reasonably foreseeable in light of a particular defendant’s conduct.  (Id. at p. 131.)  In other words, the Court must “evaluate more generally whether the category of negligent conduct at issue is sufficiently likely to result in the kind of harm experienced.”  (Id.)  “Thus, the issue is not whether it was reasonably foreseeable [the perpetrator’s] conduct would injure [plaintiff], but whether it is reasonably foreseeable the failure of school administrators to take reasonable measures to prevent sexual abuse will injure students.”  (Id.)   

 

As the court held in Doe v. United States Youth Soccer, supra, at p. 1132, sexual abuse by members “of an organization that provide[s] activities exclusively for children” -- like an elementary school district – “is reasonably foreseeable, even where the organization ‘had no knowledge that [the employee] had previously sexually or physically abused anyone or had a propensity to do so.’” “In light of the disparity between school administrators and minor students in knowledge and control over the school environment, and the trust parents place in schools to protect their children, school administrators who fail to prevent sexual abuse are not absolved of moral responsibility simply because they did not have ‘actual knowledge’ an employee previously engaged in sexual misconduct.”  (Lawndale, at pp. 172-73.) “Safeguarding children from sexual abuse – ‘[o]ne of society’s highest priorities [citations omitted] – weighs strongly in favor of imposing a duty on school districts to take reasonable measures to identify and respond to potential misconduct, even before a district knows a specific employee has previously engaged in sexual misconduct.”  (Id. at 135.)  The Court finds that the issue of foreseeability weighs against limiting liability in this case.

 

2.     Certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury

The second factor, the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury can be assessed from the fact that LAUSD does not contest that Ayon in fact sexually assaulted Plaintiffs.  Plaintiffs here have testified that Ayon physically and emotionally abused them and then threatened them if they revealed the abuse.  Plaintiffs’ claims therefore involve both tangible and intangible harm, such as emotional distress. This factor does not warrant limiting claims, like Plaintiffs’ here, that are based on physical sexual abuse and assault. In addition, where a plaintiff seeks to recover for emotional distress for such abuse, “[c]ourts have recognized that ’[t]he significant emotional trauma caused by childhood sexual abuse is well documented.” (Archbishop, at p. 678.) 

 

The Court finds that the factor of certainty weighs against limiting LAUSD’s liability for the alleged conduct.

 

3.      

4.     Connection with Defendant’s conduct

Here, Defendant LAUSD’s allegedly negligent act was a failure to, among other things, “adequately and properly investigate, hire, train, and supervise their staff to protect their students from harm caused by unfit and dangerous individuals hired as staff.”  (A.C. First Amended Complaint, ¶38.)  As noted by the Court in Lawndale: “[a] school district that fails to reasonably supervise employees and students increases the likelihood that an employee will sexually abuse a student.”  (Lawndale at 133.)  Therefore, the Court holds that the connection between the alleged injury and Defendant LAUSD’s conduct is established.

 

Moreover, Plaintiff A.Z. testified that a school employee, “Coach Jasmine,” saw him alone with Ayon in the auditorium “under the stage”, in violation of School rules.  Regardless of whether the actually saw Ayon abusing A.Z., which A.Z. believes she did, this violation of the rule against a staff member being alone with a student has a clear connection with the injury alleged by Plaintiffs.

 

5.     Moral Blame

School administrators who fail to prevent sexual abuse are not absolved of moral responsibility simply because they did not have ‘actual knowledge’ an employee previously engaged in sexual misconduct. (Lawndale at p. 135.)  The Court finds that moral blame can be attributable to the actions of LAUSD in this case, which weighs against limiting liability in this special relationship.

 

 

6.     Policy of preventing future harm

LAUSD does not and indeed cannot argue that there is not a strong public policy in favor of preventing future harm from sexual abuse of minors.  This factor therefore weighs against limiting liability for LAUSD in this case.

 

7.     Extent of the burden to defendant and consequences to the community of imposing a duty

Here, LAUSD admits that it trained Ayon each year of his employment on child abuse policies. (UMF 21.)  Therefore, there is no reason to create an exception to LAUSD’s duties where it has already recognized the risks and undertaken the burden to provide such training. (Lawndale at p. 137.)

 

8.     Insurance

As in Lawndale, no evidence has been provided by either side in this litigation regarding this factor and it will therefore not be weighed for or against imposing liability on LAUSD.

 

9.     Summary of Rowland Factors

The Rowland factors overwhelmingly weigh against limiting LAUSD’s duty in this case and in favor of allowing this case to proceed.

 

Is there a Material Issue of Fact as to Foreseeability?

The Court finds that there remains a triable issue of material fact as to whether LAUSD had notice before the ripped-pants incident in April 2019 that Ayon was sexually assaulting students.

 

Here, LAUSD’s relies upon evidence from Plaintiffs’ depositions, including the deposition of Plaintiff A.C., in which he denies having told adults at his school about the abuse by Ayon, to support its position that LAUSD could not have foreseen that Ayon would sexually abuse A.C. or any other of the Plaintiffs.  Plaintiffs counter this evidence with other excerpts from A.C.’s deposition in which AC testified that he did tell adult staffers at the School about the abuse and a declaration in which he declares that he told those staffers in the Fall of his kindergarten year (2018) and they took no action until the ripped-pants incident in April of 2019.

 

While LAUSD objects to Plaintiffs’ evidence on the ground that a party cannot present his own deposition testimony in opposition to a motion for summary judgment and that a party’s declaration cannot be used when it contradicts the party’s deposition testimony, this general rule does not apply here.   While the Court was not given the entire transcript of A.C.’s – or indeed any other witness’s -- depositions, from the excerpts provided, the Court has grave doubts about the reliability of the excerpts provided by LAUSD.  It appears, at least in part, that when A.C.  testified that he did speak with staff at the School about his abuse by Ayon before April of 2019, the questioning attorney objected to his own question on the ground that it must have been ambiguous and re-asked the question, sometimes using words that the child Plaintiff may not have understood, until the testimony used by LAUSD was adduced.  (See, e.g., A.C. Depo., 51:19-52-24.)

 

The Court notes that at the time of the alleged abuse of A.C., he was in kindergarten.  His original complaint was filed on July 8, 2019, so at which time Plaintiff A.C. was apparently only  six years old.  His deposition was taken on October 27, 2021, at which time he would have been approximately eight years old.  In no case cited by LAUSD for the proposition that a declaration cannot be used when it contradicts a prior deposition is the deponent who seeks to submit an allegedly contradictory declaration a minor, much less a child as young as eight years old, as A.C. was at the time of his deposition. 

 

Moreover, the rule cited by LAUSD only applies if the subsequent declaration contradicts unequivocal deposition testimony. (Tiffany Builders, LLC v. Delrahim (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 536, 547.) In the instant case, not only is the deponent/plaintiff in question a child, but both sides cite to testimony in his same deposition session that they each claim supports their position – which is far from the type of unequivocal testimony as to which a subsequent declaration cannot be used to contradict it.

 

Specifically, LAUSD cites to Page 53 lines 4-25 of Plaintiff A.C.’s deposition to support their alleged UMF that Plaintiff A.C. never told LAUSD of Ayon’s alleged sexual misconduct while a student. (UMF 23.)  However, Plaintiffs’ citation to page 51 at line 19 to page 52 at lines 1 through 19 contradicts that testimony, stating:

 

Attorney:         You never told Miss Carmen that Mr. Danny did anything improper to you, did you?

A.C.:               Yes, I did.

[colloquy between counsel]

Attorney:         Did you tell Miss Carmen that Mr. Danny did something to you in the bathroom?

A.C.                 I told both of them.  I told Miss Carmen and Miss Jasmine.

 

As noted above, A.C. also provided a declaration stating that he informed Miss Jasmine of Ayon’s abuse in the Fall of 2018 and that she told him to be quiet and sit down, and that Ayon abused him after that time.

 

The testimony of A.Z. also creates a material issue of fact regarding notice to LAUSD before April 2019.   A.Z. testified as follows:

 

Attorney:         Do you believe that any of the other staff at Beyond the Bell should have known what Mr. Danny was doing to you or to anybody else?

A.Z.:                Yeah.

[Objection]

Attorney:         Why do you think the staff should have known?

[Objection]

A.Z.:                Once me and Mr. Danny were in the auditorium, and Ms. Jasmine walked in to use the bathroom, and then he was sitting next to me touching me.  I’m pretty sure she saw or seen that. (A.Z. Depo., p. 159:2 to 160, line 14.) 

 

Therefore, whether or not Ms. Jasmine actually saw the abuse, A.Z. has provided evidence that she saw A.Z. alone with Ayon, which should have put LAUSD on notice of the potential abuse.

 

A.Z.’s testimony is evidence that a staff member of the School actually saw A.Z. alone in the auditorium with Ayon before the ripped-pants incident. A.C.’s declaration also clearly states that he reported Ayon’s conduct to Miss Jasmine in “the Fall of [A.C.’s] kindergarten year” which was also before the ripped-pants incident. 

 

Although the Court agrees that other testimony by A.C. states that he did not report abuse by Ayon until after the ripped-pants incident, his other testimony at his deposition to the contrary and in his declaration, together with his very young age, creates a triable issue of material fact such that neither summary judgment nor summary adjudication[2] is appropriate here.

 

Based on the evidence produced by Plaintiffs, the Court finds that to the extent that foreseeability is an issue, a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether school officials were put on notice by A.C. that Ayon was abusing him or by A.Z.’s being seen alone with Ayon by Miss Jasmine.  As such, neither summary judgment nor summary adjudication is warranted under these circumstances.

 

 

            CONCLUSION        

The Court finds that the Motion fails to meet its burden to establish that there are no triable issues of material fact regarding the claims alleged in the Complaints.  The Court therefore DENIES the Motion.

BACKGROUND

             On May 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative one-page complaint (“Complaint”) against Defendant Nidal A. Barakat (“Defendant”) for Violations of Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance.  On July 31, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

Subsequently, Plaintiff filed motions for leave to file an amended complaint, which the Court either denied or continued to allow proper service on the Defendant.

 

On March 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint, which the Court granted on April 30, 2024.  In its April 30, 2024 Minute Order, the Court indicated that an amended complaint is to be filed within fifteen days.

 

On May 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File Third Amended Complaint (the “Motion”).  Plaintiff now seeks to add Catherine Durickas as a defendant in this action and to add additional causes of action.  The Motion is unopposed.  

 

DISCUSSION

Code of Civil Procedure Section 473 permits the trial court in its discretion to allow amendments to pleadings in the furtherance of justice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) Code of Civil Procedure Section 576 provides that any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) There is a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the pleadings at any stage of the proceeding.  (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.)  An application to amend a pleading is addressed to the trial judge’s sound discretion.  (Id.)  If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.  (Morgan v. Superior Court of Cal. In and For Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)  Where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party, the liberal rule of allowance prevails.  (Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564.)

 

Under California Rules of Court rule 3.1324, a motion for leave to amend a pleading must be accompanied by a declaration that sets forth: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made sooner.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)

 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)

 

            Here, the Motion is accompanied by Plaintiff’s declaration stating that he has “recently discovered evidence indicating that Catherine Durickas, Apartment Manager, played a significant role in the events described” in his complaint.  (Declaration of Tohid Naeem, ¶ 2.)  Plaintiff further states that he seeks to add Catherine Durickas as a defendant to ensure that all responsible properties are held accountable, and that an amendment is necessary for the proper adjudication of his claims.  (Id., ¶ 3-4.)  Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff has complied with Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

 

            The Motion includes a copy of the proposed amended pleading.  Since the proposed amended pleading is entirely different from the original pleading (which merely states “Violations of Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance”), the Court finds that Plaintiff has substantially complied with Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a) even though the Motion does not state which allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted and where, and does not state which allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading and where.  In fact, the Complaint sought to be amended contains no allegations.

 

Finally, the Court finds no indication that present Defendant would be prejudiced by allowing the amendment.  As long as no prejudice to the defendant is shown, the liberal policy regarding amendment prevails and it is an abuse of discretion to refuse the amendment.  (Mesler v. Bragg Mgmt. Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 297.)  The Court notes, however, that trial is set for November 19, 2024 and if the new defendant seeks additional time for discovery, the trial will be continued.

 

RULING

The Motion is GRANTED.  The Third Amended Complaint is to be filed and served within 15 days of this order.

BACKGROUND

             This case arises from a lending relationship between Plaintiffs and Defendant PCH.  On January 31, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a lawsuit against Defendants and on June 26, 2024, they filed their First Amended Complaint (the “FAC”), the currently operative pleading.  The FAC alleges claims for Breach of Contract, Fraudulent Conveyance (actual and constructive and aiding and abetting same), Common Law Fraudulent Transfer (and aiding and abetting same), Larceny, Breach of Fiduciary Duty (and aiding and abetting same), Fraud, Negligent Misrepresentation, Accounting and Declaratory Relief. 

 

            In the FAC, Plaintiffs allege, in pertinent part, that “Pursuant to the Side Letters for the second set of loans, the Second 4E and Mikhaylov Loans, and the binding Restructuring [Letter of Intent (the “LOI”)], PCH assigned the PDG Notes [three promissory notes in a total amount of approximately $10,621,254 (the “Notes”) between OBM and PDG, Carpenteria and Rincon) to Plaintiffs and the other lenders.”  (FAC, ¶136.)

 

The Memorandum refers to an alleged assignment to Plaintiffs and “other [unidentified] lenders,” who are presumably lenders separate and distinct from Plaintiffs.  The parties agree that the Notes are the only substantial asset owned by OBM and that OBM is a wholly owned subsidiary of Defendant PCH.  An interpleader action regarding the Notes is currently pending in the Santa Barbara County Superior Court as Pacific Dutch Group, LLC, et al., v. OBM PDG, LLC Case No. 24CV0182 (the “Interpleader Action”).

 

On June 5, 2024, Judge Stephen I. Goorvich of this Court issued writs of attachment on behalf of Plaintiffs and against PCH “for any property of a defendant who is not a natural person for which a method of levy is provided.”  Writs of attachment to non-natural persons do not apply to shares of stock.  (Code of Civil Procedure, Sections 488.300, et seq.)

 

            On July 19, 2024, Plaintiffs presented to this Court an Ex Parte Application for a Temporary Restraining Order (“TRO”) to enjoin Defendants from proceeding with an allegedly fraudulent transfer consisting of a proposed restructuring transaction (the “Farfalla Transaction”) for the debt of PCH.  The details on the Farfalla Transaction are set out in a memorandum sent to “Certain Creditors of Pacific Consolidated Holdings Group, Inc.” dated July 11, 2024 (the “Memorandum”).  In the Memorandum, PCH represented that while it “presently does not believe it has any material asset value at this time,” the one asset it has is its 100% ownership interest in OBM, and that OBM has an interest as the holder of the Notes from the Dutch Group.  The Memorandum states that while OBM “historically held interests in” the Dutch Group [Declaration of Omar Mangalji (“Mangalji Decl.”), ¶ 3], that ownership interest was the subject of a “comprehensive buyout” by the Dutch Group. (Mangalji Decl., ¶7.)

 

In connection with a settlement of issues between OBM and the Dutch Group, the Dutch Group and its related entities CPL and Rincon each gave a promissory note to OBM. The Notes were all executed as of January 19, 2022 and were in the original amounts of $19,128,071.96 (the Dutch Group), $1,382,806.50 (CPL) and $919,910 (Rincon), for a total amount due to OBM of $21,430.788.46.  For the purposes of this order, the three promissory notes will be referred to as the “Notes” and the Dutch Group, CPL and Rincon will be referred to as the “Dutch Group.”

 

The Memorandum proposed to creditors, including Plaintiffs, that they exchange their position as creditors of PCH for member interests in a new entity, Farfalla Capital Investments, LLC (“Farfalla”), in return for which PCH would enter into a Secured Promissory Note in favor of Farfalla (the “Farfalla Note”) in a principal amount not to exceed the approximate value of the Notes, which would be secured by a first-priority, perfected lien in PCH’s entire ownership interest in OBM, the holder of the Notes.  The Memorandum is silent as to whether or when payments would be made by PCH on the Farfalla Note.

 

The Memorandum represents that Farfalla “will” receive a first-priority security interest in “PCH’s entire ownership interest in OBM,” but not a first-priority, perfected lien in the Notes themselves.  Farfalla therefore does not receive the Notes themselves but only receives a security interest in the company that owns the Notes.   Meanwhile, the Notes are the subject of an interpleader action in the Santa Barbara Court which Dept. 1 of this Court will be asked tomorrow to transfer to this Department.    

 

No representation is made that the Notes are not in default, that PCH will make payments on a set schedule, or at all, that regular payments, or any payments, will be made by the Dutch Group under the Notes or that all, or in fact any payments made under the Notes will in turn be paid to Farfalla.  Under the terms of the Farfalla Transaction, however, PCH would effectively retain no assets, since its one asset is pledged to Farfalla.  The fact that if Plaintiffs did not agree to enter into the Farfalla Transaction they would be able to pursue their creditor’s claim against PCH would therefore be meaningless.

 

The Court is not ruling whether or not Plaintiffs have a security or an assignment interest in the Notes themselves.  Plaintiffs, however, have alleged contract claims which Judge Goorvich has held are likely to prevail.  Plaintiffs have also alleged claims in this lawsuit for fraudulent transfers which are sufficiently generally pled to cover the Farfalla Transaction.  The Farfalla Transaction would result in the transfer of what PCH itself describes as the “one exception” to its belief that it has no material asset value.

 

As the Memorandum states: “[i]f OPBM PDG fails to meet its obligations to [Farfalla] under the Secured Promissory Note, [Farfalla], in addition to the traditional remedies granted a secured lender, will have the right to foreclose on and take possession of PCH’s entire ownership interest in OBM PMG and its assets as a going concern.”  OBM’s assets consist of the Notes.  Without the assets of OBM, i.e., the Notes, PCH has no “material asset value.” 

 

In return, the members of Farfalla would be entitled to “distributions from Farfalla from funds PCH pays to Farfalla on the Farfalla Note,” but only at the Manager’s discretion.  Even those potential payments are not guaranteed or even set by PCH in any way.   While the original amount of the Notes was $21.4 million in January 2022 and there were stipulated monthly payments from the Dutch Group to OBM which presumably reduced the value of the Notes to approximately $10,621,254 over a period of two years (a reduction in principal value of approximately $10,778,746)[1], only “some of [those payments] were occasionally remitted by OBM PDG to PCH.”  (Mangalji Decl., ¶ 8.) 

 

Moreover, under the Memorandum, not only is there no requirement that all payments received by PCH on the Notes will be paid to Farfalla, but in fact “THERE IS NO GUARANTEE THAT YOU WILL EVER RECEIVE ANY DISTRIBUTION FROM [FARFALLA] OR ANY PAYMENTS WITH RESPECT TO THE PCH OBLIGATIONS ASSIGNED PURSUANT TO THE PROPOSED RESTRUCTURE.”  The interest rate on the Notes is 0%.

 

The Memorandum set a deadline of July 19, 2024, close of business, for creditors to enter into a non-binding agreement to become members of Farfalla or to retain their status as creditors of PCH after the creation of Farfalla.  PCH has represented to the Court that – even without Plaintiffs’ participation – the “significant participation” required to effectuate the Farfalla Transaction has been met, such that if the PI is not issued, it will go forward.  If the Farfalla Transaction goes forward, Plaintiffs will have no assets from which to satisfy their

 

Under the Farfalla Transaction as contemplated, Plaintiffs therefore have the option either to: 1) accept the offer of membership in Farfalla and lose their current claims against Plaintiffs, with some vague possibility of distributions from Farfalla if PCH pays Farfalla on the Farfalla Note; or 2) reject or otherwise not respond to the offer and retain their claims as against PCH, which would then become an entity with no assets. 

 

After oral argument on the Ex Parte Application, the Court expressed a tentative decision to grant the TRO in order to maintain the status quo pending full briefing and argument on a Preliminary Injunction (“PI”).  In light of this tentative decision, the parties instead stipulated to the issuance of an Order to Show Cause (“OSC”) for this date why a PI should not be ordered: (1) restraining and enjoining Defendants PCH and OBM from proceeding with the Frafala Transaction or an Assignment for Benefit of Creditors (“ABC”); and (2) restraining and enjoining OBM and PDG and PCH from taking any other actions to transfer PCH’s interest in OBM, and PCH and OBM’s claimed interests in the Notes throughout the pendency of this action.

 

DISCUSSION

Initially, the Court finds that the potential effect of the Farfalla Transaction is to create an effective lien on the Notes as part of the first security interest conveyed in OCM, which owns the Notes, while at the same time allowing PCH to continue to enjoy the benefits of potential payments to be made on the Notes (albeit subject to the possibility of making payments on the Farfalla Note).  The Farfalla Transaction would therefore effect a transfer of the Notes within the meaning of   CC Section 3439.01(i).

 

“In deciding whether to issue a preliminary injunction, a court must weigh two ‘interrelated’ factors: (1) the likelihood that the moving party will ultimately prevail on the merits and (2) the relative interim harm to the parties from issuance or nonissuance of the injunction.  . . . .  The trial court's determination must be guided by a ‘mix’ of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff’s showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support an injunction.” (Butt v. State of California (1992) 4 Cal.4th 668, 677-78 (citations omitted).) 

 

The Court therefore considers the above two factors, as follows:

 

Likelihood of Prevailing

Judge Goorvich has held that Plaintiffs have a substantial likelihood of prevailing on their breach of contract claims, at least, for the amount of their debt, which is in excess of $10 million. (June 5, 2024 Order granting Applications for Writs of Attachment at pp. 8-10.)  The Court further finds that the allegations of the Sixth Cause of Action of the FAC sufficiently allege a basis for proceeding on a fraudulent conveyance claim regarding the Farfalla Transaction under Civil Code (“CC”) Section 3439.04(a)(1).

 

“A fraudulent conveyance is a transfer from the debtor of property to a third person undertaken with the intent to prevent a creditor from reaching that interest to satisfy its claim.” (Nautilus, Inc. v. Yang (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 33, 39; CC, Section 3439.04(a).)  A transfer is fraudulent “if the debtor made the transfer (1) with actual intent to hinder, delay, or defraud any creditor, or (2) without receiving reasonably equivalent value in return, and…intended to, or reasonably believed or reasonably should have received, that he or she would incur debts beyond his or her ability to pay as they became due.” (Kirkeby v. Superior Court (2004) 33 Cal.App.4th 642, 648.)

 

In addition to making fraudulent transfers voidable, the Uniform Fraudulent Transfers Act (“UFTA”) also permits a plaintiff to seek “[a]n injunction against further disposition by the debtor or a transferee, or both, of the asset transferred or other property of the transferee.” (CC Section 3439.07(a)(3)(A).)  Whether a conveyance is made with fraudulent intent is a question of fact, and proof often consists of inferences from the circumstances surrounding the transfer. (Filip v. Bucurenciu (“Filip”) (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 825, 835.)  Civil Code Section 3439.04 sets forth the factors that determine actual intent. These factors are: (1) whether the transfer or obligation was to an insider; (2) whether the debtor retained possession or control of the property; (3) whether the transfer or obligation was disclosed or concealed; (4) whether before the transfer was made or obligation was incurred, the debtor had been sued or threatened with suit; (5) whether the transfer was subject of substantially all the debtor’s assets; (6) whether the debtor absconded; (7) whether the debtor removed or concealed assets; (8) whether the value of the consideration received by the debtor was reasonably equivalent to the value of the asset transferred or the amount of the obligation; (9) whether the debtor was insolvent or became insolvent shortly after a substantial debt was incurred; and (10) whether the debtor transferred the essential assets of the business to a lienor that transferred the assets to an insider of the debtor.

 

There is no minimum number of relevant factors that must be present before the scales tip in favor of finding actual intent to defraud.  (Filip at 834.)  The Court therefore analyzes the relevant factors.

 

(1)  Whether the transfer or obligation was to an insider

The Farfalla Transaction would cause PCH to issue a promissory note in the full amount of the Notes, secured by PCH’s stock in OBM, to an entity which it has created for the purpose of avoiding its debts.  PCH has no means to pay on the Secured Promissory Note except by using payments that it may receive on the Notes.  Thus, the income stream from the Notes is the only asset of PCH since the OBM stock itself does not produce income.  Therefore, an effective lien on the payments on the Notes – PCH’s only source of income and assets – is to be effectively transferred to Farfalla, an entity created by PCH for the purpose of eliminating its own debt.  Farfalla is an insider of PCH in this transaction.  The Court finds that this factor weighs in favor of the Farfalla Transaction being a fraudulent transaction.

(2)  Whether the debtor retained possession or control of the property

PCH retains ownership of the stock in OBM while retaining the right to income from the Notes as the 100% owner of OBM.  This factor weighs in favor of designation of the Farfalla Transaction as a fraudulent transaction.

(3)  Whether before the transfer was made or obligation was incurred, the debtor had been sued or threatened with suit

Plaintiffs had filed suit before this transaction was offered to PCH’s creditors. This factor weighs in favor of designation of the Farfalla Transaction as a fraudulent transaction.

(4)  Whether the transfer was of substantially all of the debtor’s assets

See factor (1).  This factor weighs in favor of designation of the Farfalla Transaction as a fraudulent transaction.

(5)  Whether the value of the consideration received by the debtor was reasonably equivalent to the value of the asset transferred or the amount of the obligation

The consideration is cancellation of PCH’s debt and other claims from the lenders other than Plaintiff in an amount not established and so this factor cannot be evaluated.

(6)  Whether the debtor was insolvent or became insolvent shortly after a substantial debt was incurred

If the Farfalla Transaction closes, PCH would become indebted in the amount of the Secured Promissory Note, with its only asset encumbered in an equal amount, rendering it insolvent. This factor weighs in favor of designation of the Farfalla Transaction as a fraudulent transaction.

 

 The Court finds that the balance of the Section 3439.04 factors weighs in favor of a finding that there is a substantial likelihood that Plaintiffs will prevail on their fraudulent transfer claim under CC Section 3439.04. 

           

1.     The Relative Interim Harm to the Parties

a.     Harm to Plaintiffs

In the absence of an injunction, Plaintiffs would be harmed because they would be required to choose between maintaining their claims against an effectively insolvent entity and trading those claims for an interest in an entity that does not guarantee any payments whatsoever. 

b.     Harm to Defendants

If this Court were to issue this injunction, Defendants would be unable to close the Farfalla Transaction.  As the Court does not intend to enjoin Defendants from entering into an Assignment for the Benefit of Creditors, they would be in no worse position than they were in before this proceeding was begun.  While PCH claims that it would be harmed because the Farfalla Transaction is the only way that it can avoid insolvency, as it has reached this position through the borrowing that has put it in this position, the relative harm to the Parties weighs in favor of Plaintiffs who lent the money rather than in favor of Defendants, who borrowed those funds and did not pay them back.
