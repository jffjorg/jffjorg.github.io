DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV00173    Hearing Date: May 2, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

KATIE ANN MARIE ABBETT,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

24TRCV00173

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

SP PLUS CORPORATION, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          May 2, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants SP Plus Corporation, SP Plus Property Management Inc., Le’Shey Damari Fuqua, and 405 Parking

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Katie Ann Marie Abbett

(1)   Demurrer to Complaint

(2)   Motion to Strike

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the third cause of action and SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the fourth and sixth causes of action.  

The motion to strike is DENIED as to paras. 35, 37 (under first cause of action for assault), 41, 43 (under second cause of action for battery), and 48, 50 (under third cause of action for IIED) and prayer for punitive damages and GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to 56, 58 (under fourth cause of action for gross negligence).

BACKGROUND

            On January 18, 2024, plaintiff Katie Ann Marie Abbett filed a complaint against SP Plus Corporation, SP Plus Property Management, Inc., Jamison Properties, LP, 405 Airport Parking, Le’Shea Damari Fuqua, G. Marc Baumann, Kristopher Roy, and Wenyu Blanchard for (1) assault, (2) battery, (3) IIED, (4) gross negligence, (5) premise liability, and (6) vicarious liability. 

            On February 13, 2024, plaintiff filed a request for dismissal as to defendants G. Marc Bauman, Kristopher Roy, and Wenyu Blanchard.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

            Demurrer

Defendants demur to the third, fourth, and sixth causes of action on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

The complaint alleges that on December 4, 2023, plaintiff entered 405 Parking structure located at 9800 La Cienega Blvd., Inglewood to park her vehicle and leave at the premise while she was leaving the U.S. for an overseas trip.  Complaint, ¶1.  After returning from her trip, on December 17, 2023, plaintiff pre-paid for the premise parking fee and returned to her vehicle to exit the premise.  Id., ¶2.  After entering her vehicle and when she drove to the gate to exit the premise, at about 11:30 p.m., plaintiff scanned her pre-paid ticket to exit the premise; however, she was unable to do so.  Id., ¶3.  Thereafter, defendant Fuqua approached plaintiff, refused to allow her to exit the premise, and told her that the parking fee remained unpaid.  Id., ¶4.  Plaintiff politely explained to Fuqua and showed her that she had paid the requisite fee.  Id., ¶5.  Fugua again refused and asked for additional fees to be paid, which plaintiff complied with and politely paid for the requested additional fees.  Id., ¶6.  After paying the fee, Fuqua refused plaintiff to exit the premise; instead, she went around her car and hit/scratched it.  Id., ¶7.  When plaintiff noticed that Fuqua was hitting/scratching/damaging her vehicle, she cracked her driver-door open to exit and take pictures of damage to her vehicle.  Id., ¶8.

The complaint further alleges that Fuqua walked towards plaintiff’s driver door and slammed it on her.  Id., ¶9.  Plaintiff again cracked her door open to exit and Fuqua slammed the door again.  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff’s cracking her driver door open and Fuqua slamming the door on her repeated about three times.  Id., ¶11.  Plaintiff managed to step out of her vehicle; Fuqua walked towards her in a threatening manner, touched her body, battled her, knocked her to the ground, hit her in the face, and attempted to choke her.  Id., ¶12.  Plaintiff’s face began to bleed when Fuqua battled and battered her.  Id., ¶13.  Plaintiff got back on her feet, began to back away from Fuqua when Doe 1 shoved and knocked plaintiff to the ground.  Id., ¶14.  Plaintiff got back on her feet again, grabbed her phone, and began video recording.  Id., ¶15.  Said video depicts Fuqua yielding a knife in her hand.  Id., ¶16.  The video further shows plaintiff’s bleeding face.  Id., ¶17.  Plaintiff managed to get into her vehicle, moved her vehicle to a corner inside the premise, and called the Inglewood Police Department.  Id., ¶18.  Inglewood Police arrived, took statements, and provided plaintiff with a police card that had Fuqua’s full name written on it.  Id., ¶20.  Plaintiff was informed that incidents similar to what she experienced had happened to others.  Id., ¶21.  The premise is owned, leased, controlled, managed, or maintained by entity defendants.  Id., ¶22.

            Third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress

The tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress is comprised of three elements:  (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffered severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) the plaintiff's injuries were actually and proximately caused by the defendant's outrageous conduct.  McMahon v. Craig (2009) 176 Cal. App. 4th 222, 234.  “In order to meet the first requirement of the tort, the alleged conduct . . . must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”  Id. at 234-35. Whether conduct is sufficiently extreme and outrageous so as to be actionable may be determined as a matter of law.  Cochran v. Cochran (1998) 65 Cal. App. 4th 488, 494. To avoid demurrer, the plaintiff must allege such acts with great specificity.  Yau v. Santa Margarita Ford, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal. App. 4th 144, 160-61.  Mere indignities, insults, threats, annoyances, petty oppressions and the like are not enough.  Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal. 4th 1035, 1051.  

The complaint alleges that in committing the acts described, defendants engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct that transcended the bounds of human decency.  Complaint, ¶45.  Defendants intended to cause and did cause plaintiff to experience severe physical injury and emotional distress and they each acted with reckless disregard of the high probability that plaintiff would suffer such injuries.  Id., ¶46.

Defendants argue that the allegations are insufficient and do not show outrageous conduct.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations meet the elements.

The Court finds that the allegations are sufficient.  As alleged, defendant Fuqua’s conduct was extreme and outrageous.

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the third cause of action.

            Fourth cause of action for gross negligence

California does not recognize a distinct common law cause of action for gross negligence apart from negligence.  Jimenez v. 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc. (2015) 237 Cal. App. 4th 546, 552, n.3; Eriksson v. Nunnink (2011) 191 Cal. App. 4th 826, 856, n.18 (“[i]n reality, California does not recognize a distinct cause of action for ‘gross negligence’ independent of a statutory basis”). “Numerous California cases have discussed the doctrine of gross negligence.  Invariably these cases have turned upon an interpretation of a statute which has used the words 'gross negligence' in the text.”  Cont’l Ins. Co. v. Am. Prot. Indus. (1987) 197 Cal. App. 3d 322, 329.  Gross negligence is different from ordinary negligence in degree, not in kind.  Anderson v. Fitness Internal, LLC (2016) 4 Cal. App. 5th 867, 881.  Because gross negligence is simply a degree of negligence, the elements of a claim for gross negligence is the same as one for ordinary negligence.  Rosencrans v. Dover Images, Ltd. (2011) 192 Cal. App. 4th 1072, 1082.  To set forth, “gross negligence” the plaintiff must also allege conduct by the defendant involving either “want of even scant care” or “an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct.”  Jimenez, supra, at 555.

The complaint alleges that defendant Fuqua’s extreme and outrageous conduct constitutes an extreme departure from what a reasonably careful cashier in Fuqua’s situation would have done to prevent harm to others, including plaintiff.  Complaint, ¶52.  Doe 1 knew or should have known that his conduct would harm plaintiff.  Id., ¶53.  Defendants knew or should have known their assault and battery of plaintiff in all likelihood (highly probable) that will harm plaintiff, and they breached their duty of care to plaintiff when they assaulted and physically battered her.  Id., 54.  Given the business in which the entity defendants are engaged (demand for parking fees and interactions with customers) and/or given that similar incidents had occurred on the premise, the entity defendants had advanced knowledge, yet consciously disregarded the safety of visitors.

Defendants argue that gross negligence is not an independent cause of action.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that Fuqua’s conduct was “reckless.”

The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient as this claim is not a distinct common law cause of action apart from negligence.  Further, plaintiff has not pled a statutory basis or elements of negligence.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Sixth cause of action for vicarious liability

            Defendants argue that this claim is not an independent cause of action.  The Court notes that defendants improperly cited to trial court orders, which the Court declines to consider.

            In opposition, plaintiff requests leave to amend to assert a cause of action for negligent hiring and supervision, which plaintiff asserts is identical to “vicarious liability” and “just an alternate name for it.”

            The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient as “vicarious liability” is not a cause of action.  Rather it is a legal theory for imposing liability on one defendant for the acts of others.  “Vicarious liability ‘means that the act or omission of one person . . . is imputed by operation of law to another,’ without regard to fault.”  Chee v. Amanda Goldt Property Management (2006) 143 Cal. App. 4th 1360, 1375 (citation omitted).

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

            Motion to Strike

            Defendants request that the Court strike paras. 35, 37 (under first cause of action), 41, 43 (under second cause of action), 48, 50 (under third cause of action), and 56, 58 (under fourth cause of action) and prayer for punitive damages.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).  Also, “[i]nasmuch as Civil Code section 3294 requires as a prerequisite to the recovery of punitive damages that the defendant ‘has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,’ the cases have uniformly recognized that proof of negligence, even gross negligence, or recklessness is insufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.”  Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87 (citations omitted).

The allegations are sufficient to show oppression, fraud, or malice under the first, second, and third causes of action.  Fuqua’s conduct rises to the level of malice because it shows that she intended to cause injury or despicable conduct which is carried on by defendants with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.  Further, punitive damages “are typically awarded for intentional torts such as assault and battery, . . . intentional infliction of emotional distress, . . .” Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 1188, 1212.

The allegations are insufficient as to paras. 48 and 50 under the fourth cause of action for gross negligence in light of the ruling on the demurrer and that “[i]nasmuch as Civil Code section 3294 requires as a prerequisite to the recovery of punitive damages that the defendant ‘has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,’ the cases have uniformly recognized that proof of negligence, even gross negligence, or recklessness is insufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.”  Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87 (citations omitted).

The motion to strike is thus DENIED as to paras. 35, 37 (under first cause of action for assault), 41, 43 (under second cause of action for battery), and 48, 50 (under third cause of action for IIED) and prayer for punitive damages and GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to 56, 58 (under fourth cause of action for gross negligence).

 

ORDER

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the third cause of action and SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the fourth and sixth causes of action.   

The motion to strike is DENIED as to paras. 35, 37 (under first cause of action for assault), 41, 43 (under second cause of action for battery), and 48, 50 (under third cause of action for IIED) and prayer for punitive damages and GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to 56, 58 (under fourth cause of action for gross negligence).

            Moving defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.
