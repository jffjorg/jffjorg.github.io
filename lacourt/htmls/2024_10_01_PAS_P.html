DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV24836    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT VAGABOND INN CORPORATION AND VISTA INVESTMENTS MGMT. CO.’S MOTION TO BIFURCATE IN PART and DENYING IN PART

I. INTRODUCTION

This is a wrongful death and survival action. Plaintiffs JP, AD, and XP, through their Guardians Ad Litem—Dominica Mitchell, Lynette Grullon, and Tewitt Powell—and the Estate of Kevin Powell, Sr. (hereinafter “Plaintiffs”), filed this action against Defendants First to Serve, Inc., and Vagabond Inn Corporation (hereinafter "Vagabond") (collectively, “Defendants”). Plaintiffs assert claims for (1) wrongful death; (2) negligence; (3) survival action; and (4) negligent hiring, training, and retention. Plaintiffs filed the original complaint on July 6, 2021, and subsequently filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC) on January 1, 2022.

On December 22, 2020, decedent Kevin Powell, Sr. suffered a fatal epileptic seizure while living at Vagabond as part of Project Roomkey, a transitional housing program. Plaintiffs allege that, around 4:30 AM, Powell’s neighbors were alarmed by his loud grunting and crying—typical signs of an epileptic seizure. Knowing Powell’s history of seizures, the neighbors alerted Nurse Olivia Cobain, an employee of Vagabond, and urged her to conduct a wellness check and provide medical assistance. Plaintiffs claim that Nurse Cobain and Vagabond staff failed to act, resulting in Powell’s death.

On August 21, 2024, Vagabond filed a motion to bifurcate the trial into liability and damages phases. Plaintiffs opposed the motion on September 3, 2024, and Defendants filed a reply on September 9, 2024. The Court held a hearing on September 24, 2024, and ordered both Plaintiffs and Vagabond to submit supplemental briefs on the issue of bifurcation. Plaintiffs submitted their supplemental brief on September 24, 2024, and Vagabond submitted its brief on September 26, 2024.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

Civ. Proc. Code §598, permits a court to bifurcate a trial as to the issues of liability and damages when the ends of justice and the pursuit of judicial economy is best served. 

“The court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order, no later than the close of pretrial conference in cases in which such pretrial conference in cases in which such pretrial conference is to be held, or, in other cases, no later than 30 days before the trial date, that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any party thereof in the case . . .”  (CCP § 598.)  Where trial of the issue of liability as to all causes of action precedes the trial of other issues or parts thereof and the decision of the court or jury is in favor of the allegedly liable party, judgment in favor of that party shall be entered and no trial of other issues in the action against that party will be had.  (Ibid.)  If the decision of the court or jury on the issue of liability is against the party allegedly liable, the trial of the other issues shall be had before the same or another jury as ordered by the court. (Ibid.) 

“Section 598 of the Code of Civil Procedure gives the trial court power to order ‘that the trial of the issue of liability shall precede the trial of any other issue in the case . . . ‘Its objective is avoidance of the waste of time and money caused by the unnecessary trial of damage questions in cases where the liability issue is resolved against the plaintiff.’ Cohn v. Bugas (1974) 42 Cal.App.3d 381, 385.  However, the court cannot order the separate trial of an issue of liability when because of the nature of the case it is necessary to prove the plaintiff’s damages to establish that liability. In, Cook v. Superior Court (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 832, 834,  the Court could not bifurcate an issue of liability from damages in a legal malpractice case where it was necessary for plaintiff to prove his former attorney was negligent in the prosecution of a claim and but for that negligence, plaintiff would have obtained a more favorable result, and the factors essential to proof of lability would also be factors essential to proof of damages.

Civil Code Section 3295(d) states “[t]he court shall, on application of any defendant, preclude the admission of evidence of that defendant's profits or financial condition until after the trier of fact returns a verdict for plaintiff awarding actual damages and finds that a defendant is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud in accordance with Section 3294.”  Civ. Code § 3295(d).  “As an evidentiary restriction, section 3295(d) requires a court, upon application of any defendant, to bifurcate a trial so that the trier of fact is not presented with evidence of the defendant's wealth and profits until after the issues of liability, compensatory damages, and malice, oppression, or fraud have been resolved against the defendant.”  Torres v. Automobile Club of Southern California (1997) 15 Cal.4th 771, 777-778.

 

III. DISCUSSION

Defendants argue that bifurcating liability and damages is necessary to promote judicial efficiency and prevent undue prejudice (Motion, p.3). They assert that liability can be determined by a few key witnesses, and a separate trial on damages is premature unless liability is first established. The events surrounding the decedent’s death were brief, and only witnesses with direct knowledge, such as Defendant employees and hotel residents, are relevant to liability. Defendants emphasize that Plaintiffs were not present during the decedent’s medical emergency and had limited contact with him before his death, making their testimony irrelevant to liability. (Motion, p.4,6)

Defendants also argue that bifurcation would spare the minor Plaintiffs from unnecessary testimony and prevent the jury from being influenced by emotional factors during the liability phase. (Defendants Supp. Brief, p.3) They contend that a two-day liability phase would suffice, given the limited witnesses and straightforward facts, making bifurcation more efficient and less prejudicial.

Finally, Defendants argue that bifurcating punitive damages is necessary to prevent undue prejudice, as Civil Code section 3295(d) requires evidence of a defendant’s financial condition to be introduced only after a finding of liability, malice, oppression, or fraud. (Defendants Supp. Brief, p.4) 

Plaintiffs oppose bifurcation, arguing that liability and damages, including punitive damages, are closely linked and cannot be separated into three phases. They contend that much of the evidence for liability, such as testimony about the decedent's medical condition and Defendants' actions, is also relevant to damages. Trifurcation, they argue, would lead to witness overlap, repeated testimony, and unnecessary complications, undermining judicial efficiency. (Opposition, p.3-4)

Plaintiffs further assert that trifurcation would not promote judicial economy but rather prolong the trial with duplicative evidence and testimony. (Opposition, p.3-4) They maintain that addressing all issues in a single trial would be more efficient and reduce delays. (Opposition, p.3-4)

Lastly, Plaintiffs argue that trifurcation would unfairly prejudice them by depriving the jury of important context during the liability phase. (Plaintiffs Supp. Brief, p.4) The decedent’s death and the resulting harm to his family are critical to understanding Defendants' alleged negligence. Plaintiffs also emphasize their constitutional right to a full jury trial, asserting that separating liability and damages would hinder the jury’s ability to fully grasp the case.  (Opposition, p.5)

At the hearing on September 24, 2024, the Court instructed the parties to file supplemental briefs regarding the motion to bifurcate. Specifically, the Court noted that if Plaintiffs were to seek punitive damages, bifurcation would be required. Plaintiffs correctly pointed out that bifurcation of all damages would effectively result in trifurcation, as Civil Code section 3295(d) mandates that evidence of a defendant's financial condition cannot be introduced until after a verdict awarding actual damages and a finding of malice, oppression, or fraud has been rendered.

The Court agrees with Plaintiff’s position that trifurcation—dividing the trial into separate phases for liability, compensatory damages, and punitive damages—would impose an undue burden on the witnesses, particularly the minor Plaintiffs, and others who would need to travel to testify multiple times. Trifurcation would also tax already scarce judicial resources.  Trifurcation would prolong the trial by requiring the jury to engage in up to three rounds of deliberations, which would necessarily be preceded by three rounds of jury instructions. 

However, pursuant to Civil Code section 3295(d), the Court is required, upon a defendant's application, to bifurcate the issue of punitive damages. This ensures that evidence regarding Defendants’ financial condition will only be admissible after liability and a finding of malice, oppression, or fraud. As Plaintiffs are pursuing punitive damages in this case, the Court finds it necessary to bifurcate punitive damages from the liability and compensatory damages phase of the trial.

Therefore, the Court grants Defendants’ motion to bifurcate the issue of punitive damages from the liability and compensatory damages phases. However, the Court does not find it appropriate to trifurcate the trial into three phases.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants' motion to bifurcate punitive damages and DENIES Defendants' motion to bifurcate compensatory damages.

IV. CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court GRANTS Defendants' motion to bifurcate punitive damages.

The Court DENIES Defendants' motion to bifurcate (trifurcate) compensatory damages.

Defendant is ordered to give notice.
