DEPARTMENT 45 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All rulings shown here are TENTATIVE ONLY, and thus oral argument WILL be heard. All Counsel are still required to attend.

Case Number: 23STCV07443    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

 

JOHN VASQUEZ,

 

                             Plaintiff,

 

                              vs.

 

THOMAS P. SCHMALZRIED, M.D., et al.,

 

                              Defendants.

 
Case No.:  23STCV07443                                         

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER

 

 

 

Complaint Filed:  04/05/23

Trial Date:  03/10/25

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             July 8, 2024

Moving Party:             Defendants Thomas P. Schmalzried, M.D. and Thomas P. Schmalzried, M.D., A Professional Corporation

Responding Party:       Plaintiff John Vasquez

 

(1)   Demurrer to the Complaint

 

(2)   Motion to Strike

 

The court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the first cause of action of the Complaint, without leave to amend.

 The court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the sixth cause of action of the Complaint, with 20 days’ leave to amend.

The court OVERRULES the demurrer to the third, fourth, and fifth causes of action of the Complaint.

The court DENIES the motion to strike.

 

Background

This is a product liability case involving an allegedly defective hip implant, the Pinnacle Hip System. On April 5, 2023, Plaintiff John Vasquez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against defendants Thomas P. Schmalzried, M.D. (“Schmalzried”), Thomas P. Schmalzried, M.D., A Professional Corporation (“TPS”) (collectively, “Defendants”), Medical Device Business Services, Inc. (f/k/a DePuy, Inc.), et al., alleging six causes of action for (1) Strict Product Liability, (2) Negligence, (3) Fraud, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Breach of Implied Warranties, and (6) Breach of Express Warranty.

On July 14, 2023, Defendants filed a demurrer to the first, third, fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action in the Complaint, and a motion to strike portions of the Complaint.

Defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike came on for hearing on May 22, 2023. The Court found that the parties did not meet and confer as required under CCP §§ 430.41(a) and 435.5. The Court continued the hearing to this date. Defendants were ordered to file meet-and-confer declarations no later than seven court days prior to the continued hearing date. Both parties were granted leave to file supplemental briefing. (5/22/24 Minute Order.)

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a supplemental opposition to Defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike.

On June 26, 2024, Defendants supplemental papers in support of their demurrer and motion to strike.

 

Legal Standard

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

CCP § 430.41(a) states, in relevant part: “Before filing a demurrer . . . the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.”

 

Request for Judicial Notice

            Plaintiff requests judicial notice of four orders in three different California Superior Court cases, Pool v. DePuy Orthopaedics, et al., LASC Case No. BC465008, Marquay v. Johnson & Johnson Services, Inc., et al., SFSC Case No. 20STCV24678, and Newton v. Johnson & Johnson Services, Inc., et al., SFSC Case No. 21STCV08729.

            Defendants request judicial notice of an order in Rosie Morris v. DePuy Orthopaedics, Inc. et al., SFSC Case No. CJC-10-004649.

            The requests are granted pursuant to Evidence Code § 452(d). The Court takes judicial notice of the existence of the records, but not the truth of the matters contained in the records. (Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1565.)

 

Discussion

Meet and Confer

Counsel for Defendants declares that he met and conferred telephonically with Plaintiff’s counsel on June 13, 2024. (Cohen Supp. Decl., ¶ 2.) During the call, Defendants’ counsel identified the causes of action subject to demurrer and the legal basis for Defendants’ demurrer. Plaintiff agreed to dismiss his first cause of action for strict product liability, but the parties were otherwise unable to reach an agreement. (Id., ¶ 4.) The meet-and-confer declaration has been met.

 

First COA for Strict Product Liability

Counsel for Defendants represents that Plaintiff agreed to dismiss the first cause of action in the Complaint for Strict Liability Design Defect. (Cohen Supp. Decl., ¶ 4.) The demurrer is granted as to this cause of action.

 

Third and Fourth COAs for Fraud and Negligent Misrepresentation

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiff must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

“The tort of negligent misrepresentation is similar to fraud, except that it does not require scienter or an intent to defraud. . . . [T]he same elements of intentional fraud also comprise a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, with the exception that there is no requirement of intent to induce reliance . . . .” (Tenet Healthsystem Desert, Inc. v. Blue Cross of California (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 821, 845, internal citation omitted; CACI No. 1903.)

Plaintiff properly pled fraud against Schmalzried: Schmalzried made misrepresentations and material omissions to Plaintiff regarding the Pinnacle Hip System’s safety and effectiveness (Compl., ¶¶ 112-113, 80, 120, 121); Schmalzried knew that those representations were false but made them with the intent of inducing Plaintiff’s reliance (id., ¶ 114); Plaintiff justifiably relied on Schmalzried (id., ¶¶ 116, 80, 123); and Plaintiff was injured as a consequence (id., ¶¶ 117, 81, 82, 84, 124).

Plaintiff also properly pled fraud against TPS: Schmalzried made the false representation that the Pinnacle Hip System is “safe and effective” to Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s orthopedic surgeon, on behalf of TPS, in Schmalzried’s capacity as TPS’s director, officer, and sole shareholder. (Id., ¶¶ 6, 80.) Schmalzried made misrepresentations and omissions in writing in brochures that were given to Plaintiff’s orthopedic surgeon. (Id., ¶ 55.)

The demurrer is overruled as to the third and fourth causes of action.

 

Sixth COA for Breach of Express Warranty

“An express warranty ‘is a contractual promise from the seller that the goods conform to the promise. . . .’ ” (Dagher v. Ford Motor Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 905, 928.) “The essential elements of a cause of action under the California Uniform Commercial Code for breach of an express warranty to repair defects are (1) an express warranty to repair defects given in connection with the sale of goods; (2) the existence of a defect covered by the warranty; (3) the buyer’s notice to the seller of such a defect within a reasonable time after its discovery; (4) the seller’s failure to repair the defect in compliance with the warranty; and (5) resulting damages.” (Orichian v. BMW of North America, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1322, 1333-1334, internal citations omitted; CACI No. 1230.)

Here, Plaintiff failed to plead the notice requirement. Nowhere in the Complaint does Plaintiff allege that he informed either Schmalzried or TPS of the defective nature of his hip replacement after he first discovered in October 2020 that the Pinnacle Hip System could be hurting him. (Compl., ¶ 85.) Defendants were not given a chance to repair any alleged defect.

Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is sustained with 20 days’ leave to amend.

 

Fifth COA for Breach of Implied Warranties

“Unlike an express warranty, ‘the implied warranty of merchantability arises by operation of law’ and ‘provides for a minimum level of quality.’” (Isip v. Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 19, 26-27, internal citations omitted.) “‘Implied warranty of merchantability’ or ‘implied warranty that goods are merchantable’ means that the consumer goods meet each of the following: [¶] (1) Pass without objection in the trade under the contract description. [¶] (2) Are fit for the ordinary purposes for which such goods are used. [¶] (3) Are adequately contained, packaged, and labeled. [¶] (4) Conform to the promises or affirmations of fact made on the container or label.” (Civ. Code, § 1791.1, subd. (a); CACI No. 3210.) The core test of merchantability is fitness for the ordinary purpose for which such goods are used. (Isip, supra, at 26-27.)

Plaintiff has sufficiently pleaded the Pinnacle Hip System’s abnormally dangerous nature. (See Compl., ¶¶ 51-54 [high numbers of complaints and injuries from patients who received a Pinnacle hip transplant]; ¶¶ 58-79 [recall of a substantially identical hip system].) Plaintiff alleges that the Johnson & Johnson defendants have received more than 2,500 reports claiming that the Pinnacle Hip System failed. (Compl., ¶ 52.) Plaintiff was injured as a result of buying and using Defendants’ hip system. (Id., ¶ 131.)

The demurrer to the fifth cause of action is overruled.

 

Motion to Strike

Defendants’ motion to strike allegations regarding punitive damages is denied. As discussed above, Plaintiff properly alleged a cause of action for fraud, which is sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages.

 

            It is so ordered.

Motion to Seal

The Court considered the moving papers.

            The motion is denied.

Background

            Plaintiff filed a lawsuit for wrongful termination in 2011. In 2014, the Court entered judgment against Plaintiff. Now, ten years later, Plaintiff seeks to seal the records in this lawsuit. Plaintiff also requests that the Court “order any website publishing information about this case to take down the information. If an order to seal is not possible”, Plaintiff asks that the court issue a “redaction order” to protect Plaintiff’s identity.

            Plaintiff is acting pro se. The motion is unopposed.

Legal Standard

Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open to the public.¿ (CRC Rule 2.550(c).)¿ Therefore, pleadings, motions, discovery documents, and other papers may not be filed under seal merely by stipulation of the parties; a prior court order must be obtained by way of noticed motion.¿ (CRC Rule 2.551(a)&(b)(1).)¿

“If documents are filed in court before the motion is granted, the right to move for an order sealing the documents is waived. The court cannot entertain a later motion to seal documents that are already a matter of public record. [Savaglio v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc., supra, 149 CA4th at 601, 57 CR3d at 224].” ¿(Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 9(I)-I.)

California Rules of Court, Rule 2.551(b)(2), sets out the means of service for a motion to seal.¿It states: “A copy of the motion or application must be served on all parties that have appeared in the case. Unless the court orders otherwise, any party that already possesses copies of the records to be placed under seal must be served with a complete, unredacted version of all papers as well as a redacted version.” (CRC, Rule 2.551(b)(2).)

Discussion

            Plaintiff’s motion cannot be granted. First, Plaintiff did not file proof of service of the motion on the other parties that have appeared in this case. Second, Plaintiff’s motion is untimely. This case was disposed of ten years ago. (See Savaglio v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc., (2007) 149 Cal. App. 4th 588, 601 [“any reading of rules 2.550 and 2.551 that encourages an open-ended time frame for filing a motion to seal records long after the underlying substantive matter has been decided would defeat the purpose of the rules”].)
            The motion is denied.

            It is so ordered.
