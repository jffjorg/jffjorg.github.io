DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19VECV00966    Hearing Date: November 16, 2023    Dept: W

NOEL LUSTIG, M.D., INC., dba PSYCHIATRIC MEDICAL GROUP v. MARC L. NEHORAYAN, M.D., A PROFESSIONAL CORPORATION, et al.

 

plaintiff/cross-DEFENDANT Noel Lustig, M.D.’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories nos. 121-125, 140 and 141 (Set four) and demand for inspection nos. 176, 179, and 180

 

Date of Hearing:        November 16, 2023                          Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        19VECV00966

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Noel Lustig, M.D. dba Psychiatric Medical Group 

Responding Party:     Defendant/Cross-Complainant Marc L. Nehorayan, M.D., A Professional Corporation

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Goldberg Decl. ¶¶6-10.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Noel Lustig, M.D., Inc. d/b/a Psychiatric Medical Group (“PMG”) brings this case, alleging the misappropriation of receivables due PMG by Defendants Marc L. Nehorayan, M.D., A Professional Corporation (“Nehorayan Corp.”) and its principal Marc Nehorayan (“Dr. Nehorayan”).  On July 10, 2019, PMG filed its original complaint.  On April 10, 2023, PMG filed its third amended complaint (“TAC”) alleging: (1) breach of fiduciary duty; (2) conversion; (3) fraud; (4) breach of written contract; (5) unfair business practices; (6) unjust enrichment and constructive trust; (7) accounting; (8) breach of duty of loyalty; and (9) aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duty.

 

On July 28, 2020, Cross-Complainants Nehorayan Corp. and Dr. Nehorayan filed their original cross-complaint against PMG.  On November 7, 2022, Cross-Complainants filed their third amended cross-complaint (“TACC”), alleging: (1) conversion; (2) money had & received; (3) imposition of constructive trust; (4) breach of contract; (5) accounting; and (6) aiding and abetting conversion and negligence. 

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Noel Lustig, M.D. dba Psychiatric Medical Group’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Nos. 121-125, 140-141 is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Noel Lustig, M.D. dba Psychiatric Medical Group’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents is GRANTED, IN PART.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Noel Lustig, M.D. dba Psychiatric Medical Group move this court for an order compelling Defendant/Cross-Complainant Marc L. Nehorayan, M.D., A Professional Corporation to provide verified further responses, without objections, to Special Interrogatories, Set Four Nos. 121 through 125, 140, and 141 and Demands for Inspection Nos. 176, 179, and 180.

 

Interrogatories

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, a court may order a party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the court finds that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.  (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. 

 

Plaintiff argues Special Interrogatories Nos. 121 through 125 seek to have Defendant identify the checks payable to PMG and/or Dr. Lustig, which Defendant deposited into its and/or Dr. Nehorayan's bank accounts from January 1, 2010 through December 31, 2014. Plaintiff contends Defendant's counsel has admitted to having stacks of these checks yet Defendant's initial responses to these interrogatories simply consisted of a myriad of boilerplate objections and the supplemental responses simply refer to a mass of documents produced by a third party. Moreover, Special Interrogatories Nos. 140 and 141 seek to have Defendant list the Initial Evaluation Reports and Bills and Requests for Authorization of Psychology Services issued by Dr. Nehorayan through PMG for services he rendered up through March 31, 2015, for which Defendants claim PMG received the payment and for which PMG had no interest. Accordingly, the information sought is directly relevant to the claims asserted by Plaintiff and Defendant’s objections are without merit and their responses are evasive.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues these “make work” interrogatories would require that Defendant compile information that is equally available to Propounding party (the checks at issue) and then type descriptions of each check into a special interrogatory response. Moreover, PMG’s overbroad demand for “Initial Evaluation Reports and Bills” has nothing whatsoever to do with Nehorayan Corp.’s claims and defenses in this case and everything to do with whatever case they are attempting to cobble together. Defendant also contends the only way to determine which “Initial Evaluation Reports and Bills” correspond to payments made on patient files where Nehorayan Corp. was either the sole unpaid biller on the file or where the amount owed to it at that time exceeded a settlement payment is to re-review each and every one of the thousands upon thousands of payments made during the four year period requested.

 

The court finds Defendant’s objections without merit and evasive. As noted by Plaintiff, these interrogatories seek to confirm the dates and amounts of such checks during that time period. Moreover, Defendant’s responses do not meet the requirements for invoking Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230. Specifically, they do not in “sufficient detail [] permit the propounding party to locate and to identify, as readily as the responding party can, the documents from which the answer may be ascertained.” (CCP §2030.230.)

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request compelling further responses to special interrogatories, nos. 121-125, 140-141.

 

Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $4,710.00 against Defendant Nehorayan Corporation and its counsel, David Mayes and Gabriel Law Group, PC, for reasonable attorneys' fees and costs incurred in bringing this instant motion. Defendant asks for their own sanctions in the sum of $4,037.50 against PMG and their attorney of record, Alan Goldberg. However, the court denies both requests with there being substantial justification on both sides. (See CCP §2030.300(d).)

 

Demand for Production

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the Court may order a responding to serve a further response to a request for production when the Court finds that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

 

Plaintiff argues Demand No. 176 seeks all reconciliations performed on the PMG checks (which were payable to PMG and/or Dr. Lustig as sole or co-payees) that Defendant deposited into its bank account during the period of January 1, 2010 through December 31, 2014. Moreover, Demands Nos. 179 and 180 seeks liens that Defendants placed on patients treated 12 by Dr. Lustig and/or PMG on or before March 31, 2015. As a result, all of these demands are directly relevant to the claims in this action as set forth in the Operative Third Amended Complaint.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues they have produced code compliant responses and the reports sought are easily and equally available to PMG. First, the court finds response to No. 176 code compliant. Defendant states “After a diligent search and reasonable inquiry Responding Party responds that it has no documents in its possession, custody or control responsive to this demand.” Although Special Interrogatory No. 131 refers to six payments, Defendant has also provided it does not have them in their possession. This is sufficient.

 

Next, the court finds RPD No. 179 and 180 are not code compliant. As noted by Plaintiff, if Defendant’s contention is that the request misstates Defendant’s position, Defendant must provide it cannot comply with the Demand because no such documents exist. (CCP §2031.230.) Moreover, if Defendant refers to documents elsewhere, Defendant’s response must specifically identify any documents responsive to this demand. Lastly, Defendant’s objection that the documents sought are equally available to Plaintiff is not proper. If responding party lacks personal knowledge, the respondent “shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”  (CCP § 2030.220(c); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 406.)  A propounding party cannot force the responding party to compile information available in the public record.  (Bunnell v. Superior Court (1967) 254 Cal.App.2d 720, 723; Alpine Mutual Water Company v. Superior Court (1968) 259 Cal.App.2d 45, 54.) 

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request compelling further responses to request for production of documents, nos. 179-180.

 

Plaintiff again requests monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $2,910.00 against Defendant Nehorayan Corporation and its counsel, David Mayes and Gabriel Law Group, PC, for having to bring the second motion. Defendant also again asks for their own sanctions in the sum of $2,612.50 against PMG and their attorney of record, Alan Goldberg. However, the court denies both requests with there being substantial justification on both sides. (See CCP §2031.310(h).)

 


Case Number: 19VECV01611    Hearing Date: November 16, 2023    Dept: W

almas & son, llc v. nai capital, inc.

 

DEMURRER with motion to strike the first amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        November 16, 2023              Trial Date:       March 4, 2023

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        19VECV01611

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Patrick Ortiz

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Almas & Son, LLC  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Zampiello Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Almas & Son, LLC alleges it entered into an agreement with Defendants Nai Capital, Inc. and Patrick Ortiz where Defendants were to find Plaintiff a suitable tenant for their commercial building. Plaintiff alleges Defendants represented to Plaintiff they found a tenant who would run a legal smoke shop and ran a background check on the tenant; however, these representations were false. Plaintiff alleges Defendants did not run a background check and the tenant operated a cannabis shop in violation of the agreement and as prohibited by the Department of Cannabis Regulation.

 

On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint alleging: 1) Breach of Contract; 2) Fraud and Deceit; 3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 4) Violation of Business and Professions Code 17200; and 5) Negligence.  

 

On February 7, 2020, Defendant NAI Capital, Inc. filed Notice of Bankruptcy and Automatic Stay with the court. Matter is only stayed as to Defendant NAI Capital, Inc.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Patrick Ortiz’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED, in part with leave to amend. Defendant Ortiz’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED, with leave to amend.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant Patrick Ortiz requests the court take judicial notice of Notice of Filing of Petition for Relief Under Chapter 11 of the United States Bankruptcy Code, 11 USC Section 101, et seq., and Imposition of Automatic Stay filed on February 7. 2020 (Exh. B) and Notice of Bar Date for Filing Proofs of Claim in Chapter 11 Case, filed in United States Bankruptcy Case No. 1 :20-bk-l 0256 on June 20, 2020 (Exh. C).

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Patrick Ortiz demurs to the First Amended Complaint and to each and every cause of action therein on the grounds Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant.

 

Stay

 

Defendant Ortiz first argues Plaintiff’s FAC was filed in violation of the bankruptcy automatic stay and is therefore, subject to demurrer.

 

The court disagrees. Plaintiff was ordered to amend their complaint as to Defendant Ortiz and did such. Although the action is stayed as to Defendant NAI, Plaintiff does not have to remove their allegations containing NAI at this time.

 

Breach of Contract

 

Defendant Ortiz demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract on the grounds Plaintiff's failure to attach a signed contract dooms the pleading. Instead, Plaintiff attaches an unsigned letter, which fails to meet the elements of an enforceable contract.

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) “A written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.) “In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must allege the substance of its relevant terms.” (Id.) 

 

Plaintiff alleges on August 15, 2018, Defendant Ortiz transmitted to Plaintiff a letter agreement whereby Defendants undertook to act as Plaintiff’s agent and broker with respect to the listing of Plaintiff’s commercial real property for lease. (FAC ¶9.) Pursuant to said agreement, Defendants undertook to act as Plaintiff’s real estate agent and broker respecting the listing. (FAC ¶9.) Moreover, implied in the agreement was to act in the highest faith to their client. (FAC ¶10.) Plaintiff further alleges they asked whether Defendant Ortiz had done a background check on Bugarin, to which Defendant Ortiz responded, both orally and in writing, that he followed the standard protocol and rules in entering into the lease with Bugarin. (FAC ¶12.) Defendants breached the written agreement by committing acts which are alleged in paragraph. (FAC ¶23.)

 

Plaintiff does not address this argument. Moreover, although the complaint references an attached agreement, there is no agreement attached to the complaint on file with the court. It is still unclear how Defendant breached the agreement, when no terms of the agreement are alleged and the agreement is not attached to the complaint. Plaintiff is given one more opportunity to attempt to allege a breach of contract against Ortiz. If Plaintiff is attempting to allege Defendant breached the agreement that they would do a background check, Plaintiff must allege so.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Ortiz’s demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of contract is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Causation/Damages

 

Next, Defendant Ortiz argues Plaintiffs allegations of causation and damages relied on in each of its causes of action fail to meet the standards to state claims for relief or to establish that it has suffered any cognizable damages. Defendant Ortiz further argues Plaintiff has not even sought to name the tenant, Mr. Bugarin, as a defendant in this matter.

 

The court previously found the allegations failed to include how Plaintiff has been damaged by the purported misrepresentations or breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiff amended their complaint to now allege Almas & Sons, LLC’s owner, Mr. Dayani, was subsequently convicted of the above criminal code section as a result of the Defendants misconduct. (FAC ¶16.) Plaintiff further alleges as a result of Defendant Ortiz’s conduct, they have suffered in lost income and rent as well as expenses relating to unlawful detainer and criminal proceedings.

 

Mr. Dayani is not a named Plaintiff in this matter and Plaintiff does not allege Almas & Sons, LLC was also subject to fines. However, the court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged damages by alleging as a result of Defendant Ortiz’s fraudulent statements, negligence, breach of fiduciary duty and violation of Business and Profession Code 17200, Plaintiff incurred expenses relating to the tenancy, lost income and rentals.

 

The court OVERRULES the demurrer on these grounds.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendant seeks to strike Paragraphs 33, 38 and Plaintiff’s Prayer, Second and Third Causes of Action Paragraph 1 on the grounds Plaintiffs Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages against Defendant.

 

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .” “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id. § 3294(c)(1).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2).) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to defendant with the intention on the part of defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).) 

 

The court finds Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged facts supporting a claim for punitive damages. Plaintiff has not alleged Ortiz sought to intentionally deprive Plaintiff of their legal rights or otherwise injure Plaintiff and moreover, has not alleged Ortiz acted with the intent to vex, injure, or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the Plaintiff's rights. 

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.


Case Number: 22VECV00917    Hearing Date: November 16, 2023    Dept: W

RAUL AGUILA, et al. v. hunt properties inc., et al.

 

defendant’s motion to compel plaintiff’s discovery responses

 

Date of Hearing:        November 16, 2023                          Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00917

 

Moving Party:            Defendant H.U.N.T. Properties, Inc.

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

Hunt Properties, Inc. is the owner of the commercial real property located at approximately 7108 De Soto Avenue in Canoga Park, California (“Subject Property”). Henry Becker (“Defendant Becker”) is a manager and/or employee of Defendant Hunt.  Since about 2015, Raul Aguila (“Plaintiff Aguila”) was a tenant of the Subject Property.  Orley Onofre (“Plaintiff Onofre”) is an employee of Plaintiff Aguila.  Plaintiffs Aguila and Onofre (collectively, “Plaintiffs”), allege, beginning in January of 2022, Defendant Becker has engaged in pattern of racist and harassing behavior in an effort to drive-out Plaintiffs from the Subject Premises, and force Plaintiffs to terminate the governing lease agreement. Defendant Becker’s harassment included physical harassment, and verbal harassment.  Plaintiffs allege, when Plaintiffs made clear they refused to vacate the Subject Property despite Defendant Becker’s harassing behavior, Defendant Becker then served Plaintiffs with a 30-day Notice to Quit, notifying Plaintiffs the tenancy had ended and Plaintiffs would be required to vacate the Subject Property within thirty days. Plaintiffs allege Defendant Hunt’s employment and failure to properly train Defendant Becker constitutes negligence.  Plaintiffs further allege Defendant Becker’s conduct caused Plaintiffs’ emotional distress.

 

On July 5, 2022, Plaintiffs initiated the present action by filing a Complaint against Defendant Hunt and Defendant Becker (collectively, “Defendants”). Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) Negligence; (2) Negligent Retention and Supervision; (3) Threats of Violence in Violation of Cal. Civ. Code §§ 51.7(B); and (4) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

Defendant Becker filed a third amended cross-complaint against Plaintiffs on July 17, 2023 asserting causes of action for (1) Nuisance and (2) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

Request for entry of default was entered September 13, 2022. Plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint correct Defendant Hunt’s name to H.U.N.T. Properties, Inc.

 

The court set aside the request for entry of default on February 28, 2023.

 

On October 25, 2023, Plaintiffs dismissed their action against Defendants.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant H.U.N.T. Properties, Inc. Motion to Compel Plaintiff Raul Aguila to Respond to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

Defendant H.U.N.T. Properties, Inc. Motion to Compel Plaintiff Orley Onofre to Respond to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant H.U.N.T. Properties, Inc. move this court for an order compelling Plaintiffs Raul Aguila and Orley Onofre to respond to Defendant’s first set of form interrogatories served on May 22, 2023. Defendant also seeks monetary sanctions Plaintiffs in the amount of $2,760 against Plaintiff Aguila and $1,410 against Plaintiff Onofre.

 

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2033.280; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §§2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may also move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2033.280(b).)

 

On May 22, 2023, Defendant propounded Defendant’s first set of form interrogatories on Plaintiffs Aguila and Onofre. (Hamilton Decl. ¶2, Exh. 1.) Plaintiffs’ responses were due by June 22, 2023. (Hamilton Decl. ¶3.) Plaintiffs requested an extension, which Defendant granted; however, Defendant did not receive the responses by the extended deadline. (Hamilton Decl. ¶¶3-4, Exh. 2.) Defendant followed up with Plaintiff’s counsel and Plaintiff requested another extension. (Hamilton Decl. ¶¶4-5, Exh. 3.) At the time of the filing of this motion, Defendant has not received any response. (Hamilton Decl. ¶6.)

 

As such, Defendant’s unopposed motion to compel Plaintiffs’ discovery responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to produce verified discovery responses without objection in ten days.

 

Sanctions

 

Defendant requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiffs in the amount of $2,760 against Plaintiff Aguila and $1,410 against Plaintiff Onofre.

 

If a propounding party moves for and obtains a court order compelling a response, the court shall impose monetary sanctions against the party failing to timely respond to interrogatories and demands for inspection unless that party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c), 2033.280(c); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) 

 

Defense counsel contends they spent two hours preparing Aguila’s motion, anticipate spending 1 hour reviewing the opposition and reply, and anticipate an additional three hours attending the hearing. (Hamilton Decl. ¶7.) Counsel also incurred the $60 filing fee. (Hamilton Decl. ¶7.) Defense counsel also requests two hours for preparing Onofry’s motion, 1 hour in anticipation for reviewing the opposition and reply, and the $60 filing fee. (Hamilton Decl. ¶7.) Counsel’s hourly rate is $450.

 

Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED, in the reduced amount of $1,410 for the first motion and $960 for the second motion. The court finds only one hour necessary for attending the hearing on the two motions.

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV02839    Hearing Date: November 16, 2023    Dept: W

TRACEY PETERSON V. MASIS KHACHADOURIAN

 

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR A DISCOVERY PROTECTIVE ORDER AND FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $750.00

 

Date of Hearing:        November 16, 2023                          Trial Date:       None set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV02839

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Masis Khachadourian and Forgiato, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Tracey Peterson

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of a motor vehicle collision. Plaintiff Tracey Peterson alleges Defendant Masis Khachadourian negligently drove his vehicle, accelerating through a red light causing their vehicles to collide. On June 29, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Motion for a Discovery Protective Order and Monetary Sanctions in the amount of $750 is DENIED.

 

discusion

 

Defendants Masis Khachadourian and Forgiato, Inc. move this court for a protective order against Plaintiff’s Request for Documents 1-58 (Set One) and Notice of Deposition of Masis Khachadourian with Request for Documents 1-51 propounded against Defendant Khachadourian as well as Plaintiff’s Request for Documents 1-58 (Set One) and Notice of Remote Deposition of Forgiato’s PMK propounded against Defendant Forgiato, Inc. Furthermore, Defendants request that the depositions of both Defendants Masis Khachadourian and Forgiato, Inc. PMK be continued until after the hearing on the instant motion for protective order scheduled for November 16, 2023. Defendants contend the discovery propounded by Plaintiff’s counsel is unduly burdensome and harassing, seeking duplicative and cumulative questions in excess of the allowable number that could have been sought in discovery as provided by Code.

 

A court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party from “unwanted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.”  (CCP §2030.090(b).)  The burden of proof is on the party seeking the protective order to show “good cause” for the order he or she seeks.  (Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  A motion for a protective order “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (CCP §2030.090.)  “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (CCP §2016.040.)   

 

Meet and Confer

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendants’ attempt to meet and confer falls woefully short of a good faith attempt at an informal resolution as required by the Code and should therefore be denied. Defendants sent two letters demanding to limit the requests for production to 35, none of which is a requirement under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.010.

 

Defendants contend the meet and confer letter was not a demand in the form of an ultimatum, but rather, an invitation to Plaintiff’s counsel to meet and confer to discuss the relief requested, i.e., to pare down Plaintiff’s requests to the relevant requests for gaining the information that it reasonably needs for a simple and minor motor vehicle accident.

 

Whether or not the parties properly attempted an informal resolution to the matter, the court finds the parties cannot come to an agreement on the issues at hand. Accordingly, the court does not deny the motion on these grounds.   

 

Discussion

 

Defendants argue this is a simple motor vehicle accident. As such, there is no reason to require Defendants to respond to the 39 special interrogatories, 51/58 requests for documents and 32 requests for admissions when in fact the majority of the 39 special interrogatories and 51/58 requests for documents were duplicative.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues they propounded proper document requests on each named Defendant regarding the subject accident. Plaintiff’s discovery is specific and tailored to address the elements Plaintiff needs to prove for liability.

 

Plaintiff further argues Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.010 imposes no restriction as to the number of requests which may be propounded. Further, Defendants’ claim that “220 demands” have been made encompasses two named Defendants, by means of discovery requests and document requests in a notice for deposition. Technically, only 58 document requests were propounded on each party.

 

First, the court notes Plaintiff may request the same documents in the request for production of documents as the notice of deposition as they are different modes of discovery. (See Carter v. Superior Court (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 994, 997.) Second, the court finds Defendant has not presented good cause for limiting the discovery requests to 35 or less. While this may be a “minor injury accident” as Defendants claim, the court finds the 51/58 demands for production along with the 39 interrogatories and 32 requests for admissions not so overly burdensome.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for protective order is DENIED. The court denies Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions.
