DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV03943    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: A

22STCV03943 Shiny River, Ltd. v. Emmet Furla Oasis Films, LLC

Thursday, November 15, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANTS’ FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

I.       BACKGROUND

      The first amended complaint alleges that Plaintiff’s assignee provided 10 individual loans to Defendants for the development and production of independent motion pictures. The aggregate amount lent was $3,487,100. Defendants failed to repay. Plaintiff alleges breach of contract and related claims, fraud, common count, and for foreclosure of copyright mortgages.

II.     ARGUMENTS

      Plaintiff served a document request and special interrogatories requesting financial information of Defendants, Randall Emmet (“Emmett”), George Furla (“Furla”), and Emmett Furla Oasis Films, LLC (“Oasis”, collectively Defendants.) Defendants objected despite receiving an almost two-week extension to respond. The financial information is relevant and discoverable to prove that Defendants received producer fees that should have gone to Plaintiff according to each personal guarantee signed by Defendants agreeing not to receive any individual or corporate executive producer fees individually or through an entity they owned or controlled. Defendants breach this term.  

      In opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff failed to meet and confer. Plaintiff refused to limit the scope of the documents in any respect, even though this action concerns conduct after December 31, 2018. Defendants state the primary issue is the time period for the records at issue. Plaintiff is relying on 2015 and 2016 contracts not signed by the individual defendants. While the parties entered into a protective order, it does not give Plaintiff the right to demand financial information that do not relate to the loans at issue.

      In reply, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ breach of their personal guarantees for the 2015 and 2016 Film Loans warrant discovery of their finances in order to establish Defendants took producer fees to which they were not entitled.

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

      A motion to compel further responses to a document request is proper where the moving party believes the statement of compliance is incomplete, or a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, evasive and/or an objection is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310 (a); Code Civ. Proc., sections 2030.300 subd. (a); 2031.310 subd. (a).) All motions require the parties to meet and confer prior to filing the motion. The motion must show good cause to justify the discovery and must be supported by a meet and confer declaration. (Id. at subd. (b).)

      The scope of discovery is liberally construed in favor of disclosure “as a matter of right unless statutory or public policy considerations clearly prohibit it.” (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court of Merced County (1961) 56 Cal. 2d 355, 377-378.) The broad scope of permissible discovery includes “any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)

IV.   DISCUSSION

      Plaintiff met and conferred with defense counsel by mail sent on August 27, 2024, and by two separate telephone conversations on August 30, 2024, and September 24, 2024, without resolution. (Decl. of Alec Pierce Schulman, ¶¶ 10-13.) At issue are the following requests:

Request for Production of Documents, Set Three (“RPD”), and Special Interrogatories, Set One (“SI”) served on each defendant who served their responses consisting only of objections on August 26, 2024. (Schulman Decl., Exs. A-F.)

 

V.     LEGAL STANDARDS

      A motion to compel further responses to a document request is proper where the moving party believes the statement of compliance is incomplete, or a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, evasive and/or an objection is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310 (a); Code Civ. Proc., sections 2030.300 subd. (a).) All motions require the parties to meet and confer prior to filing the motion. The motion must show good cause to justify the discovery and must be supported by a meet and confer declaration. (Id. at subd. (b).) Plaintiff has complied with the foregoing requirements.

      The party claiming a privacy interest bears the burden of proof on the issue. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 531. [“The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.”].)

      The right of privacy protects against compelled disclosure of confidential records including tax and personal financial records pertaining to topics not placed in issue by filing the complaint. (White v. Davis (1975) 13 Cal.3d 757) [constitutional privacy]; Brown v. Superior Court (1977) 71 Cal.App.3d 141[tax and related records]; SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Superior Court (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 741, 754 [personal financial information.].)

       Where privacy rights are implicated, the requesting party must make a threshold showing that the records sought are directly relevant to Plaintiff’s claims and are essential to the fair resolution of the lawsuit. (Davis v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1014).  An order compelling disclosure must be narrowly tailored so as not to infringe on constitutional rights. Speculation that an answer may recover something helpful does not meet that threshold burden. (Fults v. Superior Court (1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 899, 901.)

      The party asserting privacy must establish the extent and seriousness of the prospective invasion. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557). Against that showing, the court must weigh the countervailing interests the opposing party identifies. (Id.). The court considers the purpose of the information sought, the effect that disclosure will have on the affected persons and parties, the nature of the objections urged by the party resisting disclosure, and whether there are less intrusive means for obtaining the requested information. (SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Superior Court (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 741, 755). Therefore, the privacy interest is not absolute; rather, the court weighs factors to determine whether the financial information should be compelled.

VI.   DISCUSSION

      Plaintiff requests financial information from January 1, 2015,to January 1, 2023. Plaintiff has established that the information sought are directly related to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for breach of the personal guarantees and the fourth causes of action for promissory fraud. Plaintiff alleges the personal guarantees expressly required Emmett and Furla to pay producer and executive producer fees to Plaintiff’s assignee, the Guarantor. (FAC, ¶ 20.) Plaintiff alleges the guarantees were necessary to ensure the solvency of Plaintiff’s assignee and honor its lending obligations. (Id.) The corporate guarantees at issue were signed beginning April 1, 2015, through August 15, 2016. (FAC ¶ 16.)

      Defendants have not established why the scope of financial records should be limited to information beginning December 18, 2018, at which time the parties agreed to restructure the unpaid debts by entering into a Prior Trade Arrangements Agreement (“PTA”). (FAC, ¶¶  26.) Notably, Plaintiff alleges that the PTA did not modify the corporate or personal guarantees. (FAC, ¶ 28.) Therefore, Plaintiff has established that the requests for financial records and information are directly relevant to Plaintiff’s allegations, and without them, Plaintiff will be deprived of the rights and remedies described in the personal guarantees as alleged.

      Defendant Oasis has not established that it is entitled to privacy in its financial information.  (Hecht, Solberg, Robinson, Goldberg & Bagley LLP v. Superior Court (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 579, 594 ["However, ‘[t]he constitutional provision simply does not apply to corporations. The provision protects the privacy rights of people.’"].) Hecht did not decide the issue of whether artificial entities have privacy rights, but rather assumed it given the “ordinary” discovery problems at issue that “fell within the recognized framework for discovery in civil cases,” namely the proper balancing of the requested discovery, its relevance to the subject matter, whether it is reasonably calculated to lead to discovery, against any countervailing interests.

      To the extent Oasis has a privacy interest, the same reasoning would apply to compel disclosure of Oasis’ financial information. Plaintiff alleges that Emmett and Furla are the managing founders and managing members of Oasis. (Complaint, ¶¶5-6.) Emmett and Furla both agreed that executive or producer fees would not be received through any entity they owned, controlled, or was affiliated with. (Complaint, ¶ 20.)

      Defendants have not shown that financial information prior to the PTA agreement is not discoverable. There is no allegation that the PTA superseded the personal guarantees, instead, Plaintiff alleges that the obligations now at issue remained unchanged according to the PTA. Nor have Defendants persuasively demonstrated the relevance of the date that Plaintiff was assigned the rights and obligations of the guarantees at issue (May 1, 2021). Therefore, the financial information consisting of ledgers, bank information, profit and loss statements, and the like are discoverable.

      Defendants’ private financial information is further protected from public disclosure given the parties’ stipulation and protective order that limits access to confidential information. (Min. Ord. , ¶ 7 filed 11/17/22.)

      Plaintiff is entitled to an award of sanctions as Defendants have not shown substantial justification for their conduct. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310 subd. (h.); (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 subd. (d).) The court awards attorney’s fees of $2,610 ($425 x 6 hours + $60 in costs) to prepare the motion and reply and to appear at the hearing.

VII. CONCLUSION

      Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED. Defendants are ordered to serve verified, complete, and code-compliant responses and production of documents without objection to the discovery at issue within 10 days. Sanctions of $2610 are payable to Plaintiff within 10 days by Defendants, Randall Emmett, George Furla and Emmett Furla Oasis Films ($870 per Defendant.)


Case Number: 24STCV12180    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: A

24STCV12180 Kizh Nation – Gabrieleno Band of Mission Indians, et al. v. The Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles, et al.

 

Friday, November 15, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DEMURRER BY DEFENDANT, LA PLAZA DE CULTURA Y ARTES, TO COMPLAINT, JOINED BY COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

I.       BACKGROUND

      The complaint alleges that Defendant wrongfully unearthed more than 100 human remains of Plaintiffs’ ancestors at the First Cemetery and re-interred the remains in a mass grave. Plaintiffs allege the mass graves are a continuing nuisance and violate provisions of the Health and Safety Code and Plaintiffs’ cultural traditions.

 

 

II.               ARGUMENTS

      Defendant, La Plaza De Cultura y Artes (“Museum”) demurrers to all claims for failure to state a cause of action and on grounds the first and fifth causes of action are barred by the applicable statute of limitations, and Plaintiffs have not alleged facts that avoid it. Plaintiffs’ contention that the graves constitute a continuing nuisance is without merit. On October 10, 2024, the County of Los Angeles (“COLA”) joined in Museum’s demurrer. (Notice of Joinder [COLA], 10/10/24.)  

      In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants’ breach of their own rules for respectful reburial creates a continuing nuisance that avoids the statute of limitations. Whether the claims are untimely is a question of fact based on the circumstances of this case. Plaintiffs have alleged facts to support continuous accrual of the statute of limitations. Plaintiffs do not rely on a theory of delayed discovery to avoid the statute of limitations. Defendants’ continuing duty to remediate the mass graves extends the limitations period.

      La Plaza contends in reply that Plaintiffs have not explained why they have delayed 12 years to file this action. Plaintiffs failed to allege any cause of action against La Plaza.

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

      A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.) In testing the complaint’s sufficiency, the court must assume the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations as well as facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded facts. The court may also consider matters properly subject to judicial notice. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

      The court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 638.) Plaintiff is required to allege facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer. Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e); (Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1126.)

      Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case "with reasonable precision and with particularity that is sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.)  Whether the Plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant. (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)

      A demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.” Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing, they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

      A pleading is required to assert general allegations of ultimate fact. Evidentiary facts are not required. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 47; Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal. App. 4th 684, 690.) However, unlike federal courts, California state courts are not a notice pleading jurisdiction, and notice alone is not a sufficient basis for any pleading. California is a fact pleading jurisdiction. Merely putting an opposing party on notice is not sufficient. (Bach v. County of Butte (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 554, 561; see Diodes, Inc. v. Franzen (1968) 260 Cal.App.2d 244, 250.)

 

IV.   DISCUSSION

A.     Demurrer based on the statute of limitations is SUSTAINED.

      The Museum contends that the first and fifth causes of action are barred by the statute of limitations, and Plaintiffs have not alleged facts to avoid the time bar.  The statute of limitations for injury based on negligence is two years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 335.1.)

      Plaintiffs allege the incidents giving rise to this action took place began on January 14, 2011. (Complaint, ¶ 22.) As Plaintiffs explained in their opposition, the first cause of action set forth the applicable laws to support a theory of negligence per se, the two-year limitations period also applies.

      Plaintiffs argue that since the wrongdoing is ongoing, the statute of limitations is not tolled due to the “continuous accrual” doctrine where in certain circumstances, recurring invasions of the same right can each trigger their own statute of limitations. 

(Aryeh v. Canon Business Solutions, Inc. (2013) 55 Cal.4th 1185, 1198.) The principle seeks to correct that inequities that would result where "parties engaged in long-standing misfeasance would thereby obtain immunity in perpetuity from suit even for recent and ongoing misfeasance. In addition, where misfeasance is ongoing, a defendant's claim to repose, the principal justification underlying the limitations defense, is vitiated." (Gilkyson v. Disney Enterprises, Inc. (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 1336, 1342.)

      The doctrine applies where an obligation arises on a recurring basis, such as a case involving periodic payments. (State of California ex rel. Metz v. CCC Information Services, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 402, 418.) The reasoning is that each new breach of an obligation provides all elements of a claim, and can then be treated as an independently actionable wrong with its own limitations period for recovery. "(Aryeh v. Canon Business Solutions, Inc. (2013) 55 Cal.4th 1185, 1199.)  If the harm arose out of a single transaction however, the doctrine does not apply. (Id.)

      The complaint does not allege facts that the Museum had a recurring duty to Plaintiffs that continues to be breached to this day. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to adhere to their obligations for the reburial of the remains according to a January 14, 2011, letter. (Complaint, ¶ 24.) Defendants allegedly did not comply with those steps resulting in the alleged harm that continues unmitigated and unresolved, as the remains were placed in a mass grave. (Complaint, ¶ 14:6-8.)

      Therefore, the misconduct arose in 2011. Plaintiffs’ contention that Defendants continue to breach their obligations by failing to mitigate is not a “recurring” transaction. Rather, the alleged misconduct flowed from the 2011 reinterment.

 

B.     Demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED.

      Plaintiffs allege that the Museum’s expansion into the area occupied by the First Cemetery, approved by the Archdiocese of Los Angeles Funeral and Mortuary Services Corporation (“Archdiocese”), led to the wrongful disinterment of Plaintiffs’ ancestors pursuant to a written letter agreement between the Museum and the Archdiocese dated October 20, 2011. (Complaint, ¶ 110.)

      The first cause of action alleges violations of various codes: Health & Safety Code, section 8113.5, which imposes criminal penalties for failing to limit one body to a single plot; Los Angeles County Code of Ordinances which is intended to “protect and preserve archaeological, historical, and paleontological resources from destruction, and avoid impacts to such resources where feasible.” (L.A.M.C. § 22.44.1570 subd. (D)(4)(e); and a section of the California Public Resources Code that prohibits interference with Native American religion or damage to cemeteries. (Pub. Resources Code, § 5097.9.)

      Plaintiffs concede that the cited codes do not expressly provide for a private right of action. Relying on Michael R. v. Jeffrey B. (1984) 158 Cal.App.3d 1059, however, Plaintiffs contend that these code sections embody a public policy, which is actionable.  Michael R. held that a statute need not provide specifically for civil damages or liability.  A “violation of a statute embodying a public policy is generally actionable even though no specific civil remedy is provided in the statute itself. Any injured member of the public for whose benefit the statute was enacted may bring the action." (Michael R. at 1067.)

      Michael R. applied negligence per se principles based on the violation of criminal statutes. The significance of identifying the statute in a civil suit for negligence “involves its formulation of a standard of conduct that the court then adopts in the determination of such liability.” (Id.) Negligence per se is not a separate cause of action; it creates an evidentiary presumption that affects the standard of care in a cause of action for negligence.”(Johnson v. Honeywell Internat. Inc. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 549, 555.) Contrary to the Museum’s reply, as it is not an independent cause of action, Plaintiffs do not need to allege elements that would entitle Plaintiffs to the presumption of negligence under the Evidence Code. (Evid. Code, § 669 ; Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Center (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1285–1286 ["… it operates to establish a presumption of negligence for which the statute serves the subsidiary function of providing evidence of an element of a preexisting common law cause of action.”]

      Therefore, to the extent Plaintiffs have mischaracterized this cause of action as an independent statutory claim, instead of subsuming it in the negligence cause of action of which it is a part, the complaint is fatally uncertain.

 

C.     Demurrer to the claim for private nuisance is SUSTAINED.

      As Defendants observe, a private nuisance is a civil wrong based on disturbance of one’s land. (Monks v. City of Rancho Palos Verdes (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 263, 302.) Plaintiffs argue they have rights as “Most Likely Descendants” to reclaim their ancestors’ remains from the First Cemetery. (Opp. 11:23-26.) Plaintiffs rely on a case that concerned property owners’ potential rights to groundwater. (Orange County Water Dist. v. Sabic Innovative Plastics US, LLC (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 343.)

      The Sabic case is not persuasive under the facts alleged in the complaint. Defendant cites People v. Reid (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 822, which recognized that human remains have not been legally treated as property although next of kin have "a temporary, quasi-property right in the body of a deceased for purposes of burial or other disposition.” (Reid at 828.) Plaintiffs allege they are “Most Likely Descendants,” not next of kin. As there are no allegations to support Plaintiffs’ rights over the 200-year old remains, this cause of action is defective. (Complaint, ¶ 5:4.)

 

D.     Demurrer to the third cause of action for public nuisance is SUSTAINED.

      A nuisance is generally defined as "one which affects at the same time an entire community or neighborhood, or any considerable number of persons, although the extent of the annoyance or damage inflicted upon individuals may be unequal.” (Civ. Code, § 3480.) A public nuisance is defined as "[a]nything which is injurious to health, including, but not limited to, the illegal sale of controlled substances, or is indecent or offensive to the senses, or an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property, or unlawfully obstructs the free passage or use, in the customary manner, of any navigable lake, or river, bay, stream, canal, or basin, or any public park, square, street, or highway, … ." (Civ. Code, § 3479.)

      Parties authorized to sue for a public nuisance are limited to the State of California, by the district attorney or county counsel, or the city attorney.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 731.) As Plaintiffs are not authorized by statute to pursue this claim, demurrer is SUSTAINED.

 

E.     Demurrer to the fourth cause of action for fiduciary duty and the fifth cause of action for negligence are SUSTAINED.

      A claim for breach of fiduciary duty requires facts to support the following elements: the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of that duty, causation and damages caused by the breach. (Mosier v. Southern California Physicians Ins. Exchange (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1022, 1044.) Plaintiffs allege that the Museum assumed a duty owed to Plaintiffs by agreeing to and authorizing the reburial plan articulated by Los Angeles County.  (Complaint, ¶ 110.)

      Plaintiffs have not alleged facts showing the existence of a fiduciary relationship between them and the Museum. A fiduciary relationship is “any relation existing between parties to a transaction wherein one of the parties is in duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party. Such a relation ordinarily arises where a confidence is reposed by one person in the integrity of another, and in such a relation the party in whom the confidence is reposed, if he voluntarily accepts or assumes to accept the confidence, can take no advantage from his acts relating to the interest of the other party without the latter's knowledge or consent." (Wolf v. Superior Court (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 25, 29.)

      Plaintiffs do not allege they were parties to the agreement between Defendants with respect to the reinterment of the remains. Plaintiffs argue in opposition that a “special relationship” can form the basis for a duty owed. Plaintiffs conflate a special relationship with that of a confidential or fiduciary relationship. A special relationship “inheres” in the nature of the relationship itself. Some examples of special relationships have been recognized as follows:

“(1) a common carrier and its passengers, (2) an innkeeper and its guests, (3) a person who owns or possesses land and persons coming onto the land, (4) an employer and its employees who, while at work, are in imminent danger or rendered helpless by injury or illness, (5) a school and its students, (6) a landlord and its tenants, (7) a custodian and those within its custody, and (8) a manufacturer or supplier of goods and the buyer or user of those goods. (Rest.3d Torts, § 40, subd. (b) [listing first seven]; see Regents, supra, 4 Cal.5th at p. 620, 230 Cal.Rptr.3d 415, 413 P.3d 656; Seo v. All-Makes Overhead Doors (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1203, 119 Cal.Rptr.2d 160 (Seo) [listing eighth].) Second, the parties may create a special relationship by entering into a contract imposing a “contractual duty” to act. (Johnson v. The Raytheon Co., Inc. (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 617, 634, 245 Cal.Rptr.3d 282; Seo, at p. 1203, 119 Cal.Rptr.2d 160; see Suarez v. Pacific Northstar Mechanical, Inc. (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 430, 438-439, 103 Cal.Rptr.3d 168; Stockberger v. U.S. (7th Cir. 2003) 332 F.3d 479, 481 (Stockberger).) Third, a special relationship entailing a duty to act can be created “by a statute or government regulation.” (McHenry v. Asylum Entertainment Delaware, LLC (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 469, 484–485.)

 

      Plaintiffs have not alleged any relationship between them and the Museum. The complaint alleges that remains were on Museum property and the Museum allegedly took part in determining how they would be reinterred.

      As previously explained. Plaintiffs have not alleged facts to support their temporary or quasi-property ownership based on the allegation that they are “most likely descendants” of remains dating more than 200 years. As Plaintiffs have not alleged facts to show that Museum owed Plaintiffs a fiduciary duty or that a special relationship exists between Plaintiffs and Museum from which a duty might otherwise arise, the claim is not adequately alleged.

      The negligence claim fails for the same reason that the claim for breach of fiduciary duty fails. Plaintiff must allege facts to establish that the Museum owed Plaintiffs a duty in particular.

V.     CONCLUSION

      Based on the foregoing, the demurrer to all causes of action is SUSTAINED as to Defendant Museum and co-defendant Los Angeles County, who joined in the demurrer. Leave to amend is ordinarily given if there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured. However, it is the plaintiff’s burden to demonstrate how he or she can amend the complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading. "]. (Association of Community Organizations for Reform Now v. Department of Industrial Relations (1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 298, 302. Plaintiffs contend all claims are viable as written. However, given the age of the claims and that Plaintiffs have not articulated a theory that would toll the statute of limitations, leave to amend is DENIED.
