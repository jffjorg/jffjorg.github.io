DEPARTMENT 54 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV43588    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: 54

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles 

 

MARK MERRENS, 

 

 

 

Plaintiff, 

 

Case No.: 

 

 

19STCV43588

 

vs. 

 

 

Tentative Ruling 

 

VIKING RIVER CRUISES, INC., et al., 

 

 

 

Defendants. 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date: March 19, 2024

Department 54, Judge Maurice Leiter 

Motion to Lift Stay

Moving Party:  Plaintiff Mark Merrens

Responding Party:  Defendants Viking River Cruises, Inc. and Rostislav “Rusty” Karchemsky

 

T/R:    PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO LIFT STAY IS DENIED.  

 

PLAINTIFF TO NOTICE.  

 

The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.  

 

BACKGROUND 

 

On December 4, 2019, Plaintiff Mark Merrens sued Defendants Viking River Cruises, Inc., Rostislav Karchemsky, and Vikram Nair, asserting fourteen causes of action for, among other claims, FEHA violations, whistleblower retaliation, and wrongful termination.

 

On December 10, 2020, the Court granted the Motion to Compel Arbitration filed by Defendants Viking River Cruises, Inc. and Rostislav “Rusty” Karchemsky.  The Court ordered the entire case stayed pending arbitration.

 

Arbitration is currently set for August 2024. 

 

Plaintiff moves to lift the stay against the individual Defendant Vikran Nair for refusing to arbitrate, or in the alternative, to lift the stay and remand arbitration proceedings against all Defendants back to court. 

 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Defendants submitted various evidentiary objections to the declaration submitted by Plaintiff in support of his motion.  The Court rules as follows on those objections.

 

Sustained: Objections 2, 7 as to “I further inquired and gave notice that without Defendant Nair’s consent to arbitration, and with Arbitrator White admission she lacked authority over Defendant Nair, Plaintiff was left with no alternative but to seek Court intervention/relief,” and 8 as to “demonstrating that the Arbitration had not power over him.”

 

Overruled: Objections 1, 3, 4, 5, and 6.

 

  

ANALYSIS 

 

The court in which the litigation is pending is required to “stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)  The purpose of Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.4 is to protect the jurisdiction of the arbitrator by preserving the status quo until the arbitration is resolved. (MKJA, Inc. v. 123 Fit Franchising, LLC (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 643, 660.)  A trial court has the discretion to lift a stay prior to the completion of arbitration only when lifting the stay would not frustrate the arbitrator's jurisdiction.  (Id.)  “[T]he trial court is not required to stay all proceedings against the defendants who are not entitled to arbitration; the court may, in its discretion, sever the action as to [those defendants] or limit any stay to those issues subject to arbitration.”  (Madden v. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (1976) 17 Cal.3d 699, 714.)

   

          As noted, on December 10, 2020, the Court granted the motion of Viking River Cruises, Inc. and Karchemsky to compel arbitration. At that time Defendant Nair had not yet appeared in the case. 

 

Plaintiff argues that Nair has refused to arbitrate, and the arbitration hearing and the case in this Court can proceed concurrently as separate claims against the individuals. Alternatively. Plaintiff asks the Court to lift the stay against all Defendants because there could be inconsistent/conflicting rulings on common issues of law or fact. 

 

          Plaintiff has not shown that Nair was served with a demand to arbitrate. Nor has Plaintiff shown that the order sending the case to arbitration applies to Nair, who had not been served at the time the motion to compel arbitration was decided. And Plaintiff has not shown that the case should proceed separately against Nair.

 

Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND 

 

On December 4, 2019, Plaintiff Mark Merrens sued Defendants Viking River Cruises, Inc., Rostislav Karchemsky, and Vikram Nair, asserting fourteen causes of action for, among other claims, FEHA violations, whistleblower retaliation, and wrongful termination.

 

On December 10, 2020, the Court granted the Motion to Compel Arbitration filed by Defendants Viking River Cruises, Inc. and Rostislav “Rusty” Karchemsky.  The Court ordered the entire case stayed pending arbitration.

 

Arbitration is currently set for August 2024. 

 

Before the Court is Defendant Vikran Nair’s motion to quash service of summons.

Sustained: Objections 1, 2, 3, and 4.

ANALYSIS 

Motion to Quash

 

“A defendant, on or before the day of his or her time to plead . . . may serve and file a notice of motion . . . [t]o quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10(a)(1).)  By filing such motion, “a defendant makes a special appearance for the narrow purpose of contesting personal jurisdiction where the summons is defective.”  (Stancil v. Superior Court (2021) 11 Cal.5th 381, 393.) 

 

“In the absence of a voluntary submission to the authority of the court, compliance with the statutes governing service of process is essential to establish that court’s personal jurisdiction over a defendant.”  (Floveyer International, Ltd. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 793; see also Calvert v. Al Binali (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 954, 961 [“To establish personal jurisdiction, it is essential to comply with the statutory procedures for service of process.”].)  “When a defendant challenges that jurisdiction by bringing a motion to quash, the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service.”  (Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1160, internal quotations omitted.) 

 

“A summons may be served by personal delivery of a copy of the summons and of the complaint to the person to be served.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 415.10.)  “‘[P]ersonal service,’ generally speaking, means the actual delivery of the process to the defendant in person . . . .”  (Wilson v. Eddy (1969) 2 Cal.App.3d 613, 617.)  Personal delivery is the “preferred way to serve a defendant” because it “is the most likely to ensure actual notice to the defendant.”  (Board of Trustees of Leland Stanford Junior University v. Ham (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 330, 336.) 

 

Discussion

 

Nair moves for an order quashing the service of summons in this action on the basis that the summons and complaint were not properly served  Nair argues that (1) contrary to the proof of personal service filed by Plaintiff on December 29, 2023, Defendant Nair was not, nor could he have been personally served on December 27, 2023, at his apartment in New Jersey because he was in California at the time; (2) no one was in Defendant Nair’s apartment on December 27, 2023, and he had not authorized anyone to accept service of process on his behalf; and (3) contrary to the proof of service filed by the Plaintiff on January 17, 2020, Defendant Nair’s usual place of business on January 13, 2020, was not 5700 Canoga Avenue, Suite 200, Woodland Hills, CA 91367, as he had not worked at that location for almost one year. Nair provides his own declaration; it shows that Nair took United Airlines flight 468 from Newark to San Francisco on December 22, 2023, and returned to New Jersey on December 28, 2023 via United Airlines flight 1900. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Nair has failed proffer sufficient evidence that supports his traveling. Plaintiff provides the proof of service filed in Los Angeles Superior Court on December 29, 2023.  It states that Defendant Nair was personally served at his address of 7 Livingston Ave., 4 Apt. 2205, New Brunswick, NJ 08901 on December 27, 2023. 

 

Defendant Nair filed a sur-reply in response to the supplemental declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel whereby counsel attests Defendant Nair was again served via personal service on February 16, 2024.  (Supplemental Declaration of Travis Beck, ¶ 2; Ex. “C.”)  Defendant Nair argues in sur-reply that Plaintiff’s December 27, 2023, and February 16, 2024, proofs of service conflict in their physical description of Defendant Nair.  (Sur-reply, pp. 2-3.)  Specifically, the former proof of service provides: “The individual appeared to be a black-haired Middle Eastern male contact 55-65 years of age, 5'6"-5'8" tall and weighing 160-180 lbs with an accent.”  The latter proof of service provides: “The individual appeared to be a brown-haired Asian male contact 55-65 years of age, 5'6"-5'8" tall and weighing 160-180 lbs with an accent.”  (Id.)

 

Since Defendant Nair challenge service of process, Plaintiff carries the burden of proving valid service.  (See Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868; BBA Aviation PLC v. Superior Court (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 421, 428-29.) 

 

The filing of a proof of service by a registered process server in compliance with statutory requirements creates a rebuttable presumption of proper service.  (Dill v. Berquist Const. Co., Inc. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441-1442; Evid. Code, § 647.)  According to the proofs of services, John Diaraneo and Michael Wienshenker personally served the summons and complaint on Defendant Nair on December 27, 2023, and February 16, 2024, respectively.  Neither person who served the papers, however, is a registered California process server.  The rebuttable presumption does not apply.  In addition, the proofs of service state that Nair confirmed his identify to the process server, but there is no information that either process server confirmed Nair’s identify. Also, the disparity in physical descriptions between the two proofs of service undermines Plaintiff’s motion.

 

Plaintiff has not met his burden of proving facts requisite to effect service. Nair’s motion to quash is GRANTED.

T/R:   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE IS GRANTED.

 

DEFENDANT TO SERVE VERIFIED RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE, WITHOUT OBJECTION, WITHIN 20 DAYS OF NOTICE OF RULING.

 

PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED.

 

PLAINTIFF TO NOTICE.

 

          The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

“If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it. . .  [t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. . . .  The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand. A party must respond to interrogatories and requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (CCP §§ 2030.260(a), 2031.260(a).) If a party to whom interrogatories or requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (CCP §§ 2030.290(b), 2031.300(b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (CCP §§ 2030.290(a); 2031.300(a).)

 

          Plaintiff Pacific Walnut Center moves to compel responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One from Defendant Joseph Esfandi. Plaintiff served the subject discovery by email and mail on December 19, 2023. (Kohan Decl. ¶ 3.) On December 20, 2023, Defense counsel replied to the email stating that he was Defendant’s new attorney. (Id. ¶ 3.) Responses were due on January 18, 2024. (Linzer Decl. ¶ 3.) Throughout the month of January of 2023 and into February of 2023, Plaintiff and Defendant engaged in settlement discussions through their counsel. (Id.) During this time, both counsel were busy with professional and personal obligations. As of the filing of the reply, Plaintiff has not received responses. (Id. at ¶ 5.) The motion is GRANTED.

 

          The Court does not find sanctions warranted here. Defendant represents that the parties were in settlement discussions through their counsel. (Linzer Decl. ¶ 3.) Imposition of sanctions would be unjust under the circumstances; Defendant acted with justification in not responding during that time. The request for sanctions is DENIED.
