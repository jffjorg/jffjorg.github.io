DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV09496    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     December 17, 2024                TRIAL DATE: March 4, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Vista Montoya Homeowners Association v. Lidia Miranda, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV09496           

 

MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH DEPOSITION NOTICE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Vista Montoya Homeowners Association

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 12/12/24

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract that was filed on March 10, 2021. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants violated the terms of a Declaration of Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions which governed the property in which the Defendants reside by allowing a leaking air conditioning unit to cause significant damage to a common area adjacent to Defendants’ property.

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Miranda’s compliance with a deposition subpoena for production of documents, and for sanctions.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Deposition Notice is GRANTED. Defendant Miranda is ordered to provide verified, code-complaint responses to the requests for production without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $1,260 against Defendant Miranda and her counsel, jointly and severally.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Miranda’s compliance with a deposition notice for production of documents.

Legal Standard

 

            Plaintiff’s does not set forth in the Notice of Motion the statutes or other authority under which it is seeking the relief requested in this motion. However, the caption of the motion expressly states that this motion is brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(a). Therefore, although Plaintiff’s notice of motion is not strictly compliant with the form of notice specified in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010, Plaintiff’s notice substantially complies with those requirements. The Court therefore will address this motion on the merits as it presents itself in the caption.

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a) provides:

 

If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(a).) Further, where production of documents is sought in connection with the deposition, the motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(1).)

 

Meet and Confer

 

A motion to compel a deposition must include a meet and confer declaration stating facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2025.450(b); 2016.040.) 

 

The declaration of Karina M. Babikian attached to the motion describes the extensive efforts undertaken by Plaintiff to meet and confer regarding the failure to produce the documents which are the subject of this motion. (Declaration of Karina M. Babikian ISO Mot. ¶ 6; Exh. 2.) Defendant’s counsel only responded once with a bare statement that documents were forthcoming. (Id.)  Plaintiff has therefore satisfied its statutory meet-and-confer obligations.

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff served Defendant Miranda with a Deposition Notice on August 29, 2024, demanding that Defendant appear for deposition and produce 29 categories of documents. (Babikian Decl. ¶3; Exh. 1.) Although Defendant appeared for deposition on September 13, she failed to produce documents or otherwise respond to the requests for production. (Id. ¶ 4.)

 

Plaintiff does not directly explain the relevance of the categories of documents sought so as to demonstrate good cause for their production, but examination of the Deposition Notice reveals that the document requests are facially relevant. Requests Nos. 1 through 6 seek documents and communications related to each of the three causes of action in the Complaint. (Babikian Decl. Exh. 1. Nos. 1-6.) Likewise, Requests 7 through 10 seek similar documents related to Defendant’s Answer or to the action as a whole. (Id. Nos. 7-10.) Requests Nos. 11 through 26 seek photographs, documents, or communications pertaining to Defendant’s balcony, the air conditioning unit on that balcony, and the turf which was allegedly installed on that balcony, all of which go directly to the factual contentions set forth in the Complaint. (Babikian Decl. Exh. 1. Nos. 11-26; Complaint ¶¶ 11-25.) Finally, Requests Nos. 27, 28, and 29 seek the Association’s Declaration of Covenants, Conditions and Restrictions, the Association’s governing documents, and any documents evidencing Defendant’s ownership of the unit, respectively. (Babikian Decl. Exh. 1. Nos. 27-29.) These materials are also facially relevant to this action.

 

As Plaintiff’s document requests demonstrate good cause on their face, Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling Defendant to produce these materials.

 

Request for Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests an award of sanctions in the amount of $2,860 against Defendant and her counsel, jointly and severally.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(g)(1) requires the Court to impose sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel deposition, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. The Court may award sanctions to a party who moves to compel discovery even though no opposition is filed. (Cal Rules of Court Rule 3.1348(a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks sanctions in the amount of $2,860, based on a total of 3 hours actually incurred at $400 per hour, plus four hours anticipated at the same rate, plus $60 in filing fees. (Babikian Decl. ¶ 8.) The Court declines to award attorney’s fees that have not been actually incurred and will therefore award reduced sanctions in the amount of $1,260, reflecting 3 hours actually incurred at $400 per hour plus $60 in filing fees.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Deposition Notice is GRANTED. Defendant Miranda is ordered to provide verified, code-complaint responses to the requests for production without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $1,260 against Defendant Miranda and her counsel, jointly and severally, to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days of this order.

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     December 17, 2024                TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Lawson Bush V v. Board of Trustees of the California State University, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 24STCV06982           

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Board of Trustees of California State University.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Lawson Bush V.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         03/20/24: Complaint filed.

·         09/05/24: Cross-Complaint filed.

·         09/06/24: Special motion to strike granted. Fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action stricken.

·         11/18/24: Request for dismissal of Cross-Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for employment discrimination, harassment, retaliation, and defamation. Plaintiff alleges that he was not selected for the position of Dean of the College of Ethnic Studies at California State University Los Angeles. Plaintiff attributes this denial to alleged statements by Defendant Melina Abdullah which he contends were defamatory.

 

Defendant Board of Trustees of California State University moves for an award of attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(c).

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Board of Trustees of the California State University’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant is awarded attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $76,100.74, representing $75,688 in attorney’s fees and $412.74 in costs.

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant Board of Trustees of California State University moves for an award of attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(c).

 

Entitlement to Fees

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(c) entitles a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike, otherwise known as an anti-SLAPP motion, to recover their attorney’s fees and costs. The amount of attorneys’ fees awarded must be reasonable. (See, e.g., Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1131.) The prevailing defendant may only recover fees and costs incurred in connection with the motion to strike. (Lafayette Morehouse, inc. v. Chronical Publishing Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1383.) This limitation nevertheless permits recovery of consequential fees, such as those incurred litigating the fee award (Ketchum v. Moses, supra, 24 Cal.4th at 1141) and on appeal. (Evans v. Unknow (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1490, 1499-1500 [Section 425.16(c) permits recovery of appellate fees because they are not expressly precluded by the statutory language].) Examples of matters which are excluded from a fee award under this provision include, inter alia, fees for “attacking service of process, preparing and revising an answer to the complaint” and “obtaining the docket at the inception of the case.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325.)

 

On September 6, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s special motion to strike the fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action alleged in the Complaint. (September 6, 2024 Minute Order.) Defendant is therefore entitled to an award of reasonable attorney’s fees and costs by the express language of Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(c).

 

Reasonableness of Fees

 

            Although Defendant moves for a total award of $88,443.47 in fees and costs, comprised of a claimed $79,957.97 in fees and costs through the filing of the motion, plus $8,485.50 in fees and costs associated with the reply and the hearing, closer examination of the papers indicates that Defendant’s arithmetic is incorrect. Specifically, Defendant appears to have calculated $75,545.23 in attorney’s fees and $412.74 in costs through the filing of the instant motion but totals that value incorrectly to $79,957.97. (See Declaration of Kevin L. Vick ISO Mot. ¶ 9.) Moreover, close reading of the billing records provided by Defendant reveals that the total fees incurred through the initial filing are $75,172.50, not either amount referenced above. Applying the proper calculations, Defendant is seeking an award of $75,172.50 in attorney’s fees and $412.74 in costs incurred up through the filing of the motion, plus $7,495.50 in fees associated with the reply, plus $990 in anticipated fees, for a total of $84,070.74 in requested fees and costs. (Supplemental Declaration of Kevin L. Vick ISO Reply ¶ 2.)

 

Reasonable attorney fees are ordinarily determined by the Court pursuant to the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-1096; Margolin v. Regional Planning Com. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 999, 1004 [“California courts have consistently held that a computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys' fee award.”].) “[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award….”  (Ibid.) In setting the hourly rate for a fee award, courts are entitled to consider the “fees customarily charged by that attorney and others in the community for similar work.” (Bihun v. AT&T Info. Sys., Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 976, 997 [affirming rate of $450 per hour], overruled on other grounds by Lakin v. Watkins Associated Indus. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 644, 664.)  The burden is on the party seeking attorney’s fees to prove the reasonableness of the fees. (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 603, 615.) 

 

The Court has broad discretion in determining the amount of a reasonable attorney’s fee award, which will not be overturned absent a “manifest abuse of discretion, a prejudicial error of law, or necessary findings not supported by substantial evidence.” (Bernardi v. County of Monterey (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1393-1394.)  The Court need not explain its calculation of the amount of attorney’s fees awarded in detail; identifying the factors considered in arriving at the amount will suffice. (Ventura v. ABM Indus. Inc. (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 274-275.) 

 

            Here, Defendant requests a total award of $84,443.47 in requested fees and costs. The bulk of these fees, totaling $75,172.50, not $75,545.23 nor $79,957.97 as claimed by Defendant, are based on 7.5 hours of paralegal time at $195 per hour, 87.3 hours of attorney time at $355 per hour, 69.9 hours of attorney time at $495 per hour, and a further 16.4 hours of attorney time by a separate lawyer at the same $495 per hour rate incurred through the filing of this motion. (See Vick Decl. Exh. 2.) Defendant requests an additional $7,495.50 in fees actually incurred in connection with the reply, based on 14.7 additional hours of attorney time at $355 per hour, plus 4.6 hours of attorney time at $495 per hour. (Supp. Vick Decl. Exh. 4.) Defendant further requests $990 in anticipated fees, reflecting 2 hours of attorney time at the $495 hourly rate. (Supp Vick Decl. ¶ 2.) Finally, Defendant requests $412.74 in costs. (See Vick Decl. Exh. 2.)

 

            Defendant has provided itemized billing records indicating the attorney or paralegal who performed each task, the date of the work, and the hours, rate, and amount billed, with relatively detailed descriptions of each task. (Vick Decl. Exh. 2., Supp Vick Decl. Exh. 4.) Attorney Kevin Vick testifies to his own experience and $495 hourly rate, as well as that of his attorney colleagues Jean-Paul Jassy (also $495 per hour), Nicholas Hartmann ($355 per hour) and paralegal Jason Kang ($195 per hour.) (Vick Decl. ¶¶ 4-7.) The records show that research and drafting tasks were generally, but not always, assigned to more junior attorneys—i.e., Attorney Hartmann—charging lower rates, while reserving strategic and editing pursuits and client communication to more senior attorneys. Although Attorney Vick appears to have performed significant research and drafting work during the initial stages of preparing the Special Motion to Strike in May 2024, Attorney Hartmann increasingly took on those tasks as the case proceeded. (Vick Decl. Exh. 2; see also Supp Vick Decl. Exh. 4.) Defendant’s billing records therefore demonstrate that counsel exercised billing judgment in the apportionment of attorney time.

 

            Plaintiff argues that the fees sought by Defendant are unreasonable because they are excessive or contain duplicative work and block billing, with specific identification of billing entries which he contends are improper. The Court first notes that Defendant seeks fees for activities beyond those necessary to support its anti-SLAPP motion, by including in its billing records various communications about Defendant Abdullah’s joinder in Defendant’s motion as well as defense counsel’s review of Abdullah’s cross-complaint and discussions about Abdullah’s potential response to the complaint. To account for these non-compensable hours, the Court reduces the fee award by $990 to cover 2 hours of work at the $495 rate. The Court has conducted its own review of defense counsel’s billing records and concludes that, while there is no improper block billing, the records reflect some excessive and duplicative work, including multiple reviews and revisions by several attorneys of the moving and reply papers and input by three attorneys on the six-page opposition to the ex parte application. The Court, thus, reduces the remaining fees sought in the moving papers by $5,000 to trim from the award any unnecessary or unjustified fees. The Court also shaves $990 from the fees sought in drafting the reply brief in support of this fee motion based on its view that expending 18 hours on a simple ten-page reply is excessive.  Thus, the Court finds that Defendant is entitled to recover reasonable fees in the total amount of $75,688, plus costs of $412.74.

 

Plaintiff also asserts, without explanation or evidence, that the work undertaken by Defendant’s counsel to prepare the Special Motion to Strike would have been necessary to litigate the remaining claims. Mere assertions such as this are not persuasive, nor are Plaintiff’s wholly irrelevant contentions that Plaintiff is “not an abuser of the legal process” and “did not intend to drive up fees.”

 

            Finally, Plaintiff request that the Court stay enforcement of any fee order until the conclusion of the case, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 918. Although the Court ordinarily may stay enforcement of any judgment or order under that section, subdivision (b) prohibits the Court, without consent of the adverse party, from staying enforcement of an order more than 10 days beyond the last date to appeal that order if that order would be stayed on appeal only by posting an undertaking. (Code Civ. Proc. § 918(b).) Fee awards pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(c) fall within the purview of that subdivision. (Dowling v. Zimmerman (2001) 85 Cal.App.4th 1400, 1432.) Moreover, Plaintiff offers no explanation or evidence of why good cause exists for such an order beyond a bare assertion that the fee award “would create undue hardship for Plaintiff.” (Opposition p.13:12.)

 

            In sum, Defendant has demonstrated both its entitlement to fees and costs and the reasonableness of a slightly reduced award of fees and costs. The Court therefore awards Defendant, the Board of Trustees of California State University, a total of $76,100.74, representing $75,688 in attorney’s fees and $412.74 in costs.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant Board of Trustees of the California State University’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant is awarded attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $76,100.74, representing $75,688 in attorney’s fees and $412.74 in costs.
