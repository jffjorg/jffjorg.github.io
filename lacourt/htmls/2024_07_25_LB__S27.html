DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00062    Hearing Date: July 25, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Hany Hegazy, Taha Deif, Moustafa Ahmed Shokr, and TMH, LLC filed this action against Defendants, Giang Hong and Hai Hong on 2/10/22.  Plaintiffs added Suong Thi Trinh as a doe defendant.  Notably, Giang Hong has passed away. 

 

On 12/13/22, Trinh filed a demurrer to the complaint.  Prior to the hearing, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint on 3/15/23.  The FAC included causes of action for conversion, fraud and deceit, breach of written lease contracts, and breach of oral contract. 

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants agreed to lease commercial property to them, and represented to them the property was zoned for the use that they intended.  Plaintiffs allege they spent $70,000 building out the property, but were unable to obtain permits to operate their intended mini-market and smoke shop because the premises were not zoned for such use. 

 

On 12/07/23, the Court sustained Trinh’s demurrer in part and granted his motion to strike in part.  The Court granted leave to amend.  On 12/27/23, Plaintiffs filed their operative Second Amended Complaint.  The SAC includes causes of action for conversion, fraud, and breach of written lease contracts. 

 

  1. Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages, as well as a variety of other allegations. 

 

a.     Punitive Damages

Defendant argues punitive damages cannot be awarded because Defendant is defending the suit in his capacity as Decedent’s successor-in-interest, as the actual defendant, Giang Hong, died before suit was filed.  Defendant also argues fraud, malice, or oppression is not pled.

 

Any opposition to the motion was due on or before 7/10/24.  Plaintiffs have not filed timely opposition to the motion, and the Court will not consider late-filed opposition papers.  In light of the lack of opposition, the Court finds Plaintiffs have abandoned their claim for punitive damages, and grants the motion without leave to amend. 

 

b.     Non-Economic Damages

Defendant also moves to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for noneconomic damages, including emotional distress, anxiety, and humiliation.  Again, in light of the lack of opposition, the Court finds Plaintiffs have abandoned this claim, and the motion to strike is granted without leave to amend.   

 

c.     Accounting

Plaintiffs’ complaint requests an accounting.  In light of the lack of opposition, the motion to strike all references to an accounting is granted without leave to amend.

 

  1. Conclusion

The motion to strike is granted without leave to amend.  Defendant is ordered to file an answer to the SAC, with the subject allegations deemed stricken, within ten days.

 

  1. OSC

The parties are reminded that there is an OSC re: failure to file proof of service and an OSC re: failure to prosecute on calendar concurrently with the hearing on the motion to strike.  The Court asks the parties to make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on the motion to strike and OSC. 


Case Number: 22LBCV01046    Hearing Date: July 25, 2024    Dept: S27

On 6/20/24, the parties appeared for a status conference re: settlement.  The parties represented that the settlement was contested, and the Court specially set a hearing for a motion to enforce settlement for 7/25/24.  The Court’s minute order indicated, “Papers are to be filed per code.” 

 

No party has filed a timely motion to enforce the settlement.  The Court will not consider a late-filed motion.  The Court therefore takes the motion to enforce settlement off calendar.    

 

The Court sets a status conference re: settlement for August 20, 2024.

Case Number: 23LBCV01090    Hearing Date: July 25, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Cheryl Garlow Garcia filed this action against Defendant, John F. Kellogg for damages and injunctive relief arising out of the parties’ joint ownership of Nominal Defendant, Island Donuts, LLC.  Plaintiff’s operative complaint is her First Amended Complaint, which she filed on 2/16/24.  The FAC includes causes of action for judicial expulsion, conversion, improper distributions, breach of fiduciary duty, breach of contract, and injunctive and declaratory relief.    

 

2.     Demurrer

a.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant demurs to the sixth cause of action in the FAC for injunctive and declaratory relief, contending it fails to state a claim and is uncertain.  He contends the body of the FAC fails to indicate what declaration Plaintiff seeks by way of the cause of action.  He contends the cause of action is uncertain because it improperly groups two causes of action into one. 

 

Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, contends she clearly alleges a controversy relating to the parties’ claims to Island Donuts, which controversy must be resolved by way of judicial declaration. 

 

Defendant, in reply, argues the opposition does nothing to clarify the allegations of the FAC, and the demurrer should be sustained. 

 

b.    Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

c.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares the attorneys met and conferred by telephone, but were unable to resolve their dispute.  The demurrer is therefore ripe for judicial determination.

 

d.     Analysis

Plaintiff’s FAC, in the caption, indicates she is asserting a sixth cause of action for “injunctive and declaratory relief.”  The sixth cause of action in the body of the FAC is labeled as one for “preliminary injunction.”  Defendant correctly notes that this defect renders the FAC uncertain.  Notably, the body of the sixth cause of action contains absolutely no facts seeking a judicial declaration or stating what declaration Plaintiff desires.  It contains only a paragraph incorporating all other paragraphs by reference (many of which allege a contractual dispute, but none of which allege a specific desired judicial declaration), and then one paragraph indicating Plaintiff seeks an injunction against Defendant.

 

Plaintiff’s opposition does nothing to clarify the uncertainty.  Rather than conceding the sixth cause of action is ONLY for injunctive relief, she argues the sixth cause of action adequately states a claim for declaratory relief. 

 

Plaintiff must amend her complaint to clarify whether she seeks declaratory relief, injunctive relief, or both.  If she seeks both, she must assert the causes of action separately from each other and detail exactly what judicial declaration she seeks. 

 

e.     Conclusion

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff must file an amended complaint within twenty days.  Defendant must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.

A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he emailed Plaintiff’s attorney to meet and confer, and followed up with a phone call the next day, but Plaintiff’s attorney did not respond.  Plaintiff’s attorney is admonished to participate in the meet and confer process in the future in connection with this and other actions.

 

c.     First Cause of Action, Declaratory Relief

i.              Allegations of First Cause of Action

Plaintiff’s first cause of action seeks the following declarations:

a. So long as the Association has been dissolved, Defendants are not authorized by law to conduct any business of the Association other than such as is necessary for the winding up of the corporation;

b. Defendants are not authorized by law to collect assessments or any other monies from the membership, including Plaintiff, for any purpose other than actions relating to the winding up of the corporation; 

c. Defendants are not authorized to incur debts on behalf of the Association for any purpose other than actions relating to winding up the corporation; 

d. Defendants are not authorized to take enforcement action against any member, including Plaintiff, for allegedly violating any purported Association governing documents unless and until (i) such governing documents have been lawfully adopted by the Association and/or its members, recorded and/or deliver to the members (as applicable) and according to law; 

e. Defendants are not authorized to take such action as set forth in subsection d. above unless and until the Association becomes a lawfully operating entity; 

f. Plaintiff is not obligated to pay any monies to the Association or anyone acting on behalf of the Association, until such time as the Association is authorized to conduct business in the state of California;

g. In order to conduct regular business of the Association, Defendants are required by law to cure the status of the Association with the Secretary of State such that the Association is either reinstated as a corporation in good standing; or is legally reorganized as a lawful unincorporated association;

h. Upon winding up the corporation, all Association assets are to be distributed equally to each member of the Association.

 

ii.             Analysis

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action, contending that, since the HOA no longer exists per the allegations of the complaint, the cause of action is unintelligible and subject to demurrer for uncertainty.  Additionally, Defendant contends the declaratory relief cause of action is derivative of the accounting cause of action, and because it fails, the declaratory relief cause of action also fails.

 

Plaintiff correctly notes, in opposition to the demurrer, that he alleges the HOA has been dissolved, but continues to operate, such that there is no uncertainty in the allegations of the complaint.  Plaintiff also correctly notes, in opposition to the demurrer, that the declarations sought are not derivative of the accounting cause of action, and the declaratory relief cause of action therefore does not rise and fall with the accounting cause of action, which seeks separate and distinct relief.

 

Defendant, in reply, drops the two arguments made in demurrer and instead contends the declarations sought are retroactive in nature and not prospective in nature, such that they cannot support a cause of action.  The argument is improper because it is made for the first time in reply.  Additionally, the declarations sought clearly concern the parties’ rights and responsibilities going forward, and therefore the demurrer is not well-taken. 

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action for declaratory relief is overruled.

 

d.     Second Cause of Action, Accounting

Plaintiff’s second cause of action seeks an accounting of the monies she has wrongfully paid to the association, how those monies have been used, and whether the monies have been used lawfully.  The parties agree on the law in this regard.  They both agree that an accounting is appropriate if either (a) the parties stand in a fiduciary relationship, and/or (b) there is a balance due by the defendant to the plaintiff that can only be determined through an accounting. 

 

Defendant contends neither exist here.  Plaintiff concedes there is no fiduciary relationship.  Defendant contends there is no need for an accounting because Plaintiff has equal knowledge of what amounts she has paid to Defendant.  Defendant fails to address the purpose of the accounting, which is not to determine what amounts Plaintiff paid to Defendant, but how those amounts were used by Defendant and whether that use was lawful. 

 

The demurrer is overruled.

e.     Final Note

While not raised by the parties, the Court has a concern about this case in general.  Plaintiff alleges the HOA has been dissolved.  It is the Court’s understanding that an HOA must exist and govern a community such as the one at issue in this case.  While not directly relevant to the ruling on the demurrer, the Court does ask the parties to meet and confer in an attempt to determine how the community should be governed going forward in light of the dissolution of the HOA.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV02391    Hearing Date: July 25, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Jane KR Doe, a minor, by and through her GAL, Jane TR Doe, filed this action against Defendants, LBUSD, Jill Baker, Rebecca Caverly, and Brianna Flotree for damages arising out of sexual abuse perpetrated on Plaintiff by another student at the school.  The individual defendants are the superintendent, principal, and teacher allegedly responsible for allowing the abuse to occur.

 

2.     Motion to Compel Further Responses

a.     Parties’ Positions

Plaintiff propounded RPDs on LBUSD, and some of the RPDs sought production of documents relating to the student who perpetrated the abuse.  The RPDs sought documents such as prior discipline records of the student, communication between authorities at the school concerning the student, prior complaints/litigation/reports of abuse by the student against others, etc.  Defendant responded by asserting various objections, including that it was obligated to assert the privacy rights of the third party student under the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA).  Plaintiff concedes Defendant was obligated to assert the FERPA objection, and Defendant can only produce the documents with a court order to do so.  Plaintiff therefore filed this motion to compel further responses, pursuant to which Plaintiff seeks a court order overruling the FERPA objection and compelling production of the subject documents.

 

Defendant opposes the motion.  It argues Plaintiff failed to meet and confer prior to filing the motion and failed to make a showing of good cause in the moving papers.  It contends Plaintiff failed to show a compelling need for the documents that overcomes the third party’s right to privacy.  It contends Plaintiff was required to serve the third party student with notice of this motion.  Finally, it contends that, to the extent there is other litigation that has resulted in confidential settlement, Plaintiff improperly seeks to compel production of documents related to a confidential settlement. 

 

Plaintiff, in reply, contends the meet and confer efforts were sufficient.  Plaintiff contends both good cause and a compelling need have been shown.  Plaintiff contends the parties have been working in good faith to draft a mutually agreeable protective order concerning the private information.  Plaintiff contends notice to a third party is not necessary. 

 

b.     Meet and Confer

The Court finds the showing of meet and confer was sufficient.  Plaintiff’s attorney filed a meet and confer declaration with the moving papers.  More importantly, a FERPA objection must be asserted by a defendant, and can only be overruled by court order.  It is, therefore, unclear how additional meet and confer efforts could have resolved the parties’ issues. 

c.     Good Cause/Compelling Need

The Court finds the good cause and compelling need requirements are satisfied.  While the moving papers could have been more detailed in this regard, the need for the documents is obvious.  Plaintiff is asserting a claim for failure to supervise, etc., against the defendants, and an element of that claim would be notice to the defendants that the student was engaging in abusive behavior.  The requested documents go directly to that issue, such that the good cause and compelling need requirements are, on their face, established.

 

d.     Notice

The most difficult issue presented is whether the third party was entitled to notice of this motion.  The law in this regard is as follows:

The person whose privacy is involved must be given notice of the discovery request and an opportunity to object to the invasion of the person's privacy prior to disclosure.  Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 652, 658;  Life Technologies Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 640, 656. 

 

Alternatively, the court may order the party from whom disclosure is sought to ask the third persons for their permission to disclose confidential information. Disclosure is then permitted only as to those who give permission; and counsel are barred from contacting those who do not. Colonial Life & Accident Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1982) 31 Cal.3d 785, 794-795. 

 

The court must decide which notice method is appropriate in a particular case.  Olympic Club v. Superior Court (1991) 229 CA3d 358, 364-365.

Also, a litigant “may assert the privacy rights of third parties.” [County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2021) 65 CA5th 621, 635. 

Plaintiff asks the Court to use the second method, having Defendant ask the student for permission to obtain the records.  The Court is fine with this method, but notes that, in the event the student declines the request, Plaintiff will need to reserve a new hearing date on the motion and serve the motion on the student before the Court can grant the relief requested. 

 

e.     Prior Settlements

Defendant’s final contention is that the requests seek prior litigation relating to the student and, to the extent there was a prior settlement and the prior settlement was confidential, those records cannot be produced.  To the extent these records exist, the parties must meet and confer.  A protective order can be used to protect any confidential information. 

 

f.      Conclusion

Defendant is ordered to ask the third party for permission to produce the requested documents.  If the third party refuses, then Plaintiff may re-file the motion (or an amended variation on the motion if preferred, it need not be exactly the same motion already filed) with proper notice to the student. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.
