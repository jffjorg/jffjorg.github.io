DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20SMCV00406    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Rosamond Ranch LP v. South La Cienega Holdings LLC, et al.

CASE NO.:                20SMCV00406

MOTION:                  Motion for Terminating, Monetary Sanctions

HEARING DATE:   9/24/2024

 

 

Legal Standard

           

If a party fails to obey a court order compelling it to provide a discovery response, “the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction . . . In lieu of or in addition to this sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction . . ..” (CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2030.300(e), 2031.300(c), 2031.320(c).) Misuse of the discovery process, which includes disobeying a court order to provide discovery, is conduct subject to sanctions. (CCP § 2023.010(g).) Possible sanctions are:

 

(a) [A] monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct….

 

(b) [A]n issue sanction ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process. The court may also impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.

 

(c) [A]n evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.

 

(d) [A] terminating sanction by one of the following orders:

 

(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

 

(2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

 

(3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

 

(4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.

 

(e) [A] contempt sanction by an order treating the misuse of the discovery process as a contempt of court.

 

(CCP § 2023.030.)

 

 

Analysis

 

Defendants/Cross-Complainant South La Cienega Holdings, LLC, WKM Corporation and Promontory Holdings LLC move for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendants Rosamond Ranch, LP, Calitex LLC, Elias Shokrian, Derek Boisse, and Neri Sarra. Defendants request that the Court strike the complaint of Plaintiff Rosamond, and strike the answer of Cross-Defendants Rosamond, Calitex, Shokrian, Boisse and Sarra to the cross-complaint. Defendants/Cross-Complainant also request monetary sanctions of $50,235.00 against Cross-Defendants Elias Shokrian, Rosamond Ranch, LP, and Calitex, LLC and their counsel of record.

 

Willful Discovery Abuse

 

The party seeking to impose sanctions need only show the failure to obey earlier discovery orders. (Puritan Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 171 Cal.App.3d 877, 884 [interpreting former statute dealing with “refusal” to comply].) However, numerous cases hold that severe sanctions (i.e., terminating or evidentiary sanctions) for failure to comply with a court order are allowed only where the failure was willful. (R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495; Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545; Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.)

 

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination. Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.  If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted: continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse. A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992, citations omitted.)  Thus, before issuing a sanction, a trial court “should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery and, in choosing a sanction, should attempt to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.” (Id.)

 

Discovery sanctions are therefore not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information.¿(Electronic Funds Solutions, LLC v. Murphy¿(2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1183.)

Sanctions “should not give the aggrieved party more than what it is entitled to, and should not be used to punish the offending party.”  (Karlsson v. Ford Motor Co. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1202, 1217.)

 

For example, “[d]estroying evidence in response to a discovery request after litigation has commenced would surely be a misuse of discovery within the meaning of section 2023 as would such destruction in anticipation of a discovery request.” (Cedars-Sinai Med. Ctr. v. Sup.Ct. (1998) 18 Cal.4th 1, 12 [dictum]; see R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 497 [“A traditional remedy for spoliation of evidence is discovery sanctions under section 2023.”]; Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1227 [terminating sanction imposed for allowing destruction of relevant records].) A party moving for discovery sanctions based on spoliation of evidence must show the responding party had destroyed evidence to the moving party’s prejudice, i.e., a “substantial probability of damaging the moving party’s ability to establish an essential element of his claim or defense.” (Williams, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th at 1227.) The burden then shifts to the responding party to prove the moving party did not suffer prejudice from the loss of the documents.¿(Id. at 1226-27.)

 

Among other orders, Cross-Complainant contends that Cross-Defendants have failed to comply with this Court’s August 27, 2024, discovery order compelling production of documents responsive to Cross-Complainants’ propounded discovery. Cross-Complainant asserts that Cross-Defendants: a) failed to produce documents maintained by Shokrian and Sarra, as well as custodian James Irving; b) did not re-produce the Boisse and leasing@calitexllc.com mailboxes with metadata intact; and c) spoliated evidence, to wit, the emails maintained by Shokrian, Sarra, and presumably Irving and approximately 25% of the metadata for emails maintained in the Boisse and Leasing@Calitexllc.com.

 

Cross-Complainants demonstrate that Cross-Defendants willfully failed to obey a number of discovery orders. On December 9, 2022, January 6, 2023, December 1, 2023, and August 8, 2024, the Court granted certain motions to compel further discovery responses and production from Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants. At these hearings, the Court repeatedly noted that Cross-Defendants provided evasive discovery responses, and intentionally withheld documents. Of note, one counsel for Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants, Mr. Evans, filed a declaration in opposition to the December 9, 2023, hearing, attempting to avoid sanctions on himself and Cross-Defendant Sarra. Mr. Evans explained that Mr. Shokrian made the decisions as to what information or documents to provide in discovery served on October 24, 2022, and that certain limitations were placed by Mr. Shokrian which prevented counsel for continuing to represent Plaintiff/Cross-Defendants in the litigation. (Knox Decl., Ex. E.) In light of the pattern of discovery abuse detailed on the record, Mr. Evans’s claim rings true.

 

Most relevant here, on August 8, 2024, the Court ordered Cross-Defendant Rosamond Ranch LP to:

a. re-produce the 80,000 plus page production of documents electronically in a means suitable for use on an e-discovery platform (e.g., Relativity, DISCO, etc.);

b. include RFPs 121-129 as part of the scope of review in order to produce or identify in

any prior production documents that are responsive to set four RPDs;

c. produce documents responsive to the set five RPDs, subject to a Stipulated Protective Order; and

d. produce “the Rosamond Ranch, L.P. partnership agreement in response to RFP No. 119,” among the Set Four RPDs.

 

In a court-ordered report concerning the discovery, Cross-Complainant explained that on August 7 and 21, 2024, Cross-Defendants provided a folder containing .pdf images and several native files, but did not produce data/load, or text files. On August 23, 2024, they provided an export of 6,155 documents from their ESI platform in their native format (e.g., .msg), but still had not provided a data/load file. Cross-Defendants had provided a data export to two Excel sheets summarizing certain metadata regarding the files produced. The only documents reflected among the Excel sheets were for Derek@Calitexllc.com or “Leasing” email addresses used by Mr. Boisse. Apparently, Cross-Defendants had only searched these two email addresses. The excel sheet reflected that Cross-Defendants did not search the mailboxes of Shokrian, Sarra, or Konuk.  Further, approximately 25% of the documents were missing attendant metadata, and some documents’ metadata appeared manipulated.

 

At the August 27, 2024, conference concerning these discovery issues, the Court ordered “counsel for cross-defendant to file further productions of documents by Friday, August 30, 2024 [and] to file a declaration summarizing the scope of the further production by September 4, 2024.” In compliance with this order, Cross-Defendants provided the declaration of their e-discovery vendor and computer technician. (Martinez Decl.; Tashbook Decl.) They explained that there were delays in producing the .PST files because of the large file-sizes, and that they were using a slower exporting process to obtain usable .PST files for the custodians’ emails, including for Shokrian, Sarra, Boisse, Irving, and the leasing address. They further explained that production was ongoing and would be completed imminently.

 

            In its court-ordered supplemental brief, Cross-Complainant explains that on the day of the discovery cut-off, September 9, 2024, Cross-Defendants finally produced over 1.7 million pages of responsive documents that had previously not been produced. Cross-Complainant notes that nearly all of these purportedly responsive documents were previously withheld until literally the last day of discovery. Further, Cross-Defendant’s technician, Tashbook, stated that “all custodians’” mailboxes were exported for this case some years ago, but on a smaller scale. (Tasbhook Decl., ¶ 10.) Cross-Complainant points out that this supports the contention that the previously un-produced mailboxes were exported, yet intentionally not produced.

 

Cross-Complainant asserts that Cross-Defendants have improperly over produced the entirety of their email boxes instead of only responsive documents. The Court concurs that Cross-Defendants’ excessive production problematic, though not independently worthy of sanctions. Generally, the Court would not sanction responding parties for overinclusion in their production. Naturally, a responding party should include all potentially responsive documents, fairly erring on the side of overinclusion. However, producing the entirety of the custodians’ mailboxes, including emails completely unrelated to the underlying events, would be designed to frustrate the purposes of discovery. Further, the history of repeated “document dump” style production evinces Cross-Defendants’ intent to overburden Cross-Complainant with the discovery process.

 

Cross-Complainant notes that, despite this overinclusion, Cross-Defendants have also failed to produce all responsive and relevant emails in the latest production. For instance, Cross-Complainant notes that Cross-Defendants confirmed that the oldest email they have for Mr. Konuk is dated December 30, 2020. However, Mr. Konuk started working for Calitex in January 2020 (and its affiliate Califco in January 2019). (Knox Decl., ¶ 27; Ex. U.) Likewise, Cross-Defendants only produced emails from Mr. Shokrian starting from February 22, 2019. (Rep. Knox Decl., ¶ 11.) Yet, in earlier productions, Cross-Defendants provided emails to or from Shokrian dating as far back as late 2017. (Supp. Knox Decl., ¶9, Ex. Y.) Curiously, emails from 2017-2018 would be the most relevant to this action, as this is when the underlying negotiations occurred leading up to the January 2018 execution of the Master Lease and various sub-leases. Earlier emails may bear directly on Defendant’s defenses or Cross-Complainant’s fraud claims. Cross-Complainant thus demonstrates that there are likely further responsive documents which have been either been deleted or are being withheld.

 

Cross-Complainant observes that this repeated discovery abuse has delayed, and potentially out-right prevented, discovery on several issues: 1) the measure of Plaintiff’s claimed damages (RPD Nos. 1-6, 15-30, 105-109 to Rosamond); 2) Plaintiff’s mitigation of damages (RPD Nos. 1-6, 64, 65, 68); 3) Plaintiff’s breach of the Lease by withholding consent to sublease (RPD Nos. 64, 65); 4) the fraudulent/false nature of representations made to Cross-Complainants (RPD Nos. 15-30); 5) Cross-Defendants’ knowledge of the contractual relationship between SLCH and Promontory, WKM, and MM (RPD Nos. 9-14, 36 – 39, 43-47, 70-72); and 6) Cross-Defendants’ efforts to interfere with those contractual relationships (RPD Nos. 17-24). This delay precluded Cross-Complainants from proceeding with deposition discovery until after the discovery cutoff. (Knox Decl., ¶ 12.) On the literal eve of the discovery cut-off, Cross-Defendants disclosed dozens of third-party witnesses with potentially relevant information to the mitigation defense. For instance, the documents identify numerous prospective replacement tenants that were interested in leasing the property after Cross-Complainants surrendered the premises in September 2020. (Supp. Knox Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Cross-Complainants thus show that there is a “pattern of discovery abuse” which has led to the loss of potentially relevant evidence which might justify terminating sanctions. (Karlsson v. Ford Motor Co. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1202, 1215.) Without further relief, Cross-Defendants’ abuse has deprived Cross-Complainants of a fair opportunity to explore fully any leads obtained from discovery that should have been produced on Defendants/Cross-Complainant defenses and claims. Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff/Cross-Defendants to justify their delays and failures to obey the above discussed orders.

 

Cross-Defendants flatly deny any intent to thwart discovery, intentionally delay, or prejudice anyone’s claims or defenses. (See Boisse Decl., Martinez Decl., Shokrian Decl., and Gomez Decl.) Cross-Defendants now claim that they are being unfairly targeted over discovery mistakes for which they had no knowledge. They claim that until August 2024, Rosamond and Calitex were unaware they had an obligation to produce emails from custodians other than the Derek Boisse .PST files and those from the leasing@calitexllc.com address. (Boisse Decl., ¶¶ 6-7; Shokrian Decl., ¶¶ 5, 10.) They claim they never received instructions from an attorney to search and produce emails from any other custodians on the email services housed in our offices. (Id.) Further, Shokrian disclaims ever having instructed anyone to withhold emails or otherwise obstruct the production of documents in this case. (Shokrian Decl., ¶¶ 5, 10.) He claims that Mr. Evans’s declaration is simply false and an unauthorized disclosure of confidential attorney-client communications. (Id.) They claim that, on the contrary, Mr. Evans instructed Plaintiff/Cross-Defendants on how to respond to discovery and he never once mentioned that the original production of the emails were non-compliant and must be redone. (Shokrian Decl., ¶¶ 9-10; Boisse Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.) They further claim that had any prior counsel of record taken steps similar to those Rosamond and Calitex’s current counsel, they would have produced all of their responsive custodian .pst files years ago. (Shokrian Decl., ¶¶ 6-9; Boisse Decl., ¶¶ 7-8.) They also argue that this is a Code of Civil Procedure section 473 matter, but do not explain how this code might apply here.

 

The Court finds these self-serving denials to be unpersuasive and contradictory to other evidence. First, Mr. Shokrian attested to searching for and producing responsive documents (Rep. Knox Decl., Exs. BB, JJ, LL), but now states he was never informed of what responsive documents he needed to search for or produce.  Mr. Shorkian’s denials are not only contradicted by the evidence previously submitted by Mr. Evans, but also by common sense.  The Court has sanctioned Cross-Defendants over $20,000, yet they would have the Court believe that they never addressed the reason behind those sanctions with their attorney(s). Moreover, the opposition does not attempt to address many of Cross-Complainants’ contentions. Cross-Defendants do not clarify the most important part of this motion: have they produced all responsive documents? Cross-Defendants flatly fail to explain the missing years’ worth of emails from Shokrian and Konuk’s mailboxes. (Knox Decl., Ex. Y.) They do not explain why these emails are missing; they simply conclude that Konuk’s pre-December 30, 2020, emails aren’t there. (Martinez Decl., ¶ 20.)

 

Lesser sanctions have not apparently curbed Plaintiff/Cross-Defendants’ discovery abuse. The record shows that over $20,000 in sanctions did not deter them from further discovery misconduct. Thus, non-monetary sanctions are warranted.

 

Terminating Sanctions

 

To avoid terminating sanctions, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant should be prepared to explain:

 

1) Why Cross-Defendants no longer have access to Shokrian’s pre-February 2019 emails?;

 

2) Why Cross-Defendants no longer have access to Konuk’s pre-December 2020 emails?;

 

3) Why Cross-Defendants were under the impression that they only had to produce emails from custodians other than the Derek Boisse .PST files and those from the leasing@calitexllc.com address?; and

 

4) Why did Cross-Defendants produce their entire email boxes without any apparent attempt to cull unresponsive documents?

 

If Cross-Defendants cannot provide a satisfactory answer to these questions, then the Court is inclined to grant terminating sanctions in light of manifest and willful discovery misconduct. Terminating sanctions against Plaintiff’s operative complaint against Defendants is justified because the discovery abuse prevented discovery on their mitigation of damages defense. Terminating sanctions against Cross-Defendants’ answer (excluding Sarra) to the Cross-Complaint is likewise justified because the discovery abuse prevented direct discovery on Cross-Complainant’s claim of fraud.

 

 

Other Sanctions

 

If Cross-Defendants show that the above discovery abuses were not willful, but merely negligent, and that document production is complete, then the Court would be inclined to award monetary sanctions and consider further non-monetary sanctions and/or relief. For example, if the case is to continue, then the Court must mitigate the prejudice caused by the delays by continuing trial and re-opening discovery to allow for completion of depositions. The Court will confer with counsel on estimations for completion of discovery.

 

In any event, the Court is inclined to issue monetary sanctions in the noticed amount of $50,235.00 against Cross-Defendants Elias Shokrian, Rosamond Ranch, LP, and Calitex, LLC, with 50% of the sanction ($25,117.50) to be jointly and severally payable against counsel of record, Charles Gomez of Gomez Law APC.

 

The Court currently is not inclined to impose issue or evidentiary sanctions, as no such sanction was noticed, and no separate statement was provided. (CRC Rule 3.1345(a)(7).) However, depending on what occurs at the hearing, the Court may set an OSC re: imposition of non-monetary sanctions and request supplemental briefing on a specific issue or evidentiary sanction. Such sanctions may include:

 

1. Plaintiff can make no showing regarding damages sustained as a result of the alleged breach of contract;

2. Plaintiff failed to take reasonable steps to mitigate its damages, if any, from the alleged breach of contract;

3. Plaintiff breached the Lease with SLCH by unreasonably failing to agree to a replacement sublessee for Promontory;

4. Cross-Defendants were aware of the subleases between SLCH, on the one hand, and Promontory, WKM, and MM on the other hand;

5. Cross-Defendants took actions intended to, and which did, interfere with those subleases;

6. Cross-Defendants represented to Cross-Complainants that if Promontory ceased payment on its sublease with OKV for the shared 3051 Property, then Rosamond would enter into a new direct lease at the same rate and for the same terms as the sublease (the “Misrepresentation”);

7. At the time they made the Misrepresentation, they knew it to be false, and intended Defendants and Cross-Complaints to rely on the same; and

8. Cross-Complaints reasonably relied on the Misrepresentations.

 


Case Number: 23SMCVP00421    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: M

23SMCVP00421:  Prang v. Assessment Appeals Board:

 

 

The Assessor argues that the Board had authority to reconsider its decision regarding the valuation of the property, or that this Court has such jurisdiction and should order reconsideration. Specifically, the First Amended Petition (“FAP”) requests that the Court direct the Los Angeles County Assessment Appeals Board No. 6 (the “Board”) to reverse its finding that Petitioner was bound by, and the Board was without authority to, reconsider Petitioner’s value recommendation of $3,774,000 ,and to schedule a hearing on appeal application number 2012-006911 to determine the base year value of the Property as of the April 20, 2007 transfer date as described below. (FAP at 1.) According to the FAP, the value recommendation was made by a former member of Petitioner’s staff who is now a criminal defendant and was made corruptly and fraudulently in a scheme to lower assessed values of property in exchange for payments. The Petition affects the property tax valuation of Real Parties-in-Interests’ real property located at 416 North Rockingham Avenue, Los Angeles CA 90049 also known as Assessor Identification Number 4406-006-006 (the “Property”).

 

There are two essential requirements to the issuance of an ordinary writ of mandate under Code of Civil Procedure section 1085: (1) a clear, present and ministerial duty on the part of the respondent, and (2) a clear, present and beneficial right on the part of the petitioner to the performance of that duty.  (California Ass’n for Health Services at Home v. Department of Health Services (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 696, 704.) “An action in ordinary mandamus is proper where … the claim is that an agency has failed to act as required by law.” (Id. at 705.) “Normally, mandate will not lie to control a public agency's discretion, that is to say, force the exercise of discretion in a particular manner. However, it will lie to correct abuses of discretion. In determining whether a public agency has abused its discretion, the court may not substitute its judgment for that of the agency, and if reasonable minds may disagree as to the wisdom of the agency's action, its determination must be upheld. A court must ask whether the public agency's action was arbitrary, capricious, or entirely lacking in evidentiary support, or whether the agency failed to follow the procedure and give the notices the law requires.”  (County of Los Angeles v. City of Los Angeles (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 643, 654.)   

 

The court independently reviews legal questions, such as questions of statutory construction. (City of Alhambra v. County of Los Angeles (2012) 55 Cal.4th 707, 718.) To the extent “purely legal issues involve the interpretation of a statute an administrative agency is responsible for enforcing, [the court] exercise[s] [its] independent judgment, ‘taking into account and respecting the agency's interpretation of its meaning.’”  (Housing Partners I, Inc. v. Duncan (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 1335, 1343; see also Yamaha Corp. of America v. State Bd. Of Equalization (1998) 19 Cal.4th 1, 11.) 

 

An agency is presumed to have regularly performed its official duties.  (Evid. Code § 664.)  Generally, the petitioner “bears the burden of proof in a mandate proceeding brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 1085.”  (California Correctional Peace Officers Assn. v. State Personnel Bd. (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1133, 1154.)  

 

Petitioner argues that the “trier of fact” has the inherent “equitable” authority to vacate the Board’s decision. (See Rev. & Tax. Code, § 1605.5.) Petitioner cites no authorities which illuminate this equitable authority for the Board or the Court. Petitioner cites section 1605.5, which provides in relevant part that “The county board shall hear applications for a reduction in an assessment in cases in which the issue is whether or not property has been subject to a change in ownership… [and] The county board shall hear and decide issues with respect to penalties assessed under Section 463, 482, or 504 where those issues arise in connection with an application timely filed under Section 1603 or 1605…” (Emphasis added.) The section does not provide any ministerial duty which could support a section 1085 writ. Further, Petitioner’s cited duties (equitable and fiduciary) simply do not apply to the petition at hand. The cases only refer to court judgments, not administrative hearings. Petitioner has put forth insufficient authority that an “equitable” authority creates a “clear, present and ministerial duty” on the part of the Board.

 

The only question of law posed by Petitioner regards the Board’s decision not to disturb the valuation of the property at $3.7 million. Independently reviewing the relevant legal interpretations, the court also sees no abuse of discretion considering that Rev. & Tax. Code section 51.5 limited the assessor’s authority to correct the valuation here. Depending on the nature of the error, section 51.5 establishes time limits on an assessor's authority to correct a base year value. (William Jefferson & Co. v. Orange Cnty. Assessment Appeals Bd. No. 2, (2014) 228 Cal. App. 4th 1, 10.)   The relevant section states:

 

“(a) Notwithstanding any other provision of the law, any error or omission in the determination of a base year value pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (a) of Section 110.1, including the failure to establish that base year value, which does not involve the exercise of an assessor's judgment as to value, shall be corrected in any assessment year in which the error or omission is discovered.

(b) An error or an omission described in subdivision (a) which involves the exercise of an assessor's judgment as to value may be corrected only if it is placed on the current roll or roll being prepared, or is otherwise corrected, within four years after July 1 of the assessment year for which the base year value was first established.” (Emphasis added.)

 

This section allows an assessor to correct any base-year value error or omission within 4 years after enrolling an assessment so long as that error did not “involve the exercise of an assessor’s judgment as to value.” (Id.)

 

The court in William Jefferson explains:

 

Judgmental errors subject to the four-year time limit typically involve a claim the assessor's determination of the base year value failed to reflect the property's fair market value. [Citation.] Nonjudgmental errors subject to correction at any time include (1) “an assessor's erroneous determination that a change of ownership occurred” [Citation.]; (2) “the assessor's failure to set a new base year value upon a change of ownership” [Citation.]; (3) “defects of a mechanical, mathematical, or clerical nature, not involving judgment as to value, where it can be shown from papers in the assessor's office or other evidence that the defect resulted in a base year value that was not intended by the assessor at the time it was determined” [Citation.] and (4) “errors or omissions that result from the taxpayer's fraud, concealment, misrepresentation, or failure to comply with legal requirements for furnishing information” [Citation.]”

(William Jefferson & Co., Inc. v. Assessment Appeals Board (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1, 10, emphasis added.)

 

The question is whether the assessor's erroneous determination would include the pled circumstances. The term erroneous generally connotes something in error, mistaken, or misled. The Assessor argues that a correction of an erroneous determination that the Property had changed ownership did not involve an exercise of the assessor ‘s judgment about value. However, the challenged decisions by the Assessor were not that the Assessor mistakenly set a base year value, but that the Assessor improperly valued the Property due to extrinsic fraud. The Board found, as matters of fact, that it was “indisputable” that the as of March 17, 2010, the November 2007 transfer was viewed by both parties as a 100 percent re-assessable change in ownership, the parties agreed that the $3.774 million base year value and made such representations to the board through a voluntary stipulation valid, the board duly equalized the base year value of the subject property at $3.77 million based on evidence presented by both parties, and that the stipulation was still binding despite any evidence of fraud or the 2012 the assessor’s reconsideration and redetermination of its 2007/2008 determination of the November 2007 transaction was a brief assessable change in ownership event. The Assessor’s initial decision to view the 2007 transfer was a re-assessable change in ownership certainly involving an issue of judgment. Further, the Court cannot view the Assessor’s 2012 redetermination as a failure to set a new base year value upon a change of ownership. Such determinations necessarily “involved” the valuations of the property. Indeed, the Board determined that it was without authority to reconsider Petitioner’s value recommendation of $3,774,000, which was the ultimate question posed below.

 

Thus, the petition is denied.
