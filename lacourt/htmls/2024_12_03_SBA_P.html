DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV16419    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: P

Plaintiff’s Motion to Tax Costs

 

Moving Party: Defendant Titan Wheel Corporation            

Responding Party: Plaintiffs Pedro Chavarin and Maria Chavarin; Plaintiffs Luz Santoyo et al.

 

RULING

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply. Plaintiffs’ motions to tax costs are GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.


PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On April 30, 2021, Plaintiffs Pedro Chavarin et al. filed a complaint against Taylor Machine Works Inc. (“Taylor”); Cal-Lift, Inc. (“Cal-Lift”); Titan International Inc. (“Titan”); OTR Wheel Engineering Inc. (“OTR”); Rimex USA; Global Mining Products, Inc.; Pacific Maritime Association (“PMA”); American President Lines (“APL”); CMA/CGM; EQT Infrastructure III (“EQT”); P5 Infrastructure LLC (“P5”); and Does 1-100 alleging causes of action for strict products liability; negligence; and loss of consortium.

 

On May 11, 2021, Plaintiffs Luz Santoyo Castillo et al. filed a complaint against Taylor; Cal-Lift; Titan; OTR; Bridgestone America, Inc.; PMA; CMA/CGM; EQT; P5; Crane Certification Services, Inc.; Parkhouse Tire Inc.; and DOES 1-100 alleging causes of action for strict products liability; negligence – wrongful death; negligence – survival action; survival action; and loss of consortium.

 

On April 2, 2024 and April 4, 2024, Titan filed motions for summary judgment against Chavarin et al. and Santoyo et al. On August 26, 2024, the court ruled in Titan’s favor for both motions for summary judgment.

 

On October 21, 2024, Chavarin et al. and Santoyo et al. filed a notice of appeal.

 

On October 28, 2024, the Chavarin Plaintiffs filed a motion to tax costs. Titan filed an opposition on November 15, 2024. The Chavarin Plaintiffs filed a reply on November 21, 2024.

 

On October 30, 2024, the Santoyo Plaintiffs filed a motion to tax costs. Titan filed an opposition on November 15, 2024.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

On May 15, 2019, a shipping container top-handler’s front right wheel exploded, killing Jose Santoyo and injuring Pedro Chavarin. (Chavarin Complaint, ¶ 1.) Plaintiffs alleged that the explosion was caused by defective design and manufacturing. (Chavarin Complaint, ¶ 3.) The right wheel and its components were manufactured by Titan. (Chavarin Complaint, ¶ 4.)

 

Titan filed motions for summary judgment against the Chavarin and Santoyo Plaintiffs. Titan prevailed in both motions.

 

The Santoyo Plaintiffs, Pedro Chavarin, and Maria Chavarin (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) now seek to strike several costs from the memorandums, as they were not necessary to the litigation or duplicative charges.

           

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

“Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.  This means that the prevailing party is entitled to all of his costs unless another statute provides otherwise.  Absent such statutory authority, the court has no discretion to deny costs to the prevailing party.”  (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 128-129 (citations and internal quotations omitted); see also Code of Civ. Proc. § 1032, subd. (b).)  

 

Allowable costs under Code of Civil Procedure§1033.5 must be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation, and must be reasonable in amount. An item not specifically allowable under §1033.5(a) nor prohibited under subdivision (b) may nevertheless be recoverable in the discretion of the court if they meet the above requirements (i.e., reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount). If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-774.) Whereas, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Id.) Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion.  (Id.)  However, because the right to costs is governed strictly by statute, a court has no discretion to award costs not statutorily authorized. (Id.)

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Moving Parties’ Argument

 

Plaintiffs argue that their motions should be granted as Titan seeks double recovery. Further, Titan seeks to recover some costs which are unreasonable or unrecoverable under Code Civ. Proc. section 1033.5.

 

Opposing Party’s Argument

 

Titan argues that the Plaintiffs jointly prosecuted the case against Titan, and thus the costs should be awarded jointly and severally against Plaintiffs. Further, all of the costs Titan seeks to recover were reasonably necessary to the litigation.

 

Reply Argument

 

The Chavarin Plaintiffs argue that the filing of an appeal stays proceedings, such as the instant one. Thus, the court should not rule on this motion until the appeal concludes. Further, apportionment is appropriate because the Chavarin and Santoyo Plaintiffs have separate lawsuits, separate counsel, and separate cost bills.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Stay of Proceedings

 

A motion to tax costs filed after the entry of a final judgment is a “’matter embraced in the action and not affected by the order appealed from.’” (Hennessy v. Superior Court of California in and for City and County of San Francisco et al. (1924) 194 Cal. 368, 372.)

 

Thus, the trial court may hear a motion to tax costs even after an appeal has been filed in the underlying matter.

 

B.  Apportionment of Fees

 

Plaintiffs object to many of the requested costs on the grounds that Titan will receive double recovery, as it failed to divide shared expenses between the two sets of Plaintiffs. Titan argues in opposition that it was not required to apportion fees and that it may seek costs jointly and severally from Plaintiffs as they jointly litigated the cases on identical theories of liability. In support, Titan cites to Acosta v. SI Corp., (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1370, 1373 and argues that if any Plaintiff pays more than they are liable for, they may seek contribution. Further, Titan is at risk of losing half of its incurred expenses if one set of Plaintiffs abandon their appeal. In reply, Plaintiffs note that the facts of Acosta are too dissimilar to be instructive. In Acosta, the court allowed a prevailing defendant to collect costs jointly and severally from 101 plaintiffs that were represented by the same counsel and pursued a joint theory of liability, despite the fact that their individual damages differed. (Id. at p. 1376.) Here, the Santoyo and Chavarin Plaintiffs were represented by separate counsel in separate lawsuits. Though the cases are related, they were never consolidated. Further, Titan’s argument that it might lose one-half of its costs is merely hypothetical and should only be addressed after the conclusion of both appeals.

 

The court agrees with Plaintiffs. Though these cases were similar, the Plaintiffs retained separate counsel and did not jointly prosecute the actions. Thus, apportionment of costs among the Plaintiffs is proper.

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motions to tax any duplicative costs by 50%, including deposition fees, travel and lodging expenses, court reporter fees, and interpreter fees.

 

C.  Item 2 – Jury Fees

 

Plaintiffs seek to strike $150.00 from the memorandum of costs, as the case never went to a jury trial. Further, Titan can seek reimbursement for the jury fees paid, and it would be unreasonable to allow Titan to recover the fees from Plaintiffs. Titan argues that it would not be entitled to a refund, so it must be allowed to recover the fee from Plaintiffs.

 

The court has held that a non-refundable jury fee is a recoverable expense under Code Civ. Proc. section 1033.5, subd. (a)(1), even when the case ends in summary judgment. (Naser v. Lakeridge Athletic Club (2014) 277 Cal.App.4th 571, 576.)

 

Thus, the court DENIES Plaintiffs’ motion to tax the jury fees.

 

D.  Item 4 – Deposition Costs

 

1.  Deposition of Michael Anthony Podue

 

Plaintiffs seek to strike the cost of this deposition, as it was taken after summary judgment was granted and not necessary to the litigation. Titan does not dispute this.

 

Accordingly, the court finds that Podue’s deposition was not necessary to the litigation and GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to tax both memorandums of costs by $1,265.55.

 

2.  Depositions of Mark Jurisic, Curtis Shaw, Duane Brazil, Mathew Hillyer, Jeffrey Brown Vol.

     1-2, Dante Cupello, Matthew Dickenson, Pedro Chavarin Vol.1-2, Mark Rankin, Leonard

     Hensel, Lee Hamre, Thomas Vadnais, Jeffrey Olsen, and Pedro Ramos

 

Plaintiffs seek to tax these costs as Titan seeks double recovery. Plaintiffs argue that the total costs of the depositions, $32,950.06, should be split evenly between the sets of Plaintiffs. Thus, each memorandum of costs should be taxed by $16,475.03 to prevent Titan from obtaining a double recovery.

 

The court agrees and GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motions to tax each memorandum by $16,475.03.

 

E.  Item 13 – Interpreter Fees

 

Titan requests $1,410.00 in interpreter fees. Plaintiffs seek to tax half of the requested amount, as there is no basis for awarding the costs jointly and severally against Plaintiff.

 

As discussed above, the court finds that the Plaintiffs did not jointly prosecute the case and GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to tax this cost by $705.00 in each memorandum of costs.

 

F.  Item 16 – Other Costs

 

1.  Court Reporter Fees

 

Plaintiffs seek to strike this cost entirely as the court did not order a court reporter. Thus, the fee was not necessary to the litigation. Titan argues in opposition that a record of the proceeding was necessary in the event that Plaintiffs filed an appeal, as they have done.

 

Court reporter fees are a recoverable expense under Code Civ. Proc. section 1033.5. Thus, the court DENIES Plaintiffs’ motion to strike the court reporter fee. Titan is entitled to recover $1,745.00 for the court report fees.

 

G.  Miscellaneous

 

1.  Travel and Lodging Costs

 

Plaintiffs seek to strike Titan’s travel costs incurred in connection with the depositions of Thomas Vadnais and Mark Rankin, as remote depositions were available. Thus, Titan’s counsel was not required to travel for these depositions and should not be able to recover these costs. Titan argues in opposition that its witnesses were not required to attend a remote deposition without the physical presence of their counsel. In support, Titan cites to Segal v. Asics America Corporation (2020) 264 Cal.Rptr.3d 161, which held that a trial court did not abuse its discretion by refusing to tax a defendant’s travel expenses because the deposition involved complex issues. Here, the court finds that complexity of the issues necessitated counsel’s physical presence to prepare Titan’s witnesses.

 

Code Civ. Proc. section 1033.5 provides that travel expenses to attend depositions are recoverable. The statute does not impose restrictions based on the availability of remote depositions.


Accordingly, the court DENIES Plaintiffs’ motions to strike travel and lodging costs. Titan is entitled to recover $2,159.24 for such costs.

 

H.  Undisputed Fees

 

1.  Investigative Expenses

 

Plaintiffs object to Titan’s investigative expenses in the amount of $1,527.65 as such fees are expressly unrecoverable. Titan concedes that they may be taxed.

 

Thus, the court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to tax each memorandum of costs by $1,527.65

 

2.  Photocopying Charges

 

Plaintiffs object to these costs as they are expressly unrecoverable under Code Civ. Proc. section 1033.5, subd. (b)(3). Titan concedes that they may be taxed.

 

Thus, the court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to tax each memorandum of costs by $29.71.

 

3.  Service of Process

 

The Chavarin Plaintiffs object to $413.66 in subpoena fees, as two of the invoices are for records that are unrelated to their case. The Santoyo Plaintiffs object to $413.66 in subpoena fees on the same grounds. Titan concedes that $413.66 should be stricken from each memorandum.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motions to tax each memorandum by $413.66.

 

4.  Attorneys’ Meals

 

Plaintiffs object to Titan’s costs for attorney meal expenses in the amount of $345.63 as meals are not expressly allowed under Code Civ. Proc. section 1033.5. Titan concedes that these expenses may be taxed.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motions to tax each memorandum by $345.63.

 

5.  Miscellaneous Conference Room

 

Plaintiffs object to Titan’s conference room fee, totaling $675.00, incurred during the deposition of Thomas H. Vadnais as the conference room was not necessary to the litigation, only convenient or beneficial. Titan concedes that this expense may be taxed.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to tax each memorandum by $675.00.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motions are GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

“Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.  This means that the prevailing party is entitled to all of his costs unless another statute provides otherwise.  Absent such statutory authority, the court has no discretion to deny costs to the prevailing party.”  (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 128-129 (citations and internal quotations omitted); see also Code of Civ. Proc. § 1032, subd. (b).)  

 

Allowable costs under Code of Civil Procedure§1033.5 must be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation, and must be reasonable in amount. An item not specifically allowable under §1033.5(a) nor prohibited under subdivision (b) may nevertheless be recoverable in the discretion of the court if they meet the above requirements (i.e., reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount). If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. Case Number: 24TRCV02378    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Strike Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. Section 425.16

 

Moving Parties: Defendants Minh Tri Van Nguyen et al.     

Responding Party: Plaintiff Janna Pharmacy

 

RULING

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply. Defendants’ special motion to strike is DENIED.


PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On July 17, 2024, Plaintiff Janna Pharmacy d/b/a Apotheco (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Minh Tri Van Nguyen (“Nguyen”); Envy Pharmacy (“Envy”); MTN Pharmacists (“MTN”); Tien Minh Vo (“Vo”); and DOES 1-100, inclusive (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Conversion; (4) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (6) Unfair Competition; and (7) Declaratory Relief.

 

On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) against the same Defendants, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Conversion; (4) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (6) Violations of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1030(A); (7) Violations of the Unauthorized Access to Computers Act, Cal. Penal Code § 502(C); (8) Unfair Competition; and (9) Declaratory Relief.

 

On October 31, 2024, Defendants filed a special motion to strike the FAC. Plaintiff filed an opposition on November 18, 2024. Defendants filed a reply on November 22, 2024.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

On July 17, 2023, Nguyen was promoted to Plaintiff’s pharmacist-in-charge. (FAC, ¶ 16.) By statute, Nguyen was responsible for ensuring the pharmacy’s compliance with state and federal laws. (FAC, ¶ 17.) Among such laws were the federal Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (“HIPAA”) and California’s Confidentiality of Medical Information Act (“CMIA”). (FAC, ¶ 19.) Likewise, Nguyen’s employment letter required him to ensure Plaintiff’s compliance with applicable laws and “secure and appropriate handling of Apotheco information.” (FAC, ¶ 20.)

 

In April 2024, Nguyen resigned from his position to pursue other opportunities. (FAC, ¶ 5.) Plaintiff alleges that Nguyen intended to use stolen data to solicit Plaintiff’s patients and set up a competing pharmacy. (FAC, ¶ 5.)

 

On October 27, 2023, Nguyen exported 74,000 lines of patient data (the “Data”), including payment information, phone numbers, dates of birth, and prescriptions, to his personal email account without permission. (FAC, ¶ 21.) The information was contained into two unencrypted Excel sheets labeled “Fax Blast1” and “Fax Blast2.” (FAC, ¶ 21.)

 

On October 12, 2023, Nguyen also emailed himself a PDF report (“Report”) from Plaintiff’s delivery vendor, containing the names, addresses, and prescription information of about 2,000 of Plaintiff’s patients. (FAC, ¶ 22.)

 

Shortly before he resigned, Nguyen forwarded himself the names and birthdays of six patients who were receiving a lucrative branded medication using the blind copy (“bcc”) feature. (FAC, ¶ 23.)

 

Envy received its facility license on April 15, 2024. (FAC, ¶ 28.) Nguyen resigned from Plaintiff on April 26, 2024. (FAC, ¶ 28.)

 

On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff discovered the data breach and subsequently sent cease and desist letters to Nguyen and Envy. (FAC, ¶¶ 31-32.) In the following weeks, Defendants refused to return or destroy the stolen Data. (FAC, ¶ 36.)

 

Since the data breach, Plaintiff has sent breach notification letters to over 26,000 patients. (FAC, ¶ 41.)

 

Defendants now seek to strike all nine causes of action, or in the alternative, paragraphs 36-38.

 

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

“Litigation of an anti-SLAPP motion involves a two-step process. First, the moving defendant bears the burden of establishing that the challenged allegations or claims arise from protected activity in which the defendant has engaged.  Second, for each claim that does arise from protected activity, the plaintiff must show the claim has “at least ‘minimal merit.’  If the plaintiff cannot make this showing, the court will strike the claim.” (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1009.) 

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of three documents: (1) Envy’s license found on the California Board of Pharmacy’s website; (2) the U.S. Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services’ National Provider Identifier Registry; and (3) MTN’s statement of information found on the California Secretary of State’s website.

 

Pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (c), the court may take judicial notice of the acts of the legislative, executive, or judicial departments of any state. Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice are GRANTED.


Evidentiary Objections

 

Defendants’ Evidentiary Objections No. 5, 16, and 17 are SUSTAINED. The remaining objections are OVERRULED.

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

The gravamen of Plaintiff’s FAC relates to Defendants’ refusal to unconditionally return or destroy the Data, which is protected by the litigation privilege. Because the FAC is based on these litigation activities, the FAC is unripe. Further, Plaintiff cannot prove the elements of any cause of action. Thus, Defendants’ motion should be granted in its entirety.

 

Opposing Party’s Argument

 

Defendants’ motion should be denied because it is untimely. Further, litigation privilege does not apply because the communications between counsel, alleged in paragraphs 36-38, are not the source of Defendants’ liability. Even if Defendants’ conduct could be considered protected speech activity, Plaintiff can establish that it will prevail on the merits.

 

Reply Argument

 

The Anti-SLAPP motion was timely because the FAC alleged new activity. Plaintiff added paragraphs 36-38 to withstand demurrer and supply elements of several causes of action, which means that such causes of action are subject to a special motion to strike. Moreover, Plaintiff simply cannot prevail on the merits.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

1.  Timeliness of the Motion

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ motion is procedurally defective as it was filed more than sixty days after the earliest complaint that contains the cause of action. Defendants contend that the motion is timely, as their motion primarily hinges on privileged communications between counsel, and the first complaint did not contain any such allegations. Thus, the motion could not have been brought any sooner.

 

The court agrees that the motion is timely. Plaintiff amended its original complaint to add paragraphs 36-38, which describe communications between the parties’ counsel. Such communications implicate the litigation privilege and were absent in the original complaint. Thus, Defendants could not properly bring this motion until the FAC was filed.

 

2.  First Prong: Protected Activity

 

On the first prong, “courts are to consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability. The defendant’s burden is to identify what acts each challenged claim rests on and to show how those acts are protected under a statutorily defined category of protected activity.” (Bonni, 11 Cal.5th at 1009.) “A defendant need only make a prima facie showing at this stage.” (Ojjeh v. Brown (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 1027, 1035–1036.) 

 

Courts may “determine whether particular acts alleged within the cause of action supply the elements of a claim or instead are incidental background. This approach is consistent with Baral, which reaffirmed that ‘[a]ssertions that are ‘merely incidental’ or ‘collateral’ are not subject to section 425.16. [Citations.] Allegations of protected activity that merely provide context, without supporting a claim for recovery, cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute.’” (Bonni, supra, at p. 1012, quoting Baral, supra, 1 Cal.5th at p. 394, other citations omitted.) 

 

Defendants argue that each of Plaintiff’s nine causes of action rely on the allegations contained in paragraphs 36-38, which describe Defendants’ refusal to sign a declaration, defense counsel’s refusal to unconditionally destroy or return the Data and Report, and defense counsel’s refusal to sign an “Attorney’s Eyes Only” designation. Defendants contend that such communications trigger subdivisions (e)(1) and (e)(2) of Code Civ. Proc. section 425.16, which protect “any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law” and “any written or oral statement made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law,” respectively.

 

While Defendants correctly assert that communications between counsel in anticipation of litigation are protected activity under the statute, the court must determine whether paragraphs 36-38 actually supply the elements of each claim or are merely incidental to each cause of action. If the allegations are incidental or merely supply context for the cause of action, they cannot be stricken.

 

A.  First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

 

Plaintiff incorporates paragraphs 36-38 to this cause of action by reference. However, the conduct underlying this claim is Nguyen’s failure to abide by Plaintiff’s information security policies and comply with state and federal privacy laws, in violation of his employment letter.

 

Thus, the communications between counsel, though incorporated by reference, are not used to supply any element of a breach of contract cause of action. Accordingly, Defendants have failed to establish that this cause of action is based on protected activity, and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to establish the second prong.

 

B.  Second Cause of Action for Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

 

The elements of a claim for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage include “(1) an economic relationship between the plaintiff and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional or negligent acts on the part of the defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the plaintiff proximately caused by the acts of the defendant.” (Crown Imports, LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1404, citations, brackets, and quotation marks omitted.) Further, “the alleged interference must have been wrongful by some measure beyond the fact of the interference itself. For an act to be sufficiently independently wrongful, it must be unlawful, that is, it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Ibid., citation, ellipsis, and quotation marks omitted.) 

 

Similarly, Plaintiff incorporates paragraphs 36-38 by reference. However, the source of Defendants’ liability is Nguyen’s use of the Data and Report to solicit clients for the other Defendants, which interfered with Plaintiff’s economic relationships with its clients. Further, because Nguyen sent the Data and Report to an unencrypted email address, violating state and federal privacy laws, Plaintiff’s business suffered harm.

 

Thus, the communications between counsel do not supply any element of this cause of action. Accordingly, Defendants have failed to establish that this cause of action is based on protected activity, and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to establish the second prong.

 

C.  Third Cause of Action for Conversion

 

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s conversion cause of action does partially rely on allegations that “Defendants took and have refused Plaintiff’s demand for repossession of property belong to Plaintiff” and “Defendants are wrongfully holding and refusing to return Plaintiff’s Data…”

 

Thus, the allegations in paragraphs 36-38 are not merely incidental to this cause of action. Accordingly, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to establish that it can prevail on the merits.

 

D.  Fourth Cause of Action for Misappropriation of Trade Secrets

 

“Under the [California Uniform Trade Secrets Act], a prima facie claim for misappropriation of trade secrets requires the plaintiff to demonstrate: (1) the plaintiff owned a trade secret, (2) the defendant acquired, disclosed, or used the plaintiff’s trade secret through improper means, and (3) the defendant’s actions damaged the plaintiff.” (Sargent Fletcher, Inc. v. Able Corp. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1658, 1665; see also Civ. Code, § 3426.1) “‘Trade secret’ means information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process, that: [¶] (1) Derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and [¶] (2) Is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.” (Civ. Code, § 3426., subd. (d).) “‘Improper means’ includes theft, bribery, misrepresentation, breach or inducement of a breach of a duty to maintain secrecy, or espionage through electronic or other means.” (Civ. Code, § 3426.1, subd. (a).)  

 

Plaintiff alleges that it was the owner of two trade secrets: the Data and Report. Without authorization and in violation of his employment letter, Nguyen accessed the Data and Report to use the information contained therein to solicit customers for his new employers.

 

Thus, the communications between counsel were merely incidental to this cause of action, as they do not supply any of the elements. Defendants have not met their initial burden, and Plaintiff is not required to show a probability of succeeding on the merits.

 

E.  Fifth Cause of Action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.)

 

Plaintiff argues that as its PIC, Nguyen owed Plaintiff a fiduciary duty to ensure compliance with state and federal laws. Nguyen breached this obligation by emailing himself client’s personal information without permission, which necessitated the sending of data breach notifications to the 26,000 affected patients.

 

Thus, the communications between counsel are not the source of liability and are merely incidental to this cause of action. Defendants have failed to establish that this cause of action is based on protected activity, and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff.

 

F.  Sixth Cause of Action for Violations of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1030(a)

 

The statute provides that “[w]hoever… intentionally accesses a computer without authorization or exceeds authorized access, and thereby obtains… information from any protected computer… shall be punished as provided in subsection (c) of this section.” (18 U.S.C. §1030, subd. (a)(2).) “[T]he term ‘protected computer’ means a computer… which is used in or affecting interstate or foreign commerce or communication, including a computer located outside the United States that is used in a manner that affects interstate or foreign commerce or communication of the United States…” (18 U.S.C. §1030, subd. (e)(2)(B).)

 

“Any person who suffers damage or loss by reason of a violation of this section may maintain a civil action against the violator to obtain compensatory damages and injunctive relief or other equitable relief. A civil action for a violation of this section may be brought only if the conduct involves [one] of the factors set forth in subclauses (I), (II), (III), (IV), or (V) of subsection (c)(4)(A)(i). Damages for a violation involving only conduct described in subsection (c)(4)(A)(i)(I) are limited to economic damages.” (18 U.S.C. §1030, subd. (g).) 18 U.S.C. §1030(c)(4)(A)(i)(I) provides, in pertinent part, as follows: “loss to [one] or more persons during any [one]-year period… aggregating at least $5,000 in value…”

 

Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action arises from Nguyen’s unauthorized access to and removal of client’s personal information from Plaintiff’s computer systems. Paragraphs 36-38, though incorporated by reference, do not supply the elements of this claim. Rather, they provide additional context that Defendants are still in possession of the Data and Report, information that they obtained through unauthorized access to Plaintiff’s computer systems.

 

Accordingly, Defendants have failed to meet their initial burden, and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff.

 

G.  Seventh Cause of Action for Violation of the Unauthorized Access to Computers Act, Cal. Penal Code § 502(c)

 

The statute makes certain conduct involving unauthorized access to and modification of computer data a public offense. Under the statute, it is a felony to access, alter, delete, or otherwise use data accessed knowingly and without permission from a computer, computer system, or computer network, as part of a scheme to defraud, deceive, or extort. (Penal Code § 502, subds. (c)-(d).) An individual who suffers damages as a result a violation of the CDAFA may bring a civil action against the violator for compensatory damages, equitable relief, and recovery of reasonable attorney’s fees. (Penal Code § 502, subd. (e)(1)-(2).)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Nguyen accessed Plaintiff’s computer systems to obtain large volumes of patient information, which he removed from Plaintiff’s systems without permission. Consequently, Plaintiff incurred damages because it had to undertake breach notification procedures.

 

Thus, the communications between counsel are merely incidental to this cause of action. Defendants have failed to meet their initial burden, and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff.

 

H.  Eighth Cause of Action for Unfair Business Practices

 

To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), a plaintiff must establish that the defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants schemed to steal Plaintiff’s information to solicit Plaintiff’s patients and/or the prescribing physicians. To accomplish this scheme, Nguyen violated state and federal privacy laws to send himself the Data and Report, which he used to solicit clients away from Plaintiff.

 

The communications between counsel do not supply an element of this claim. Thus, Defendants have failed to meet their initial burden, and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff.

 

I.  Ninth Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief

 

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.) 

 

Plaintiff asserts that a controversy has arisen between the parties regarding whether Nguyen’s access to and use of the Data and Report were lawful. Plaintiff asserts that Nguyen did not have its permission to send the information to his personal email address, while Defendants assert that this was common practice for Plaintiff’s employees.

 

Thus, the allegations in paragraphs 36-38 do not supply the elements of this claim and are merely incidental to Plaintiff’s cause of action. Accordingly, Defendants have failed to meet their initial burden, and the burden does not shift to Plaintiff.

 

3.  Second Prong: Probability of Prevailing on the Merits

 

Once a defendant demonstrates that protected conduct is at issue, the plaintiff must show that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited. (See Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 88-89.) “Precisely because the statute (1) permits early intervention in lawsuits alleging unmeritorious causes of action that implicate free speech concerns, and (2) limits opportunity to conduct discovery, the plaintiff's burden of establishing a probability of prevailing is not high: We do not weigh credibility, nor do we evaluate the weight of the evidence. Instead, we accept as true all evidence favorable to the plaintiff and assess the defendant’s evidence only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s submission as a matter of law. Only a cause of action that lacks ‘even minimal merit’ constitutes SLAPP.” (Overstock.com, Inc. v. Gradient Analytics, Inc. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 688, 699.) 

 

The SLAPP statute’s second element--a “probability of prevailing”-- means a “reasonable probability of prevailing, not prevailing by a preponderance of the evidence. For this reason, a court must apply a “summary-judgment-like” test, accepting as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff and evaluating the defendant’s evidence only to determine whether the defendant has defeated the plaintiff’s evidence as a matter of law.”(Gerbosi v. Gaims, Weil, West & Epstein, LLP (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 435, 444.)

 

As Defendants have not met their initial burden for the first, second, or fourth through ninth causes of action, the court will only address the merits of the conversion cause of action.

 

A.  Third Cause of Action for Conversion

 

In support of this cause of action, Plaintiff offers the Declaration of Jonathan Tang (“Tang”), the Director of Operations at Apotheco Pharmacy Group, the company that acquired Janna in 2022, and Nguyen’s supervisor. Tang declares that, to his knowledge, other employees did not use their personal email addresses for work-related purposes, as it would be against Apotheco’s policies. (Tang Decl., ¶ 5.) Tang further declares that Nguyen could have requested a work laptop, which would have allowed him to access Plaintiff’s information from home, so there was no reason for him to email himself client information. (Tang Decl., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff’s counsel also declares that he ran a search of Nguyen’s Apotheco email account, and the only emails ever sent from Nguyen’s work account to his personal account containing patients’ information were the ones at issue in this lawsuit, undermining the assertion that Nguyen regularly sent himself patient files as part of his job duties. (Newborn Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

In opposition, Defendants offer the Nguyen’s Declaration, in which he states that it was common for Plaintiff’s employees to email themselves pharmacy files. (Nguyen Decl., ¶ 4.) Nguyen declares that he only used the FaxBlast files to update Plaintiff’s contacts on pricing details for a drug sold by Plaintiff. (Nguyen Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

Defendants also argue that a conversion is only created when the plaintiff makes a demand for repossession that is refused by the defendant. Defendants contend that Plaintiff assented to the Defendants’ possession of the data because it never gave Nguyen clear instructions on how to return the Data. Courts have previously held that a demand for repossession is only necessary to establish liability for a conversion if the defendant lawfully acquired possession of the property with the owner’s consent. (Flennaugh v. Heinrich (1948) 89 Cal.App.2d 214, 221.) Here, Plaintiff alleges that Nguyen’s initial possession of the Data was unlawful, as Plaintiff did not give him permission to export the Data. Thus, Plaintiff did not need to make a demand for repossession to establish a conversion. Here, the conversion cause of action would begin to accrue when Nguyen sent the data to himself without Plaintiff’s consent.

 

Thus, upon accepting as true all evidence offered by Plaintiff and weighing Defendants’ evidence only to determine if it defeats Plaintiff’s evidence as a matter of law, the court finds that Plaintiff’s evidence is sufficient to establish that it did not give Nguyen permission to export Plaintiff’s client data to his personal email account.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff has shown a probability of success on the merits.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ special motion to strike is DENIED in its entirety.

 

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.
