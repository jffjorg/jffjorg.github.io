Case Number: 21STCV41913    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 78

 

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles 

Department 78 

¿ 

ELIZABETH'S FOOD CO., INC., 

Plaintiff(s), 

vs. 

ALEJANDRO CASTRO, et al., 

Defendant(s). 

Case No.: 

21STCV41913 

Hearing Date: 

October 30, 2024 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER (1) DENYING MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT, AND (2) CONTINUING MOTION FOR CLAIM OF EXEMPTION 

 

I. MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT 

A. Background 

On November 12, 2021, plaintiff Elizabeth's Food Co., Inc. (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against defendants Alejandro Castro (“Castro”), Juan Leon, Olympic Pallets, Inc. and Does 1 through 10 alleging defendants Castro and Juan Leon took Plaintiff’s property by selling and converting its value into cash to Olympic Pallets, and taking those proceeds for themselves while they were employed by Plaintiff in its warehouse. The complaint sets forth three causes of action for (1) conversion, (2) negligence, and (3) sale and concealment of stolen property. 

Defendants Juan Leon and Olympic Pallets, Inc. have since been dismissed from the action. (Req. for Dismissal, May 23, 2022 & Aug. 25, 2022.) 

B. Relevant Procedural History 

On August 9, 2022, Plaintiff filed proof of service that the summons and complaint was served on Castro via substituted service to co-occupant “John Doe” at 9815 Madison Ave., South Gate, CA 90280 (“9815 Madison Ave.”) on August 4, 2022. (Proof of Service, Aug. 9, 2022.) 

On November 22, 2022, Plaintiff obtained entry of default against Castro. 

On July 25, 2023, default judgment was entered against Castro in the amount of $54,883.82.  

On January 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed proof of service of the Notice of Entry of Judgment on Castro at 9815 Madison Ave. 

On August 2, 2024, Castro filed the instant motion to set aside entry of default and default judgment.  

On October 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion. 

Any reply was due on or before October 23, 2024. No reply has been filed to date. 

C. Legal Standard 

CCP § 473.5, subdivision (a) provides, “When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. (CCP § 473.5, subd. (a).) 

A motion to set aside under CCP § 473.5 based on lack of proper service of the action, such that defendant had no actual notice of the action in time to defend, requires the defendant to act with diligence upon learning of judgment. (Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal. App. 4th 175, 180.) “[Citation] [“it does not require a showing that plaintiff did anything improper.... [T]he defaulting defendant simply asserts that he or she did not have actual notice]; [Citation].” (Ibid.) “Upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (c).) 

Accordingly, “ ‘a default judgment entered against a defendant who was not served with a summons in the manner prescribed by statute is void.’ ” (Ibid.) Whether the lack of jurisdiction appears on the face of the judgment roll, or is shown by extrinsic evidence for a judgment that appears valid on its face, “in either case the judgment is for all purposes a nullity—past, present and future.” (OC Interior Servs., LLC v. Nationstar Mortg., LLC (2017) 7 Cal. App. 5th 1318, 1331.) If the judgment is not void on its face, the time limitations of Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 apply. (Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 175, 180-81; Schekel v. Resnik (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th Supp. 1, 3-4 (“[t]he Rogers court held that the time limitation set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 applies by analogy to motions for relief from default judgment valid on its face but otherwise void for improper service” [citing Rogers v. Silverman (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1114, 1124.]).) 

D. Discussion 

1. Section 473.5(a) 

“An individual may be served by substitute service only after a good faith effort at personal service has first been made: the burden is on the plaintiff to show that the summons and complaint ‘cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered’ to defendants. [Citations.] Two or three attempts to personally serve a defendant at a proper place ordinarily qualifies as ‘reasonable diligence.’” (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 389.) If the summons and complaint cannot be personally delivered with reasonable diligence, then a copy may be served at the person’s “dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge of his or her office, place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box…who shall be informed of the contents thereof and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first class mail, postage prepaid, to the person to be served…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20, subd. (b).) 

The proof of service Plaintiff filed satisfies the requirements of Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20 and is valid on its face. Further, because service was effectuated by a registered process server, Plaintiff is entitled to a presumption of valid service. (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara¿(2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390; see also Evid. Code § 647.) This requires Castro to submit sufficient evidence to overcome the presumption of valid service.¿¿ 

Castro declares he moved out of 9815 Madison Ave. in October 2021 after marital disagreements at home, that he did not reside at that location on August 4, 2022, and that no males resided at 9815 Madison Ave. (Castro Decl. ¶¶ 5, 7, 8.) Outside of his declaration, Castro’s admits that he never filed a change of address, but contends he was not trying to hide because his gas bill and a DMV notice was mailed to him at 909 N. Sloan Avenue, Compton, CA 90221, attached as Exhibits 6 and 7. The Court notes that Exhibits 6 and 7 are completely illegible. Further, Castro contends that according to the affidavit of diligence, the process server was informed that he did not live there. 

Plaintiff’s counsel declares Castro did have actual notice of the lawsuit, because Plaintiff’s counsel reached out to Defendant’s counsel, who represented Castro in the separate wage and hour arbitration proceeding (JAMS Case No. 1220073726), and inquired whether Castro’s counsel would accept service on Castro’s behalf. (Park Decl. ¶ 4.) Additionally, Plaintiff proffers the deposition transcript of Castro, wherein his February 21, 2024 testimony in JAMS Case No. 1220073726 provides the following testimony: 

“Q. And where do you currently reside? 

A. My legal address is 9815 Madison Avenue, South Gate, California, 90280. 

Q. Do you have other addresses? 

A. Yes. 

Q. What other addresses do you have? 

A. Friends', family members' addresses. 

Q. Okay. So my question is, where do you currently reside? Do you reside at 9815 Madison Avenue? 

A. Yes and no. 

Q. Okay. Could you explain why it's yes or no? 

A. After the situation, I had marital issues. 

Q. Okay. So other than 9815 Madison Avenue, what's the other address where you reside? 

A. I would like to keep that personal.” 

(Park Decl. ¶ 2; Exh. 2 at 15:25 & 16:1-16.) 

Based on the conflicting evidence presented, and the inferences drawn therefrom, the Court finds that Castro has not overcome the presumption of valid service at 9815 Madison Ave. and that he lacked actual notice of the lawsuit. (Wyoming Pac. Oil Co. v. Preston (1959) 171 Cal. App. 2d 735, 741 [When conflicting evidence is presented at a hearing of a motion to set aside a judgment, the weight and sufficiency of the evidence, its construction and the inferences to be drawn therefrom is within the sound discretion of the trial court, and should not be disturbed absent a clear showing of abuse].) Therefore, the time limitations of Section 473.5 apply. The Court notes that because Plaintiff filed a proof of service demonstrating Castro was served with a copy of the entry of judgment on January 2, 2024, this Motion should have been filed no later than 180 days later, that is, July 1, 2024. (Code Civ. Proc., §473.5, subd. (a).) Castro’s request for relief under this section was filed on August 2, 2024 and therefore untimely. Further, it is not accompanied by a copy of his proposed responsive pleading as required by Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5, subdivision (b).  

As a result, the request for relief from default and default judgment on this basis is denied. For the same reason as above, Castro’s alternative request to quash service is denied. 

2. Section 473(b) 

Castro argues that if the Court determines that service was proper, that default be set aside pursuant to CCP §473(b) because of inadvertence, mistake, surprise, or excusable neglect, and that he filed the motion within a reasonable amount of time. 

CCP § 473(b) states, in pertinent part (emphasis added), “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” Unlike the mandatory relief provision pertaining to attorney mistake, the general rule is that the six-month period within which to bring a motion to vacate under the discretionary provision of § 473 runs from the date of the default and not from the judgment taken thereafter. (Rutan v. Summit Sports, Inc. (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 965, 970.) 

Here, the date of default was November 22, 2022. Thus, the Motion under this section should have been filed no later than six months later, which was May 22, 2023. The instant motion was filed on August 2, 2024. Further, Castro failed to provide an accompanying copy of the Answer or other proposed pleading to be filed. As such, the Court is unable to grant relief under this section. 

D. Conclusion 

Castro’s motion to set aside default and default judgment is DENIED. 

 

II. MOTION FOR DETERMINING CLAIM OF EXEMPTION 

  1. Background 

On January 11, 2024, Elizabeth's Food Co., Inc. (“Judgment Creditor”) filed an Application for Issuance of Writ of Execution, Possession or Sale against Alejandro Castro (“Judgment Debtor”) in the amount of $54,883.82.  

On April 29, 2024, Judgment Creditor obtained a Writ of Execution. 

Judgment Creditor provides that on May 29, 2024, Judgment Debtor filed a Claim of Exemption with the Los Angeles County Sheriff’s Department (“the Levying Officer”) claiming that the funds in the bank account, specifically the January 10, 2024 Workers’ Compensation Compromise and Release Settlement in the amount of $14,875.00, are exempt pursuant to Cal. Civ. Proc. Code §§ 704.130 and 704.160. Judgment Creditor avers that the Claim was served on its counsel on May 31, 2024 by the Leving Officer by U.S. mail. 

On June 13, 2024, Judgment Creditor filed the instant motion an order determining the claim of exemption. Judgment Creditor contends that Judgment Debtor has failed to meet his burden of tracing the exempt funds as required by Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 703.080. Judgment Creditor asserts that Judgment Debtor has failed to provide any evidence, and that his Claim of Exemption must therefore be denied. 

To date, Judgment Debtor has not submitted any brief or other documents in connection with the motion. 

  1. Legal Standard 

“Except as otherwise provided by statute, property that has been levied upon may be claimed to be exempt as provided in this article.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., §703.510.) A claimant may make a claim of exemption by following the procedure in CCP §703.520. 

CCP §703.580 provides:¿¿ 

  1. The claim of exemption and notice of opposition to the claim of exemption constitute the pleadings, subject to the power of the court to permit amendments in the interest of justice.¿ 

  1. At a hearing under this section, the exemption claimant has the burden of proof.¿ 

  1. The claim of exemption is deemed controverted by the notice of opposition to the claim of exemption and both shall be received in evidence. If no other evidence is offered, the court, if satisfied that sufficient facts are shown by the claim of exemption (including the financial statement if one is required) and the notice of opposition, may make its determination thereon. If not satisfied, the court shall order the hearing continued for the production of other evidence, oral or documentary.¿ 

  1. At the conclusion of the hearing, the court shall determine by order whether or not the property is exempt in whole or in part. Subject to Section 703.600, the order is determinative of the right of the judgment creditor to apply the property to the satisfaction of the judgment. No findings are required in a proceeding under this section.¿ 

  1. The court clerk shall promptly transmit a certified copy of the order to the levying officer. Subject to Section 703.610, the levying officer shall, in compliance with the order, release the property or apply the property to the satisfaction of the money judgment.¿ 

  1. Unless otherwise ordered by the court, if an exemption is not determined within the time provided by Section 703.570, the property claimed to be exempt shall be released.¿ 

"Money in a judgment debtor’s deposit account that is not otherwise exempt under this chapter is exempt to the extent necessary for the support of the judgment debtor and the spouse and dependents of the judgment debtor." (Code Civ. Proc., § 704.225.) "Money in the judgment debtor’s deposit account in an amount equal to or less than the minimum basic standard of adequate care for a family of four for Region 1, established by Section 11452 of the Welfare and Institutions Code and as annually adjusted by the State Department of Social Services pursuant to Section 11453 of the Welfare and Institutions Code, is exempt without making a claim." (Code Civ. Proc., § 704.220.) (This amount at the time of the Senate Bill deliberation was $1,724.)¿ 

At a hearing on a claim of exemption, “the [judgment debtor] has the burden of proof” in demonstrating that the property claimed exempt is indeed exempt. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 703.520, subd. (b), 703.580, subds. (b), (c).) In meeting this burden, the judgment debtor must establish the right by evidence or facts; an affidavit which merely follows the language of the statute and states nothing more than conclusions of law is insufficient.¿(Le Font v.¿Rankin¿(1959)¿167¿Cal.App.2d 433, 435.) 

  1. Discussion 

“Within 10 days after service of the notice of claim of exemption, a judgment creditor who opposes the claim of exemption shall file with the court a notice of opposition to the claim of exemption and a notice of motion for an order determining the claim of exemption and shall file with the levying officer a copy of the notice of opposition and a copy of the notice of motion. Upon the filing of the copies of the notice of opposition and notice of motion, the levying officer shall promptly file the claim of exemption with the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 703.550.) 

The Court does not have complete information to rule on this hearing because the claim of exemption itself is missing. The Court cannot rely solely on Judgment Creditor’s general argument that Judgment Debtor does not satisfy his burden without assessing the actual claim. The Court will continue the hearing so that the defect may be cured.¿ 

  1. Conclusion 

Based on the foregoing, the Hearing on Claim of Exemption/Third-Party Claim is CONTINUED to________________.

Case Number: 21STCV44967    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 78

 

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles

¿ 

EDDER RODRIGUEZ, 

Plaintiff(s), 

vs. 

GENERAL MOTORS LLC, et al., 

Defendant(s). 

Case No.: 

21STCV44967 

Hearing Date: 

October 30, 2024 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Edder Rodriguez (“Plaintiff”) filed initiated this action against defendant General Motors LLC (“GM”) under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. The Complaint alleges that on October 24, 2011, Plaintiff entered into a warranty contract with GM regarding a 2012 Chevrolet Sonic vehicle identification number 1G1JC6SH3C4109012 (the "Vehicle"), which was manufactured and or distributed by GM. (Compl. ¶ 9.) Defects and nonconformities to warranty manifested themselves within the applicable express warranty period, including but not limited to, the battery; the battery; the engine; among other defects and non-conformities. (Id. ¶ 14.) GM failed to promptly replace or repurchase the Vehicle. (Id. ¶ 19.) The Complaint sets forth four causes of action for (1) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(d), (2) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(b), (3) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(a)(3), and (4) breach of implied warranty of merchantability under Civil Code §§ 1791.1, 1794, and 1795.5. 

On February 23, 2024, GM filed the instant motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication against all four causes of action in the Complaint. 

On October 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. 

On October 25, 2024, GM filed a reply. 

 

II. PROCEDURAL STANDARD 

As a preliminary matter, the Notice states that GM moves for summary adjudication in the alternative. 

CRC, Rule 3.1350(b) provides, “If made in the alternative, a motion for summary adjudication may make reference to and depend on the same evidence submitted in support of the summary judgment motion. If summary adjudication is sought, whether separately or as an alternative to the motion for summary judgment, the specific cause of action, affirmative defense, claims for damages, or issues of duty must be stated specifically in the notice of motion and be repeated, verbatim, in the separate statement of undisputed material facts.” Further, subdivision (h) of Rule 3.1350 provides a sample chart of the proper format for separate statements in a motion for summary adjudication. 

Because GM’s separate statement fails to conform with CRC, Rule 3.1350, and the memorandum of points and authorities itself treats the motion solely as a motion for summary judgment, the Court will consider it as such. In other words, if there remains at least a single triable issue of material fact, the entire motion must fail. 

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (a).) “[I]f all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law,” the moving party will be entitled to summary judgment. (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (f)(2).) 

The moving party bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact, and if the party does so, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850; accord Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant...has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff...to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(c).) 

 

IV. DISCUSSION 

GM argues that since all four of Plaintiff’s causes of action require proof that Plaintiff is a “buyer” as defined by the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“SBA”), and because Plaintiff has no evidence that Plaintiff therefore lacks standing. GM asserts lack of standing on three noticed grounds: (1) no evidence that Plaintiff bought the Vehicle, (2) no evidence that Plaintiff paid for the Vehicle, and (3) no evidence that the Vehicle was worth less due to any alleged defect. 

The SBA should generally be construed in a manner that will bring its benefits into action (Murillo v. Fleetwood Enterprises, Inc. (1998) 17 Cal.4th 985, 990). The civil remedies section of the SBA (Civ. Code § 1794) grants standing and a cause of action to “any buyer of consumer goods who is damaged by a failure to comply with any obligation under this chapter or under an implied or express warranty or service contract…”¿ (Civ. Code, § 1794(a).)¿ Civ. Code, section 1791(b) defines a buyer as “any individual who buys consumer goods from a person engaged in the business of manufacturing, distributing, or selling consumer goods at retail.” (Civ. Code § 1791(b).) The plain language of Civ. Code, section 1791 et seq., does not limit the definition of a “buyer” to a retail contract. Rather, the plain language of section 1791 provides for a broad construction of a “buyer” or “retail buyer” in connection to a retail purchase of a consumer good from a retail seller. 

GM argues Plaintiff is not a “buyer” because he testified at his deposition that he is not in possession of any documents that demonstrate the Vehicle was registered to him, or that he had title for the Vehicle. GM also argues Plaintiff has not shown he was “damaged” because Plaintiff has not provided any proof that he paid for the Vehicle other than his belief that he paid for it, and that Plaintiff has no documents of the private sale in which he testified he received $500 in cash. In support thereof, GM provides Plaintiff’s deposition, Plaintiff’s discovery responses, Vehicle Delivery Information and Summary, and a copy of the 2012 Chevrolet Limited Warranty and Owner Assistance Information Booklet. 

To prevail on a motion for summary judgment, there must be no issue requiring a trial as to any fact that is necessary under the pleadings, such that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal. 4th at 843.) The Court finds that GM has not met its prima facie burden that no triable issues of material fact exist. Plaintiff’s testimony regarding the purchase, financing, and sale of the Vehicle is not so “factually devoid” of responses from which an absence of evidence, either directly or circumstantially, may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.) In arguendo, even if GM had met its prima facie burden of production, there exists at least a triable issue of material fact to preclude granting the motion for summary judgment. 

Plaintiff, in opposition, argues he testified that he purchased the Vehicle on October 24, 2011 from GM’s authorized retail dealership Jessup Chevrolet (Jessup Auto Plaza) located at 6811 Highway 111, Cathedral City, CA 92234. To finance the purchase, Plaintiff took out a loan on October 24, 2011, through USAA Federal Savings Bank in the amount of $11,000.00, with a finance charge of $2,470.82, totaling $13,470.82 in payments. The Security Agreement provides that the loan amount would be paid directly to Jessup Auto Plaza, and the purpose of the Security Agreement was to purchase the Vehicle. Further, the Release of Lien Document verifies that the Vehicle was used as collateral for the loan. Plaintiff avers that in May 2016, he purchased an extended warranty for the Vehicle called the HM Protection Plan, which extended the warranty coverage to May 2, 2019 or 102,467 miles, whichever came first. Additionally, Plaintiff asserts that GM relies on non-binding and unpublished federal cases regarding damages, and that Plaintiff is qualified to provide evidence of his own testimony regarding damages. 

Drawing all reasonable inferences in the light most favorable to opposing party, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated a triable issue of material fact. Plaintiff testified he paid $20,000-$21,000 for the Vehicle. (Kay Decl. ¶4; Exh. A - Plf. Depo. 23:5-9; 28:19-24.) The Security Agreement (Rodriguez Decl. ¶ 4; Exh. 1) evinces that Plaintiff obtained a loan in the amount of $11,000 to purchase the Vehicle as described in the Complaint, and that $11,000 was paid to Jessup Auto Plaza. The “Release of Lien” for the collateral lists Plaintiff as the registered owner of the Vehicle. (Rodriguez Decl. ¶ 14; Exh. 3) Additionally, the Vehicle Delivery Report provides that the Vehicle was delivered to “EF Rodriguez” on October 25, 2011. (Law Decl. ¶ 5; Exh. 3.) Thus, although there is no evidence of a Purchase Agreement or vehicle title registration in Plaintiff’s name, the combination of Plaintiff’s deposition testimony, the Security Agreement, and delivery report provides a measure of evidence that he had purchased the Vehicle. (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21 [On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.].) 

Any ambiguity concerning the reliability of Plaintiff’s testimony concerning the full purchase price and the Vehicle’s diminished worth is for the fact finder to determine. Plaintiff’s reliance on non-analogous and primarily federal cases such as Khachatryan v. Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A. Inc., 2009 WL 9537648 [Party moved pursuant to Federal Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 50(b) – judgment as a matter of law in a jury trial], Von Grabe v. Sprint PCS (2003) 312 F. Supp. 2d 1285 [Plaintiff’s alleged claim based on failure to disclose certain material information in a service contract under the SBA was not pled with sufficient specificity in the amended complaint], Weinberg v. Whatcom Cnty. (2001) 241 F.3d 746 [plaintiff in land-use litigation failed to offer evidence establishing amount of damage suffered as a result of tort action for negligence against the county arising from county issuing stop work order, revoking permits, and vacating short plats], Piscitelli v. Friedenberg (2001) 87 Cal. App. 4th 953 [pertaining to damages for loss of profits/prospective profits, wherein reasonably reliable evidence must make reasonably certain their nature, occurrence, and extent; appellate court found that claim for lost commissions not entirely speculative as a matter of law based on damages expert’s testimony], et al., are unavailing. 

Lastly, GM’s brief and undeveloped contention that it was not a party to any sales or lease transaction with Plaintiff is irrelevant. Due to the concern for the ultimate consumer, the SBA does not require privity of contract. The general rule is that privity of contract is required in an action for breach of either express or implied warranty and that there is no privity between the original seller and a subsequent purchaser who is in no way a party to the original sale. (See Lewis v. Terry (1896) 111 Cal. 39, 44.) However, a claim for violation of the SBA arises by virtue of a retail buyer’s purchase of a vehicle from a retail seller, and not necessarily the contractual relationship between a plaintiff (buyer) and a defendant (seller). Otherwise, the SBA would require buyers to have privity of contract under the initial sale contract from which the SBA’s warranties arose. 

 

V. CONCLUSION 

Accordingly, GM’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

On July 31, 2024, plaintiffs Herbert Mazorow and Carmen Mazorow (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) initiated this unlawful detainer action against defendants Alvin Smith, Jr. (“Smith”), Jamal Kevell Parker, and Does 1 to 10. 

On August 14, 2024, in pro per defendant Smith filed the instant motion to quash service of summons and complaint on the grounds that he was not personally served with the summons and complaint because he found the documents on the floor outside the premises. 

On October 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition contending that no proof of service had been filed prior to the filing of Smith’s motion. Further, Plaintiffs argue the Court granted Plaintiffs’ request to serve the summons and complaint by posting on September 18, 2024. 

Any reply was due on or before October 23, 2024. No reply has been filed to date. 

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD 

“[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction. [Citation.]” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1444.)¿¿ 

When a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process, the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving the facts requisite to an effective service. (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.) “The filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper. However, the presumption arises only if the proof of service complies with the applicable statutory requirements.” (Floveyor Internat., Ltd. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 790.)¿ 

“ ‘On a motion to quash service of summons, the plaintiff bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that all jurisdictional criteria are met. [Citations.] The burden must be met by competent evidence in affidavits and authenticated documents; an unverified complaint may not be considered as supplying the necessary facts.’ [Citation.]”¿ (Brown v. Garcia (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 1198, 1203; see also Sheard v. Superior Court (1974) 40 Cal.App.3d 207, 211 [“[W]here a defendant properly moves to quash service of summons the burden is on the plaintiff to prove facts requisite to the effective service.”].) 

 

III. DISCUSSION 

There was no proof of service filed prior to the instant motion for Smith to quash, nor does Smith provide any information on when the purportedly defective service was noticed by him. Plaintiffs contend they do not assert that the summons and complaint were served prior to the Court signing the order to post. 

The process statutes should be liberally construed to effectuate service and uphold the jurisdiction of the court if actual notice has been received by the defendant, and in the last analysis the question of service should be resolved by considering each situation from a practical standpoint. (See Espindola v. Nunez (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 1389; Ellard v. Conway (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 540, 544-45.)  

Here, the Court granted Plaintiff’s application to serve Smith by posting the summons and complaint on the premises in a manner most likely to give Smith actual notice, and by immediately mailing a copy of the summons and complaint to Smith’s last known address. (App. & Order, Sept. 18, 2024.) On October 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed proof of service that the summons and complaint were served in accordance with the approved application by posting, which creates the rebuttable presumption that service was proper. It is also undisputed that Smith has actual notice of the action, considering his statement that he found the summons and complaint at the front door. 

 

IV. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, Smith’s motion to quash service of the summons and complaint is DENIED.
