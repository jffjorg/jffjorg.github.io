Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F51, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2251.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 23CHCV02568    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: F51

MOTIONS TO COMPEL DISCOVERY RESPONSES

(Requests for Production of Documents and Special Interrogatories Set One)

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23CHCV02568

  

Motions Filed: 5/16/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor Ivan Ho (“Plaintiff”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant/Judgment Debtor Krikor Barsoumian, in pro per (“Defendant”)

NOTICE: OK

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: Orders compelling Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s first set of Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”) and Special Interrogatories. Plaintiff further requests monetary sanctions against Defendant in the combined amount of $2,000.00.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The unopposed motions are granted. Defendant is ordered to provide objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s first set of discovery requests within 30 days. The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $200.00.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a contract action in which Plaintiff alleges that he and Defendant entered into a written contract in which Defendant agrees to customize Plaintiff’s motor vehicle. (Compl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff alleges that on 1/12/23, Defendants “breached the contract by failing to finish the customization of the subject automobile, such that Plaintiff finally had to have the motor vehicle towed from their SHOP and remove same from Defendants’ further possession and control.” (Id. at ¶ 7.)

 

On 8/24/23, Plaintiff filed the instant action against Defendants, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Written Contract; (2) Open Book Account; (3) Conversion; (4) Fraud; and (5) Money Had and Received. On 1/25/24, the Court entered default against Defendants. On 1/29/24, the Court entered a default judgment against Defendants in the amount of $106,539.59.

 

 

On 3/4/24, Plaintiff served the subject discovery requests on Defendant. (Decl. of Lawrence A. Strid ¶ 3.) On 5/16/24, Plaintiff filed the instant motions to compel Defendant’s responses thereto. No oppositions have been filed to date.

 

ANALYSIS

 

To aid in enforcement of a money judgment, a judgment creditor may demand that the judgment debtor produce documents for inspection and copying that are in the debtor’s possession, custody, or control. (Code Civ. Proc. § 708.030.) The judgment creditor may move for an order compelling a response to a request for production of documents if the judgment debtor fails to serve a timely response within 30 days. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.300, subd. (b); 2031.260, subd. (a).) Furthermore, the judgment debtor who fails to serve a timely response “waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product” unless the Court grants it relief upon motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

 

A judgment creditor may propound written interrogatories to the judgment debtor in the manner provided by Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.010 through 2030.410, requesting information to aid in enforcement of a money judgment. (Code Civ. Proc § 708.020.) A judgment creditor may move for an order compelling a response to interrogatories if the judgment debtor fails to serve a timely response within 30 days. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, subd. (b); 2030.260, subd. (a).) Furthermore, the judgment debtor who fails to serve a timely response “waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product” unless the Court grants it relief upon motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

 

Here, as Plaintiff served the subject discovery requests on Defendant by mail on 3/4/24, the last day for Defendant to respond was 4/8/24. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1013, subd. (a).) Despite being granted an extension to respond, Defendant has not provided responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests to date. (Strid Decl. ¶¶ 3–9.) The Court notes that Defendant has failed to oppose the instant motions. Accordingly, the Court grants the instant unopposed motions to compel Defendant to provide responses to the subject discovery requests.

 

Sanctions 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300, subd. (c).)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290, subd. (c).)

 

Here, Plaintiff requests $2,000.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant. This amount includes (1) 1.5 hours of Plaintiff’s attorney’s time spent preparing each motion; and (2) an anticipated 1 hour appearing at each of the hearings, at counsel’s hourly rate of $400.00 per hour. (Strid Decl. ¶ 10.) While the Court grants the instant unopposed motions, it notes that discussions regarding the subject discovery responses were directed to Defendant’s former attorney, who recently withdrew from representing Defendant. Accordingly, the Court finds it reasonable to impose $200.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant.

 

CONCLUSION 

 

The unopposed motions are granted. Defendant is ordered to provide objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s first set of discovery requests within 30 days. The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $200.00.


Case Number: 23STCP01878    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: F51

SEPTEMBER 11, 2024

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES

(Requests for Production of Documents, and Form Interrogatories, Set One)

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23STCP01878

 

Motions filed: 5/20/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Natalie Davidson (“Plaintiff”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant Zoya Vladimirskaya (“Defendant”)

NOTICE: OK

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: Orders compelling Defendant’s further responses, within 10 days, to the following discovery requests:

·         Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”), Set One, Nos. 5, 17, 18, 19, 20, and 21;

·         Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 14.1 and 15.1.

Plaintiff also seeks to impose a total of $3,720.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant and her counsel.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The unopposed motions are granted. Defendant is ordered to provide further responses to the subject discovery requests within 30 days. The Court imposes $860.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant, payable within 45 days.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action concerns a business dispute between Plaintiff and Defendant, who are equal sole shareholders, directors, and officers of nominal defendant Cardea Hospice & Palliative Care (“Cardea”). (Compl. ¶¶ 7–9.) Plaintiff alleges that on 2/7/23, Defendant tendered her resignation as an officer of Cardea and offered to sell her shares. (Id. at ¶ 10.) Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant, inter alia, wrongfully converted corporate funds to herself and others, attempted to evict Cardea from its rental unit, and demanded that the business be shut down. (Id. at ¶ 12.)

 

On 5/31/23, Plaintiff filed her complaint, individually and on behalf of Cardea, alleging against Defendants the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty (against Defendant); (2) Conversion (against Defendant); (3) Money Had and Received (against Defendant); (4) Declaratory Relief (against all defendants); and (5) Removal Pursual to Corporations Code § 304 (against all defendants). On 9/14/23, Defendants filed their answer.

 

 

 

On 8/23/23, Plaintiff served the subject discovery requests on Defendant. On 2/5/24, Defendant served her responses thereto.

 

On 3/7/24, Defendant filed the instant motions to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to the subject discovery requests. No oppositions have been filed to date.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.    Meet and Confer 

 

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300, subd. (b)(1); 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel declares that on 2/5/24, he sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter discussing the issues raised in the instant motions, but the parties were unable to informally resolve the issues. (Decl. of Paul Tokar ¶¶ 8–9.) Therefore, the Court finds that counsel has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300, subdivision (b)(1) and 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2).

 

B.     Defendant’s Discovery Responses

 

1.      RFP Nos. 5, 17, 18, 19, 20, and 21

 

California law requires a responding party to respond to each request for production of documents with either a statement of compliance, a representation that the party lacks the ability to comply, or an objection to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210, subd. (a).) “A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.)

 

A propounding party may move for an order compelling further response to a request for production if it decides that “a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks further responses to RFP Nos. 5, 17, 18, 19, 20, and 21, which seek documents relating to payments Defendant received from Cardea, and her communications with certain individuals, current and former Cardea employees, and Cardea’s Certified Public Accountants. (Ex. A to Tokar Decl.) Defendant’s responses thereto state the following: “After conducting a diligent search and making reasonable inquiry, responding party is unaware of the existence of any documents responsive to this request as Plaintiff has taken all reports and records from the corporate office and has denied me access.” (Ex. C to Tokar Decl.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s statement of inability to comply is evasive because she maintains access to her business email account, where such responsive documents may be located. (Pl.’s RFP1 Sep. Stmt.; Tokar Decl. ¶ 10.) Therefore, Plaintiff argues that “many of the requests ask for documents and communications that are readily in Vladimirskaya’s possession, custody, and control. Vladimirskaya has no reason for not being able to provide said documents.” (Pl.’s RFP1 Mot. 4:18–20.)

 

The Court notes that Defendant has failed to oppose the instant motions, therefore the Court is not persuaded that Defendant lacks the ability to comply with the subject RFPs by making a diligent search of her business and personal email accounts for the requested documents. “The object of discovery is to prevent surprise at trial,” therefore a responding party may not delay “producing what was clearly in his possession and failed to make a diligent search for records he could easily have obtained.” (Manlin v. Milner (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 1004, 1025.) Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Defendant’s responses to the subject RFPs are evasive. Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to Plaintiff’s RFPs, Set One, Nos. 5, 17, 18, 19, 20, and 21.

 

2.      Form Interrogatory Nos. 14.1 and 15.1

 

“The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered; (2) An exercise of the party’s option to produce writings; or (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.” (Code Civ Proc. § 2030.210, subd. (a).) “Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Code Civ Proc. § 2030.220.)

 

A propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses to interrogatories if any of the following apply: “(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete; (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate; or (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300, subd. (a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks to compel Defendant’s further responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 14.1 and 15.1. Form Interrogatory No. 14.1 seeks the identities of any persons Defendant contends violated any laws, which thereby caused the subject incident. Form Interrogatory No. 15.1 seeks facts and documents supporting each of Defendant’s affirmative defenses.

 

In response to Form Interrogatory No. 14.1, Defendant stated: “Yes. CA Health & Safety Code § 1746 Section 1601(a)(1), (b), and C(3) of the California Corporations Code Corp. Code § 213 Corp. Code § 1600(c).” (Pl.’s FROG1 Sep. Stmt. 2:9–13.) As Plaintiff observes, this response is incomplete as it fails to identify the name, address, and telephone number of each person involved in the subject incident that Defendant contends violated any laws thereby causing the incident.

 

In response to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1, Defendant provided a list of factual allegations, identities of persons with purported knowledge of those facts, and stated that she is unable to locate or produce any responsive documents because of her inability to access corporate records. (Id. at 3:5–6:16.) Plaintiff argues that “none of the responses above cite or relate to any of the affirmative defenses alleged. Accordingly, Davidson does not know of [sic] the items recited relate to the third, sixth, or tenth affirmative defense.” (Id. at 6:21–23.) The Court again notes that Defendant has failed to oppose the instant motions and justify her discovery responses in any way, and further reiterates the above discussion regarding Defendant’s ability to conduct a diligent search of her email accounts.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Defendant’s responses to the subject interrogatories are incomplete and evasive. Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Nos. 14.1 and 15.1.

 

C.    Sanctions 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories [or RFPs], unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290, subd. (c); Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (h).) Additionally, “the court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030, subd. (a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff requests $1,860.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant per motion, accounting for (1) one hour of Plaintiff’s attorney’s time spent drafting each motion; and (2) an additional anticipated 3 hours preparing each reply and appearing for the hearing, at counsel’s hourly rate of $450.00. (Tokar Decl. ¶ 10.) Plaintiff also seeks to recover $60.00 in filing fees per motion. (Ibid.)

 

In granting the instant unopposed motions, the Court finds it reasonable to impose $860.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The unopposed motions are granted. Defendant is ordered to provide further responses to the subject discovery requests within 30 days. The Court imposes $860.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant, payable within 45 days.
