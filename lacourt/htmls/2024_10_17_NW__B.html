DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 19STCV22667    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: B

 

Hearing:              Thursday, October 17, 2024

Notice:             OK

Re:                         WESLEE DAVIS JR., et al. vs SINGER VEHICLE DESIGN, et al. (19STCV22667) 

 

 

Petitioner Shannon Escott’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

 

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Shannon Escott’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 27, 2019, Plaintiffs Weslee Davis Jr. (“Claimant”), by and through his guardian ad litem Shannon Escott (“Petitioner”), and the Estate of Weslee Davis Sr., decedent, filed a complaint against Defendants Singer Vehicle Design (“Singer”), the Estate of Ryan Blanks (“Blanks”), the State of California (“State”), the County of Los Angeles (“County”), the City of Los Angeles (“City”), and DOES 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging wrongful death and dangerous condition on public property. The complaint arises from a June 20, 2018, incident in which Blanks, within his scope of employment through Singer, negligently operated a motor vehicle and collided with decedent, resulting in death to them both.

 

On January 28, 2020, Petitioner filed an application for appointment of guardian ad litem for Claimant, which was approved.

 

On October 11, 2022, Petitioner filed a Petition to Approve Compromise of Disputed Claim as to Minor Claimant (“Minor’s Comp.”). The Court denied the petition for filing deficiencies.

 

On September 6, 2023, Petitioner filed a second Minor’s Comp. The Court denied the petition as it declined to approve the creation and funding of the proposed trust at that time. (April 8, 2024, Minute Order.)

 

On May 3, 2024, Petitioner filed a third Minor’s Comp. The Court denied the petition for trust instrument deficiencies.

 

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On July 1, 2024, Claimant filed the instant Petition.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and, until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Ibid. at 1606.) 

 

Prob. Code § 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to Prob. Code § 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows: 

 

The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator), costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or incompetent person. (Prob. Code, § 3601, subd. (a).) 

 

In cases pursuant to Prob. Code § 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 7.955(a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (CRC Rule 7.955(a)(2).)

NADIA FARRA, ET AL. VS BLOOMINGDALE’S llc, et al.

 

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

 

Date of Hearing:  October 17, 2024                            Trial Date:  None set

Department:   B                                                          Case No.: 21STCV14026

 

Moving Party:             Schindler Elevator Corp. and Bloomingdale’s, LLC

Responding Party:       None

                                   

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 13, 2021, Plaintiff Nadia Farra (“Nadia”), an adult by and through her guardian ad litem Sym Farra, and Sym Farra (“Sym”)(collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants Bloomingdale’s LLC (“Bloomingdale’s”), Macy’s, Inc. (“Macy’s) and DOES 1 to 20 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action: (1) general negligence; and (2) premises liability. Plaintiffs allege they sustained serious injuries when, on April 19, 2018, while travelling down on an escalator at a Bloomingdale’s store, the escalator shook or hesitated, causing Plaintiffs to fall down the escalator.

 

On August 21, 2023, Plaintiffs filed an amendment to their complaint identifying Schindler Elevator Corp. (“Schindler”) as DOE 1.

 

On August 15, 2024, Defendants Bloomingdale’s and Schindler filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions.

 

The motion remains unopposed.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ motion is GRANTED .

 

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 permits the Court, in its discretion, to impose the following types of terminating sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process”:

 

(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

 

(2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

 

(3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

 

(4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.

(CCP § 2023.030(d).) Conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process” includes failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Ibid., § 2023.010(d) and (g).)

 

“Imposition of sanctions for misuse of discovery lies within the trial court's discretion, and is reviewed only for abuse.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 991.) “The trial court should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery[.]” (Ibid., 992.) Sanctions should be tailored to the harm created by the withheld discovery and should not be used as punishment. (Ibid.)

 

The court should also consider whether the discovery misconduct was “willful” and whether the requesting party was prejudiced by the other’s failure to comply. (Do It Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 37.)

 

Lastly, courts have held that terminating sanctions are warranted when a party repeatedly fails to propound adequate discovery responses, even after the court imposed monetary sanctions. (Liberty Mutual Fire Ins. Co. v. LcL Administrations, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1093.) For example, in Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, the Court of Appeal held that five motions to compel created a record “replete with evidence of [appellant’s] failures to answer discovery requests.” (Ibid., 279–280.)

 

Defendants argue that terminating sanctions are warranted because Plaintiffs have failed to respond to Defendants’ discovery requests, failed to comply with prior court orders, and failed to pay ordered sanctions. As a result, Defendants seek to dismiss the entire action with prejudice.

 

On December 23, 2023, Defendants propounded upon each Plaintiff a set of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Request for Production of Documents. (Casolari Decl. ¶ 2.) Responses were due on January 16, 2024. (Ibid.) On January 30, 2024, Defendants asked Plaintiffs for their responses, having received none. (Ibid. at ¶ 3.) On February 6, 2024, Plaintiffs represented over the phone that they had sent their responses, but Defendants ultimately never received them. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 4 and 5.) Defendants sent another inquiry on March 4, 2024, with no response. (Ibid. at ¶ 6.) As a result, on Defendants filed six motions to compel Plaintiffs’ discovery responses, which were granted on July 15, 2024. (Ibid. At ¶ 7.) The Court ordered Plaintiffs to serve their responses by August 4, 2024. (Ibid.; Exh. B [copy of the Court’s ruling].) The Court also imposed sanctions due by August 14, 2024. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs never complied. Defendants attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiffs before filing this motion to no avail. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 8 and 9.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs’ conduct as outlined above is an abuse of the discovery process, subject to sanctions. “A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) Here, Plaintiffs have largely remained silent in this action for the past year and have failed to comply with court orders, to comply with numerous discovery request and to pay court ordered sanctions. The Court does not believe additional monetary sanctions will compel Plaintiffs to act. Therefore, the Court grants the Motion and dismisses the action with prejudice.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On May 7, 2021, Plaintiff Jason Gray (“Plaintiff”), by and through his guardian ad litem Gregory Gray, filed a complaint against Defendants City of Rancho Pablos Verdes (“RPV”), City of Rolling Hills Estates (“Rolling Hills”), and DOES 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one complaint of negligence. The complaint arises from a September 20, 2020, incident, when Plaintiff allegedly suffered serious injuries when thrown off his bike due to dangerous conditions on the sidewalk.  

On June 5, 2024, the matter was reassigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.  

On June 20, 2024, RPV filed a motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s claim. 

On October 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition. 

On October 11, 2024, RPV filed their reply. 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

            RPV moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint for negligence. RPV alleges that RPV does not own or control the sidewalk which caused Plaintiff’s injuries, and therefore cannot be held liable to Plaintiff as a matter of law. (Takaoka Decl ¶¶ 5 and 6.) 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)  “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out

the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence. 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)      

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections 

Plaintiff’s October 3, 2024, evidentiary objections are sustained as to Nos. 1 and 2; the street improvement plans of Rolling Hills High School are not authenticated, nor does RPV lay a sufficient foundation for introducing them.  

 

Plaintiff’s objection to No. 3 is overruled as it falls within the business records hearsay exception under Cal. Evid. Code § 1272(a) and (b). 

 

Defendant’s Burden 

RPV meets their initial burden. 

RPV argues that they are not liable to Plaintiff as a matter of law because RPV does not own the subject sidewalk.  

"One who owns, possesses, or controls [property] has a duty to act reasonably to protect others from a dangerous condition [stemming from] that property.” (Soto v. Union Pacific Railroad Co. (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 168, 177.) “The ‘crucial element’ for imposing a duty in such circumstances is control . . . the rationale being that whoever has the means to control the property can take steps to prevent the harm.” (Ibid.) Conversely, “[a] defendant cannot be held liable for the defective or dangerous condition of property which it did not own, possess, or control.” (Isaacs v. Huntington Memorial Hospital (1985) 38 Cal.3d 112, 125.) 

                Here, RPV establishes facts showing they do not own the subject sidewalk. RPV asserts that, on November 23, 2020, they received Plaintiff’s Claim for Damages concerning the underlying incident. (RPV Separate Statement of Facts (“RPV-SSF”) No. 2; Takaoka Decl. ¶ 4.) On December 16, 2020, Plaintiff’s Claim was rejected after RPV determined it did not own the sidewalk in Plaintiff’s Claim. (RPV-SSF No. 3; Takaoka Decl. ¶¶ 5 and 6; Exh. B.) RPV claims that the sidewalk is within Rolling Hills Estates’ city limits. (RPV-SSF No. 7; Knell Decl. ¶ 6; Exh. L.) In the past eight years, RPV has not received any maintenance orders or complaints related to this sidewalk. (RPV-SSF No. 12; Awwad Decl. ¶¶ 8-9, 12-13.) Furthermore, no records indicate RPV’s ownership of the sidewalk. (RPV-SSF No. 13; Takaoka Decl. ¶ 5; Ex. O.) Additionally, RPV claims it never performed repairs on the sidewalk. (RPV-SSF No. 23; Awwad Decl. ¶ 11, Ex. O.) 

Therefore, RPV set forth sufficient evidence demonstrating they do not own, possess, or control the property which caused Plaintiff’s injuries. 

The burden now shifts to Plaintiff. 

 

Plaintiff’s Burden 

Plaintiff admits that RPV does not own the subject sidewalk. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts (“P-SSF”) No. 7.) However, Plaintiff asserts that RPV owns Silver Spur Road which abuts the sidewalk. (Ibid.; Exh. 7.) Plaintiff also asserts that RPV owns a public right-of-way easement over the sidewalk. (P-SSF No. 13.) Furthermore, Plaintiff claims that RPV had actual notice of the sidewalk’s condition when it was assigned a calculated pavement condition index in 2019. (P-SSF No. 14; Exh. 7.) In 2022, RPV selected Willdan Engineering “to provide engineering and support services for the rehabilitation of the roadway segment of Silver Spur Road from the Rancho Palos Verdes city limit north of Hawthorne Boulevard to Elkmont drive.” (Exh. 6.) Willdan’s proposal included “assessing the pavement, sidewalk, and curb and gutter.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff claims this maintenance shows RPV exercised control over the subject sidewalk.

An entity may be liable for injuries to pedestrians on sidewalks abutting their property, as evaluated by the degree of maintenance performed. (Alpert v. Villa Romano Homeowners Ass’n (2008) 81 Cal.App.4th 1320, 1334-1335.) “ . . . A landowner or possessor of land has a duty to take reasonable measures to protect persons from dangerous conditions on adjoining land when the landowner or possessor exercises possession or control over that adjacent land.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff provides evidence of an assessment which shows RPV may have exercised control over the subject sidewalk. On summary judgment, the opposing party’s affidavits or declarations are liberally construed. (Corwin v. Los Angeles Newspaper Service Bureau, Inc. (1971) 4 Cal.3d 842, 851-852.) Therefore, Plaintiff meets their burden by providing evidence showing a triable issue of material fact as to RPV’s control over the sidewalk. Accordingly, RPV’s motion is denied.

 

V.       CONCLUSION

             A triable issue of material fact exists as to RPV’s control over the sidewalk. Accordingly, RPV’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

II.    LEGAL STANDARDS

            Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 598 provides in pertinent part that, “[t]he court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof.” Similarly, CCP § 1048(b) provides in pertinent part that, “[t]he court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues.”  

            The Court’s discretionary authority to sever claims and try them separately may also be employed to avoid undue prejudice to a party. (Stencel Aero Engineering Corp., v. Superior Court (1976) 56 Cal.App.3d 988.) Courts have inherent power to regulate the order of trial, and therefore can entertain a motion to bifurcate at any time—even during the trial itself. (McLellan v. McLellan (1972) 23 Cal.App.3d 343, 353.)  

            The discretion of the trial court to bifurcate claims and try them separately is broad and will not be disturbed on appeal, except for abuse. (National Electric Supply Co. v. Mt. Diablo Unified School Dist. (1960) 187 Cal.App.2d 418; see Buran Equip. Co. v. H & C Investment Co. (1983) 142 Cal.App.3d 338, 343-344 [commending the trial court for bifurcating the trial and ordering a specific issue be tried first, “since, if it had been correctly decided, trial would not have been required of any other issues.”].) 

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Defendants request bifurcation of Plaintiffs’ claims for the liability phase of the trial from the damages phase. Defendants argue that, due to the emotional nature of the wrongful death claim, bifurcation is appropriate to avoid undue prejudice to Defendants. Defendants also argue that bifurcation would not prejudice Plaintiffs since trying the issue of liability will take a similar length of time as it would if tried separately or jointly.

Plaintiffs argue that bifurcation would unduly delay trial, as bifurcating liability and damages necessitates two voir dires, two opening statements, two sets of jury instructions, and so on. Further, Plaintiffs argue that bifurcating “will not shield the jury from hearing about Decedent’s [alleged] damages,” thus making Defendants’ prejudice argument moot. (Opp. p. 6:22-23.) 

The Court agrees with Plaintiffs. This action involves one claim of wrongful death. The fact pattern is not complex. Therefore, bifurcation would not likely result in judicial economy or efficiency; quite the opposite if in a bi-furcated trial a jury finds defendants liable. While Defendants note the number of witnesses at each potential phase of trial as a reason for bifurcation, the Court responds that bifurcation could result in some witnesses having to testify twice. Therefore, for this single-issue action, bifurcation would unduly lengthen trial, trial preparation by the parties and prejudice Plaintiffs.  The Court finds that the interests of justice are not served in this instance by bifurcating the issue of liability from damages.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

             Defendants’ Motion to Bifurcate is DENIED. Moving party is ordered to give notice.
