DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV13023    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 12, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV13023

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Lithia Motors, Inc., Lithia Motors Support Services, Inc., and Van Nuys-L, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Kenneth Oleesky
 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Kenneth Oleesky (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Lithia Motors, Inc., Lithia Motors Support Services, Inc., and Van Nuys-L, Inc. (“Van Nuys-L,” collectively, “Defendants”) for disability discrimination.  Plaintiff contends Defendants did not accommodate his disability, which resulted from a COVID-19 infection.  Defendants move for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication. 

      Defendants fail to specify any issues for summary adjudication in their notice of motion.  The Court cannot grant relief that is not set forth in the notice of motion.  As such, the Court cannot grant this relief. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(a).)  Further, as Defendants fail to specify any issues for summary adjudication in their notice of motion, Defendants have not “repeated, verbatim, in the separate statement of undisputed material facts” each issue on which Defendants seek summary adjudication and identified “each supporting material fact claimed to be without dispute with respect to” each issue.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1350(b), (d).) Accordingly, the Court denies the motion for summary adjudication as procedurally improper. “There is little flexibility in the procedural imperatives of [Code of Civil Procedure section 437c], and the issues raised by a motion for summary judgment (or summary adjudication) are pure questions of law.  As a result, section 437c is unforgiving; a failure to comply with any one of its myriad requirements is likely to be fatal to the offending party.”  (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1607.)  

The Court considers the motion as a motion for summary judgment only.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

EVIDENCE

Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection No. 2 is sustained.  Plaintiff’s other evidentiary objection is overruled.

            Defendants object to the authentication of exhibits, not to the contents of the exhibits themselves.  The testimony authenticating the exhibits is not objectionable.  Accordingly, Defendants’ objections are overruled.

DISCUSSION

First and Third Causes of Action: Discrimination and Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodations

            Plaintiff’s first cause of action is for discrimination under the Fair Employment and Housing Act, or FEHA.  To state such a claim, the employee must demonstrate “(1) the employee's membership in a classification protected by the statute; (2) discriminatory animus on the part of the employer toward members of that classification; (3) an action by the employer adverse to the employee's interests; (4) a causal link between the discriminatory animus and the adverse action; (5) damage to the employee; and (6) a causal link between the adverse action and the damage.”  (Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  “A defending employer seeking summary judgment in a discrimination case may meet its burden by showing that one or more of these prima facie elements is lacking, or that the adverse employment action was based on legitimate nondiscriminatory factors.  [¶]  If the employer has met its burden by showing a legitimate reason for its conduct, the employee must demonstrate a triable issue by producing substantial evidence that the employer's stated reasons were untrue or pretextual, or that the employer acted with a discriminatory animus, such that a reasonable trier of fact could conclude that the employer engaged in intentional discrimination or other unlawful action.”  (Cucuzza v. City of Santa Clara (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1038, internal citation omitted.) 

Plaintiff’s third cause of action is for failure to provide reasonable accommodations.  As Plaintiff’s employers, Defendants had to reasonably accommodate any of Plaintiff’s known disabilities to enable Plaintiff to perform the essential functions of Plaintiff’s job, unless doing so would produce undue hardship to Defendants’ operations.  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (m); 2 Cal. Code Regs., § 11068, subd. (a).)  In the operative second amended complaint, Plaintiff alleges COVID-19 caused him to have trouble breathing, chronic exhaustion, and fatigue; his doctor placed him on leave from work, which Defendants initially approved; however, Defendant stopped approving Plaintiff’s requests for leave and terminated Plaintiff’s employment on December 13, 2021.  (Second Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 18-26.) 

In moving for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s claims for discrimination and failure to provide reasonable accommodations, Defendants satisfy their burden with evidence that Defendants had a legitimate reason to terminate Plaintiff’s employment. (Kelly v. Stamps.com Inc. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1097-98.)  Plaintiff submitted a series of documentation concerning his inability to work:  On January 4, 2021,[1] Plaintiff requested a leave of absence through March 1; on January 22,  Plaintiff submitted documentation from his healthcare provider, which reflect Plaintiff could not perform any of his job functions from December 21, 2020 through January 31; on February 1, Plaintiff submitted additional documentation, which stated Plaintiff was unable to work through February 15; then, on February 15, Plaintiff submitted further documentation indicating Plaintiff could not work through February 28; documentation Plaintiff submitted on February 26 revealed he could not work through March 15; on March 16, Plaintiff submitted additional documentation, this time stating he could not work through March 28; and through paperwork Plaintiff submitted on March 17, he indicated he could not perform any job functions though March 28.  Defendants accepted and approved all of Plaintiff’s requests noted above. (Declaration of Diana Rusnick in Support of Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, ¶ 10, Exhibit A.)

Plaintiff then began to similarly submit documentation in which he requested reasonable accommodations.  For example, on March 19, Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation in which he related he could not work at all through March 28 because he was totally incapacitated, but he would be able to return to work without any restrictions on March 29; on March 29, Plaintiff submitted another request for a reasonable accommodation, in which Plaintiff’s healthcare provider stated he could not work at all through April 9 but also related Plaintiff could return to work without any restrictions on April 10; and on April 9, Plaintiff submitted a request for a reasonable accommodation, in which Plaintiff’s healthcare provider stated Plaintiff could not work at all through April 26 but could return to work without any restrictions on April 27. (Rusnick Decl., ¶ 10, Exhibit A.)

  This pattern of Plaintiff requesting continued reasonable accommodations due to being totally incapacitated before, and a couple of times after, the time for his return to work without restrictions had expired continued up through November 30 when Plaintiff submitted another request for accommodation in which his healthcare provider stated he could not work at all through December 9 but could return to work without restrictions on December 10.  Thereafter, Plaintiff did not submit another request for accommodation, nor did Plaintiff appear for work on December 10 through 14.  (Rusnick Decl., ¶ 10, Exhibit A.)  Defendants had accepted and approved all of the requests for accommodations up to this time.  On December 15, when Rusnick called Plaintiff and asked him if he intended to return to work on December 29 based an oral request he had made on December 10 for a further leave extension, Plaintiff responded he did not know when he could return to work because his doctor was keeping him off work.  (Id., ¶¶ 14, 15.)  Defendants concluded they could not accommodate what amounted to an indefinite leave for Plaintiff and terminated his employment as of that date.  (Id., ¶¶ 15, 16, Exhibits B, C.)  

Plaintiff does not dispute these facts.  However, in response to Defendants’ discovery, Plaintiff stated, “Rather than engaging in the mandatory good faith interactive process or offering reasonable accommodations for Plaintiff’s disabilities, on or about December 13, 2021, Defendants discriminated and retaliated against Plaintiff and wrongfully terminated him because of his disabilities and need for reasonable accommodations.  . . .  ¶  However, Defendants’ explanation for terminating Plaintiff’s employment was mere pretext for discrimination and retaliation as Plaintiff’s medical leave did not pose an undue hardship.  Defendants had been able to accommodate Plaintiff’s medical leave up to that point without any hardship.  Moreover, Defendants engage in ‘evergreen recruiting,’ meaning it always has open job postings at its dealerships.  Therefore, there are always open positions at Defendants’ dealerships that could have been offered to Plaintiff once he was cleared to return to work.”  (Declaration of Sara G. Noel in Support of Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, Exhibit E, p. 11.)  Plaintiff further stated he “needed an accommodation to perform his job, including, but not limited to, an extended leave of absence, a modification or adjustment to his job that would enable Plaintiff to perform the essential functions of the job, a modified work schedule, modifying an employer policy, a temporary assignment to a vacant position, and other similar accommodations.”  (Id., Exhibit E, p. 18.) 

When asked to state all evidence in support of his claim he could perform the essential duties of his job for Defendants, Plaintiff stated he “did so . . . before [he] was physically disabled as a result of Covid-19 . . . .”  (Noel Decl., Exhibit F, p. 4.)  Plaintiff added he was not able to perform his job duties at the time Defendants terminated his employment.  (Id., Exhibit F, p. 5.)  But Plaintiff declared he could have performed his job if Defendants had “changed Plaintiff’s job responsibilities or work schedule; . . . reassigned Plaintiff to a vacant position; . . . modified or provided Plaintiff with equipment or devices; . . . provided Plaintiff with a further three- to four-week leave of absence without causing themselves an undue hardship; . . . reassigned Plaintiff to a vacant remote sales position; . . . provided Plaintiff with more breaks; . . . allowed Plaintiff to sit down while working; . . . created a hybrid schedule for Plaintiff, allowing him to work both from home and Defendants’ dealership . . . .”  (Id., Exhibit F, p. 7.)  However, the documentation from Plaintiff’s healthcare provider Plaintiff provided to Defendants while he worked for Defendants stated Plaintiff was wholly incapacitated and unable to work at all.  The only accommodation that could address complete incapacitation was a leave of absence.

A finite leave of absence may be a reasonable accommodation if the employee will resume work after the leave expires.  (2 Cal. Code Regs, tit. 2, § 11068, subd. (c).)  However, ‘“[r]easonable accommodation does not require the employer to wait indefinitely for an employee's medical condition to be corrected.”’  (Hanson v. Lucky Stores, Inc. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 215, 226-227.)  In the instant case, Defendants have presented evidence demonstrating the only accommodation available to address Plaintiff’s condition was an indefinite leave.  That accommodation is unreasonable as a matter of law.  Defendants’ evidence meets their burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for disability discrimination or for failure to provide reasonable accommodations because Defendants could not reasonably accommodate Plaintiff’s condition.  Defendants, therefore, have shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of fact as to whether Defendants discriminated against Plaintiff because of his disability or failed to reasonably accommodate that disability.  (See Soria v. Univision Radio Los Angeles, Inc. (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 570, 591-592 [when an employer rebuts any presumption of discrimination by producing evidence that its action was taken for a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason, the employe must then show that reason was actually a pretext for discrimination]e employer’s proffered nondiscriminatory reason].)

In opposition, Plaintiff advances the testimony of three of Defendants’ persons most knowledgeable on specified matters.  First, Diana Rusnick (“Rusnick”) testified Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment because of the undue hardship of his ongoing and indefinite leave of absence.  (Plaintiff’s Compendium of Evidence in Support of Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment (Compendium), Exhibit 1, pp. 106-107.)  Rusnick also testified Plaintiff represented he could not commit to any return date.  (Id., Exhibit 1, p. 112.)  This evidence does not raise triable issues of material fact.  Rather, it tends to confirm Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment because Defendants could not accommodate Plaintiff’s request for an indefinite leave.

Rusnick also testified Defendants could have accommodated part-time work for Plaintiff.  (Compendium, Exhibit 1, p. 292.)  However, as set forth above, at the time Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment, the documentation Defendants had received showed Plaintiff was wholly incapacitated such that an indefinite leave was the only accommodation available.  Likewise, Rusnick’s testimony Defendants did not ask for alternative accommodations for Plaintiff (id., Exhibit 2, p. 402) is irrelevant because Plaintiff’s documentation revealed he was incapacitated.  (Id., Exhibit 2, p. Lithia0053.)  As such, no accommodation other than indefinite leave was available. 

Second, Gina Morse (“Morse”) testified, because Plaintiff presented Defendants with documentation he was unable to work, Defendants could not permit Plaintiff to return to work without documentation from his healthcare provider Plaintiff could return to work.  (Compendium, Exhibit 3, p. 145; see also Exhibit 7, p. 50.)  Plaintiff, therefore, argues Defendants could not have expected Plaintiff to return to work as scheduled, because Plaintiff had not submitted the pertinent documentation.  This is immaterial, and the argument is unavailing.  Again, Defendants were not required to accommodate Plaintiff’s need for an indefinite leave.

Third, Angela Taber (“Taber”) testified Defendants consider leave of more than one year indefinite.  (Compendium, Exhibit 5, p. 183.)  This testimony is immaterial.  Regardless of whether Defendants had such a policy, Plaintiff was unable to provide Defendants with a timeframe for his return to work.  As such, his leave was indefinite.

Plaintiff argues he could have returned to work on December 30 without restrictions.  In support of this argument, Plaintiff cites a notation in his medical record, which reflects that Plaintiff had received a note placing him off work until December 29.  (Compendium, Exhibit 37, p. 002390.)  But this is not evidence Plaintiff could return to work without accommodations on December 30.  Further, Plaintiff ignores the uncontroverted evidence that every note from Plaintiff’s medical providers had an end date, and Plaintiff failed to return to work after the expiration of those dates. 

Therefore, Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendants discriminated against Plaintiff and failed to provide reasonable accommodations.

Second Cause of Action: Failure to Engage in a Good Faith Interactive Process

An employer must engage in a “timely, good faith interactive process . . . in response to a request for reasonable accommodation by an employee or applicant with a known physical or mental disability or known medical condition.”  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (n); see 2 Cal. Code Regs, tit. 2, § 11069, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff alleges he “was able to perform the essential functions of his job as an Internet Sales Manager with reasonable accommodations, including but not limited to a medical leave of absence or remote work with intermittent breaks for resting and recovery. Providing PLAINTIFF with such accommodations would not have caused DEFENDANTS any undue hardship.”  (Second Amended Complaint, ¶ 103.)  However, as set forth above, Defendants provided Plaintiff with a leave of absence from January 4 through December 15.  According to Plaintiff’s medical providers, Plaintiff was completely incapacitated and could not work at all.  As such, the only accommodation available was a leave of absence.  However, because an indefinite leave of absence is not a reasonable accommodation as a matter of law, and Defendants did provide Plaintiff with the accommodation he requested until it became unreasonably burdensome on Defendants, Defendants satisfied their burden of showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on the claim for failure to engage in a good faith interactive process.  Thus, Defendants shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact concerning this claim.

            As set forth above, Plaintiff advances evidence other accommodations for Plaintiff, such as part-time work, would not have presented an undue hardship for Defendants.  However, the documentation Plaintiff provided to Defendants showed Plaintiff was incapacitated and unable to work indefinitely.  Defendant was not required to accommodate this request.  Likewise, Plaintiff’s argument Defendants should have hired a vocational specialist to evaluate alternative arrangements for Plaintiff is unavailing.  Plaintiff’s medical paperwork informed Defendants he was off work.  As such, no accommodation other than leave was appropriate.  Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendants failed to engage in the interactive process.

Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action: Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Retaliation

            Plaintiff’s claims for failure to prevent discrimination and retaliation are derivative of his claims for discrimination and retaliation.  Where triable issues of material fact exist as to discrimination and retaliation claims that preclude summary adjudication, those issues likewise preclude summary adjudication on claims for failure to prevent discrimination and retaliation.  (Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 288.)  As set forth above and below, Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendants discriminated or retaliated against Plaintiff.  Plaintiff likewise fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendants failed to prevent discrimination or retaliation.

Fifth Cause of Action: Retaliation

To prevail on a claim for retaliation, Plaintiff must establish “by a preponderance of the evidence, that retaliation for an employee's protected activities was a contributing factor in a contested employment action. . . . Once the plaintiff has made the required showing, the burden shifts to the employer to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.” (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 718.)

As set forth above, Defendants advance evidence that Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment because they could not accommodate Plaintiff’s request for essentially an indefinite leave. Thus, Defendants shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of fact as to whether Defendants improperly terminated Plaintiff’s employment.  Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues as to whether Defendants’ termination of Plaintiff was pretextual. 

Seventh Cause of Action: Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy

California law provides “a tort remedy when an employer's discharge of an employee contravenes the dictates of public policy.” (Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 167, 177.)  As set forth above, Defendants have presented evidence of terminating Plaintiff’s employment for valid reasons.  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden of showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for wrongful termination in violation of public policy.  Defendants thereby shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact in contravention.  Plaintiff has not raised triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendants’ termination of Plaintiff was pretextual.

Eighth Cause of Action: Failure to Timely Pay Wages

An employer who discharges an employee must immediately pay all compensation due and owing. (Lab. Code, § 201, subd. (a).)  The employer must pay the employee “at the place of discharge . . . .”  (Lab. Code, § 208.)  At the time Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment, Plaintiff had not worked at his worksite in 49 weeks.  As such, Defendants could not tender his outstanding compensation at the worksite.  Further, Plaintiff maintained he was incapacitated, and could not come to work.  Defendants’ evidence is that they sent Plaintiff a check for his outstanding wages by FedEx overnight on December 15, the date he was terminated, and the check arrived on December 17.  (Declaration of Gina Morse in Support of Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, ¶ 27.)  Under these circumstances, the payment was not untimely. 

Defendants met their burden by showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on this claim and shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact.  Plaintiff does not dispute these facts.  As such, Plaintiff fails to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendants failed to pay wages due at the time of his termination.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is granted

Defendants are ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

DEPARTMENT

CASE NUMBER

23STCV02273

MOTION

Motion to Quash Subpoena

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Jane Doe

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.

 

MOTION

              Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) moves for a protective order to quash the subpoena Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Defendant”) served on Deponent Angela Aiello (“Deponent”).  Defendant opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

If a subpoena requires deposition testimony or the production of documents, the Court may quash the subpoena entirely or modify it.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  In ruling on a motion to quash, “the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff contends, while she worked for Defendant, her co-worker Eric Pagel (“Pagel”) sexually harassed and assaulted her.  Defendant served a subpoena on Deponent, in which Defendant seeks to depose Deponent and asks Deponent to produce documents.  Plaintiff has a right to privacy in “various private or sensitive information regarding [her] personal life . . . .”  (Hooser v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 997, 1003-1004.)  This includes Plaintiff’s friendship with Deponent.  Accordingly, the Court cannot order disclosure of information regarding Plaintiff’s friendship with Deponent unless it is directly relevant to this litigation.  (See Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-856.)  

However, Deponent is a percipient witness in this action, as Plaintiff has indicated she had dinner with Deponent on the night after the alleged assault.  Plaintiff also testified she texted Pagel photographs of Plaintiff and Deponent.  Defendant is entitled to undertake discovery of such matters as Deponent’s observations of Plaintiff during the period immediately after the alleged assault, as Plaintiff testified she was distraught.  Plaintiff also testified she told Deponent about having been assaulted by Pagel.  Defendant is, therefore, entitled to discovery concerning what Plaintiff told Deponent.  Finally, Plaintiff testified she told her husband about Pagel’s alleged assault at a dinner party at Deponent’s home.  (See Opposition, pp. 4-5.)  Accordingly, Defendant is entitled to depose Deponent in that regard. 

The motion to quash is denied.  However, the Court concludes Defendant’s subpoena to Deponent is overbroad.  The entirety of Plaintiff’s communication history between Plaintiff and Deponent and Deponent and Plaintiff’s husband is not pertinent to this action.  Accordingly, the Court limits the subpoena for production of documents to communications between Deponent and Plaintiff and Deponent and Plaintiff’s husband from: (1) January 14 to April 30, 2020; (2) May 22 to June 30, 2020; (3) August 6, 2020 to December 31, 2021; (4) January 1 to July 5, 2023; (5) October 1 to November 1, 2023; and (6) January 1, 2024 to the present.  Defendant indicates it is amenable to these limitations.  (Opposition, pp. 8-9.)

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 24STCV04065    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 12, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV04065

MOTIONS

Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendants New Pershing Apartments, L.P. and SRHT Property Management Company

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs Donna Bolden, Loretta Hartfield, Jane Burton, Barron McCall, Manuela Guillen, Michael Rimson, Raul Perez, Deon Smith, Lee Shackleford, Burnell Jackson, Melody Mann, Ernest Thomas, Robyy Acosta, Dwayne Gooden, John Amadie, Brian King, Larann Tibben, Taletha McDaniels, Larry Allensworth, Rebekah Sutton, Raven Anderson, Jerry Graves, Toni Rivera, Irene Valencia, Francis Nepomuceno, Donald Coleman, Edward Hanible, Chrishawn Bowser, Glenn Work, and Carlos Perez

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Donna Bolden, Loretta Hartfield, Jane Burton, Barron McCall, Manuela Guillen, Michael Rimson, Raul Perez, Deon Smith, Lee Shackleford, Burnell Jackson, Melody Mann, Ernest Thomas, Robyy Acosta, Dwayne Gooden, John Amadie, Brian King, Larann Tibben, Taletha McDaniels, Larry Allensworth, Rebekah Sutton, Raven Anderson, Jerry Graves, Toni Rivera, Irene Valencia, Francis Nepomuceno, Donald Coleman, Edward Hanible, Chrishawn Bowser, Glenn Work, and Carlos Perez (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants New Pershing Apartments, L.P. and SRHT Property Management Company (“Defendants”) for habitability violations.  Defendants move to strike portions of the first amended complaint.  Plaintiffs oppose the motion. 

ANALYSIS

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322(b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Defendants move to strike the punitive damages allegations.  In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.”  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  A plaintiff must allege that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.) 

Plaintiffs allege various habitability violations existed in their rental units, including inadequate weather protection, internal water leaks that resulted in mold and dampness, vermin infestation, heating, ventilation, and electrical wiring issues, and physical damage to amenities, and Defendants failed to remedy these conditions.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 44-46, 50-57.)  Plaintiffs further allege Defendants’ “conduct [was] oppressive, willful, malicious, negligent and was carried out with a conscious disregard for Plaintiffs’ legal rights and safety.  Defendants acted purposefully in order to vex, injure and annoy Plaintiffs for the purpose of forcing them to abandon their legal prerogative and further to punish them for seeking redress of their concerns regarding the condition of the Subject Property with housing authorities.”  (Id., ¶ 63.)  Plaintiff do not allege specific facts to support these conclusions; for example, details of any particular action that was taken or not taken, specifically by whom, and when. 

Further, Plaintiffs allege Defendants are entities.  “[T]he imposition of punitive damages upon a corporation is based upon its own fault.  It is not imposed vicariously by virtue of the fault of others.” (City Products Corp. v. Globe Indemnity Co. (1979) 88 Cal. App. 3d 31, 36.) “Corporations are legal entities which do not have minds capable of recklessness, wickedness, or intent to injure or deceive.  An award of punitive damages against a corporation therefore must rest on the malice of the corporation’s employees.  But the law does not impute every employee’s malice to the corporation.  Instead, the punitive damages statute requires proof of malice among corporate leaders: the officers, directors, or managing agents.” (Cruz v. Home Base (2000) 83 Cal. App. 4th 160, 167, internal quotations and citation omitted.)  Although Plaintiffs generally1mention Martha Villalba and Eboni Vasquez are directors of Defendants (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 47), Plaintiffs do not allege with specificity which of Defendants’ officers, directors, or managing agents acted with oppression, fraud, or malice towards Plaintiffs and how they did so.  Accordingly, the Court strikes the prayer for punitive damages but grants Plaintiffs an opportunity to amend.

Defendants argue the Court should strike Plaintiffs’ allegations about events that happened after the property at which Plaintiffs reside entered court-ordered receivership, because Defendants had no control over the property after that point.  To determine whether Defendants had control over the property at a given point, the Court must consider extrinsic evidence, which the Court cannot do in ruling on the motion to strike.  (CPF Agency Corp. v. R&S Towing Service (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1014, 1032.)  Further, a motion to strike cannot be used as a “‘line item veto’ for the civil defendant . . . .” (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1683.) The motion to strike these allegations is, therefore, denied.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages against Defendants is granted with leave to amend.  Defendants’ motion to strike is otherwise denied.

Plaintiffs are to file an amended complaint within 10 days of their receipt of this order.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice and to file proof of service of same.
