DEPARTMENT 30 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STCV20490    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: 30

Calendar # 8

Date: 1/17/2025

Case Name: TAMARA L. JOHNSON vs KIA MOTORS AMERICA

Case # 20STCV20490

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES

 

Tamara L. Johnson (Plaintiff) moves for an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $160,203 against Kia America, Inc. (Defendant). Plaintiff’s separate request for costs will be heard on February 7 at the hearing for Defendant’s motion to tax costs. Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees is granted in part.

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

“[A]s a general rule, attorney fees are not recoverable as costs unless they are authorized by statute or agreement.” (People ex rel. Dept. of Corporations v. Speedee Oil Change Systems, Inc. (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 424, 429.) If the buyer prevails on an action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, “the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (d).)

“In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.” (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).) The court may determine fees pursuant to the section upon notice and motion by a party. The party prevailing on the contract is that “who recovered a greater relief in the action on the contract.” (Id., § 1717, subd. (b)(1).) The court may also determine that there is no prevailing party.

The attorney bears the burden of proof as to “reasonableness” of any fee claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5).) This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered. (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.) A plaintiff’s verified billing invoices are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed were necessarily incurred. (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence. General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.” (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 488.)

In determining whether the requested attorney’s fees are “reasonable,” the Court’s “first step involves the lodestar figure—a calculation based on the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the lawyer’s hourly rate. The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of facts specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2008) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 92.) In determining whether to adjust the lodestar figure, the Court may consider the nature and difficulty of the litigation, the amount of money involved, the skill required and employed to handle the case, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case. (EnPalm LLC v. Teitler (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 770, 774 (EnPalm).)

 

Under Civil Code section 1794, courts must first make an initial determination of the actual time expended before ascertaining whether that amount of time and the amount thus charged are reasonable. (Morris v. Hyundai Motor America (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 24, 34.) In making such a determination, courts are to consider the complexity of the case, procedural demands, skill exhibited by counsel, and results achieved. (Ibid.) If the time expended or resulting charge are not reasonable under these circumstances, the trial court must award a lesser amount. (Ibid.) It is the prevailing buyer’s burden to show that the fees incurred were allowable, reasonably necessary to the litigation, and reasonable in amount. (Ibid.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $106,802 with a lodestar multiplier of 0.5 for $160,203 in total fees against Defendant. Plaintiff is the prevailing party for the purposes of this fee motion under a settlement agreement entered into by Defendant. (Kirnos Decl. ¶ 13, Ex. C.) Defendant does not contest that Plaintiff is entitled to fees; rather, Defendant argues that the rates and hours claimed by Plaintiff are unreasonable, and that Plaintiff should not be entitled to a lodestar multiplier. The Court finds that Plaintiff’s claimed rates are reasonable along with most of Plaintiff’s claimed hourly totals. However, the Court does not find a lodestar multiplier appropriate in this case.

 

Plaintiff’s Evidentiary Objections

 

Plaintiff objects to the declaration of Jacqueline Chinery on various grounds. Plaintiff’s second objection is sustained on the basis that paragraph 8 is overly argumentative. Plaintiff’s other objections are overruled, as Chinery provides a factual foundation for any opinions.

 

Reasonableness of Hours 

 

            Plaintiff alleges 252.9 billable hours total over the 4.5 years of litigation in this case. (Kirnos Decl., Ex. A.) These hours are split among 24 different attorneys billing at various rates. (Id. ¶ 28.) Some of Plaintiff’s hourly entries are unbilled to reduce duplicative entries and eliminate intra-office communications or double billing for meetings. (Id. ¶ 22, Ex. A.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s work included written discovery, motion practice, depositions of Plaintiff and Defendant’s person most qualified and custodian of records, a vehicle inspection, attempts at mediation, and trial preparation. (Id. ¶ 12.)

 

Defendants challenge several of Plaintiff’s billing entries, including the following: (1) 11.6 hours for meetings among Plaintiff’s attorneys; (2) 7.7 hours for the creation of standard trial documents; (3) 15.7 hours for unspecified trial preparation; (4) 13.3 hours to oppose standard motions in limine; (5) 7.7 hours to review Plaintiff’s deposition; (6) 4.9 hours to review the deposition of Plaintiff’s expert; (7) 4.9 hours to review the deposition of Defendant’s person most knowledgeable; (8) 34.2 hours for different attorneys to prepare for and attend trial calls; (9) .8 hours to respond to an OSC issued because Plaintiff’s attorney’s failed to appear at a CMC; (10) 6.1 hours for a new attorney to review the case file in 2024; (11) 10.6 hours to generate a fee motion; and (12) 11.6 hours for the unsuccessful prosecution for fraud and punitive damages claims which are not entitled to fees.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff is not entitled to recover fees in connection with claims for fraud not brought under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. “When a cause of action for which attorney fees are provided by statute is joined with other causes of action for which attorney fees are not permitted, the prevailing party may recover only on the statutory cause of action.” (Atkins v. Enterprise Rent-A-Car Co. of San Francisco (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1127, 1133.) However, if certain issues are common to causes of action for which fees are permitted and for which they are not, fees need not be apportioned. (Ibid.) “All expenses incurred on the common issues qualify for an award.” (Ibid.) Here, the issues concerning Plaintiff’s fraud claims are roughly equivalent to the Song-Beverly claims, as both involve defects with the vehicle. (See Santana v. FCA US, LLC (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 334, 349 [finding that claims for fraud and claims under Song-Beverly involve similar evidence].) Thus, these hours are reasonable.

 

Defendant challenges the hours spent by Plaintiff’s counsel meeting to discuss the case. However, such coordination is a necessary part of litigation, and the time billed for discussing the case, 11.6 hours, is proportional to the time alleged working on the case in total, 252.9 hours. Thus, these hours are reasonable. Similarly, the Court also finds the time spent by Plaintiff’s counsel reviewing depositions for the purpose of preparing the case to be reasonable.

 

Defendant also challenges hours spent creating trial documents, preparing for trial, attending trial calls, opposing motions in limine, and reviewing documents in preparation for these tasks. While Defendant offered to settle the case before trial, Plaintiff achieved a better settlement after trial preparation had finished. This implies that the work created better circumstances for Plaintiff, not to mention that hours were spent opposing motions made by Defendant. Thus, these hours are reasonable.

 

Defendant objects to the 10.6 hours spent on the present fee motion and reply. However, this time is necessary given the objections presented by Defendant. Much of Plaintiff’s motion and reply are not boilerplate. Thus, these hours are reasonable as well.

 

Defendant objects to the 0.8 hours billed by Plaintiff’s counsel to attend an OSC regarding their own failure to appear at an earlier CMC. This was not a necessary litigation expense. (Kirnos Decl., Ex. A at p. 3.) Thus, these hours are unreasonable. Accordingly, the Court finds 252.1 total hours alleged by Plaintiff’s counsel to be reasonable.       

 

Reasonableness of Rates 

 

Requested rates are reasonable if they are “within the range of reasonable rates charged by and judicially awarded comparable attorneys for comparable work.” (Children’s Hospital & Medical Center v. Bonta (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 740, 783 (Bonta).) The Court may consider the sworn declarations of attorneys regarding their customary fees, hourly rate determinations in other cases, and the prevailing fees in the community as evidence of the reasonable market rate. (Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 1009.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges various hourly rates for the 24 attorneys doing work on the present case. (Kirnos Decl. ¶ 28.) The rates have increased marginally for each of the attorneys over the course of the litigation, reflecting their increasing levels of experience. (Ibid.) Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff’s alleged rates reasonable.

 

Lodestar Multiplier

 

Plaintiff argues that counsel is entitled to a 0.5 lodestar multiplier due to the contingent nature of this case. The Court may consider the nature and difficulty of the litigation, the amount of money involved, the skill required and employed to handle the case, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances when deciding on a lodestar multiplier. (EnPalm, supra, 162 Cal.App.4th at p. 774.) While this case was taken on contingency, the formulaic nature of Song-Beverly actions provides fodder for a negative multiplier. Thus, these factors cancel, and the Court does not award a lodestar multiplier in this case.

 

Accordingly, the Court awards $106,572 in attorney’s fees to Plaintiff from Defendant ([$106,802 alleged] – [$230 unreasonable] = [$106,572]).

 


Case Number: 21STCV36109    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: 30

 

Calendar # 10

Date: 1/17/2025

Case Name: JONATHAN EAGEN vs DOCTOR LASER MEDICAL INC., et al.

Case # 21STCV36109

 

MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT

 

 

Doctor Laser Medical, Inc., Dr. Laser Medspa, PC, Dr. Laser, LLC, and Joseph Campana (Defendants) move for an order enforcing the settlement agreement between him and Jonathan Eagen (Plaintiff). The motion is denied.

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6; see Mesa RHF Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 913, 917.)

 

B.     Discussion 

  

 On March 18, 2024, the parties entered into an oral settlement agreement (Agreement) which was read into the record. The Agreement provided that: (1) Plaintiff would dismiss all Defendants with prejudice; (2) Plaintiff would provide a general release of all Defendants with a Civil Code section 1542 waiver; (3) Plaintiff and his counsel would be obligated to pay $2,500 in fees and costs; (4) Defendants would waive all fees and costs in excess of $2,500; (5) the payment would be made within 30 days; and (6) Defendants would release their malicious prosecution and abuse of process claims against Plaintiff and his attorneys. (Cohon Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)

 

If the terms of a settlement are agreed to in open court, the determination of whether the parties entered into a binding settlement requires a court to consider the following: (1) whether the material terms of the settlement were explicitly defined; (2) whether the supervising judicial officer questioned the parties regarding their understanding; and (3) whether the parties expressly acknowledged their understanding of the agreement. (In re Marriage of Assemi (1994) 7 Cal.4th 896, 911 (Assemi).)

 

Here, the parties explicitly defined the terms. (Cohon Decl. ¶ 3.) The Court questioned the parties, asking if counsel “heard the [recitation] of the terms.” (Id., Ex. A.) And the parties acknowledged their understanding. (Ibid.) Thus, the Agreement constitutes a binding settlement under section 664.6, subdivision (a), and Assemi.

 

Plaintiff argues that the present motion is moot because he has already agreed to the settlement. The parties to pending litigation must stipulate to a settlement either in a signed writing or orally before the Court for judgment to be entered pursuant to those terms. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).) Here, the parties agreed to terms orally before the Court. (Cohon Decl. ¶ 3.) The terms agreed to in that stipulation constitute the terms of the Agreement. The Court need not mandate Plaintiff’s signature on a separate written agreement. Accordingly, Defendants’ motion is denied.

 

The Court does not award sanctions.

 

Defendants seek sanctions in the amount of $7,980. “A trial court may order a party, the party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (a).) Here, it does not appear that Plaintiff’s actions are intended to cause delay. Rather, Plaintiff has attempted to comply with the Agreement, already stipulated to in open court as allowed for by section 664.6. Thus, sanctions are not warranted.

 

 

The Court denies the motion.

            Plaintiff requests that the Court compel Defendants to provide responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for production, and deem any requests for admissions admitted. If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, that party waives any objection and “[t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The Court shall make such an order unless the responding party provides responses in substantial compliance before the hearing. (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) If a party to whom an inspection demand is directed fails to serve a timely response, that party waives any objection, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response, and the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party who unsuccessfully opposes such a motion unless they acted with substantial justification. (Id., § 2031.300.) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, that party waives any objection, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response, and the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party who unsuccessfully opposes such a motion unless they acted with substantial justification. (Id., § 2030.290.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff served requests for admission, requests for production, form interrogatories, and special interrogatories on Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. (Volkswagen) and LAD-AU, LLC (LAD) on October 24, 2024. (Cha Decl. ¶ 2.) Written responses were due on December 2. (Ibid.) Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter to each Defendant on December 9, requesting responses within one week. (Id. ¶ 3, Ex. B.) The Court deems these efforts sufficient. While LAD has represented that it would provide responses before the present hearing, making the present motions moot, to this date neither Defendant has provided evidence of responses.

 

             Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motions to compel responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and inspection demands with respect to each Defendant. Additionally, the Court orders that the genuineness of each request for admission be admitted with respect to each Defendant.

 

            The Court must order sanctions regarding each motion to deem requests for admissions admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) However, the Court need only grant sanctions with respect to the motions to compel if a party unsuccessfully opposes such a motion. Here, Volkswagen did not oppose the present motions to compel, while LAD did. The Court notes that Plaintiff’s motions to compel and motions to deem admitted are nearly identical and thus does not credit Plaintiff’s time estimations with respect to each motion. Thus, the Court awards half of the amount requested as to each Defendant regarding Plaintiff’s motions to deem matters admitted, at $642.50 per Defendant. Additionally, the Court awards $2,455 in sanctions against LAD for unsuccessfully opposing the motions to compel, reflecting the cost of drafting a single motion to compel. (Cha Decl. ¶ 5.) Accordingly, the Court awards $642.50 in sanctions against Volkswagen, and $3,097.50 in sanctions against LAD.

Conclusion:

 

            The Court grants Plaintiff’s motions to compel and motions to deem matters admitted. The Court awards $642.50 in sanctions against Volkswagen and $3,097.50 in sanctions against LAD, payable within 30 days.
