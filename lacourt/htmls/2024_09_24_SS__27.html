Case Number: 20STCV41794    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

Hearing Date: 9/24/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV41794 DEEANNA LINSMAIER vs WAIL BUSHARA, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Defendant Wail Bushara

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: The Court Will Discuss Scheduling with the Parties

 

On November 2, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present case, alleging she was injured while a passenger in a car operated by Defendant Bushara. The car driven by Bushara was sideswiped by another vehicle operated by the now-deceased Matthew Muldoon. On July 11, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication regarding Defendant Bushara's third-party contributory negligence affirmative defense. On August 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed another motion for summary adjudication as to various elements of her causes of action against Defendant Bushara. The MSA is scheduled for May 14, 2025, and the current trial date is set for January 30, 2025. Plaintiff requests the court to continue the trial date so her new motion for summary adjudication can be heard.

Defendant filed an opposition arguing that Plaintiff's previous MSA concerning contributory negligence, which was denied, should bar Plaintiff's new MSA regarding liability. This argument is grounded in California Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(f)(2), which states: “A party shall not move for summary judgment based on issues asserted in a prior motion for summary adjudication and denied by the court unless that party establishes, to the satisfaction of the court, newly discovered facts or circumstances or a change of law supporting the issues reasserted in the summary judgment motion.” Defendant argues that Plaintiff has not introduced any newly discovered facts or changes in law to justify revisiting the issues.

Plaintiff’s newly filed motion for summary adjudication seeks adjudication on several elements of Plaintiff’s first cause of action for vehicular negligence and second cause of action for general negligence, including the existence of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages. Although the court had previously dismissed Plaintiff’s motion regarding Defendant’s defense of contributory negligence, the current motion, which focuses on the elements of Plaintiff’s claims, is separate and distinct from the earlier issue of contributory negligence defense.  For example, even if Defendant is found liable for negligence, he may still invoke the defense of comparative negligence to lessen the damages awarded. Moreover, the MSA pertains to the various elements of negligence. The defense of comparative negligence is not relevant to the court’s adjudication of these elements, such as whether Defendant owed a duty to Plaintiff.

While it is true that part of Plaintiff's new MSA deals with the calculation of damages, and Plaintiff might be precluded from filing such a motion without a stipulation and court order under CCP §437c(t), this issue relating to damages is only one of the several issues to be adjudicated in the MSA.

Defendant has not provided any legal authority for the court to deny a continuance based on these two grounds: CCP 437c(f)(2) and CCP §437c(t).

On the other hand, numerous courts of appeal have held that a trial court cannot refuse to consider a motion for summary judgment that is timely filed "A trial court may not refuse to hear a summary judgment filed within the time limits of [Code of Civil Procedure] section 437c. [Citation.] Local rules and practices may not be applied so as to prevent the filing and hearing of such a motion." (Sentry Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 526, 529); accord First State Inc. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 324, 330 [invalidating case management order to the extent it precluded filing motions pursuant to section 437c ]; Wells Fargo Bank v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 918, 923 [local court rule that "require a party filing a complex summary judgment motion to file the motion six months before the date set for trial is void and unenforceable because it is inconsistent with section 437c"].).

The MSA was filed on August 30, 2024, and the trial date is set for January 30, 2025. The MSA was filed more than 107 days before the trial, and therefore the motion is timely. (Cole v. Superior Court (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 84, 88.)

Defendant argues that the motion to continue trial was not timely filed, as it was served 16 rather than 18 court days prior to trial as required for electronic service. However, a party's appearance at the hearing of a motion and opposition to the motion on its merits constitutes a waiver of any defects or irregularities in the notice of motion. (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697.) Here, Defendant contested the motion on its merits and therefore has waived any notice defects. Furthermore, the court finds it a waste of judicial resource to deny this motion without prejudice for Plaintiff just to bring the same motion back in 18 court days.

While Plaintiff’s MSJ should be heard prior to trial, the Court has the ability to control its own calendar and, as it turns out, the Court may be able to set a date for the MSJ that does not require a change in the trial date.  Accordingly, the Court will discuss scheduling with the parties.

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 9/24/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV25601 MIGUEL MONTES vs JACK T CHU, et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Jack Chu and Esther Pang

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS IS GRANTED.

 

Background

On December 14, 2023, Defendants Jack Chu and Esther Pang served Plaintiff with their Form Interrogatories (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One) and a Request for Production of Documents (Set One). On March 28, 2024, Defendants propounded Requests for Admissions (Set One) on Plaintiff.

Plaintiff was previously represented by Edward M. Morgan of Downtown L.A. Law Group, but the Court granted his withdrawal on March 28, 2024.

Plaintiff did not serve discovery responses to Defendants' first set of discovery requests, and as a result, Defendants moved the court to compel initial responses. On June 6, 2024, the Court heard Defendants’ motion to deem RFAs admitted. Plaintiff did not file an opposition or appear at the hearing. The Court granted Defendants' motion and deemed the contents of Defendants' RFA, Set One, admitted, and ordered Plaintiff to pay sanctions in the amount of $1,320 by June 26, 2024. On July 17, 2024, the Court heard Defendants’ Motions to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production of Documents (Set One). Plaintiff did not file an opposition or appear at the hearing. The Court granted Defendants’ Motions and ordered Plaintiff to provide the discovery responses and pay sanctions in the amount of $2,000 to Defendants by August 17, 2024.

To date, Plaintiff has not provided responses to any of the discovery requests nor paid any of the sanctions. Defendants now move the court for terminating sanctions.

Legal Standard¿ 

¿¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030 gives the court the discretion to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in a misuse of the discovery process. Misuse of the discovery process includes failure to respond to an authorized method of discovery or disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (d), (g).) A court may impose terminating sanctions by striking pleadings of the party engaged in misuse of discovery or entering default judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).) A violation of a discovery order is sufficient for the imposition of terminating sanctions. Collison & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1620.¿ 

¿ 

A court should consider the totality of the circumstances, including conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful, the detriment to the propounding party, and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain discovery. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) If a lesser sanction fails to curb abuse, a greater sanction is warranted. (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.) However, “the unsuccessful imposition of a lesser sanction is not an absolute prerequisite to the utilization of the ultimate sanction.” (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 787.) Before any sanctions may be imposed the court must make an express finding that there has been a willful failure of the party to serve the required answers. (Fairfield v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 113, 118.) Lack of diligence may be deemed willful where the party understood its obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply. (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 787; Fred Howland Co. v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1966) 244 Cal.App.2d 605, 610-611.) The party who failed to comply with discovery obligations has the burden of showing that the failure was not willful. (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 788; Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250; Evid. Code, §§ 500, 605.) 

 

Discussion 

 

The Court finds a history of discovery abuse in this case. Plaintiff consistently failed to provide the requested discovery responses at multiple stages throughout the litigation, prompting the filing of several motions to compel. Despite the Court granting these motions, Plaintiff continued to neglect his obligations by neither serving the discovery requests nor paying the court-ordered sanctions. The Court deems Plaintiff’s repeated non-compliance as willful. Plaintiff did not file an opposition contesting the present motion nor present any argument demonstrating how his failure to comply was not willful.

 

Furthermore, based on Plaintiff’s conduct leading up to this motion, including his failure to oppose and appear at the hearings for the discovery motions, and his failure to file an opposition to the present motion for terminating sanctions, it appears to the Court that Plaintiff has abandoned his case. A lesser sanction would not likely curb further discovery abuses. Therefore, the Court grants Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions.

MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER 

Hearing Date: 9/24/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV23614 JOHN MERRIETT vs UBER TECHNOLOGIES, INC., et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Uber Technologies

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER IS GRANTED IN PART

 

Background

On May 23, 2024, the Court denied Defendant Uber's motion for a protective order as to its trade secrets without prejudice. The Court noted the absence of a proposed draft order for its review and potential adoption. The denial also stemmed from the motion's overly broad scope, seeking a blanket protective order for all "documents, testimony, or information." Further, the motion was deficient due to the absence of a meet and confer between the parties before the hearing.

After the last motion hearing, the parties conducted a meet and confer via email and phone regarding the protective order. Despite these efforts, they failed to resolve the discovery issue concerning the protective order at issue.

Legal Standard

“When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (a).) “The Court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” A protective order may direct “[t]hat the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling be made only on specified terms and conditions [or] [t]hat a trade secret or other confidential research, development, or commercial information not be disclosed, or be disclosed only to specified persons or only in a specified way.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subds. (b)(4)-(5).)

Per Civil Code section 3426.1, “Trade secret means information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process, that: (1) derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and (2) is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.” (Civ. Code, § 3426.1.)

Discussion

Defendant contends that the information or documents Plaintiff seeks pertain to Uber's relationships with its third-party drivers and the utilization of Uber’s software application by these drivers. Uber contends this information is trade secrets because Uber operates in a highly competitive environment where its business methodologies, such as the management of relationships with third-party transportation providers using Uber's Driver App. are of significant interest to its competitors. (Sauerwein Decl. ¶ 11.) The company gains economic value from the confidentiality of this information, which is neither generally known nor readily ascertainable by legitimate means. Disclosure of this commercially sensitive information would enable Uber’s competitors to enhance or develop their own applications, products, services, and operations, thus negating Uber's substantial financial investments in these domains. (Sauerwein Decl. ¶ 12.) Furthermore, all newly hired employees are required to sign a Confidential Information and Invention Assignment Agreement to safeguard Uber’s trade secrets as a condition of their employment. (Sauerwein Decl. ¶ 13.) Further, third-party companies must sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement before initiating business discussions with Uber. (Sauerwein Decl. ¶ 14.)

Uber has demonstrated that: (1) it derives economic value from keeping its interactions with third-party independent contractors and the usage of its apps by drivers confidential and (2) it undertakes reasonable efforts to maintain this secrecy.

Uber has identified several discovery requests it contends would reveal potential trade secrets:

  • Request for Production of Documents No. 34: Produce any and all documents in your possession, custody, or control that relate to, refer to, or mention your driver’s work schedule (specifically, the time of day your driver began work and the time they concluded each day) for the week prior to and including the day of the incident.
  • Request for Production of Documents No. 36: Produce any and all documents that reflect your driver’s ratings, reviews, complaints, routes, etc.
  • Request for Production of Documents No. 84: All documents provided by you to drivers in California that relate to, refer to, reflect, memorialize, evidence, or otherwise reference how drivers use the app.

Additionally, Plaintiff has made further requests for the production of documents pertaining to the noticed deposition of Uber’s person most qualified (PMQ) that might implicate trade secrets, as follows:

  • Request No. 5 To PMQ: The activities of Defendant Vincent Mack Jr. ("Vincent") behind the wheel, during a ride, at rest (or while waiting for a ride, or otherwise offline), including each and every location Vincent started, stopped, the exact times of these events, the routes taken, and distances traveled between each location for the twenty-four-hour period prior to the occurrence of the incident.
  • Request No. 8 To PMQ: The nature of the relationship between Uber and Vincent, including but not limited to any employment agreements, transportation services agreements, payment agreements, contracts, financial responsibility for vehicle accidents such as the subject incident, as defined under Assembly Bill 5 in California.

The Court finds that Uber has demonstrated how documents relating to its relationship with independent contractors and how its drivers use the app can be considered trade secrets and how Plaintiff’s discovery requests would necessitate the disclosure of such information. While Plaintiff contends that the information sought merely relates to a straightforward motor vehicle crash, including the release of the event data recorder in the subject vehicle and Defendant’s work schedule, the Court is not entirely convinced that the whole of Plaintiff’s discovery requests is disconnected from Uber’s trade secrets. Upon review of the discovery responses provided by Plaintiff, it becomes evident that several specific requests indeed relate to trade secrets, including:

  • Request for Production of Documents No. 84: All documents provided by you to drivers in California that relate to, refer to, reflect, memorialize, evidence, or otherwise reference how drivers use the app.
  • Request No. 8 To PMQ: The nature of the relationship between Uber and Vincent, including but not limited to any employment agreements, transportation services agreements, payment agreements, contracts, financial responsibility for vehicle accidents such as the subject incident, as defined under Assembly Bill 5 in California.

Plaintiff then challenges the confidentiality of the alleged trade secrets, arguing that the alleged information is open to nearly any American adult with a clean driving record. However, the Court recognizes that Uber has taken reasonable steps to protect this information, notably through requiring both employees and independent contractors to sign confidentiality or non-disclosure agreements (Sauerwein Decl. ¶¶ 11-14). Plaintiff has not presented any evidence to the contrary.

Based on the foregoing, the Court agrees with Uber that a protective order is appropriate in this case.  However, the proposed protective order submitted as Exhibit D appears similar to the one previously deemed overly broad by the court in its May 23, 2024 minute order. The definition of "Confidential" in the order covers any information in the possession of a Designating Party who believes in good faith that such information warrants confidential treatment under applicable law. "Confidential Materials" are defined as any Documents, Testimony, or Information designated as "Confidential" pursuant to this Stipulation and Protective Order, thereby effectively allowing any document, testimony, or information to be deemed confidential based on the defendant's good faith belief. This definition significantly exceeds the justification provided in the moving papers, which is limited to the relationship between Uber and its independent contractors and how its applications are used by the drivers. The Court has previously denied Uber’s request for a blanket protective order and sees no reason to deviate from its previous ruling.

Therefore, the court finds it appropriate to modify the scope of the protective order to cover only the relationship between Uber and its third-party drivers, as well as how Uber’s third-party drivers are able to access and use Uber’s software application.  Uber is directed to submit a protective order for the Court’s signature consistent with this allowed protection.

Although Uber raises the issue of privacy regarding information about Defendant Mack, the arguments and evidence presented do not address this issue.  To the extent Defendant Mack and Defendant Uber believe a protective order is necessary for information related to Defendant Mack, they can seek to stipulate to such order, or, if such stipulation cannot be reached, either party can seek protection from the Court, with appropriate explanation why such information needs to be protected.  At this time, however, the Court declines to impose a protective order concerning Defendatn Vincent’s alleged private information.

Case Number: 23STCV24697    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27¿ 

¿ 

MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS 

Hearing Date: 9/24/24¿¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV24697 TIFFANY BANK vs COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES¿ 

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA IS GRANTED IN PART; 

REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED 

 

Background

 

On October 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present slip and fall case against Defendants, including Defendant City of Los Angeles. Plaintiff alleges that she was injured when she tripped over an uneven sidewalk located near 670 S. Dunsmuir Ave, Los Angeles, CA 90036. In response to Form Interrogatory No. 6.2, which asks Plaintiff to identify each injury Plaintiff attributes to the subject incident and the area of her body affected, Plaintiff stated: "back pain, ankle pain, knee pain, body soreness, increased anxiety, insomnia, and depression."

 

On May 30, 2024, Defendant served a subpoena for Plaintiff’s insurance, medical and billing records from Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. Plaintiff alleges that the subpoena is not limited in time or scope, as it seeks “ANY AND ALL INSURANCE RECORDS, MEDICAL RECORDS, CORRESPONDENCE, PAYMENTS, CLAIMS, AND ANY OTHER DOCUMENTS CONTAINED WITHIN THE INSURANCE FILE, EXCLUDING ALL PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL INFORMATION, PERTAINING TO THE APPLICANT.”

 

Plaintiff now moves the Court to limit the subpoena for medical records to treatments of only those body parts and medical conditions that Plaintiff put at issue and from ten (10) years prior to the date of the subject incident to the present. Plaintiff also requests that the subpoena be limited to include only records from the date of the subject incident to the present.

 

Legal Standard

 

CCP section 1987.1 states that “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1(a).)¿ “In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”¿ (Id.)¿ Subdivision (b) provides that a party may make a motion pursuant to subdivision (a).¿ (Id., § 1987.1(b).)

 

¿As a general rule, all unprivileged information that is relevant to the subject matter of the action is discoverable if it would itself be admissible evidence at trial or if it appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010; Schnabel v. Superior Court. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 711.) When the information sought to be discovered impacts a person’s constitutional right to privacy, limited protections come into play for that person. (Shaffer v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 993, 999.) The protections cover both a person’s personal and financial matters. (Id.) The court must balance competing rights — the right of a litigant to discover relevant facts and the right of an individual to maintain reasonable privacy — in determining whether the information is discoverable. (Id.)

 

A motion to quash a subpoena for personal records, including medical records, must be accompanied by a declaration showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution of the dispute” between the party requesting the records and the “consumer” or “employee” whose records are involved or counsel for such person.¿ (C.C.P. §§1985.3(g), 1985.6(f)(4).)¿¿¿The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

Scope of the Subpoena

 

¿           In Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 652, the California Supreme Court found that the following should be considered by the trial court in balancing the interests between the right of civil litigants to discover relevant facts and the right of bank customers to maintain reasonable privacy regarding their financial affairs: “. . . the purpose of the information sought, the effect that disclosure will have on the parties and on the trial, the nature of the objections urged by the party resisting disclosure, and ability of the court to make an alternative order which may grant partial disclosure, disclosure in another form, or disclosure only in the event that the party seeking the information undertakes certain specified burdens which appear just under the circumstances.” (Id. at 658.)

 

¿           Although relevance is typically construed liberally (see Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 169), the standard is more stringent when a party is attempting to discover documents which are constitutionally protected by the right to privacy:

 

¿[E]ven when discovery of private information is found directly relevant to the issues of ongoing litigation, it will not be automatically allowed; there must then be a careful balancing of the compelling public need for discovery against the fundamental right of privacy.¿ . . . [I]f an intrusion on the right of privacy is deemed necessary under the circumstances of a particular case, any such intrusion should be the minimum intrusion necessary to achieve its objective . . . [meaning] the least intrusive means to satisfy the interest. Mere convenience of means or cost will not satisfy that test for that would make expediency and not the compelling interest the overriding value.¿ (Lantz v. Superior Court (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1839, 1854-1855 [internal quotes and citations omitted].)

 

¿This is true even where the party asserting the right to privacy is the party who brought the suit.¿ (See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 842 [“While the filing of a lawsuit may implicitly bring about a partial waiver of one’s constitutional right of associational privacy, the scope of such waiver must be narrowly rather than expansively construed, so that plaintiffs will not be unduly deterred from instituting lawsuits . . . . [A]n implicit waiver of a party’s constitutional rights encompasses only discovery directly relevant to the plaintiff’s claim and essential to the fair resolution of the lawsuit.”].)¿¿The burden is on the party seeking the constitutionally protected information to establish direct relevance.¿ (Harris v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 661, 665.)

 

Discussion

 

In balancing Defendant’s interest in having access to relevant documents to formulate its defense against Plaintiff’s privacy interests in protecting her medical and financial privacy, the Court finds "direct relevance" to mean documents, either financial or medical, that relate to treatments of the body parts and conditions at issue. In this case, these are treatments for Plaintiff’s back, ankle, and knee, as well as conditions such as body soreness, increased anxiety, insomnia, and depression. Defendant, in the opposition, does not allege that there are additional body parts at issue.

 

The Court does not find unfettered access to all of Plaintiff’s medical history to be directly relevant and, in cases such as this one, typically limits access to medical documents to a period of 10 years from the date of the incident to the present. The court finds 10 years to provide a good balance between protecting Plaintiff’s privacy interest while still providing Defendant with ample information to prepare its defense. 

 

The Court finds billing records relating to the treatment to be directly relevant to assess the nature and quality of treatment that Plaintiff has received related to the body parts at issue, the progress of any condition affecting the specified body parts, and to identify any unusual increases in medical expenses post-incident. These records are relevant for determining the legitimacy and extent of Plaintiff’s claimed damages. 

Similarly, correspondence in the insurance file can shed light on the extent of Plaintiff’s prior injuries and its potential effect on the present injuries of the same body parts, as well as the progression of Plaintiff's medical condition over time.

 

Thus, the Court grants the present motion in part and limits the subpoena as follows:

 

·        Medical and insurance records are limited to records reflecting or relating to treatments for back pain, ankle pain, knee pain, body soreness, anxiety, insomnia, and depression from ten (10) years prior to the date of the subject incident to the present.

 

·        Billing records are limited to records from the time of the incident and to bills for treatments for back pain, ankle pain, knee pain, body soreness, anxiety, insomnia, and depression  

 

The Court declines to issue sanctions under the circumstances, as it granted Plaintiff’s motion only in part, and the parties had a legitimate dispute regarding the scope of the subpoena. The Court finds that both parties have acted with substantial justification.
