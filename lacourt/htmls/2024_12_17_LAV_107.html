DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01156    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Jubilio Escalera,

 

Plaintiff,

 

      

   v.

 

Sergio Miramontes, et al.,

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   22VECV01156                  107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Petition for Release from Property Lien                         

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]: Denied.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

On or about January 1, 2021, Plaintiff Jubilio Escalera (“Plaintiff”) executed a written credit application and agreement for the conversion of an existing garage to an ADU. (Complaint ¶ 13.) Defendants Sergio Miramontes, Rosa Miramontes, and Does 1 through 10 were the owners or reputed owners of the construction project. (Complaint ¶¶ 7-8.) Plaintiff supplied materials for the project. (Complaint ¶ 14.) Defendants failed to remit payment to Plaintiff. (Complaint ¶ 16.)

 

On May 11, 2022, Plaintiff recorded a mechanics lien against the project for the sum of $35,000.00. (Complaint ¶¶ 17-18.) On August 12, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Sergio Miramontes, Rosa Miramontes, and Does 1 through 50, alleging a single cause of action for foreclosure of mechanics lien.

 

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff orally moved to dismiss the complaint without prejudice. (08/19/24 Minute Order.)

 

On October 2, 2024, Defendants Sergio Miramontes and Rosa Miramontes (“Defendants”) filed a petition for release of property lien. On November 7, 2024, this court denied that petition. (11/07/24 Minute Order p. 6.)

 

On December 2, 2024, Defendants filed an amended petition for release of property lien (the “Amended Petition”). No opposition has been filed.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

“The owner of property or the owner of any interest in property subject to a claim of lien may petition the court for an order to release the property from the claim of lien if the claimant has not commenced an action to enforce the lien within the time provided in Section 8460.” (Civ. Code § 8480(a).) Under section 8460, “[t]he claimant shall commence an action to enforce a lien within 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien. If the claimant does not commence an action to enforce the lien within that time, the claim of lien expires and is unenforceable.” (Civ. Code § 8460.)

 

“An owner of property may not petition the court for a release order under this article unless at least 10 days before filing the petition the owner gives the claimant notice demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the claim of lien. The notice shall comply with the requirements of Chapter 2 (commencing with Section 8100) of Title 1, and shall state the grounds for the demand.” (Civ. Code § 8482.)  The notice shall be given by personal delivery, mail in the manner provided in Section 8110, or leaving the notice and mailing a copy in the manner provided in Section 415.20 of the Code of Civil Procedure for service of summons and complaint in a civil action. (Civ. Code § 8106.)

 

Under Civil Code section 8102, the notice must include:

 

(1)   The name and address of the owner or reputed owner.

 

(2)   The name and address of the direct contractor.

 

(3)   The name and address of the construction lender, if any.

 

(4)   A description of the site sufficient for identification, including the street address of the site, if any. If a sufficient legal description of the site is given, the effectiveness of the notice is not affected by the fact that the street address is erroneous or is omitted.

 

(5)   The name, address, and relationship to the parties of the person giving the notice.

 

(6)   If the person giving the notice is a claimant:

 

(A)  A general statement of the work provided.

 

(B)  The name of the person to or for whom the work is provided.

 

(C) A statement or estimate of the claimant's demand, if any, after deducting all just credits and offsets.

 

(Civ. Code § 8102(a).)

 

The clerk shall set a hearing date not more than thirty days after the filing of the petition. (Civil Code § 8486(a).) “The petitioner shall serve a copy of the petition and a notice of hearing on the claimant at least 15 days before the hearing. Service shall be made in the same manner as service of summons, or by certified or registered mail, postage prepaid, return receipt requested, addressed to the claimant as provided in Section 8108.”  (Civ. Code § 8486(b).) “Notwithstanding Section 8116, when service is made by mail, service is complete on the fifth day following deposit of the petition and notice in the mail.” (Civ. Code § 8486(c).)

 

A petition to release a mechanic’s lien must be verified and allege all of the following:

 

(a)   The date of recordation of the claim of lien. A certified copy of the claim of lien shall be attached to the petition.

 

(b)   The county in which the claim of lien is recorded.

 

(c)   The book and page or series number of the place in the official records where the claim of lien is recorded.

 

(d)   The legal description of the property subject to the claim of lien.

 

(e)   Whether an extension of credit has been granted under Section 8460, if so to what date, and that the time for commencement of an action to enforce the lien has expired.

 

(f)    That the owner has given the claimant notice under Section 8482 demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the lien and that the claimant is unable or unwilling to do so or cannot with reasonable diligence be found.

 

(g)   Whether an action to enforce the lien is pending.

 

(h)   Whether the owner of the property or interest in the property has filed for relief in bankruptcy or there is another restraint that prevents the claimant from commencing an action to enforce the lien.

 

(Civ. Code § 8484.)

 

“At the hearing both (1) the petition and (2) the issue of compliance with the service and date for hearing requirements of this article are deemed controverted by the claimant. The petitioner has the initial burden of producing evidence on those matters. The petitioner has the burden of proof as to the issue of compliance with the service and date for hearing requirements of this article. The claimant has the burden of proof as to the validity of the lien.” (Civ. Code § 8488(a).) “If judgment is in favor of the petitioner, the court shall order the property released from the claim of lien.” (Civ. Code § 8488(b).) “The prevailing party is entitled to reasonable attorney's fees.” (Civ. Code § 8488(c).)

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff recorded the mechanics lien on May 11, 2022. (Complaint ¶¶ 17-18.) (The Mechanics Lien is dated 05/02/22, (Complaint Ex. A) but the complaint states it was recorded 05/11/22. Regardless, the complaint was filed more than 90 days after the lien was filed.) Ninety days after May 11, 2022, was August 9, 2022. Plaintiff filed the complaint on August 12, 2022. Accordingly, Plaintiff did not file the complaint within the time permitted under Section 8460 and Defendants may petition this court for an order releasing the property from the claim of lien.

 

On June 4, 2024, Defendants notified Plaintiff by FedEx overnight delivery that they would be filing a petition to expunge the lien. (Kohler Declaration ¶ 10; Ex. D.) Under section 8482, notice must be given at least ten days before filing the petition. (Civ. Code § 8482.) When notice is given by mail, “[s]ervice is complete at the time of the deposit, but any period of notice and any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended five calendar days, upon service by mail, if the place of address and the place of mailing is within the State of California.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013(a); Civ. Code § 8116(b).) Defendants filed the Amended Petition on December 2, 2024. December 2, 2024, is more than fifteen days after June 4, 2024. Thus, Defendants gave Plaintiff timely notice.

 

Defendants attach a Demand for Release of Claim of Lien, dated June 4, 2024. The notice contains Defendants’ names and address. (Kohler Declaration Ex. D p. 1.) It also contained the name and address of the contractor, Santa Fe General Construction, Inc., c/o Jublio Escalera. (Kohler Declaration Ex. D. p. 1.) Further, the notice contains the property’s address, which is sufficient to identify the site. (Kohler Declaration Ex. D p. 1.) Finally, the notice was signed by Defendants’ attorney and the notice identified him as such. (Kohler Declaration Ex. D p. 1.) Accordingly, the contents of Defendants’ notice is sufficient. (See Civ. Code § 8102(a).)

 

The hearing is set for December 17, 2024. The Amended Petition was filed on December 2, 2024.  Thus, Civil Code section 8486’s requirement that the hearing date be set for a date not more than thirty days after the filing of the petition is satisfied.

 

Defendants attach proofs of service showing Defendants served Plaintiff with the amended notice of hearing on November 12, 2024, and Amended Petition on November 19, 2024. (Notice of Hearing on Petition filed 11/12/24; Proof of Service filed 11/19/24.)  The petitioner must serve a copy of the petition and notice on the claimant at least fifteen days before the hearing. (See Civ. Code § 8486(b).) “Notwithstanding Section 8116, when service is made by mail, service is complete on the fifth day following deposit of the petition and notice in the mail.” (Civ. Code § 8486(c).) These dates are more than twenty days before December 17, 2024. Thus, Defendants timely served Plaintiff with the amended notice and Amended Petition.

 

Civil Code section 8486 also provides that “[s]ervice shall be made in the same manner as service of summons, or by certified or registered mail, postage prepaid, return receipt requested, addressed to the claimant as provided in Section 8108.” (Civ. Code § 8486(b).) A summons may be served to a party in California by personal delivery, delivery to someone else at the party’s usual residence or place of business, by mail coupled with acknowledgement of receipt, or by publication. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 415.10, 415.20, 415.30, 415.50.)

 

Defendants served Plaintiff with the amended notice of the hearing via first class mail by placing the documents “in the United States mail in a sealed envelope with postage fully prepaid . . .” and by email. (Notice of Hearing on Petition filed 11/12/24.) The Amended Petition was served via FedEx envelope, and that proof of service states that a “[s]ignature [was] not required.”

 

Civil Code section 8486 permits serving the notice and petition in the same manner as a service of summons. “A summons may be served by mail as provided in this section. A copy of the summons and of the complaint shall be mailed (by first-class mail or airmail, postage prepaid) to the person to be served, together with two copies of the notice and acknowledgment provided for in subdivision (b) and a return envelope, postage prepaid, addressed to the sender.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.30.) The proofs of service here do not comply with these requirements because they contain no indication Plaintiff was also served with two copies of the notice and acknowledgement provided for in subdivision (b) or that a return envelope was provided. Thus, Defendants did not give Plaintiff proper notice of the hearing and Amended Petition.

 

The Amended Petition alleges the claim of lien was recorded on May 11, 2022. (Amended Petition ¶ 7.) A certified copy of the Mechanic’s Lien is attached to the Amended Petition. (Amended Petition Ex. A.)

 

The Amended Petition also alleges the claim of lien was recorded in the County of Los Angeles and identifies the document number as “Document No. 20220514463.” (Amended Petition ¶ 7.) The Amended Petition alleges Defendants gave Plaintiff notice by demanding Plaintiff execute and record a release of the lien on May 6, 2024, June 4, 2024, and August 22, 2024. (Amended Petition ¶¶ 11-12, 16.) The Amended Petition also alleges Plaintiff refused to execute the Release of Mechanics Lien following Defendants’ June 4, 2024, demand. (Amended Petition ¶ 13.) Further, the Amended Petition alleges Defendants received no response to their August 22, 2024, demand. (Amended Petition ¶ 16.)  Further, the Amended Petition states Plaintiff voluntarily dismissed his complaint to foreclose the mechanics lien on August 19, 2024, and “Defendants had filed no action, nor sought relief under any law governing bankruptcy, and there was no restraint to prevent Plaintiff from timely filing a Complaint to Foreclose on the Mechanic’s Lien[.]” (Amended Petition ¶¶ 14, 16.) Finally, the Amended Petition is verified by the Defendants. (Amended Petition pdf pp. 6-7.)

 

Defendants’ service of the amended notice of hearing and Amended Petition did not comply with Civil Code section 8486. Accordingly, this court denies Defendants’ Amended Petition.

 

Defendants seek $12,964.75 in attorneys’ fees. (Petition p. 5.) Defendants have not prevailed, so this court denies the request. (See Civ. Code § 8488(c).)

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ Amended Petition is denied, without prejudice.

I.                BACKGROUND

 

On December 2, 2020, Plaintiff Mary Howard (“Plaintiff”) was injured after she fell in El Camino Shopping Center’s parking lot. (Complaint p. 4.) On November 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants El Camino Shopping Center, LLC (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10, alleging a premises liability cause of action. (Complaint p. 4.)

 

On August 13, 2024, Defendant  filed four discovery motions: (1) a Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Appear for a Deposition (“Motion to Compel Second Deposition”); (2) a Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One (“Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories”); (3) a Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Response to Request of Production of Documents, Set Two (“Motion to Compel Response to Request for Production”); and (4) a Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Response to Special Interrogatories, Set Two (“Motion to Compel Response to Special Interrogatories”). Plaintiff filed no oppositions.

 

On October 2, 2024, the parties appeared at, but did not participate in, an informal discovery conference. (10/02/24 Minute Order.) The court informed both sides that participation would in an IDC would be necessary, but neither side scheduled one. Nevertheless, the court addresses the motions as set forth below.

 

II.             MOTION TO COMPEL SECOND DEPOSITION

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

“Once any party has taken the deposition of any natural person, including that of a party to the action, neither the party who gave, nor any other party who has been served with a deposition notice pursuant to Section 2025.240 may take a subsequent deposition of that deponent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.610(a).)

 

However, “for good cause shown, the court may grant leave to take a subsequent deposition, and the parties, with the consent of any deponent who is not a party, may stipulate that a subsequent deposition be taken.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.610(b).)

 

b.     Discussion

 

On June 7, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with a notice of deposition. (Zade Declaration ¶ 8.)

The notice of deposition requested Plaintiff appear and produce documents for the deposition. (Zade Declaration Ex. B; Zade Declaration ¶ 7.) The deposition was scheduled for June 10, 2024. (Zade Declaration ¶ 8.) Plaintiff did not object to the notice of deposition. (Zade Declaration ¶ 8.) Plaintiff produced no documents at the deposition. (Zade Declaration ¶ 9.) However, Plaintiff confirmed during the deposition that responsive documents existed. (Zade Declaration ¶ 10.)

 

Defendant’s counsel states that, at the deposition, “Plaintiff testified to relevant prior injuries and preexisting conditions which were not disclosed in written discovery.” (Zade Declaration ¶ 10.) This includes Plaintiff’s “claims she suffered an aortic aneurysm, among other injuries, as a result of this trip and fall” and “a prior, previously-undisclosed blood clot [Plaintiff attributed] to the 2015 Porter Ranch gas leak.” (Zade Declaration ¶ 10.) Defendant’s counsel further states that “Plaintiff’s current counsel represented Plaintiff in the 2015 Porter Ranch litigation” and “Plainitff confirmed documents reflecting relevant medical treatment existed and would be provided to her counsel, Ms. Kruger, to be turned over to Defendant’s counsel.” (Zade Declaration ¶ 10.) 

 

The parties suspended the deposition and agreed Plaintiff would attend a second deposition. (Zade Declaration ¶ 12.) The next day, Defendant’s counsel and Plaintiff’s counsel agreed that Plaintiff would “provide the medical records from the 2015 Porter Ranch claim” and “produce the questionnaire completed and produced as discovery in the Porter Ranch claim and any other medical records reflecting prior claims, such as the ‘fender benders’ Plaintiff testified to during deposition yesterday.” (Zade Declaration ¶ 13.) During a July 17, 2024, status conference, Plaintiff’s counsel stated that “a search had been conducted and no documents had been found.” (Zade Declaration ¶ 17.)

 

The court may grant leave to allow a second deposition if there is good cause. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.610(b).) Defendant has shown good cause exist because Plaintiff indicated at the first deposition that responsive documents exist but failed to produce any responsive documents at that deposition. Further, although Plaintiff’s counsel purportedly represented at a status conference that no such responsive documents exist, Defendant should have the opportunity at a second deposition to inquire about Plaintiff’s representation that responsive documents existed.

Thus, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Second Deposition is granted. Plaintiff is ordered to appear at a second deposition and produce responsive documents within thirty (30) days’ notice of this court’s order.

 

c.      Sanctions

 

Defendant requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the sum of $2,370.00. (Motion to

Compel Second Plaintiff’s Second Deposition p. 7.) This includes the $60.00 motion filing fee and

7.7 hours of attorneys’ fees for preparing the Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Second Deposition,

reviewing the opposition, preparing a reply, and attending the hearing. (Zade Declaration ¶¶ 20

22.)

 

Defendant argues sanctions are warranted under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.030 and 2023.010, which reference sanctioning a person engaging in a misuse of the discovery process. (Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Second Deposition p. 6.)

 

Defendant also cites section 2025.450(g)(1), which authorizes sanctions when a party, “without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice[.]” (Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Second Deposition p. 6.)

 

This court is not convinced that Plaintiff engaged in a misuse of the discovery process as described in Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010. Further, given Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel have given inconsistent representations regarding whether responsive documents exist, this court also declines to award sanctions under section 2025.450(g)(1).

 

III.           MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES

 

a.      Timeliness

 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(c).) This deadline is jurisdictional, and courts are without authority to grant untimely motions.¿ (Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)¿When verifications are provided electronically, the deadline is extended by two court days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

 

Plaintiff served verified responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories on January 23, 2024. (Zade Declaration Ex. B p. 14.) On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff agreed to provide supplemental responses, and executed a verification to the supplemental Form Interrogatories. (Zade Declaration Ex. C.)

 

Although Plaintiff agreed to provide supplemental responses, there is no evidence in the record

that the parties agreed to extend Defendant’s deadline to file the Motion to Compel Further

Responses to Form Interrogatories.  Accordingly, Defendant was required to file this motion

within forty-five days after Plaintiff served the supplemental verified responses on June 11, 2024, plus an additional two court days because it was served electronically.

 

Forty-five days after June 11, 2024, was July 26, 2024. Two court days after July 26, 2024, was

July 30, 2024. Defendant filed the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories

on August 13, 2024.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories was untimely filed.

 

IV.           MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION

 

a.     Meet and Confer

 

“Unlike a¿motion¿to¿compel¿further¿responses, a¿motion¿to¿compel¿responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet¿and¿confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)

 

Thus, Defendant was not required to file a meet and confer declaration for the Motion to Compel Response to Request for Production.

 

b.     Legal Standard

 

“I]f a party to whom¿a¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed fails to serve a timely response to it,… [t]he party to whom the¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed waives any objection to the demand [and]…[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the¿demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.300.) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that . . . (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).)

 

“[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(c).

 

c.      Discussion

 

On June 28, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with the Request for Production of Documents, Set Two. (Zade Declaration ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s responses were due on July 29, 2024. (Zade Declaration ¶ 4.) Plaintiff did not serve Defendant with responses. (Zade Declaration ¶ 9.)

 

Thus, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Response to Request for Production is granted. Plaintiff is

ordered to serve Defendant with responses within thirty (30) days’ notice of this order.

 

d.     Sanctions

 

Defendant seeks $1,380.00 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff. (Motion to Compel Response to Request for Production p. 4.) Defendant’s declaration combines the time spent on this Motion with the Motion to Compel Response to Special Interrogatories. Accordingly, this court addresses the sanctions relating to the Motion to Compel Response to Request for Production below.

 

V.              MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSE TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES

 

a.     Timeliness

 

There is no time limit to file a motion to compel when no responses have been served. (Sinaiko

Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 411

[“The 45-day limit . . . applies only to motions to compel further responses to interrogatories under § 2030.300 . . . Section 2030.290 contains no such limitation[.]”])

 

b.     Meet and Confer

 

“Unlike a¿motion¿to¿compel¿further¿responses, a¿motion¿to¿compel¿responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet¿and¿confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 404.)

 

Thus, Defendant was not required to file a meet and confer declaration for the Motion to Compel Response to Special Interrogatories. Regardless, Defendant’s counsel’s declaration states counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter, but Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. (Zade Declaration ¶¶ 6-7.)

 

c.      Legal Standard

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response . . . [that party] waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290.) “The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).)

 

d.     Discussion

 

On June 28, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with Special Interrogatories, Set Two. (Zade Declaration ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s responses were due on July 29, 2024. (Zade Declaration ¶ 4.) Plaintiff did not serve Defendant with responses. (Zade Declaration ¶ 9.)

 

Thus, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Response to Special Interrogatories is granted. Plaintiff is

ordered to serve Defendant with responses within thirty (30) days’ notice of this order.

 

e.      Sanctions

 

Defendant seeks $1,380.00 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff for the Motion to Compel response to Special Interrogatories. (Motion to Compel Response to Special Interrogatories p. 4.) In combination with the Motion to Compel Response to Request for Production, this includes a $60.00 motion filing fee, 4.5 hours of attorneys’ fees for preparing the two motions and supporting documents, and 4.5 hours for reviewing the oppositions, preparing replies, and appearing at the hearing on these motions. (Zade Declaration ¶¶ 10-12.)

 

This court finds that Plaintiff did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances make imposing sanctions unjust.

Accordingly, this court grants Defendant’s request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $960.00, reflecting a $60.00 motion filing fee, two hours for preparing the two motions and related documents, and one hour for attending the hearing on these motions, at $300.00 per hour.

COURT’S TENTATIVE DECISION RE:     

 Demurrer with Motion to Strike

 

 

(1) Sustain the demurrer with leave to amend; (2) Grant the motion to strike (as it pertains to punitive damages) without leave to amend.

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

This is a legal malpractice case. On February 29, 2024, Plaintiffs Nikal, LTD, AJ Rock, LTD, Blue Sky Rainbow, LP, GNM, L.P., and Animal Kingdom, L.P. (“Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants Areg Allen Sarkissian, Sarkissian Law Group, Inc., (“Defendants”) and Does 1 through 100, alleging twenty-three causes of action for breach of a written contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duty.

On May 1, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer and motion to strike. On October 11, 2024, this court sustained the demurrer, granted the motion to strike, and granted Plaintiffs leave to amend the complaint.

 

On November 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint.

 

On November 20, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer with motion to strike. On December 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed oppositions. On December 10, 2024, Defendants filed a reply.

 

II.             PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

A.    Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., §¿430.40(a).)¿ 

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).)¿¿ 

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. “Any period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

 

Plaintiffs served Defendants with the second amended complaint by email on November 12, 2024. Defendants filed the demurrer and motion to strike on November 20, 2024. Accordingly, the demurrer and motion to strike were filed within 30 days after service of the complaint.

 

The hearing is set for December 17, 2024. Defendants served Plaintiffs with the demurrer and motion to strike by email on November 20, 2024. Sixteen court days before December 17, 2024, plus an additional two court days for electronic service, was November 19, 2024. Thus, the demurrer and motion to strike were untimely. 

 

Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281.) This court will exercise its discretion and consider the motion to strike and demurrer.

 

The oppositions and reply were timely filed under section 1005.

 

B.    Meet and Confer

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)¿ 

¿ 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) also requires parties to meet and confer before filing a motion to strike.¿ 

 

Defendants attach no declarations to the demurrer or motion to strike stating Defendants satisfied the meet and confer requirements. Thus, this court finds that Defendants did not satisfy their meet and confer obligations.

However, section 430.41, subdivision (a)(4) states that “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” Similarly, section 435.5, subdivision (a)(4) states that “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to grant or deny the motion to strike.”

Accordingly, although Defendants failed to satisfy their statutory obligations, this court may not overrule the demurrer or deny the motion to strike on those grounds.

III.           DEMURRER

 

A.    Legal Standard

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

 

B.    Discussion

 

Defendants argue: (1) Plaintiffs lack standing to bring this suit because the causes of action in the first amended complaint are defective; and (2) Plaintiffs have improperly been joined in a single lawsuit. (Demurrer pp. 7-12.)

 

i.       Standing

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs lack standing to bring this suit because the causes of action presented in the first amended complaint are defective. (Demurrer p. 7.) Specifically, Defendants argue, “[P]laintiffs’ claims for breach of contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duty lack merit. For breach of contract, they have not shown any material breach by the defendant, who fulfilled all contractual obligations. The legal malpractice claim is unsupported, as there is no evidence of deviation from the standard of care. Additionally, the breach of fiduciary duty claim is baseless, as the defendant consistently adhered to fiduciary obligations of loyalty, care, and good faith. Without evidence to support these claims, the essential elements for each cause of action are unmet, making the claims legally meritless.” (Demurrer pp. 7-8.)

 

Defendants’ arguments appear to ask this court to weigh evidence that has not yet been presented by Plaintiffs against evidence Defendants improperly submit within the demurrer. ¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs., supra, 153 Cal.App.4th at p. 1315.) This court must court assume the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) Accordingly, at this stage, this court is not considering whether there is “evidence” supporting causes of action and is solely concerned with whether Plaintiffs have properly plead their causes of action. Defendants’ arguments are meritless.

 

ii.               Joinder

 

Defendants argue that the Plaintiffs have improperly been joined in a single suit, despite there being no single transaction or occurrence. (Demurrer p. 11.)

 

A party may demur to a complaint because “[t]here is a defect or misjoinder of parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(d).)

 

“All persons may join in one action as plaintiffs if: (1) They assert any right to relief jointly, severally, or in the alternative, in respect of or arising out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences and if any question of law or fact common to all these persons will arise in the action; or (2) They have a claim, right, or interest adverse to the defendant in the property or controversy which is the subject of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc, § 378.)

 

Courts broadly construe section 378. (Petersen v. Bank of America Corp. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 238, 250.) “A common interest in the subject matter of the action has been found to exist where four plaintiffs sued upon four separate causes of action on common counts for money had and received in connection with purported sales of securities in violation of the Corporate Securities Act. [Citations.] And a right to relief arising out of the same transaction or series of transactions exists where several plaintiffs sue for personal injuries suffered in the same accident [citations]; where two persons are falsely imprisoned by the same acts committed at the same time [citation]; where taxpayers join to challenge the validity of assessments or recover taxes [citations]; where holders of separate oil leases sue for an alleged trespass resulting from the drilling of one well by defendant [citation]; and where plaintiffs whose causes of action are based upon misrepresentation or conspiracy and fraud allege a single scheme, depending on the same basic misrepresentations and leading to a series of transactions exactly similar in kind and manner of operation [citations].” (Moe v. Anderson (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 826, 832.)

 

In Aghajl v. Bank of America, N.A. (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1118, 1121, the Court held a trial court did not abuse its discretion in sustaining a demurrer without leave to amend because:

 

Each of the plaintiffs that counsel addressed had unique transactions or experiences giving rise to their alleged claims. For example, plaintiff Isabel Felix alleged that Bank of America added fees, interest, and other charges to her principal balance when it acted as servicer of her loan, while plaintiff Clifton Hunter alleged that Ocwen sent him a letter stating that it was sending a representative to his rental property to change the locks and winterize the property, charged him for additional insurance on the property when it was not needed, and sent a man to break into the property when no one was there. Plaintiff Joseph Rankin, on the other hand, alleged that Bank of America issued mortgage interest statements that underreported the amount of mortgage he paid, and plaintiff Fernando Burgos alleged that Nationstar charged him more than was agreed to in a loan modification agreement. Each of these claims clearly arises from a different transaction or occurrence and presents distinct questions of law or fact. Therefore, even if plaintiffs presented sufficient facts to state valid UCL claims (which they did not), they could not be presented in a joint action under section 378.

In contrast, in Petersen v. Bank of America Corp. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 238, 247, a trial court sustained a demurrer without leave to amend based on a misjoinder of plaintiffs. On appeal, the Court found joinder was permissible and “identified the two core aspects of the common plan alleged in the third amended complaint that necessarily will entail common evidence (1) whether Countrywide deliberately encouraged dishonest appraisals and (2) whether Countrywide encouraged its loan officers to conceal loan terms” which “devolve into several questions of law or fact bearing on liability . . .” (Id. at p. 252.)

In this case, this court sustained the demurrer to the complaint because Plaintiffs had not alleged the Plaintiffs could be joined in one action.

 

Plaintiffs amended the complaint by adding the following paragraphs to the first amended complaint.

 

At all material times, Landmark Global Management, LLC dba Landmark Realty and Property Management ("Landmark") was and is a limited liability company organized and existing under the laws of the State of California. At all material times, Landmark has acted as an agent and property manager for each of the plaintiffs named herein and, in that capacity, referred each of the plaintiffs to defendants for the handling of their legal needs (including without limitation representation in unlawful detainer cases and landlord-tenant disputes) and was the point of contact for all communications with defendants relating to the legal services for which they were retained.

 

The claims alleged herein against defendants and each of them arose out of a common nexus involving a series of transactions on behalf of each of the plaintiffs,. These transactions were all materially similar in kind and manner of operation, to wit, the litigation of claims for unlawful detainer as against tenants of each of the plaintiffs named herein, all of whom were principals of Landmark and referred to defendants by Landmark. At all material times, Landmark was the agent for each of the plaintiffs as to each of the unlawful detainer cases enumerated herein, the conduit whereby each of the plaintiffs retained defendants and the party to whom defendants reported as to all legal affairs they handled on behalf of the plaintiffs. The legal services by defendants were performed at the behest of Landmark for the benefit of each of the plaintiffs, thus rendering the services rendered to each plaintiff part of a series of interrelated transactions and occurrences sufficient to meet the criteria for joinder under Code of Civil Procedure §378.

 

(FAC ¶¶ 8-9.)

 

The allegation that each Plaintiff had the same agent may mean there will be common facts and issues among the Plaintiffs. (FAC ¶¶ 8-9.) However, as alleged in the first amended complaint, this case involves five different Plaintiffs, alleging twenty-three causes of action, for nine separate underlying matters.  (See Opposition to Motion to Strike p. 3.) Like Aghajl v. Bank of America, N.A., supra, 247 Cal.App.4th at p. 1121, where each plaintiff had unique transactions and experiences giving rise to their claims, each Plaintiff here alleges different circumstances giving rise to the causes of action for breach of contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duties. For example, each Plaintiff alleges they entered into a contract or contracts with Defendants at separate times. (FAC ¶¶ 18, 37, 56, 75, 83, 91, 110, 129, 148.) The first amended complaint further alleges separate, distinct facts for how Defendants committed legal malpractice as to each Plaintiff. (FAC ¶¶ 27, 46, 65, 100, 119, 138, 157.) And the first amended complaint alleges separate, distinct facts for how Defendants breached their fiduciary duties to each Plaintiff. (FAC ¶¶ 32, 51, 70, 105, 124, 143, 162.) Other than the fact the Plaintiffs used the same agent, each of these transactions is alleged to be entirely separate from the others. Accordingly, the Plaintiffs causes of action do not arise out of the “same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences.” (Code Civ. Proc, § 378.)

Accordingly, this court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

IV.           MOTION TO STRIKE

 

A.    Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.)

 

B.    Discussion

Defendants argue this court should strike the punitive damages claim in the first amended complaint. (Notice of Motion p. 2.)

Plaintiffs argue Defendants’ motion to strike is deficient because California Rule of Court, rule 3.1322 requires “[a] notice of motion to strike a portion of a pleading [to] quote in full the portions sought to be stricken except where the motion is to strike an entire paragraph, cause of action, count, or defense.” (Opposition to Motion to Strike p. 2.)

Defendants’ notice of motion to strike states that Defendants “move for an order to strike the following portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint  . . . Punitive damages as to the third, sixth, ninth, fourteenth, seventeenth, twentieth, and twenty-third cause of action” and “[s]ame transaction or [o]ccurrence.” (Notice of Motion to Strike.) Accordingly, Defendants’ notice of motion does not quote in full the portions sought to be stricken. Nor do Defendants seek to strike entire paragraphs, causes of action, counts, or defenses.

In its October 11, 2024, ruling, this court informed Defendants they failed to follow the California Rules of Court by filing a sixty-page demurrer and stated “[a]lthough this court does not decline to consider the demurrer, this court reminds Defendants of the existence and importance of abiding by the California Rules of Court.” (10/11/24 Minute Order.) Likewise, here, this court will consider the substance of Defendants’ motion. However, Defendants are instructed to comply with all applicable procedural rules in the future.

In its October 11, 2024, order, this court granted the motion to strike, with leave to amend, as it related to the punitive damages allegations, stating:

This court finds that Plaintiffs’ allegations do not rise to the level of fraud, oppression, or malice.  Accordingly, this court sustains the motion to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations, with leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court, supra, 180 Cal. App.3d at pp. 303-304 [“[A] motion to strike paragraphs in a complaint should be granted with leave to amend where . . . the defect, though one of substance, may possibly be cured by supplying omitted allegations, and the plaintiff has not had a fair opportunity to do so”].)

 

Defendants argue the first amended complaint has not cured this defect because “Plaintiffs failed to make any substantive amendments to remedy the issues related to punitive damages in their amended complaint.” (Motion to Strike p. 6.) Further, in the reply, Defendants state that “in the amended complaint Plaintiffs have made no new allegations that rise to the level of fraud, oppression, or malice. The only change in the first amended complaint is two paragraphs to support Plaintiffs’ position that all five Plaintiffs can join together in this lawsuit.” (Reply p. 5.)

Plaintiffs’ opposition to the motion to strike argues that punitive damages are available as a matter of law for breach of fiduciary duty, which is the same argument Plaintiffs made in opposing Defendants’ first motion to strike. (Opposition to First Motion to Strike pp. 9-10; Opposition to Motion to Strike pp. 5-8.) Plaintiffs do not argue in their opposition that they added any allegations to the first amended complaint relating to the punitive damages claim that were not in the complaint. Accordingly, this court grants Defendants’ motion to strike as to the punitive damages claim.

Defendants also argue the paragraphs referring to Landmark Global Management, LLC, should be stricken because they are irrelevant. (Motion to Strike p. 7.) This court sustained the demurrer based on misjoinder of parties. Accordingly, this issue is moot.

V.              LEAVE TO AMEND

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, ‘leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.’” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.)¿ 

When a Plaintiff fails to describe “what additional facts [the Plaintiff] would plead . . . [the Plaintiff] has not satisfied [their] burden of showing that [the Plaintiff] can amend the complaint to cure its defects.” (Mohler v. County of Santa Clara (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 418, 428.)

This court finds there is a reasonable possibility Plaintiffs can cure the defect relating to the misjoinder of parties and grants Plaintiffs leave to amend as to that issue.

As to the punitive damages issue, Plaintiffs were given an opportunity to amend the complaint to add allegations addressing the punitive damages issues but failed to do so. Moreover, Plaintiffs’ opposition cites nothing showing Plaintiffs can successfully add allegations to the first amended complaint to cure this deficiency.

Accordingly, this court grants the motion to strike without leave to amend as to the punitive damages claims in the first amended complaint.

VI.           CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend. This court also grants the motion to strike, without leave to amend, as it relates to the punitive damages claim.
