ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On October 28, 2022, Plaintiff Nationwide Insurance Company of America (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Andrea Nicole Johnson (“Defendant”) and Does 1-10 alleging a common counts cause of action.

 

On January 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation, Settlement Agreement, and Release (the “Settlement Agreement”).

 

On February 3, 2023, the Hon. Mark E. Windham signed an order dismissing the action and retained jurisdiction under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. (2/3/23 Order.)

 

On July 31, 2024 Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement (the “Motion”).

 

No opposition was filed.   

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6: 

 
(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following: 

 

(1) The party. 

(2) An attorney who represents the party. 

(3) If an insurer is defending and indemnifying a party to the action, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the party’s behalf. This paragraph does not apply if the party whom the insurer is defending would be liable under the terms of the settlement for any amount above the policy limits.” 

 

The Settlement Agreement filed by Plaintiff was signed by Plaintiff’s authorized representative Lisa Berry and by Plaintiff’s counsel on January 20, 2023, and by Defendant on January 27, 2023. (1/27/23 Settlement, pp. 5-6.)

 

The Settlement provides the matter would be settled for a total sum of $5,248.46 to be paid by Defendant at a rate of $90.98 per month, beginning February 1, 2023 and continuing on the first day of each month thereafter until paid in full. (1/27/23 Settlement, ¶ 3.)

 

The Settlement further states that failure to make a timely payment constitutes a default and, in the event of default, there is no grace period or notice of default to be given to Defendant. (1/27/23 Settlement, ¶ 6.) When default occurs, the Settlement states “Judgment may be entered against Defendant without notice in the sum of $4,902.58, plus first appearance fees in the sum of $225.00 less payments received.”  (1/27/23 Settlement, ¶ 6.)

The Court finds the Settlement to be valid and enforceable under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. Plaintiff provides evidence that Defendant made partial payment. (Smith Decl., ¶ 6.) As of the date this Motion was filed, however, Plaintiff had received neither further payments from Defendant nor any emails stating she will pay any amount. (Smith Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff also presents evidence that, although the Settlement states the parties agreed to no grace period, Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to reach out to Defendant regarding the overdue payment to no avail. (Smith Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

However, the Court notes various issues with the numerical amounts and calculations used by Plaintiff as well as Plaintiff’s requested Judgment sum.

 

Plaintiff requests that a judgment of $3,203.14,[1] based on the principal amount of $1,175 plus pre-judgment costs in the amount of $4,902.58, less payment received in the amount of $1,699.44, be entered against Defendant. (Motion, p. 5; Smith Decl., p. 8.) To describe the payment amount received from Defendant, Plaintiff states Defendant “made thirteen payments of $35.88 for a total amount of $1,699.44.”  (Smith Decl. ¶ 6.)

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Plaintiff’s calculation for the Judgment differs from the calculation listed in the definite terms of the Default section of the Settlement, which states:  “If payment is not received on or before the due date as set forth above then Judgment may be entered against [Defendant] without notice in the sum of $4,902.58, plus first appearance fees in the sum of 225.00 less payments received.”  (1/27/23 Settlement, ¶ 6.) Accordingly, Plaintiff’s calculation must be modified to properly reflect the Settlement’s terms.

 

Next, the Court notes that Plaintiff made multiple mistakes when describing payments received from Defendant – namely, Smith declares Defendant made “thirteen payments of $35.88”; however, $90.98 was the agreed-upon monthly payment in the Settlement. (1/27/23 Settlement, ¶ 3.) Further, thirteen installments of $90.98 totals $1,182.74, not $1,699.44 as Plaintiff and Smith posit. (Notice of Motion, p. 2; Motion, pp. 3, 6-8; Smith Decl., ¶¶ 6-8.) Accordingly, Plaintiff must correct its Motion and supporting evidence before a judgment can be rendered.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion is CONTINUED. Plaintiff is ordered to file an Amended Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement with supplemental papers correcting the errors noted herein.

Case Number: 23STLC05858    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         Weds., October 9, 2024

Case Name:                             DealerSync, Inc. v. US Auto Leasing, Inc. d/b/a Prestige Auto Sales

Case No.:                                23STLC05858 

Motion:                                   Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default and Default Judgment

Moving Party:                         Defendant US Auto Leasing, Inc. d/b/a Prestige Auto Sales

Responding Party:                   Plaintiff DealerSync, Inc.

Notice:                                    OK

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Defendant US Auto Leasing d/b/a Prestige Auto Sales’s Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default and Default Judgment is GRANTED. 

 

The Default entered on 02/29/2024 and Default Judgment entered on 05/03/2024 are hereby set aside and vacated.

 

                                                Defendant is ordered to file its proposed answer within ten (10) days of notice of this Court’s order.

 

The Court sets trial in this action for October 7, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. in Department 25 of the Spring Street Courthouse.

 

Discovery and all other trial related deadlines are to comport with the new trial date.

Parties must comply with the trial requirements as set forth in the court's Third Amended Standing Order for Limited Civil Cases (effective February 24, 2020).

All trial documents are to be electronically filed at least ten (10) days prior to the trial date.

Parties should be prepared to submit a JOINT Trial Readiness Binder / Exhibit Binder, and to personally appear on the date of trial.

Plaintiff’s Request for Defendant to pay a penalty for compensatory legal fees and costs is DENIED.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

            In March 2018, Plaintiff DealerSync, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) and Defendant US Auto Leasing, Inc. d/b/a Prestige Auto Sales (“Defendant”) and Does 1-10 entered into a software license agreement, wherein Plaintiff agreed to license proprietary dealership management software products for the “Premier” package to Defendant. (Compl., ¶ 9.) In February 2021, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a software license agreement (“Agreement”), wherein Plaintiff agreed to “license proprietary dealership management software products for 12 months for the ‘Elite’ Package, which required Defendant to pay $1,699 per month.”  (Compl., ¶ 9.)  Defendant renewed the Agreement for an additional year but cancelled the Agreement six months later.  (Compl., ¶¶ 11-13.) Plaintiff provided its services pursuant to the Agreement, and Defendant failed to pay the full balance owed on the Agreement. (Compl., ¶¶ 10-17.)

 

On September 13, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this action against Defendant and Does 1-10 alleging (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Open Book Account, (3) Account Stated, and (4) Goods and Services Rendered. Plaintiff filed proof of service indicating the summons and complaint were served by personal service on Defendant’s registered agent for service of process, “Edward Paul Hiskall at 2900 Medinah Way, Modesto CA 95355.”

 

            Defendant failed to file an Answer, and the Court entered default against Defendant on February 29, 2024.  

 

            On May 3, 2024, the Court entered a default judgment against Defendant. However, it appears the default judgment was not served upon Defendant until May 31, 2024. (5/31 Notice of Entry of Judgment, p. 5.)

 

            On August 6, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default and Default Judgment (the “Motion”) on the grounds of mistake and/or extrinsic mistake. Defendant attached a declaration from its President who admitted he made the mistake, an attorney declaration, and an Answer to the Complaint.

 

            On September 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition.

 

            On September 30, 2024, Defendant filed a Reply.  

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Defendant moves to set aside the entry of default and default judgment on the grounds of mistake and/or extrinsic mistake pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b).  Specifically, Defendant contends it failed to timely respond to Plaintiff’s complaint because its President Nofan Jamaleddin mistakenly believed this case was another case – namely, Bolton v. US Auto Leasing, Inc., et al., Case No. CV-23-001284 in Stanislaus County Superior Court – also filed against Defendant. (Motion, ¶ 1, Exh. A, Jamaleddin Decl., ¶ 3.)  As the subject matter of the lawsuit regarded advertising costs for a business in Stanislaus County, Defendant mistook the matter for Bolton, which was litigated in Stanislaus County, and “failed to recognize the issue until the Judgment was served upon it.”  (Motion, p. 2.)  Due to this mistake, Defendant believed the documents in this matter were already sent to Defendant’s counsel and an Answer did not need to be filed. (Motion, ¶ 1, Exh. A, Jamaleddin Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) 

 

OPPOSITION

 

            In Opposition, Plaintiff contends Defendant failed to show it is entitled to equitable relief because neither the Motion nor accompanying declarations provide reasoning for why Defendants believe they have a meritorious defense.  Further, Plaintiff contends Defendant’s Motion should be denied because it does not appear Defendant exercised due diligence in getting the judgment set aside. (Opposition, pp. 4-5.) Finally, Plaintiff argues that in the event the Court grants Defendant’s Motion, the Court should direct Defendant to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs totaling $1,000 to Plaintiff and its counsel, Madison Law, APC. (Opposition, p. 6.)

 

REPLY

 

            In Reply, Defendant rejects Plaintiff’s arguments in their entirety and alleges Plaintiff argues “two completely conflicting positions.” (Reply, p. 1.) Defendant further contends this is an “honest mistake by the moving [D]efendant,” and Defendant acted with diligence by notifying defense counsel of his mistake as soon as he became aware of it. (Reply p. 2.) Finally, Defendant concedes to pay Plaintiff’s request for costs up to $1,000. (Reply, p. 2.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.                   Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default and Default Judgment

 

A.    Legal Standard

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief.  (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)  Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b) contains two distinct provisions for relief from default.  The first provision is discretionary and broad in scope, providing: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).) 

 

The general underlying purpose of Section 473, subdivision (b) is to promote the determination of actions on their merits.  (Even Zohar Const. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 838-839.)  “Apart from any statute, courts have the inherent authority to vacate a default and default judgment on equitable grounds such as extrinsic fraud or extrinsic mistake.” (Bae v. T.D. Service Co. of Arizona (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 89, 97.)  A motion seeking such equitable relief “‘may be brought on such ground even though the statutory period [for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b)] has run.’ [Citation.]”  (Ibid.)  Unlike motions to vacate under section 473, where any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief, “[w]hen a default judgment has been obtained, equitable relief may be given only in exceptional circumstances.” (Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 981 (“Rappleyea”).) 

 

Notably, in limited civil cases, the grounds for equitable relief are extended to include “inadvertence or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 86.)

 

To obtain equitable relief for extrinsic mistake or fraud, the defaulting party must demonstrate: (1) a meritorious defense; (2) a satisfactory excuse for not defending the original action; and (3) diligence in seeking to set aside the default once the default was discovered. (Rappleyea, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 884.) ¿

 

II.                Discussion

 

A.    Timeliness

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Defendant’s Motion was filed on August 6, 2024, which is less than six months after the entry of default on February 29, 2024.

 

 Here, Defendant appears to raise a claim for relief due to mistake by a party, which is a form of discretionary relief that requires application for relief from default or default judgment to be filed no later than six months from the time of entry of default. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).) While Defendant’s attorney, Jakrun Sodhi, submitted a declaration in support of the Motion, Sodhi does not appear to admit any mistake besides that of Defendant. (Sodhi Decl.)

 

Since Defendant filed the Motion within six months from the entry of default, Defendant’s Motion is thus timely as it pertains to a request for discretionary relief from default or default judgment.

 

As there is no six-month time limit for moving to set aside a default and default judgment based on extrinsic mistake or fraud, Defendant’s Motion is also timely as it pertains to this request as well.

 

B.     Discretionary Relief

 

While Plaintiff appears to have interpreted the Motion to solely request equitable relief for extrinsic mistake, the Court notes Defendant also cites to and makes a request regarding discretionary statutory relief.  (Motion, ¶¶ 3-4.) Given the Court has already determined the Motion was timely filed, the Court will address the merits of a claim for discretionary relief by way of party mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).

 

Here, the Court finds there is sufficient evidence to warrant setting aside the default judgment against Defendant. Indeed, Defendant submitted evidence that its President, Nofan Jamaleddin, admits: “There were two cases I was served on or around the same time.  I mistakenly thought the documents on this instant matter were sent to my counsel to defend my case. Unfortunately, the documents that were sent to my counsel were the documents for the [Bolton case] not for this instant case. At no point during this time did I realize that an Answer needed to be filed.”  (Motion, ¶ 1, Exh. A, Jamaleddin Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.)  Defendant also submitted evidence that Defendant’s attorney, Jakrun Sodhi, confirms his office represented Defendant in the Bolton matter, which was litigated.  (Sodhi Decl. ¶ 4.)

 

            The Court finds there is sufficient evidence to suggest Defendant’s lack of response to the Complaint (or any papers received in this matter thereafter) was due to his continuous mistaken belief of a material fact – namely, the instant case was in all respects a completely different case. (See Hopkins & Carley v. Gens (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1410 [defendant’s burden to demonstrate “that due to some mistake, either of fact or of law, of himself or of his counsel, or through some inadvertence, surprise or neglect which may properly considered excusable, the judgment or order from which he seeks relief should be reverse”] (emphasis in original).) 

 

Accordingly, the Court finds the entry of default was caused by Defendant’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. And, having considered the moving papers and evidence, the Court finds this Motion to set aside default and default judgment was timely, and the requisite proposed responsive pleading (Answer) was filed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).) 

 

C.    Equitable Relief

 

Given this Court’s finding on discretionary relief, the Court declines to address Defendant’s claim for equitable relief. However, the Court notes Defendant failed to put forth cognizable arguments as to the elements for extrinsic mistake in the Motion, and only cursorily addressed the elements in its Reply brief.

 

D.    Fees and Costs

 

“Whenever the court grants relief from a default, default judgment, or dismissal based on any of the provisions of this section, the court may do any of the following:

 

(A) Impose a penalty of no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) upon an offending attorney or party.

(B) Direct that an offending attorney pay an amount no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) to the State Bar Client Security Fund.

(C) Grant other relief as is appropriate.”

 

(Code of Civ. Proc., § 473(c).)

 

Plaintiff requests the Court to direct Defendant to pay a penalty of $1,000 to Plaintiff and its counsel, Madison Law, APC, for reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs. (Opposition, p. 6.) Defendant agrees to pay this request should the Court order. (Reply, p. 2.)

 

While the Court may impose a penalty of up to $1,000 in sanctions, as noted in the cited authority above, the award is discretionary. Here, the circumstances of the reasonable mistake by Defendant’s President, Nofan Jamaleddin, do not seem to warrant a sanctions penalty against Defendant. Moreover, to the extent Plaintiff seeks a sanctions award to compensate for costs it incurred as a result of Defendant’s mistake, the requests for either $1,000 or $789.95 are not sufficiently supported. (Opp., Sirey Decl., ¶9.) 

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request.

           

III.             Conclusion

           

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Set Aside the Entry of Default and Default Judgment is GRANTED.

 

The default entered on February 29, 2024, and Default Judgment entered on May 3, 2024, are HEREBY SET ASIDE AND VACATED.  Defendant is ordered to file its proposed answer within ten (10) days of this Court’s order.

All trial documents are to be electronically filed at least ten (10) days prior to the trial date.

Parties should be prepared to submit a JOINT Trial Readiness Binder / Exhibit Binder, and to personally appear on the date of trial.

 

Plaintiff’s Request for Defendant to pay a penalty for compensatory legal fees and costs is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

 

On February 22, 2024, Petitioner Quality Loan Service Corp. (“Petitioner”) commenced this petition regarding unresolved claims and deposit of undistributed surplus proceeds of trustee’s sale.

 

On March 1, 2024, Petitioner filed an unopposed motion to deposit surplus funds and to discharge petitioner.

 

On May 9, 2024, the Court heard Petitioner’s motion, but found it did not meet the requirements of Civil Code § 2924j because Petitioner failed to (1) include the date of sale of the property, (2) include a description of the property, (3) list the names and addresses of all person sent notice, (4) provide an affirmative statement of due diligence, (5) provide the amount of surplus to be deposited under Civil Code § 2924j(c), (6) provide a copy of the trustee’s sale guarantee or statement regarding other relevant information regarding the priority of the potential claimants under Civil Code § 2924j(c), and (7) provide an explanation or support for the $3,137.32 in attorney’s fees and costs sought. The Court continued Petitioner’s motion until June 27, 2024, so that Petitioner could file supplemental papers addressing these deficiencies.

 

On May 20, 2024, Petitioner’s counsel, Matthew E. Podmenik, submitted a supplemental declaration (“Podmenik Supplemental Declaration” or “Podmenik Supp. Decl.”) addressing the deficiencies requested by the Court.

 

Due to exigent circumstances, the Court continued the hearing on the Petitioner’s supplemental papers until August 7, 2024. At the hearing on August 7, 2024, Petitioner represented that it had been contacted by potential heirs or claimants to the surplus funds. The Court again continued the hearing on Petitioner’s motion regarding discharge of Petitioner until October 9, 2024, so that the Petitioner could file a supplemental declaration regarding potential claimants.

 

On October 1, 2024, Petitioner’s counsel, Megan Boyd, filed a supplemental declaration (“Boyd Supplemental Declaration” or “Boyd Supp. Decl.”) addressing the Petitioner’s communications with potential claimants, as requested by the Court.

OPPOSITION

 

            None filed.

 

REPLY

 

            None filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.                   Legal Standard

If there is a surplus after payment of a secured obligation, a trustee who does not file an interpleader must serve by mail a prescribed notice of such surplus on all persons entitled to notices of default under Civil Code §§ 2924j(b) & (c). If a conflict exists that the trustee cannot resolve with due diligence, the trustee may, on additional notice, file with the court a declaration of the unresolved claims and deposit into court the amount remaining in dispute. (Civ. Code, §§ 2924j(b), (c).) A summary procedure is provided for the court's resolution of the claims, which must be heard within 90 days of the deposit. (Civ. Code, §§ 2924j(c), (d).)

            Further, “If, after due diligence, the trustee is unable to determine the priority of the written claims received by the trustee to the trustee's sale surplus of multiple persons or if the trustee determines there is a conflict between potential claimants, the trustee may file a declaration of the unresolved claims and deposit with the clerk of the superior court of the county in which the sale occurred, that portion of the sales proceeds that cannot be distributed, less any fees charged by the clerk pursuant to this subdivision. The declaration shall specify the date of the trustee's sale, a description of the property, the names and addresses of all persons sent notice pursuant to subdivision (a), a statement that the trustee exercised due diligence pursuant to subdivision (b), that the trustee provided written notice as required by subdivisions (a) and (d) and the amount of the sales proceeds deposited by the trustee with the court. Further, the trustee shall submit a copy of the trustee's sales guarantee and any information relevant to the identity, location, and priority of the potential claimants with the court and shall file proof of service of the notice required by subdivision (d) on all persons described in subdivision (a).” (Civ. Code, § 2924j(c).)

II.                Discussion

The Court is satisfied that the Podmenik Supplemental Declaration establishes Petitioner’s compliance with due diligence requirements, pursuant to Civil Code § 2924j(c). First, the Podmenik Supplemental Declaration establishes that the real property was sold at a non-judicial foreclosure sale on October 19, 2023. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Second, the declaration provides the legal description for the property, which states that the property “is situated in the City of Long Beach, County of Los Angeles, State of California, and is described as follows: Lot(s) 131 of Tract No. 13031, in the City of Long Beach, County of Los Angeles, State of California, as per map recorded in book 258 page(s) 32 to 35 inclusive of maps, in the Office of the County Recorder of said county. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Third, the declaration provides that, on November 11, 2023, counsel’s office sent Notice of Surplus Funds From Trustee’s Sale to all potential claimants and their addresses of record, and establishes that such Notice was sent to the following parties: (1) Murlene Everett, (2) The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, (3) The Secretary of Housing and Urban Development c/o Seattle Mortgage Company, (4) The Estate of Murlene Everett, Deceased, (5) The Heirs or Devisees of Murlene Everett, Deceased, and (6) The Estate of Murlene Everett, Deceased c/o Tiasseika Everett, Petitioner. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A.)

 

Fourth, the declaration establishes that Petitioner, through its counsel, did not receive any claims from the Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, and, thereafter, mailed a Second Notice of Surplus Funds to all parties entitled to notice on January 4, 2024. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶ 7; Exh. C.) However, because Petitioner received no responses, it was unable to determine how to distribute the remaining surplus funds and, thus, encountered a conflict it could not resolve without assistance of the Court. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶ 8.)

 

Fifth, the declaration establishes that the amount of surplus funds in dispute totals $26,946.80, and provides the calculation used to determine such amount. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶¶ 10, 11.)

 

Sixth, the declaration provides a true and correct copy of the Trustee Sale Guarantee and surplus endorsements. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. B.)

 

Seventh, the declaration provides that the $3,137.32 in costs and attorney’s fees requested were incurred by the Petitioner in connection with the distribution of the proceeds. (Podmenik Supp. Decl., ¶ 9.)

Further, the Court is satisfied that the Boyd Supplemental Declaration establishes Petitioner’s due diligence requirements as to Petitioner’s communications with potential heirs or claimants and justifies discharging Petitioner’s remaining duties.

 

First, the Boyd Supplemental Declaration establishes that, prior to filing the instant petition and motion, Petitioner did not receive any written communications from any potential heirs, was contacted by phone by potential heir Tiasseika Everett on multiple occasion beginning on November 27, 2023. (Boyd Supp. Decl., ¶ 9.) The declaration further provides that Petitioner provided potential heir Tiasseika Everett with information regarding how to resolve her claims on the surplus funds, but that, since December 18, 2023, Petitioner has not received any further communication from her, even after Petitioner mailed the Second Notice of Surplus Funds or Notice of Petition to Deposit Surplus Funds. (Boyd Supp. Decl., ¶¶ 8, 9, 10, Exh. C, Exh. D; Podmenik Supp Decl., ¶ 8.)

 

Second, the Boyd Supplemental Declaration establishes that Petitioner was also contacted by two other potential heirs, Trent and Anthony Everett, who were present at the May 9, 2024, Hearing. (Boyd Supp. Decl., ¶ 12.) On July 29, 2024, Petitioner was contacted once again by potential heir Anthony Everett. (Boyd Supp. Decl., ¶ 13.) Petitioner provided him with the relevant case number so that he could track the status of this case and requested further information to aid his potential claim to the surplus funds, however no information was received by Petitioner. (Ibid.) On August 13, 2024, potential heir Anthony Everett again contacted Petitioner by phone, and Anthony Everett confirmed that he and Trent Everett share a mailing address. (Boyd Supp. Decl., ¶ 15.) Petitioner advised Anthony Everett to appear at this Hearing, set for October 9, 2024, and file a direct claim with this Court. (Ibid.) Although Petitioner requested a copy of Anthony Everett’s photo ID and a copy of the death certificate for Murlene Everett, to date, Petitioner states that they have received no such information. (Ibid.)

Petitioner’s supplemental declarations in support of the motion to deposit surplus funds and discharge Petitioner demonstrates a good faith, diligent effort to determine the priority of potential claimants to the surplus funds, and a conflict to determine such priority and standing. Thus, Petitioner’s Motion to Discharge the Petitioner is GRANTED.

Tentative Ruling:                    Petitioner Sherwood Mobile Home Park’s First Amended Petition to Declare Mobile Home Abandoned is GRANTED.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 6, 2024, Petitioner Sherwood Mobile Home Park (“Plaintiff” or “Petitioner”) filed the Petition for Judicial Declaration of Abandonment of Mobile Home against Lawrence F. Fitzgerald and DOES 1 through 10 (collectively, “Defendants or Respondents”).

 

On or about April 1, 2024, Petitioner caused to be posted a Thirty (30) Day Notice of Belief of Abandonment on the subject mobilehome and sent the certified mail pursuant to Civil Code Section 798.61(b). (Petition ¶ 5(c); First Amended Petition (“1AP”) ¶ 5(c).)

 

More than thirty (30) days elapsed since this Notice was posted and mailed, and none of the Respondents, nor any other person claiming an interest in said mobilehome paid to Petitioner the amounts owed or arranged to reclaim the subject mobilehome. (Johnson Decl. ¶¶ 7-8.)

 

As of January 2024, Respondent has not paid rent or storage costs on the premises. (Johnson Decl. ¶¶ 6-7.) The monthly storage costs owed from February 1, 2024 through August 1, 2024 are $3,145.57 ($473.51 per month). (Johnson Decl. ¶ 9.) Petitioner also requests attorney’s fees and costs. (Petition ¶¶ 5-6; 1AP ¶¶ 5-6.)

 

On September 9, 2024, this Court found that Plaintiff failed to show by a preponderance of evidence that the criteria for an abandoned mobilehome has been satisfied.

 

The Court was satisfied that Plaintiff’s Petition met the following statutory criteria:

1)      The Petition included a Title Search showing Respondent as the registered owner;

2)      Plaintiff filed the Thirty (30) Day Notice of Abandoned Mobile Home; and

3)      Plaintiff and presented the ledger balance report showing unpaid amounts.

However, the Court observed that Plaintiff’s Petition did not meet the statutory requirements based on the following:

1)      Plaintiff failed to provide the total amount of judgment sought for unpaid rent;

2)      Plaintiff did not declare in the petition whether the management intended to sell the contents of the abandoned mobilehome before its disposal;

3)      Plaintiff failed to declare in the petition that the management will dispose of the abandoned mobilehome, and therefore will not seek a tax clearance certificate as set forth in Section 5832 of the Revenue and Taxation Code consistent with the requirements of § 789.61(f); and

4)      Plaintiff failed to declare in the petition that the management intends to file a notice of disposal with the Department of Housing and Community Development and complete the disposal process consistent with the requirements of § 789.61(f).

 

This Court continued Plaintiff’s Petition for a Judicial Declaration of Abandonment of Mobile Home to October 9, 2024, to allow Petitioner to file a revised Petition.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Petitioner petitions the court to enter a judgment of abandonment under California Civil Code §798.61. This Petition is made under California Civil Code §798.61, on the grounds that Lawrence F. Fitzgerald Jr. who on information and belief is the registered owner of the mobilehome occupying space 72 at the mobilehome park at 3753 East Avenue I Lancaster, California 93535, has abandoned that home. Plaintiff seeks a judgment for unpaid rent from January 2024. Plaintiff also seeks judgment allowing recovery for storage charges from February 1, 2024, through August 1, 2024; for costs of suit; and for reasonable attorney fees.

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Petition for Judgment of Abandonment of Mobile Home

A.                Legal Standard

California Civil Code §798.61 states:

(a)(1) As used in this section, “abandoned mobilehome” means a mobilehome about which all of the following are true:

(A) It is located in a mobilehome park on a site for which no rent has been paid to the management for the preceding 60 days.

(B) It is unoccupied.

(C) A reasonable person would believe it to be abandoned.

(D) It is not permanently affixed to the land . . .

(b) After determining a mobilehome in a mobilehome park to be an abandoned mobilehome, the management shall post a notice of belief of abandonment on the mobilehome for not less than 30 days, and shall deposit copies of the notice in the United States mail, postage prepaid, addressed to the homeowner at the last known address and to any known registered owner, if different from the homeowner, and to any known holder of a security interest in the abandoned mobilehome. This notice shall be mailed by registered or certified mail with a return receipt requested.

 

(c)(1) Thirty or more days following posting pursuant to subdivision (b), the management may file a petition in the superior court in the county in which the mobilehome park is located, for a judicial declaration of abandonment of the mobilehome. A proceeding under this subdivision is a limited civil case. Copies of the petition shall be served upon the homeowner, any known registered owner, and any known person having a lien or security interest of record in the mobilehome by posting a copy on the mobilehome and mailing copies to those persons at their last known addresses by registered or certified mail with a return receipt requested in the United States mail, postage prepaid.

 

(2) To dispose of an abandoned mobilehome pursuant to subdivision (f), the management shall also do all of the following:

(A) Declare in the petition that the management will dispose of the abandoned mobilehome, and therefore will not seek a tax clearance certificate as set forth in Section 5832 of the Revenue and Taxation Code.

(B) Declare in the petition whether the management intends to sell the contents of the abandoned mobilehome before its disposal.

(C) Notify the county tax collector in the county in which the mobilehome park is located of the declaration that management will dispose of the abandoned mobilehome by sending a copy of the petition by first class mail.

(D) Declare in the petition that management intends to file a notice of disposal with the Department of Housing and Community Development and complete the disposal process consistent with the requirements of subdivision (f).

 

(d)(1) Hearing on the petition shall be given precedence over other matters on the court's calendar.

 

(2) If, at the hearing, the petitioner shows by a preponderance of the evidence that the criteria for an abandoned mobilehome has been satisfied and no party establishes an interest therein at the hearing and tenders all past due rent and other charges, the court shall enter a judgment of abandonment, determine the amount of charges to which the petitioner is entitled, and award attorney's fees and costs to the petitioner. For purposes of this subdivision, an interest in the mobilehome shall be established by evidence of a right to possession of the mobilehome or a security or ownership interest in the mobilehome.

 

(3) A default may be entered by the court clerk upon request of the petitioner, and a default judgment shall be thereupon entered, if no responsive pleading is filed within 15 days after service of the petition by mail.”

 

(California Civil Code § 789.61)

 

 

1.      Merits

 

Here, Petitioner has successfully shown by a preponderance of evidence that the criteria for an abandoned mobilehome has been satisfied. As the Court’s prior order states, Petitioner has previously shown that (1) Respondent is the registered owner, through an included a Title Search (Johnson Decl. Ex. 2); (2) Plaintiff filed the Thirty (30) Day Notice of Abandoned Mobile Home dated April 1, 2024 (Id. at ¶ 6; Ex. 1); and (3) Plaintiff presented the ledger balance report showing the unpaid amounts (Ibid; Ex. 4).

 

Furthermore, as the Court’s prior Order required, Plaintiff’s petition provides the total amount of judgment sought for unpaid rent, or monthly storage costs, in $3,1457.57. (1AP, ¶ 6.) Plaintiff’s petition also declares that the management intends to sell the contents of the abandoned mobilehome and does not intend to dispose of it. (Id., at ¶ 7.) Plaintiff’s petition also states that, within 10 days following a judgment of abandonment, the management will enter the mobilehome and complete an inventory of the contents and submit the inventory to court. (Ibid; Civil Code § 789.61(e).) Additionally, Plaintiff’s petition states that the mobilehome and its contents, if any, will be sold at a public sale. (Ibid.) Finally, the Plaintiff’s petition declares that appropriate notice will be provided to the county tax collector by requiring the purchaser who remains in the community to pay current all past due taxes owed to the county and State. (Id. at ¶ 8; Civil Code § 789.61(e).)

 

III.       Conclusion

           

            Therefore, Plaintiff’s Petition for Judicial Declaration of Abandonment of Mobile Home is GRANTED.
