DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 22STCV08433    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 56

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

GABRIELA HERNANDEZ,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES PROBATION

DEPARTMENT; LOS ANGELES COUNTY

PROBATION DEPARTMENT; TOYEA SIMS; KIM

PICKETT and DOES 1 to 20,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.

                             

 

      CASE NO.:  22STCV08433

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

Date: October 9, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, erroneously sued as County of Los Angeles Probation Department and Los Angeles County Probation Department (“Defendant” or the “County”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff GABRIELA HERNANDEZ (“Plaintiff”)

 

            The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

            This action arises out of an employment relationship.  The operative second amended complaint (the “SAC”) alleges: (1) discrimination; (2) harassment; (3) retaliation; (4) failure to

prevent retaliation/harassment/discrimination; (5) violation of the Family Medical Leave Act; (6)

failure to accommodate; and (7) failure to engage in good faith interactive process.  On August 17, 2023, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings to the second

cause of action for harassment.[1] 

 

            On June 12, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication (the “Motion”) on the ground that no triable issue of facts exists on Plaintiff’s remaining six causes of action.  Plaintiff filed an opposition on September 25, 2024, and Defendant filed a reply on October 2, 2024. 

                       

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense.  (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. 

To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

First Cause of Action: Disability Discrimination

Government Code section 12940 provides that it is unlawful for an employer to refuse to hire or employ a person; to discharge a person from employment; or to discriminate against the person in compensation or in terms, conditions, or privileges of employment on the basis of physical disability and medical condition.  (Govt. Code, § 12940(a).) 

 

In order to successfully assert a claim for discrimination, Plaintiff must satisfy the requirements of the three-step McDonnell Douglas test.  (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 354-55.)  Generally, a prima facie case requires showing that (1) plaintiff was a member of a protected class; (2) she was qualified for the position she sought or was performing competently in the position she held; (3) plaintiff suffered an adverse employment action, such as termination, demotion, or denial of an available job; and (4) some other circumstance suggests discriminatory motive.  (Id. at 355.) 

 

Once a plaintiff has established a prima facie case, there is a “rebuttable” but “legally mandatory” presumption of discrimination.  (Id. at 355.)  The burden then shifts to the defendant to rebut the presumption by producing admissible evidence that the defendant’s “action was taken for a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason.”  (Id. at 355-356.) 

 

Finally, if the defendant meets its burden, “the presumption of discrimination disappears.”  (Id. at 356.)  The plaintiff must then show that the defendant’s legitimate reason is merely pretext.  (Id.)  “Pretext may be inferred from the timing of the discharge decision, the identity of the decision-maker, or by the discharged employee's job performance before termination.”  (Hanson v. Lucky Stores, Inc. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 215, 224.)  “Pretext may [also] be demonstrated by showing that the proffered reason had no basis in fact, the proffered reason did not actually motivate the discharge, or, the proffered reason was insufficient to motivate discharge.”  (Id.) 

 

“When seeking summary judgment or summary adjudication in an employment discrimination case, the burdens established by the McDonnell Douglas framework are altered. The ‘employer, as the moving party, has the initial burden to present admissible evidence showing either that one or more elements of plaintiff's prima facie case is lacking or that the adverse employment action was based upon legitimate, nondiscriminatory factors.’ [Citation.]” (Zamora v. Security Industry Specialists, Inc. (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 1, 32.)  “If the employer satisfies its initial burden, it will be entitled to summary [adjudication] unless the plaintiff produces admissible evidence which raises a triable issue of fact material to the defendant's showing.”  (Zamora, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at 32.)  “ ‘A plaintiff's burden is ... to produce evidence that, taken as a whole, permits a rational inference that intentional discrimination was a substantial motivating factor in the employer's actions toward the plaintiff.’ [Citation.]” (Zamora, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th 1, 58.) 

 

Here, by way of background, Plaintiff began her employment with the County’s Probation Department (the “Department”) on February 22, 2000 [Defendant’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) # 1], and was later promoted to Detention Services Officer (“DSO”) in 2003, where she was assigned to Los Padrinos Juvenile Hall (“LPJH”) (UMF #2, 3).  As a DSO, Plaintiff was a sworn peace officer with essential functions of supervising detained juveniles ranging anywhere from seven (7) to twenty-one (21) years old and physically restraining combative, aggressive and/or emotionally disturbed juveniles.  (UMF #4, 8-9) 

 

On May 20, 2006, Plaintiff’s first work injury occurred.  (UMF # 28-29) Based on the medical certifications of her primary care provider (“PCP”) that injury precluded her from “restraining minors,” “lifting, grasping, or pulling over 5 pounds,” on the basis of which her PCP “recommend[ed] a desk job”, such that the Department memorialized a temporary Work Hardening Transitional Assignment Agreement (“WHA”) that imposed workplace restrictions.  (UMF # 30-31) On May 3, 2009, Plaintiff sustained another injury and on March 30, 2010, the Department offered Plaintiff a “modified work” contract because she was unable to perform essential functions of her job.  (UMF # 32-34, 36-38) Plaintiff continued presenting medical certifications with identical workplace restrictions which triggered numerous Interactive Process Meetings (“IPMs”); based on her PCP’s certifications, and Plaintiff was assigned to various “post” assignments monitoring the perimeter, which did not involve restraining minors.  (UMF #35, 41-49) 

Sometime thereafter in July 2012, Plaintiff’s injuries were diagnosed as permanent, when Dr. Angerman opined that “[Plaintiff’s] condition reached a permanent and stationary status” (UMF # 77), making her incapable of performing the customary duties of her job as a sworn peace officer.  Defendant submits evidence that for over seven (7) months (i.e., September 2012 to May 2013), the Department had reasonably accommodated Plaintiff by agreement, with numerous WHAs that imposed “post” assignments due to her workplace restrictions not only prohibiting her from any involvement in “physical altercations or restraining of minors,” but also, limited her to “desk duty only.”  (UMF # 81-92)

 

An IPM was held on May 28, 2013, where it was determined that Ms. Hernandez would be placed on a Conditional Job Assignment pending retirement or alternative reclassification, and during which the Department explained to her the LACERA process of service retirement,  service-connected disability retirement, as well as LACERA service-connected disability salary supplement.  (UMF #93-94, 95, 97)  From October 1, 2013, through February 2014, Plaintiff was assigned to a field post at LPJH pending her reassignment, while she simultaneously presented medical certifications that continued to preclude any “contact with combative minors,” and rated her Permanently Partially Disabled (“PPD”) because Plaintiff cannot perform her usual and customary duties based on the parameters of her work restrictions.  (UMF # 99-104)

 

From 2014 to 2017, Plaintiff was still employed as a DSO, not demoted, and did not experience any loss in pay as the Department continued to accommodate Plaintiff with post assignments, while concurrently exploring accommodation options that included reassignment, to an available and open position that was within the parameters of Plaintiff’s permanent workplace restrictions, i.e., that she could not restrain minors; Plaintiff signed all Conditional Job Assignment agreements, agreeing to her accommodating assignment.  (UMF #121)  Plaintiff’s documented permanent work restrictions precluded her from any full-duty assignment because she would not be able to physically intervene and restrain, and there were no other reasonable accommodations available due to the need to ensure safety for both youth offenders and staff with able-bodied personnel to adequately respond to emergency situations.  (UMF # 127)

 

On January 9, 2018, the Department received information that a permanent disability rating had been issued, which meant that the Department could no longer consider Plaintiff for full duty vacancies because her physician verified that she could not safely perform the various duties that she previously could perform.  (UMF # 128)  On February 21, 2020, the Department sent a letter to inform Plaintiff that effective March 9, 2020, her classification would be changed from a DSO to an Intermediate Clerk (“IC”) and, subsequently, Plaintiff was permanently assigned to the Contracts and Grants Division as a reasonable accommodation.  (UMF # 138-139)

 

In her opposition, the evidence submitted by Defendant remains undisputed.  Notably, in Plaintiff’s Response and Statement of Disputed Facts, Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Separate Statement consisted mainly of “undisputed”, and in those that she disputes, Plaintiff failed to state and specify the nature of the dispute, as required by California Rules of Court rule 3.1350(f).  Thus, the evidence submitted by Defendant is largely undisputed. 

 

There is no dispute that Plaintiff is a member of a protected class (disabled employee), and that she was subjected to an adverse action (demotion); there is no evidence, however, to suggest any link between Plaintiff’s transfer or demotion and a discriminatory motive.  Neither is there any evidence supporting any inference of discrimination or that Plaintiff was treated differently from others similarly situated, or any other evidence to suggest any discriminatory motive.  Based on the evidence presented, it is apparent that due to Plaintiff’s work-related injuries, she was unable to perform the duties of a DSO.  Thus, Plaintiff’s demotion was not based on a discriminatory motive on account of her disability, but was instead based on the fact that Plaintiff was no longer capable of performing her job (i.e., DSO), which required her to physically engage and restrain combative minors. 

 

An employer has no duty to accommodate a disabled employee by making a temporary accommodation permanent if doing so would require the employer to create a new position just for the employee.  (Raine v. City of Burbank (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 1215, 1227; see also, Quinn v. City of Los Angeles (2000) 84 Cal. App. 4th 472, 482-484 [holding that no disability discrimination existed for the Los Angeles City Police Officer because he was not qualified for the job of sworn peace officer due to public safety concerns when it was discovered that the employee had a hearing impairment]; Furtado v. State Pers. Bd., (2013) 212 Cal. App. 4th 729, 746 [holding that a correctional lieutenant who sustained permanent injuries was properly medically demoted to a non-peace officer position, because he was unable to perform essential job functions, including being able to disarm, subdue and restrain an inmate, defend himself against an armed inmate and proficiently use a baton.]

 

Therefore, Plaintiff cannot prevail on this cause of action because (1) Plaintiff cannot present evidence suggesting discriminatory motive, and (2) Defendant had a legitimate non-discriminatory reason for its conduct: Plaintiff’s inability to perform the essential duties of her employment as a DSO due to permanent medical workplace restrictions, resulting in her reassignment from a DSO position to an Intermediate Clerk position.  Defendant is therefore entitled to summary judgment on the first cause of action of the SAC.

 

Third Cause of Action: Retaliation

"[I]n order to establish a prima facie case of retaliation under the FEHA, a plaintiff must show (1) he or she engaged in a 'protected activity,' (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer's action. [Citations.] Once an employee establishes a prima facie case, the employer is required to offer a legitimate, nonretaliatory reason for the adverse employment action. [Citation.] If the employer produces a legitimate reason for the adverse employment action, the presumption of retaliation '" 'drops out of the picture, '"' and the burden shifts back to the employee to prove intentional retaliation."  (Yanowitz v. L'Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1042.)  

 

Here, as discussed above, Defendant has established and Plaintiff cannot dispute, that Plaintiff’s transfer of assignment was due to Plaintiff’s inability to perform the essential duties of  a DSO, and was therefore not due to retaliation.  Defendant is therefore entitled to summary judgment on Plaintiff’s third cause of action for retaliation.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Failure to Prevent Discrimination/Retaliation

“Failure to prevent” is a derivative claim and must be predicated on an underlying claim of discrimination or retaliation.  Claims for failure to prevent discrimination fail as a matter of law when the underlying violation did not actually occur.  (Trujillo v. N. Cty. Transit Dist. (1998) 63 Cal. App. 4th 280, 289.)  Where there is no viable claim under FEHA, Defendant cannot be liable for failure to prevent that action.  (Sneddon v. ABF Freight Sys. (S.D. Cal. 2007 489 F. Supp. 2d 1124, 1132–1133.)

 

Here, since Plaintiff’s claims for discrimination and retaliation fail, there is likewise no viable claim for failure to prevent the same.  Defendant is therefore entitled to summary judgment on the fourth cause of action of the SAC.

 

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Violation of the Family Medical Leave Act / California Family Rights Act

 

Similarly, this claim fails as Defendant has demonstrated that Plaintiff’s inability to perform the functions of a DSO position was the reason for her transfer to an administrative position, not because she exercised her right to CFRA leave.   Defendant is therefore entitled to summary judgment on the fifth cause of action of the SAC.

 

Sixth Cause of Action: Failure to Accommodate

Government Code section 12940(m) provides that it is an unlawful employment practice “[f]or an employer . . . to fail to make reasonable accommodation for the known physical or mental disability of an applicant or employee.” (Govt. Code, § 12940(m)(1).)

 

“The elements of a failure to accommodate a claim are ‘(1) the plaintiff has a disability under the FEHA, (2) the plaintiff is qualified to perform the essential functions of the position, and (3) the employer failed to reasonably accommodate the plaintiff’s disability.’”  (Swanson v. Morongo Unified School Dist. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 954, 969.)

 

            Here, there is ample evidence to show that Defendant reasonably accommodated Plaintiff when she was assigned to less arduous “post” assignments in accordance with the work restrictions based on her attending physicians’ medical certifications.  Thus, Plaintiff’s claim under this cause of action similarly fails. Defendant is therefore entitled to summary judgment on the sixth cause of action of the SAC.

 

Seventh Cause of Action: Failure to Engage in an Interactive Process

Here, Defendant presented evidence that at least 15 IPM meetings were held over a ten-year period of time.  (UMF # 799, 814-819, 834, 839-840, 842, 856, 863, 865-869, 871-876, 882-885, 888-894, 895-897, 907, 908, 915)  At each IPM, assessments were done of Plaintiff’s qualifications, work restrictions, and functional capacity related to the essential functions of her job assignments.  (UMF #799, 814-819, 834, 839-840, 842, 856, 863, 865-869, 871-876, 882-885, 888-894, 895-897, 907, 908, 915)  Thus, Plaintiff cannot prevail on this claim.  Defendant is therefore entitled to summary judgment on the seventh cause of action of the SAC. 

 

In sum, the Court finds there is no triable issue of material fact as to any of the causes of action remaining in the SAC and the County is entitled to judgment on the SAC as a matter of law. 

 

RULING

            Based on all the foregoing, the Motion is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

            This is a wrongful termination action.  The Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) filed by Plaintiff on March 8, 2023 asserted the following causes of action: (1) Employment  Discrimination in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940(a)); (2) Failure to Accommodate in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940(m)); (3) Failure to Engage in a Timely & Good Faith Interactive Process in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940(n)); (4) Harassment in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940(j)); (5) Retaliation in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §§12940(h)(m)); (6) Failure to Prevent/Remedy Discrimination, Harassment and/or Retaliation in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940(k)); (7) Wrongful Discharge in Violation of Public Policy; and (8) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”).

             

            On June 13, 2024, following a successful Motion for Summary Judgment filed by Defendant, the Court entered judgment in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiff with respect to all causes of action asserted.  Defendant filed a notice of entry of judgment on July 11, 2024.

 

            On September 9, 2024, Defendant filed the present Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and Costs (the “Motion”), seeking an award of attorneys’ fees and costs in the total amount of $611,351.53 pursuant to Cal. Gov. Code. § 12965(b).  Plaintiff filed an opposition on September 25, 2024, and Defendant filed a reply on October 2, 2024.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

             Defendant objects to portions of the Declaration of Jonathan Ebrahimian, all of the documents attached to it, and certain portions of the Declaration of Ramez Wissa.  The Court declines to rule on the objections as the statements and documents objected to have no bearing on the Court’s determination in ruling on the Motion.

 

DISCUSSION

“As a general rule, the prevailing party in civil litigation is entitled to recover his or her costs as a matter of right: ‘Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.’”  (Scott v. City of San Diego (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 228 [250 Cal.Rptr.3d 432, 436] (quoting Code of Civil Procedure section 1032(b)).)  “[T]he law is now well settled that [Government Code] section 12965(b) is an express exception to the prevailing party cost provisions in section 1032, subdivision (b).”  (Huerta v. Kava Holdings, Inc. (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 74, 81.)  Pursuant to Government Code section 12965(b), “a prevailing defendant shall not be awarded fees and costs unless the court finds the action was frivolous, unreasonable, or groundless when brought, or the plaintiff continued to litigate after it clearly became so.”   (Gov. Code, § 12965(b).)

 

An unsuccessful FEHA plaintiff should not be ordered to pay the defendant’s fees or costs “unless the plaintiff brought or continued litigating the action without an objective basis for believing it had potential merit.”  (Williams v. Chino Valley Independent Fire Dist. (2015) 61 Cal.4th 97, 99-100.)  “[T]he term ‘meritless’ is to be understood as meaning groundless or without foundation, rather than simply that the plaintiff has ultimately lost his case . . . . In applying these criteria, it is important that a [trial] court resist the understandable temptation to engage in post hoc reasoning by concluding that, because a plaintiff did not ultimately prevail, his action must have been unreasonable or without foundation.  This kind of hindsight logic could discourage all but the most airtight claims, for seldom can a prospective plaintiff be sure of ultimate success.  No matter how honest one’s belief that he has been the victim of discrimination, no matter how meritorious one’s claim may appear at the outset, the course of litigation is rarely predictable.  Decisive facts may not emerge until discovery or trial.  The law may change or clarify in the midst of litigation.  Even when the law or the facts appear questionable or unfavorable at the outset, a party may have an entirely reasonable ground for bringing the suit.”  (Christiansburg Garment Co. v. Equal Employment Opportunity Comm’n (1978) 434 U.S. 412, 421-22.)

 

“Attorney fees should be awarded to a prevailing defendant ‘only where the action brought is found to be unreasonable, frivolous, meritless or vexatious,’ and ‘the term “meritless” is to be understood as meaning groundless or without foundation, rather than simply that the plaintiff has ultimately lost his case....’ ” (Jersey v. John Muir Medical Center (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 814, 831.)  Lack of merit is not the proper standard. (Ibid.)  Rather, the case must be egregious and/or baseless.  (Rosenman v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 859, 869.) A case that is at least colorable, though it is not ultimately supported with adequate evidence, is not frivolous or vexatious for the purpose of attorneys’ fees.  (Id. at 873-74.)

 

 

“[A] plaintiff’s ability to pay must be considered before awarding attorney fees in favor of the defendant.”  (Villanueva v. City of Colton (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1203.)  This is “[b]ecause the majority of cases under the FEHA involve litigants who would not have the financial means to prosecute this type of case” and “the public policy behind the FEHA is served by not discouraging them from pursuing the litigation by potentially imposing fees that could easily devastate them financially simply because a few file frivolous claims.”  (Id.) 

 

Here, Defendant asserts entitlement to statutory attorneys’ fees under the Fair Employment and Housing Act as a prevailing defendant in this action, on the ground that Plaintiff’s action was frivolous, unreasonable or groundless when brought, or that Plaintiff continued to litigate after it clearly became so.  In characterizing Plaintiff’s litigation as frivolous, Defendant argues that Plaintiff had to amend his complaint three times, and that despite multiple amendments, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrers to Plaintiff’s harassment and IIED claims.  Defendant also asserts that it was forced to file a motion for summary judgment, which should have put Plaintiff on notice that his case failed as a matter of law, but that Plaintiff chose to continue to litigate and oppose the motion.

 

The Court does not find these as sufficient reasons to warrant a fee award against Plaintiff.  There is no evidence that Plaintiff fabricated any evidence or that he  did not proceed in good faith.  Rather, the Court finds Plaintiff had a good faith belief in his claims, despite the fact that they were unsuccessful.  Thus, the Court does not conclude that Plaintiff’s causes of action were frivolous, egregious or vexatious, even though they were later resolved against him.

 

Therefore, Defendant is not entitled to attorney’s fees as a prevailing defendant pursuant to Code of Government Code section 12965.
