DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00019    Hearing Date: September 26, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Ariel Knight filed this action against Defendants, Robert Louis Wood, Jacqueline Amy Wood, The Wood Family Trust, Robert Louis Wood and Jacqueline Amy Wood as Trustees, and Henry Perry for damages arising out of the parties’ landlord-tenant relationship. 

 

Plaintiff filed her complaint on 1/18/22.  Perry filed an answer and cross-complaint on 5/23/22.  The Wood Defendants filed a demurrer and motion to strike on 7/01/22, and the Court sustained the demurrer and granted the motion to strike with leave to amend on 11/14/22.  Plaintiff filed her First Amended Complaint on 12/12/22.  The FAC included causes of action for:

·         Violation of Civil Code §1940.2 – Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment (all defendants);

·         Violation of Civil Code §1942.5 (all defendants);

·         Tortious Failure to Provide Habitable Premises (all defendants);

·         Negligence (all defendants);

·         Nuisance (all defendants);

·         Assault (Henry Perry);

·         IIED (all defendants);

·         Breach of Written Contract (all defendants).

 

On 5/09/23, the Court heard the Wood Defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike directed at the FAC.  The Court sustained the demurrer in part and overruled the demurrer in part, and the Court granted the motion to strike in part and denied it in part.  On 6/05/23, Plaintiff filed her operative Second Amended Complaint.  The SAC includes causes of action for:

·         Breach of Quiet Enjoyment;

·         Violation of Civil Code §1940.2;

·         Violation of Civil Code §1942.5;

·         Breach of Warranty of Habitability;

·         Negligence;

·         Nuisance;

·         Assault;

·         IIED;

·         Breach of Contract.

 

2.     Demurrer to FAC

  1. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares there have been numerous conversations, which appear to have occurred prior to the filing of the SAC, concerning the allegations that are the subject of this demurrer and motion to strike.  She declares that, subsequent to the filing of the SAC, she sent a meet and confer email.  The meet and confer asked Plaintiff to either stipulate to withdraw the IIED cause of action or to meet and confer further. 

 

Defendant’s demurrer is addressed to the second and third causes of action (violations of Civil Code §§1940.2 and 1942.5).  It is not addressed to the IIED cause of action.  Defendant’s meet and confer letter did not even mention the second or third causes of action.  Defendant’s motion to strike seeks to strike the prayer for exemplary damages, the prayer for punitive damages, and the prayer for statutory damages.  None of this was mentioned in the meet and confer letter either. 

 

  1. Continuance of Hearing and Guidance

i.              Initial Note

Plaintiff, at page 3, line 26 of the opposition to the demurrer, refers to the “TAC.”  This caused the Court to look in the file for a Third Amended Complaint.  There is no TAC on file.  The demurrer is directed at the Second Amended Complaint. 

 

ii.             Second Cause of Action

First, the first page of the SAC indicates the second cause of action is for violation of Civil Code §1940.2, but the caption of the cause of action indicates it is for violation of §1941.2.  It appears clear Plaintiff intends to allege a cause of action under §1940.2.  The parties must meet and confer to clarify.

 

Second, it appears the crux of the demurrer is the contention that the sham pleading doctrine bars the SAC.  Neither party, however, cites authority concerning whether and when the sham pleading doctrine applies.  The parties must meet and confer concerning the applicability or lack of applicability of the doctrine.

Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, argues the third cause of action adequately states a claim for retaliation by harassment.  Plaintiff then goes on to argue the elements of breach of warranty of habitability are met in the cause of action.  Plaintiff then concludes a claim for retaliation is pled.  Plaintiff must clarify, in meet and confer communication, whether this is a claim for retaliation by harassment or for breach of the warranty of habitability.  If it is for retaliation by harassment, Plaintiff must cite authority on point to that cause of action, rather than to a completely separate cause of action.

Plaintiff’s SAC includes a prayer for exemplary damages and also a prayer for punitive damages.  These are the same thing, and it is not clear why they are pled as two different items of damages.  In any event, again, the parties did not meet and confer concerning whether the prayer for punitive damages is adequately pled, and they must do so before the Court will hear the challenge.

 

v.             Statutory Damages

Plaintiff’s prayer for statutory damages rises and falls with her claim for violation of §1942.5, and therefore the parties’ meet and confer in this regard should address both the cause of action and the corresponding prayer for damages.

Case Number: 22LBCV00518    Hearing Date: September 26, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Pat Amaka Levison filed this action against Defendants, Mark Zuckerberg and Facebook-Meta on 9/28/22.  Plaintiff’s judicial council form complaint indicates, on the cover page, that it sounds in contract.  At ¶8 of the complaint, however, in the space where a plaintiff must list the causes of action asserted, Plaintiff does not list any cause of action.  The crux of the complaint is that someone, either from Facebook or a third party, hacked Plaintiff’s account, and despite Plaintiff’s requests, Facebook has not restored access to the account.    

 

On 2/16/23, Plaintiff filed a proof of service of unspecified documents, by mail, on Zuckerberg.  On 4/03/23, Plaintiff filed a proof of service of the summons, complaint, and related documents on Facebook-Meta by serving its registered agent for service of process.  On 4/26/23, Meta Platforms, Inc., filed this demurrer and related documents.  On 7/14/23, Plaintiff filed opposition to the demurrer. 

 

2.     Demurrer

  1. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares multiple attempts to meet and confer were made, but Plaintiff did not respond.  Plaintiff must respond to attempts to meet and confer in the future.  The Court will rule on the demurrer on its merits, as Defendant satisfied its obligation to engage in the interactive process prior to filing the pleading challenge.

 

  1. Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant seeks judicial notice of the Terms of Service that governed users’ relationship with Facebook during the relevant time period that forms the basis of the complaint.  The RJN is unopposed and granted.

 

  1. Grounds for Demurrer

Defendant contends the complaint is uncertain.  It contends the complaint fails to state a cause of action.  Finally, it contends the complaint is barred in its entirety by the Communications Decency Act. 

 

i.              Initial Note

Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, reiterates the various ways in which she believes she has been aggrieved.  She fails, however, to address any legal argument advanced in Defendant’s demurrer.  If Plaintiff wishes to pursue her claims, she must be prepared to address legal, as opposed to perceived emotional or moral, arguments. 

 

ii.             Uncertainty

The Court agrees that the complaint is uncertain.  As noted above, the caption of the complaint indicates it sounds in contract.  The body of the complaint, however, fails to allege any cause of action at all.  Notably, Defendant indicates the body of the complaint purports to include causes of action for fraud and violation of protection.  The Court has reviewed the complaint and cannot find the words “fraud” or “violation of protection” anywhere in the complaint. 

 

Plaintiff must allege a viable cause of action that exists under California law if she wishes to pursue this action.  The Court strongly encourages Plaintiff to consult with a licensed attorney, as a review of the complaint makes apparent that Plaintiff will not be able to successfully pursue this action without professional assistance.

iii.            Failure to State a Cause of Action

As noted above, the Court cannot locate a purported cause of action for fraud or violation of protection.  Defendant correctly notes that, to the extent Plaintiff purports to state a claim for violation of protection, no such claim exists. 

 

Defendant also correctly notes that Plaintiff has not pled a claim for fraud.  The elements of fraud are: (1) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of falsity (scienter); (3) intent to defraud or induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) damages.  (See Civil Code §1709.)  Fraud actions are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading.  (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal. 3d 197, 216.)

 

Since allegations of fraud involve a serious attack on character, fairness to the defendant demands that he should receive the fullest possible details of the charge in order to prepare his defense.  Accordingly the rule is everywhere followed that fraud must be specifically pleaded, such that: (a) General pleading of the legal conclusion of “fraud” is insufficient; the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged; and (b) Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner (i.e., factually and specifically), and the policy of liberal construction of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked to sustain a pleading defective in any material respect.  (Id.)  

 

“Consistent with the rule requiring specificity in pleading fraud, a complaint must state ultimate facts showing that the defendant intended or had reason to expect reliance by the plaintiff or the class of persons of which he is a member.” (Geernaert v. Mitchell (1995) 31 Cal. App. 4th 601, 608.)  A plaintiff must allege what was said, by whom, in what manner (i.e. oral or in writing), when, and, in the case of a corporate defendant, under what authority to bind the corporation.  (See Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 772, 782.)

Plaintiff has not pled any of the above elements.

 

Defendant also correctly notes that Plaintiff has not pled a claim for breach of contract.  She has not pled the existence of any contract between herself and Defendant, the terms of any such contract, or any act on the part of Defendant that constitutes a breach of a term of the parties’ contract.

 

iv.            Communications Decency Act

The Court is not ruling on the issue of whether or not the Communications Decency Act bars Plaintiff’s claims at this time.  The Court cannot determine the factual or legal basis for the claims, and therefore cannot make a meaningful determination concerning the scope of the Act or its application to the facts or legal theories advanced. 

 

  1. Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer is sustained.  Plaintiff has not articulated, in opposition to the demurrer, how she would amend if given leave to do so.  However, because this is the original complaint, and because leave to amend is liberally granted, the Court is inclined to give Plaintiff an opportunity to attempt to cure the defects in her complaint.  If Plaintiff files an amended complaint, she must ensure that the pleading makes clear what legally recognized cause of action or causes of action she is pleading.  She must also ensure all elements of any recognized cause of action are pled. 

 

Plaintiff must file and serve an amended complaint, in compliance with the above, within thirty days.  Defendant must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter. 

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV00543    Hearing Date: September 26, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Eda DiPasquale and Scott McChesney filed this unlawful detainer action against Defendant, John Cruz for possession and damages in connection with a residential tenancy. 

 

Plaintiffs filed their complaint on 3/15/23.  On 6/01/23, the Court denied Defendant’s motion to quash.  On 7/25/23, the Court overruled Defendant’s demurrer to the complaint.  On 7/31/23, Defendant filed an answer to the complaint. 

 

On 8/04/23, Plaintiff filed a request to set the case for trial.  The request includes a proof of service showing it was served on Defendant.  On 8/07/23, the Court issued a minute order setting trial for 8/23/23, which order was mailed to Plaintiff.  Plaintiff was ordered to give notice to Defendant.  On 8/23/23, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Trial, which includes proof of service showing it was mailed to Defendant on 8/11/23. 

 

On 8/23/23, the Court heard a bench trial.  Defendant did not appear, and the Court entered judgment in Plaintiff’s favor.  On 8/28/23, Plaintiff gave notice of entry of judgment.  On 9/05/23, Defendant filed this motion to vacate the judgment. 

 

2.     Motion to Vacate Judgment

Defendant moves to vacate the judgment, contending he was never given notice of the trial date.  The parties agree that, if Defendant was not given notice of the trial, the judgment must be set aside.  The issue, therefore, comes down to credibility. 

 

Boding in Defendant’s favor is the following: 

 

First, on 7/25/23, the Court overruled Defendant’s demurrer.  The minute order does not indicate any Case Management Conference is being scheduled, and typically a CMC is not scheduled in a UD case.  The next day, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Ruling and proof of service of same.  In the Notice of Ruling, Plaintiff indicates the Court set a CMC for 8/31/23.  Defendant, therefore, believed the case was going to have a CMC heard on 8/31/23.  As Defendant correctly notes in the moving papers, there was never an 8/31/23 CMC scheduled in this case. 

 

Second, Defendant provides copies of all mail he was scheduled to receive, and there is nothing from the USPS showing he would receive the notice of trial.  Defendant declares this is a true and correct copy of everything from USPS, and he never received a notice of trial.  This is, of course, a credibility determination, as Defendant could have cut and pasted the scheduled mail print-out and omitted the notice of ruling. 

 

Boding in favor of Plaintiff is the following:

 

First, Defendant filed a motion to quash and also a demurrer, but failed to appear at the hearing on either.  Defendant scheduled the hearing on the motion to quash and the demurrer well into the future, requiring Plaintiff to come in ex parte to advance the hearing on both so the case could move forward on the appropriate fast track given to UD matters.  The foregoing tends to show Defendant wishes to prolong these proceedings.  Failing to appear at trial and then filing a motion to vacate the judgment, similarly, would prolong the proceedings.

 

Second, Plaintiff’s attorney signed the proof of service of the notice of trial, and a signed proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the document was actually served.   

 

On balance, due to the very strong presumption in favor of trial on the merits, the Court is inclined to grant the motion.  The Court is, however, inclined to immediately proceed with trial on the merits at the conclusion of the hearing on this motion.  Plaintiff is already prepared for trial, as Plaintiff appeared and was prepared on 8/23/23.  Defendant must appear prepared for trial at the hearing on this motion.
