DEPARTMENT F43 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24CHCV00391    Hearing Date: January 7, 2025    Dept: F43

Dept. F43

Date: 01-07-25

Case # 24CHCV00391, J.B. Hunt Transport, Inc. v. Contractor’s Wardrobe, Inc.

Trial Date: None set.

 

DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant J.B. Hunt Transport, Inc.

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant/Cross-Complainant Contractors Wardrobe, Inc.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Order sustaining demurrer to the cross-complaint:

·         Second Cause of Action

·         Third Cause of Action

·         Fourth Cause of Action

·         Prayer for Punitive Damages

Order striking punitive damages from the cross-complaint.

 

RULING: Demurrer is sustained, with leave to amend. Motion to strike is granted.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant J.B. Hunt Transport, Inc. (Plaintiff) filed suit against defendant and Cross-Complainant Contractor’s Wardrobe, Inc. (Defendant) on February 8, 2024.  Plaintiff filed its First Amended Complaint on August 9, 2024 alleging Defendant breached an agreement with Plaintiff by failing to pay Plaintiff for services provided under the Agreement at the total costs of $1,448,158.87.

 

On September 10, 2024, Defendant filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) intentional interference with economic relations, (3) negligence interference with economic relations, and (4) Unfair Business Practices violations under Business and Professions Code section 17200.

 

The Cross-Complaint alleges that Defendant designs and manufactures high-quality home improvement products.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 1.)  On March 12, 2020, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a written agreement (the Agreement) under which Plaintiff would provide shipping services for Defendant’s products.  (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. A.)  Under Section 7(b) of the Agreement, Plaintiff agreed to exercise good faith efforts to submit, process, and timely respond to all claims by Defendant or third parties for damages caused to any cargo Plaintiff shipped under the Agreement.  (Id., ¶ 5, Exh. A at p. 4.)  Plaintiff was responsible for obtaining proofs of delivery with each customer’s signature.  (Id., ¶ 6.)  Because these proofs identified shipping discrepancies and damage, Plaintiff was the first to acquire knowledge of any freight or cargo issues and was obligated to initiate cargo claims.  (Ibid.)  However, Plaintiff demonstrated a pattern of initiating cargo claims but failing to timely process them. (Ibid.)  Even when Defendant learned about cargo issues and notified Plaintiff of the loss, damage, and discrepancies, Plaintiff continually failed to timely process the cargo claims.  (Ibid.)

 

On October 27, 2022, Defendant notified Plaintiff of these issues and that Plaintiff was in material breach of the Agreement.  (Id., ¶ 7.)  Plaintiff claimed ignorance of the cargo claims.  (Ibid.)  On December 16, 2022, Defendant submitted a Loss and Damage claim form detailing the cargo losses and damage.  (Ibid.)  On July 21, 2023, Defendant again notified Plaintiff that it was in material breach of the Agreement and that Plaintiff had thirty days to cure the breach.  (Id., ¶ 9.)  Plaintiff acknowledged the breach notice and requested dispute resolution but still failed to cure the material breaches.  (Ibid.)

 

Following the breach notice, Plaintiff ceased providing some of its contractual obligations by initiating a work stoppage and failing or refusing to provide and replace qualified drivers and equipment.  (Id., ¶ 10.)  Plaintiff’s actions caused service disruptions in Defendant’s operations and delivery delays, and Defendant lost business from many customers.  (Ibid.)  Defendant was forced to cover these losses by hiring its own trucks and drivers to timely complete its own contractual responsibilities to customers which resulted in $200,195.93 in coverage damages and $92,356.56 in service interruption charges.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff’s actions also forced Defendant to complete repairs and to replace and service trucks resulting in $41,395.50 replacements and $14,100.00 to provide 28 feet of trailer wraps.  (Id., ¶ 11.)  On August 25, 2023, Defendant issued a notice termination for cause under the Agreement with an effective termination date of September 9, 2023.  (Id., ¶ 12.)

 

Plaintiff has not addressed or resolved the cargo claims which as of September 10, 2024 include over 4,628 claims totaling approximately $1,270,314.17.  (Id., ¶ 8.)

 

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a demurrer to the cross-complaint’s Second, Third, and Fourth Causes of action and request for punitive damages.  Plaintiff also filed a motion to strike punitive damages.  Defendant filed its oppositions on December 23, 2024.  Plaintiff replied on December 30, 2024.

 

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or motion to strike, the parties must meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a), 435.5, subd. (a).)  The moving party must file and serve a meet and confer declaration stating either: (1) the means by which the parties met and conferred, that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the issues raised in the demurrer or motion to strike; or (2) that the party who filed the pleading subject to the demurrer or motion to strike failed to respond to the meet and confer request or failed to meet and confer in good faith.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(3), 435.5, subd. (a)(3).) 

 

Plaintiff’s counsel, Kyle J. Waldie, emailed a meet and confer letter to Defendant’s counsel on October 4, 2024.  (Declaration of Kyle J. Waldie, ¶ 3, Exh. B.)  The letter asked Defendant’s counsel to call to discuss further.  (Id., Exh. B. at p. 5.)  On October 7, 2024, Defendant’s counsel responded via email stating Defendant would not amend or dismiss the cross-complaint.  (Waldie Dec., ¶ 4, Exh. C, at p.1.)  Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to meet and confer according to Department 43’s local rules and section 430.41.  (Opposition, p. 1:12-13.)

 

The meet and confer requirement is not met.  However, considering the parties have replied with oppositions and replies, the Court will still decide this motion on the merits. 

 

SUMMARY OF ARGUMENTS

Plaintiff demurs to the Second, Third, and Fourth causes of action and the prayer for punitive damages in Defendant’s cross-complaint.  In the alternative, Plaintiff moves to strike Defendant’s prayer for punitive damages from the cross-complaint.  The Second Cause of Action fails to allege Plaintiff’s acts were “independently wrongful” or to identify a specific customer relationship Plaintiff interfered with through the alleged wrongful acts.  The Third Cause of Action fails for the same reasons.  The Fourth Cause of Action fails because it is derivative of the Second and Third Causes of Action.  The cross-complaint fails to sufficiently plead punitive damages because Defendant does not state whether the damages are based on fraud, malice, or oppression, Defendant does not plead a non-breach of contract claim that Plaintiff intended to harm Defendant, and Defendant does not identify what facts it is relying on in claiming punitive damages.

 

In opposition, Defendant asserts that the cross-complaint identifies multiple acts Plaintiff purposefully initiated including a work stoppage—an anti-competitive act—which are independently wrong and unlawful under Civil Code section 17200.  The cross-complaint sufficiently alleges existing customer relationships through alleging 4,628 outstanding cargo claims which are the result of Plaintiff not fulfilling its duties under the Agreement.  Defendant’s unfair business practices claim sufficiently alleges facts showing Plaintiff engaged in fraudulent and unlawful business practices in order to retaliate against Defendant for Defendant noticing Plaintiff of its breach of the Agreement.  Plaintiff cannot demur to punitive damages because they are not a separate cause of action, but related to causes of action.  Regardless, the Court should not strike punitive damages because the cross-complaint alleges that Plaintiff went beyond breaching the Agreement by engaging in intentional and retaliatory actions to harm Defendant.  These actions include purposefully initiating a work stoppage and failing to provide and refusing to replace qualified drivers and equipment as required under the Agreement, after Defendant notified Plaintiff of its material breaches.  The cross-complaint sufficiently alleges these acts were conducted with malice and oppression because Plaintiff knew of Defendant’s relationships with its customers, knew its actions would disrupt Defendant’s relationships with its customers, and willfully proceeded to undertake such actions anyway. 

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues the cross-complaint fails to plead and identify a wrongful act which is independently unlawful, and Defendant opposition merely attempts to misrepresent the legally accurate definition of “wrongful conduct.”  The cross-complaint contains conclusory statements which do not support a section 17200 claim.  The cross-complaint also fails to meet the heightened standard for pleading specific facts showing rising to the level of “despicable behavior.” 

 

ANALYSIS

Demurrer   

As a general matter, a¿party may respond to a pleading against it by demurrer on the basis of any single or combination of eight enumerated grounds, including¿that¿“the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action” and is uncertain, meaning “ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f)).  The grounds for demurring must be apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) 

 

The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.”  (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.)  “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  The court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law[.]”  (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.)  In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

            Second Cause of Action: Intentional Interference with Economic Relations

Plaintiff demurs to the Second Cause of Action on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action and that it is uncertain.

 

To plead a claim for intentional interference with prospective economic relations, the plaintiff must plead “(1) the existence, between the plaintiff and some third party, of an economic relationship that contains the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentionally wrongful acts designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm proximately caused by the defendant’s action.” (Roy Allan Slurry Seal, Inc. v. American Asphalt South, Inc. (2017) 2 Cal.5th 505, 572; see also Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1164-1165.)

 

Plaintiff’s demurrer focuses on Defendant’s failure to identify an “independently wrongful” act and failure to identify an existing economic. 

 

1.      Independently Wrongful Act

An act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, meaning it violates “some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard” rather than resulting from “an improper, but lawful, purpose of motive.”  (Korea Supply Co., supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 1159 fn. 11.)  The act may be independently wrongful as to either the plaintiff or to a third party.  (Crown Imports, LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1405.)  The act must not be privileged.  (Bed, Bath & Beyond of La Jolla, Inc. v. La Jolla Village Square Venture Partners (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 867, 881.) 

 

The California Supreme Court has held that “[a] contracting party’s unjustified failure or refusal to perform is a breach of contract [which] cannot be transmuted into tort liability by claiming that the breach detrimentally affected the promisee’s business.”  (Cates Construction, Inc. v. Talbot Partners (1999) 21 Cal.4th 28, 54 [internal quotation omitted].)  “Because a bare breach of contract, without more, is not tortious, such a breach cannot constitute independently wrongful conduct capable of giving rise to the tort of intentional interference with a prospective economic advantage.”  (Drink Tank Ventures LLC v. Real Soda in Real Bottles, Ltd. (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 528, 540.)  “Where the economic relationship between a plaintiff and a third party has ripened into an enforceable contract, that ‘contract receives greater solicitude’ and a defendant’s effort to cause a breach of that contract ‘is . . . a wrong in and of itself.’”  (Id. at p. 539.) 

 

Defendant points to multiple acts in the cross-complaint including Plaintiff’s refusal to provide some of its contractually required transportation services at several sites such as Plaintiff purposefully initiating a work stoppage and failing to provide and refusing to replace qualified drivers and equipment as required under the Agreement.  (Opposition, p. 7:10-15.)  Defendant argues these actions are independently wrongful because they constitute unfair business acts or practices under section 17200.  Defendant cites Tri-Growth Centre City, Ltd. v. Silldorf, Burdman, Duignan & Eisenberg (1989), which defines “wrongful” as actions outside “the realm of legitimate business transactions.”  (216 Cal.App.3d 1139, 1153-1154.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant misrepresents the definition of “wrongful conduct” by referencing multiple acts by Plaintiff which are not independently unlawful, but breaches of a contract.  The cross-complaint references a work stoppage but does not state facts explaining how the “work stoppage” was independently unlawful.  (Reply, p. 4:24-28.) 

 

In Korea, plaintiff established an independent wrongful act by pleading defendant violated the federal Foreign Corrupt Practices Act by bribing key government officials and offering sexual favors to key government officials to obtain the contract with Republic of Korea.  (Korea, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 1159.)  The Supreme Court affirmed the lower court’s holding that the complaint’s allegations regarding money offered and paid to foreign officials to influence a foreign government was sufficient to establish an independent wrongful act.  (Ibid.)

 

The cross-complaint alleges Plaintiff’s acts, which breached the Agreement, were independent wrongful acts.  Defendant first alleges Plaintiff failed to comply with its obligations under section 7(b) by not timely initiating cargo claims based on Plaintiff’s direct knowledge and notice from Defendant.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 6.)  Next, Defendant alleges that Plaintiff subsequently ceased its other contractual obligations by instituting a work stoppage and failing to provide qualified drivers and to replace equipment.  (Id., ¶¶ 8-11, 21-24.)  Defendant further alleges that this resulted in $1,270,314.17 in cover charges and damages to Defendant, 4,628 customer cargo claims, and Defendant lost business from customers.  (Id., ¶¶ 8-11, 21-24.)  Finally, Defendant alleges these subsequent actions constitute an independent wrongful act because they violate the Unfair Business Act, section 17200.  (Id., ¶¶ 31-36.) 

 

The cross-complaint fails to allege independently wrongful conduct because the alleged acts were breaches of a contract without anything more.  Defendant alleges Plaintiff breached its obligations under the Agreement, but does not state exactly how the acts were wrong according to a separate law, regulation, or the Constitution.  Although the independently wrongful act requirement may be satisfied by alleging a “violation of a borrowing statute like the UCL,” Defendant also fails to plead facts sufficient to support its UCL claim which the Court discusses further later in this tentative.  (CRST  Van Expedited, Inc. v. Werner Enterprises, Inc. (9th Cir. 2007) 479 F.3d 1099, 1110-1111.) 

 

2.      Existing economic relationship with probability of future economic benefit

Tort liability exists for interference with “existing contractual relations” and “existing noncontractual relations which hold the promise of future economic advantage.”  (Youst v. Longo (1987) 43 Cal.3d 64, 75; Westside Center Associates v. Safeway Stores 23, Inc. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 507, 524.)  Thus, the alleged relationship must have existed at the time the defendant’s alleged tortious acts occurred.  (Roy Allan Slurry Seal, Inc., supra, 2 Cal.5th 505, 518.)  And while the plaintiff need not provide the name of the third party, the plaintiff must allege a relationship with a “specific” third party and that the actor intended to interfere with this relationship.  (Ramona Manor Convalescent Hospital v. Care Enterprises (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 1120, 1133.)   

 

Plaintiff argues the cross-complaint fails to identify customers with whom Defendant had an existing relationship, at the time of the alleged interference, that was likely to produce economic benefits in the future.

 

Defendant opposes the demurrer citing North American Chemical Co. v. Superior Court (1997).  (59 Cal.App.4th 764, 787.)  In North American Chemical, the Court of Appeal held plaintiff sufficiently pled an economic relationship because the plaintiff specifically named the third-party customer and identified the terms of the specific relationship that defendant’s actions directly harmed.  (Ibid.)

 

In Korea, plaintiff entered into a broker agreement with a company under which plaintiff would represent the company in a bid for a government contract.  (Korea, supra, 29 Cal.4th at pp. 1141-1142.)  Plaintiff pled that its broker agreement with the company fixed plaintiff’s commission at 15 percent of the contract price meaning plaintiff would have been entitled to a $30 million commission if its client had won the contract.  (Id. at pp. 1141, 1164.)  Defendant subsequently engaged in unlawful behavior that caused the government to grant defendant’s bid, and plaintiff did not get a commission.  Thus, plaintiff had established an economic relationship that existed before the bidding process began under which plaintiff had a reasonable expectation it would have received a defined future economic benefit if defendant’s interference never occurred.  (Id. at p. 1164.) 

 

Plaintiff argues the cross-complaint fails to plead a specific existing relationship that was likely to produce economic benefits in the future because the cross-complaint alleges a relationship with Defendant and “prospective” customers of a club rather than existing relationship.  (Demurrer, p. 4:12-14.)  Plaintiff further argues that Defendant must identify a specific customer whose relationship was interfered with.  (Demurrer, p. 4:16-21.)  Plaintiff notes that the cited North American Chemical Company case does not support Defendant’s argument because the North American plaintiff identified a specific customer who had an existing relationship with the plaintiff; Defendant does not.

 

Defendant opposes noting the cross-complaint alleges that Plaintiff’s actions resulted in 4,628 customer cargo claims for more than $1.2 million.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 8.)  These claims exist because Plaintiff did not timely address them and then after being notified of the issues, Plaintiff ceased performing its other contractual obligations.  (Id., ¶ 19-25.)

 

Although Defendant alleges the existence of a relationship with its customers during Plaintiff’s alleged interference, Defendant does not identify these customers or specifically state the terms of the relationship between Defendant and the customers.  

 

Defendant alleges a contractual relationship between Plaintiff and Defendant under which Plaintiff would ship Defendant’s home-improvement products to Defendant’s retailers, distributors, and other customers and would resolve cargo claims for loss or damages.  (Id., ¶¶ 1, 4, 5, 19, Exh. A, at p. 4, section 7(b).)  Plaintiff allegedly knew about these “critical” relationships based on its experience working with Defendant.  (Id., ¶ 20.)  Although Defendant alleges the existence of a relationship with “retailers, distributors, and other customers” during Plaintiff’s alleged interference, Defendant does not identify these customers or specifically state the terms of the relationship between Defendant and the customers.  Defendant also fails to allege when these relationships formed.  Further, the cross-complaint also references both current and prospective customers and economic relationships.  (Id., ¶¶ 23, 31, 34.)

 

Accordingly, because the cross-complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to show an independent wrongful act or existing relationships with which Defendant interfered, the Court sustains Plaintiff’s demurrer to the Second Cause of Action.

 

            Third Cause of Action: Negligent Interference with Economic Relations

Plaintiff demurs to the Third Cause of Action on the ground that it fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action and that it is uncertain as pleaded.

 

To establish negligent interference with prospective economic relations, the plaintiff must plead (1) the existence of a valid contractual relationship or other economic relationship between the plaintiff and a third party containing the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant’s knowledge, actual or construed, of the relationship; (3) the defendant’s knowledge, actual or construed, that the relationship would be disrupted if the defendant failed to act with reasonable care; (4) the defendant’s failure to act with reasonable care; (5) actual disruption of the relationship; and (6) resulting economic harm.”  (Nelson v. Tucker Ellis, LLP (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 827, 839.)  “[C]onduct amounting to a breach of contract becomes tortious only when it also violates an independent duty arising from principles of tort law.”  (Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 551; see also Jones v. Kelly (1929) 208 Cal. 251, 255.)  “A contract to perform services gives rise to a duty of care which requires that such services be performed in a competent and reasonable manner.  A negligent failure to do so may be both a breach of contract and a tort.”  (North American Chemical Co., supra, 59 Cal.App.4th 764, 774.) 

 

Similar to the issues with intentional interference cause of action, Defendant’s Third Cause of Action fails to allege facts sufficient to show the existence of a valid contractual or other economic relationship between Defendant and specific third parties at the time of Plaintiff’s alleged interference. 

 

Accordingly, because the cross-complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to establish a negligent interference with prospective economic relations cause of action, the Court sustains Plaintiff’s demurrer to the Third Cause of Action.

 

            Fourth Cause of Action: Unfair Business Practices, Bus. & Prof., § 17200

Plaintiff demurs to the Fourth Cause of Action on the ground that it fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action and because it relies on the Second and Third Causes of Action, it also fails.

 

California’s Unfair Competition Law (UCL) prohibits unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business practices.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200, et seq.)  To assert a UCL claim, a plaintiff must have suffered injury in fact and lost money or property as a result of the unfair competition.  (See Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17204.)  Unfair behavior under the UCL is behavior that is “immoral, unethical, oppressive, unscrupulous or substantially injurious to consumers.” (Bardin v. Daimlerchrysler Corp. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 1255, 1260.)  The cross-complaint alleges Plaintiff’s actions were fraudulent and unlawful.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 33.) 

 

A plaintiff can plead a UCL violation under the “unlawfulness” prong by pleading that a business practice violated a predicate federal, state, or local law.  (See Cel-Tech Commc’ns, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone. Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 180.)  The cross-complaint does not allege facts stating Plaintiff violated any specific law.  To plead fraud under the UCL, the complaint must state specific facts showing members of the public are likely to be deceived.  (Massachusetts Mutual Life Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1290.)  The complaint may base this claim on “representations to the public which are untrue [or] those which may be accurate on some level, but will nonetheless tend to mislead or deceive.”  (Id. at p. 1289.)  For example, “[a] perfectly true statement couched in such a manner that it is likely to mislead or deceive the consumer, such as by failure to disclose other relevant information, is actionable under [the UCL].”  (Id. at pp. 1289-1290.)   Fraud claims must be pled with specificity, and general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645 [requiring facts showing “how, when, where, to whom, and by what means” the misrepresentations were made].)  When pleading fraud against a corporate entity, the plaintiff must “allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.”  (Tarmann v. State Farm Mutual Auto. Insurance Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.) 

 

Plaintiff argues that The Fourth Cause of Action fails because it is derivative of the Second and Third Causes of Action.  In opposition, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff went beyond breaching the Agreement by taking “retaliatory actions” through actively sabotaging Defendant’s relationships with its customers.  (Opposition, p. 8:27-28.)  In reply, Plaintiff notes that Defendant raises new facts and theories in its opposition by stating Plaintiff “took additional affirmative, retaliatory actions” without identifying any acts in the cross-complaint that rise above breach of contract.  Additionally, the opposition does not cite any alleged facts that Plaintiff’s business acts or practices were forbidden by law or intentionally deceptive.

 

The cross-complaint fails to identify fraudulent conduct that gives rise to a UCL claim.  Defendant presents two statements: (1) Plaintiff stating it was unaware of the cargo claims and (2) Plaintiff’s request for dispute resolution.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 7, 9.)  Defendant alleges Plaintiff engaged in conduct which breached its obligations under the contract—work stoppage, not replacing drivers and equipment, and not dealing with cargo claims—but this conduct does not rise to the level of deceiving or being likely to deceive the public.  The cross-complaint does not allege facts stating who made these statements, when the statements were made, or how the statements were presented to Defendant or the public.  The cross-complaint also fails to allege unlawful conduct because Defendant does not cite any law that Plaintiff’s alleged conduct violates.  Defendant also attempts to shoehorn a phrase about Plaintiff’s “breaching” acts being retaliatory, but Defendant did not allege this in its cross-complaint. Because demurrers are limited to the four corners of the cross-complaint, the Court does not consider this “new” theory of liability.

 

Accordingly, the Court sustains Plaintiff’s demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action on the grounds that the cross-complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to support a UCL claim based on fraudulent or unlawful business practices.

 

            Punitive Damages

Because punitive damages are remedies which are part of a cause of action, rather than an actual cause of action, a court cannot sustain a demurrer to a request for punitive damages.  (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 163.) 

 

Accordingly, the Court does not address Plaintiff’s demurrer to the request for punitive damages.

 

Motion to Strike  

“Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.)  A court may strike from the complaint any irrelevant, false, or improper matter.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).)   The court may also “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)  “The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437, subd. (a).) 

 

            Punitive Damages

Plaintiff moves to strike Defendant’s request for punitive damages (Page 7, Prayer, paragraph 2) from the cross-complaint.

 

To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth specific facts demonstrating the elements stated in Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721; see also Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.)  Malice is “conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (Id. at p. 725; Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).)  Oppression is “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).)  Fraud is “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)  “Malice and oppression may be inferred from the circumstances of a defendant’s conduct.”  (J. R. Norton Co. v. General Teamsters, Warehousemen & Helpers Union, Local 890 (1989) 208 Cal.App.3d 430, 444.)

 

Punitive damages are not available for claims arising from breach of contractual obligations.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  Absent an independent tort, contractual punitive damages are not awarded even if the defendant’s conduct was willful, fraudulent, or malicious.  (Myers Building Indus. Ltd. v. Interface Technology, Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 949, 959; see also Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 516; College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not identified the acts which would give rise to punitive damages or identified the wrongful act which would permit Defendant to succeed on intentional and negligent interference claims.  Because Defendant’s UCL cause of action relies on the acts giving rise to the intentional and negligent interference causes of action, this claim must also fail.  Defendant also fails to plead facts showing Plaintiff’s intent to cause injury or Plaintiff’s malice or oppression through “despicable conduct.”

 

Defendant opposes arguing that the cross-complaint shows that after Defendant notified Plaintiff of the material breaches, Plaintiff’s actions were intentional, retaliatory and harmed Defendant through loss of customers and other costs and damages.  Plaintiff  purposefully initiated a work stoppage and failed to provide and refused to replace qualified drivers and equipment as required under the Agreement.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 9-11, 19-25.)  Plaintiff’s actions were malicious and oppressive because Plaintiff knew about Defendant’s relationships with its customers, that its sabotaging actions would disrupt these relationships, and Plaintiff willfully proceeded to engage in these actions anyway.  (Id., ¶¶ 19-25.)

 

The cross-complaint’s allegations arise from Plaintiff’s alleged breach of its contractual obligations.  Defendant explicitly states that after it sent breach notices to Plaintiff, Plaintiff ceased providing “some of its contractually required transportation services . . . including by initiating a work stoppage.”  (Id., ¶ 10.)  This language indicates a breach of contract.  Further, the cross-complaint fails to sufficiently allege intentional and negligent interference causes of action, and punitive damages are not available for UCL claims.

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to strike punitive damages from the cross-complaint.

 

CONCLUSION & ORDER

The Court sustains Plaintiff’s demurrer to the Second, Third, and Fourth causes of action, with leave to amend.  The Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to strike punitive damages from the cross-complaint.
