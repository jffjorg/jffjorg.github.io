DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV18510    Hearing Date: October 25, 2023    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California
County of Los Angeles
Department 78

ANAIAH SHEHORI,
Plaintiff,
          vs.
DEFENDANT DOE 1, SUPERVISORY COMPANY, et al.,
Defendants. Case No.: 19STCV18510
Hearing Date: October 25, 2023
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 
DEFENDANT DOE 3’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

Defendant Doe 3 Local Church’s Motion for Summary Judgment/Summary Adjudication is GRANTED only as to the cause of action for sexual battery pertaining to Perpetrator 1’s alleged abuse of Plaintiff at Perpetrator 1’s home while Perpetrator 1 was babysitting Plaintiff and DENIED as to the remaining causes of action and DENIED as to the cause of action for sexual battery pertaining to Perpetrator 1’s alleged abuse of Plaintiff while he was a teacher at Plaintiff’s school.
Moving party to give notice.
I. FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for negligence, negligent hiring, negligent failure to warn, train, or educate, negligence per se, sexual battery, and sexual harassment. Plaintiff Anaiah Shehori was raised as a member of the Church of the Living Word (“Church”), a church founded by John Robert Stevens in 1951. (Compl., ¶¶22, 52.) Plaintiff alleges that she was sexually assaulted between the ages of 6 and 14 by her babysitter and later teacher, Perpetrator 1, who was raised in the same church. (Compl., ¶¶59-61.) Plaintiff also alleges that she was sexually assaulted by a high ranking member of her church, Perpetrator 2 between the ages of 11 and 16. (Compl., ¶¶62-67.) 
II. PROCEDURAL HISTORY
On May 28, 2019, Plaintiff Anaiah Shehori filed her Complaint against Defendants Doe 1 Supervisory Company, Doe 2 Family Ministry Corporation, Doe 3 Local Church, Doe 4 Religious Leader, Doe 5 Religious School, Doe 6, and Perpetrator 2.
On June 30, 2023, Defendant Doe 3 Local Church filed this motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication.  
III. LEGAL STANDARD
The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 
“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established” or that there is a complete defense. (Code Civ. Proc., section 437c(p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 
Where, “a defendant moves for summary adjudication on a cause of action for which the plaintiff has the burden of proof at trial, the defendant ‘must present evidence that either “conclusively negate[s] an element of the plaintiff's cause of action” or “show[s] that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain,” evidence necessary to establish at least one element of the cause of action. [Citation.] Only after the defendant carries that initial burden does the burden shift to the plaintiff “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action ....” ’ ” (Doe v. The Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 657, 668) ; see Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 853-854..) 
“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., section 437c(c).)

IV. EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS
Plaintiff objects to Defendant’s evidence submitted in support of the motion for summary judgment. Plaintiff’s objections are to Defendant’s separate statement and not to any specific piece of evidence. Plaintiff’s objections are overruled.
Defendant objects to Plaintiff’s evidence submitted in opposition to the motion for summary judgment.
The following objections are OVERRULED: 1, 4, 6, 7, 10, and 11.
The following objections are SUSTAINED: 2, 3, 5, 8, 9, 12, 13, 14, 15, and 16.
V. DISCUSSION
Doe 3 moves for summary judgment/summary adjudication on the grounds that there are no triable issues of material fact as to one of more essential elements of Plaintiff’s causes of action for negligence, negligence per se, negligent failure to warn, and sexual battery. Although Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges incidents of sexual abuse committed by both Perpetrator 1 and Perpetrator 2, Doe 3 only moves for summary adjudication as to the sexual abuse allegedly committed by Perpetrator 1.
1. Negligence 
Doe 3 moves for summary judgment as to this cause of action on the grounds that it (1) had no duty to protect Plaintiff while was she was at Perpetrator 1’s home and (2) it did not breach any duty to Plaintiff at that time as Defendant Doe 3 was unaware of the abuse.
Generally, the existence of duty is a question of law. (Vasilenko v. Grace Family Church (2017) 3 Cal.5th 1077, 1083.)
Generally, a person who has not created a peril does not have liability in tort for the failure to assist or protect another individual from that peril. (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 213.) 
However, there are exceptions to the general rule. In some circumstances a defendant may have an affirmative duty to protect a plaintiff from harm caused by a third party, even if that defendant did not create the risk of the harm suffered. (Id. at 215.)
A recognized exception to this general rule is when a defendant has a special relationship with either the perpetrator of the harm or the victim who suffered the harm. (Id.)
A special relationship exists where a plaintiff is particularly vulnerable and dependent upon a defendant and that defendant has some control over that plaintiff’s welfare. (Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 
Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff was a congregant at Doe 3, the Church of the Living Word located in North Hills, California (“the Church”), along with her entire family. (PUMF No. 17.) It is also undisputed that Perpetrator 1 was also a member of the Church. (PUMF No. 20.) Plaintiff was a student at a school operated by Doe 3, known as Centers of Learning (“the School”), from preschool until approximately 2001, including during the time that she alleges that she was abused by Perpetrator 1 when he was her babysitter. (PUMF No. 17.) Perpetrator 1 attended the School and later became a teacher there. Plaintiff alleges that Perpetrator 1 sexually abused her from the ages of 3 to 12 while Perpetrator 1 babysat her at his parent’s home. (UMF No. 2)
Doe 3 argues that it had no duty to protect Plaintiff from Perpetrator 1 because Plaintiff’s harm with respect to the time period before she entered high school was caused by Perpetrator 1 in his family home. 
Perpetrator 1 was a teacher at School who taught Plaintiff in high school. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 56.) It is well settled that a school has a special relationship with minor children who attend that school. Thus, Doe 3 owed Plaintiff a duty to protect her from Perpetrator 1 while he was employed at School due to the special relationship between School and Plaintiff and between School and Perpetrator 1. As a student, Plaintiff would have reasonably expected Doe 3 to protect her from Perpetrator 1, Doe 3’s employee.
Doe 3 does not mention Perpetrator 1’s employment as a teacher at School in its motion, and instead seeks summary adjudication as to the issue of duty with respect to earlier abuse which took place at Perpetrator 1’s family home. 
“For summary adjudication purposes, separate wrongful acts may give rise to separate causes of action. Whether they are pleaded in the same or single counts is not determinative.” (The Rutter Group, Civil Procedure Before Trial, §10:39.2 (citing Lilienthal & Fowler v. Superior Court (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1854; Blue Mountain Enterprises, LLC v. Owens (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 537, 549-550; and Cal. Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).)
Here, the alleged abuse consisted of separate and distinct acts by Perpetrator 1. As recognized by the Court of Appeal, “a party may present a motion for summary adjudication challenging a separate and distinct wrongful act even though combined with other wrongful acts alleged in the same cause of action.” (Lilienthal & Fowler v. Superior Court (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1854-1855.)
Because Plaintiff alleges separate acts of sexual abuse by Perpetrator 1, the acts which took place before Perpetrator 1 was hired at the School are the proper subject of a motion for summary adjudication.
Doe 3 relies solely on the declaration of Matt Walkoe to support its motion. Walkoe testifies that he is a former pastor of the Church, served as an officer and director of the Church, was a member of the Apostolic Company (“APCO”), and also taught at the School between 2014 and 2018. (Walkoe Decl., ¶¶2-4.) Walkoe alleges Perpetrator 1’s abuse of Plaintiff when she was a child was never reported to him and had it been reported, Perpetrator 1 would never have been hired as a teacher. (Id., ¶6.) Walkoe did not hear about Perpetrator 1’s abuse of Plaintiff until this case was filed. (Id., ¶7.)
Doe 3’s evidence at most shows that Matt Walkoe was not aware of Perpetrator 1’s alleged abuse of Plaintiff at the family home while Plaintiff was a student at the School. Although Walkoe testifies Perpetrator 1 would not have been hired as a teacher had the School known of the abuse, Walkoe did not teach at the School until 2014, after Plaintiff had already graduated. Additionally, Doe 3 fails to provide evidence that Walkoe was involved in the School’s administration at an earlier point. Thus, Doe 3 fails to meet its burden of demonstrating that it did not have knowledge of the abuse. 
Moreover, Defendant fails to analyze the Rowland factors and thus does not meet its moving burden for that reason as well. (Doe at Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 131.) 
Under these circumstances, the Court cannot find that as a matter of law no duty was owed and denies the motion for summary adjudication.
Doe 3 also alleges on reply that Perpetrator 1’s actions after he became a teacher did not constitute sexual assault. Defendant cannot raise a new ground or basis for summary adjudication in a reply brief. The clear basis set forth as to the lack of duty relate to the babysitting activity as set forth in Doe 3’s Motion for Summary Judgment.
2. Negligent failure to warn, train, or educate
Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s cause of action for negligent failure to warn, train, or educate fails because the Church did not have a youth protection program which it failed to implement.
Defendant cites Juarez v. Boy Scouts of America, Inc. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 377, 409-410, disapproved in part by Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 222, which involved a former boy scout who alleged that the scouts owed him a duty to take reasonable measures to protect him from sexual assault committed by his scoutmaster. The court there, applying factors from Rowland v. Christian, 69 Cal.2d 108, determined that where there was a known risk of child molestation, a special relationship did exist between the scouts and the victim to protect him from harm caused by the third party criminal conduct committed by his scoutmaster, including warning the victim about how to handle the sexual abuse.
Doe 3’s evidence, the declaration of Matt Walkoe, demonstrates that Matt Walkoe did not receive any reports of Perpetrator 1’s alleged abuse. (Matt Walkoe Decl., ¶7.) Additionally, Plaintiff had never reported the abuse to him or any other church leaders he is aware of. (Id., ¶9.) 
Doe 3’s evidence at most shows that Matt Walkoe did not receive reports of Perpetrator 1’s alleged abuse. Although Walkoe testifies he is not aware that any report had been made to other church leaders, he lacks personal knowledge to testify on what other church leaders knew. The evidence does not show that Doe 3 was unaware of Perpetrator 1’s alleged abuse that would give rise to a duty to warn of sexual abuse and how to handle such situations. Doe 3 thus fails to meet its burden of showing that no triable issue of material fact remains over whether Doe 3 had a duty to warn of Perpetrator 1’s sexual abuse. 
Summary adjudication is denied on this ground.
3. Negligence per se
Doe 3 next argues that the theory of liability under a theory of negligence per se fails because Doe 3 did not know about Perpetrator 1’s alleged abuse until after this Complaint was filed.
“The negligence per se doctrine, as codified in Evidence Code section 669, creates a presumption of negligence if four elements are established: (1) the defendant violated a statute, ordinance, or regulation of a public entity; (2) the violation proximately caused death or injury to person or property; (3) the death or injury resulted from an occurrence of the nature of which the statute, ordinance, or regulation was designed to prevent; and (4) the person suffering the death or the injury to his person or property was one of the class of persons for whose protection the statute, ordinance, or regulation was adopted.” (Spates v. Dameron Hospital Association (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 218, quotation marks omitted.) “The doctrine of negligence per se is not a separate cause of action but creates an evidentiary presumption that affects the standard of care in a cause of action for negligence.” (Johnson v. Honeywell International Inc. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 549, 555, quotation marks and brackets omitted.)
Here, Doe 3 cites Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School District (2021) 72Cal.App.5th 113, 119 in support of its argument that the Church did not have any reason to suspect the abuse before the filing of the lawsuit. There, a court ruled that mandated reporting under Pen. Code, section 11166 requires mandated reporters to report abuse and reporters breach their duty to report only if a reasonable person in a like position would have discovered the abuse and those facts would have caused a reasonable person in a like position to suspect abuse. (Id at p.139.) However, as discussed above, Doe 3’s evidence is insufficient to show Doe 3 had no knowledge of Perpetrator 1’s abuse because the evidence at most shows Matt Walkoe had no knowledge of the abuse. For the reasons set forth above, Walkoe’s lack of knowledge is not particularly meaningful given the fact that he did not even work at the school during the relevant time frame.  
Summary adjudication is denied on this ground.
4. Sexual Battery 
Doe 3 argues that the cause of action for sexual battery fails because Doe 3 cannot be held vicariously liable for Perpetrator 1’s sexual abuse. 

Civ. Code, section 1708.5 provides the following: 
(a) A person commits a sexual battery who does any of the following: 
(1) Acts with the intent to cause a harmful or offensive contact with an intimate part of another, and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results. 
(2) Acts with the intent to cause a harmful or offensive contact with another by use of his or her intimate part, and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results. 
(3) Acts to cause an imminent apprehension of the conduct described in paragraph (1) or (2), and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results. 
(Civ. Code, § 1708.5(a).)  

Section 1708.5 defines an intimate part as “the sexual organ, anus, groin, or buttocks of any person, or the breast of a female” and offensive contact as “contact that offends a reasonable sense of personal dignity.” (Id., § 1708.5(d), (f).)  
“A cause of action for sexual battery under Civil Code section 1708.5 requires the batterer intend to cause a ‘harmful or offensive’ contact and the batteree suffer a ‘sexually offensive contact.’” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1225; see also Jacqueline R. v. Household of Faith Family Church, Inc. (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 198, 208 (quoting Angie M.).)  
“[R]espondeat superior liability attaches if the activities that cause[d] the employee to become an instrumentality of danger to others’ were undertaken with the employer’s permission and were of some benefit to the employer or, in the absence of proof of benefit, the activities constituted a customary incident of employment.” (Purton v. Marriott International., Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 499,509. Because it can be difficult to demonstrate “that a corporation is directly liable for the actions of its employees or agents,” respondeat superior “can allow a plaintiff to proceed against a corporation that could have been liable under a burdensome direct liability theory.” (Presbyterian Camp & Conference Centers, Inc. v. Superior Court (2021) 12 Cal.5th 493, 515) “‘Employee[s]’ include most persons ‘in the service of an employer under any ... contract of hire’ ..., but do not include independent contractors.” (S. G. Borello & Sons, Inc. v. Dept. of Indus. Rel. (1989) 48 Cal.3d 341, 349, 256 (Borello).)
Here, Doe 3 alleges that it cannot be vicariously liable for Perpetrator 1’s earlier instances of sexual abuse which took place before Doe 3 hired Perpetrator 1 because it did not take place in an employment context. There is no dispute about that fact.
Plaintiff argues that summary adjudication must be denied because Doe 3 fails to shift the burden to Plaintiff. However, Plaintiff’s own allegations in the Complaint state that Perpetrator 1’s earlier instances of sexual abuse took place while he was babysitting Plaintiff before he was hired as a teacher at the School. Because it is undisputed that Perpetrator 1 was not working for Doe 3 as an employee or agent during the earlier instances of sexual abuse, no triable issues of material fact remain over whether Perpetrator 1 was an agent of Doe 3 at the time of the earlier sexual abuse.
Summary adjudication is granted as to this cause of action only as to the earlier instances of sexual abuse.

[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 
DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT FILED BY DEFENDANT PACIFIC UNION INTERNATIONAL, INC.

The Demurrer filed by Defendants Pacific Union International, Inc., Daniel Kim and Compass California, Inc. is OVERRULED.
Defendants’ Motion to Strike is DENIED as to punitive damages and GRANTED as to attorney’s fees. The ruling with respect to attorney’s fees applies to Moving Defendants only.
Plaintiff should be prepared to argue the issue of leave to amend with respect to attorney’s fees.
Moving party to provide notice and to file proof of service of such notice within five court days after the date of this order.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for failure to disclose, intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, and breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiff alleges that in June 2020, he entered into a residential purchase agreement with Defendants to purchase a property at 1028 South Wilton Place, Los Angeles, California 90019. Defendants did not disclose at the time of the sale that (1) the garage converted into a living unit did not pass inspections by the Los Angeles Department of Building and Safety (“LADBS”), (2) repairs of the main residence after fire damage did not pass LADBS inspections, and (3) the value of the property was substantially less than the price he offered and paid due to the defects Defendants failed to disclose.
PROCEDURAL HISTORY
On May 2, 2023, Plaintiff Yoan Koh filed his Complaint against Defendants In S. Park, John H. Lee, and Pacific Unition International, Inc. dba Compass Real Estate, Daniel Kim, and Compass California, Inc. 
On June 14, 2023, Defendant John H. Lee answered.
On July 6, 2023, Defendants Pacific Union International, Inc., Daniel Kim and Compass California, Inc. (“Moving Defendants”) filed a demurrer and motion to strike.
DISCUSSION
I. Demurrer
Moving Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s Complaint on the grounds that (1) the cause of action for failure to disclose under Civ. Code, section 1102 fails because section 1102 does not apply to real estate agents or brokers, (2) Plaintiff has failed to plead intentional misrepresentation with particularity, (3) the third cause of action for negligent misrepresentation fails because Defendants made representations that they had no reasonable grounds to believe were true, and (4) Plaintiff attaches a legal duty to Defendants that exceeds the scope of the inspection and disclosure duties under Civ. Code, section 2079.5.
A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Proc., section, section 430.30, 430.70.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) 
Meet and Confer
A party filing a demurrer “shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.41(a).) “The parties shall meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. If the parties are not able to meet and confer at least five days prior to the date the responsive pleading is due, the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension of time within which to file a responsive pleading, by filing and serving, on or before the date on which a demurrer would be due, a declaration stating under penalty of perjury that a good faith attempt to meet and confer was made and explaining the reasons why the parties could not meet and confer.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.41(a)(2).) A failure to meet and confer does not constitute grounds to sustain or overrule a demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., sections 430.41 (a)(4).)
Here, Moving Defendants’ counsel testifies that in May 2023, an attorney at his office sent meet and confer correspondence to Plaintiff’s counsel and received no response. (Slome Decl., ¶2.) Because Moving Defendants’ counsel attempted to meet and confer and received no response, Moving Defendants satisfied meet and confer requirements.
First Cause of Action – Failure to Disclose under Civ. Code, Section 1102
Defendant Pacific (the only Moving Defendant named in this cause of action) demurs to the first cause of action for failure to disclose under Civ. Code, section 1102 on the grounds that the section does not apply to real estate agents or brokers. 
Pursuant to Civil Code, section 1102, et seq., a seller is obligated to inspect and disclose all material facts that affect and relate to the condition of a property. In complying with this statutory duty of disclosure, a transfer disclosure statement form must be used. (Civ. Code, section 1102.6.) Specifically, the form provided in Civil Code, section 1102.6 requires the seller to disclose whether the seller is aware of any significant defects with the windows, foundation, or other parts of the house. Agents are required to make their disclosures based on inquiry of the sellers and diligent visual inspection of the accessible areas of the property. 
A seller’s real estate agent or broker has a statutory duty under Civil Code, section 1102 to disclose only what a reasonably competent and diligent visual inspection would reveal and that the disclosures be made in good faith. (Peake v. Underwood (2014) 227 Cal. App. 4th 428, 442 (citing Robinson v. Grossman (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 634, 642-643).) (“Peake”) Section 1102 imposes the same limited duties as Civ. Code, section 2079 to disclose visible defects which a reasonably competent and diligent visual inspection of the property would reveal. (Id.)
No transfer subject to the article shall be invalidated solely because of a violation of Civ. Code, section 1102, et seq. (Civ. Code, section 1102.13.) However, any person who willfully or negligently violates or fails to perform under these sections is liable for the actual damages suffered by the transferee. (Id.)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that Defendant Park was listed as the seller in the subject real estate purchase agreement. (Compl., ¶17.) Defendants Lee and Pacific are listed as the seller’s real estate sales person and real estate broker respectively on the agreement. (Compl., ¶¶18-19.) Defendants intentionally did not disclose facts materially affecting the value and desirability of the subject property, including the physical condition of the property, that the house had been damaged by a fire in 2018, and that the property was not in compliance with the Building Code of the City of Los Angeles. (Compl., ¶21.)
Defendant Pacific argues that section 1102 does not apply to real estate agents or brokers because the duties of agents or brokers stem from Civ. Code, section 2079. However, section 1102 imposes the same duties as section 2079, meaning both apply to agents or brokers. Additionally, the disclosure form provided in section 1102.6 includes a section specifically for agents. California courts have also held that brokers and agents owe a duty to disclose all material facts that affect and relate to the condition of a property that a reasonably competent and diligent inspection would reveal. (Peake at p. 442.) Here, Pacific offers no authority in its moving papers or reply supporting its position that section 1102 should not apply to agents and brokers. 
Plaintiff’s Complaint states that Defendant Pacific failed to disclose facts about the property, including its physical condition, which affected the condition of the subject property at the time of the sale. Read liberally and in context, these facts are sufficient to support a cause of action for failure to disclose under Civ. Code, section 1102 because the physical condition of a property could reasonably have been revealed through a reasonably competent and diligent inspection of the property.
The demurrer is overruled on this ground.
Second Cause of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation
Defendant Pacific (the only Moving Defendant named in this cause of action) next argues that the Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation because the Complaint lacks the required specificity.
The elements of intentional misrepresentation are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.) In California, fraud, including negligent misrepresentation, must be pled with specificity. (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)  
As discussed above, the Complaint alleges that Defendants failed to disclose the physical condition of the property, that the garage and repairs for fire damage did not pass LADBS inspections, and that the property’s value was significantly lower than the price Plaintiff offered and paid. Plaintiff relied on representations from Defendants that the subject property was building code compliant and had passed all inspections performed by the LADBS when he purchased the property. (Compl., ¶25.) Defendants knew the misrepresentations were false when they made them and intended the misrepresentations to induce Plaintiff to enter into the residential purchase agreement. (Compl., ¶27.) As a result of the misrepresentations, Plaintiff suffered damages of $1,060,000. (Compl., ¶30)
The Complaint specifically states that Defendants misrepresented the physical condition of the property, that the property was building code compliant, and that the property passed all building inspections. Defendants also knew the misrepresentations were false and intended the misrepresentations to induce Plaintiff into purchasing the property. Finally, Plaintiff relied on the misrepresentations to his detriment. The Court finds that because the Complaint states what misrepresentations were made, the Complaint pleads a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation with sufficient specificity. 
The demurrer is overruled on this ground.
Third Cause of Action – Negligent Misrepresentation
Defendant Pacific (the only Moving Defendant named in this cause of action) demurs to the third cause of action for negligent misrepresentation on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to plead the cause of action with specificity. Additionally, Pacific argues that Plaintiff did not allege that Plaintiff relied on the misrepresented facts, that Defendants intended to induce Plaintiff into relying on the misrepresentations, or how he was economically harmed. 
The elements of a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation are “misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, and with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented; ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation by the party to whom it was directed; and resulting damage.” (Hydro-Mill Co., Inc. v. Hayward, Tilton & Rolapp Ins. Associates, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1154, quotation marks omitted.)
Here, as discussed above, Plaintiff’s Complaint states a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation with sufficient specificity. Contrary to Pacific’s arguments, the Complaint explicitly states Defendants knew the misrepresentations were false, intended to induce Plaintiff into purchasing the home, and that Plaintiff suffered economic damages as a result of the misrepresentation. Even if the Complaint contains few details about how the misrepresentations resulted in Plaintiff’s economic losses, it is reasonable to infer from the other facts presented that Plaintiff paid more for the property than he would have had he known the property was not building code compliant.
The demurrer is overruled on this ground.
Fourth Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty
Defendants Kim and Compass California, Inc. (the only Moving Defendants named in this cause of action) demur to the fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty on the grounds that the Complaint attempts to attach a legal duty that exceeds the scope of their obligations. Specifically, Defendants Kim and Compass (who represented Plaintiff)  argue that the non-conforming work and resulting deficiencies would not have been revealed through a reasonably competent visual inspection of the property.
The elements for a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.) “Before a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.” Apollo Capital Fund, LLC v. Roth Capital Partners, LLC (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 226, 246. 
Here, the contention that the non-conforming defects would not have been revealed through a visual inspection is a dispute of fact which may not be adjudicated through a demurrer. 
The demurrer is overruled on this ground.
II. Motion to Strike
Moving Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s demand for punitive damages and attorney’s fees.
Punitive Damages 

In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.) These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code, section 3294, subd. (a).) 
“Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725 [examining Civ. Code, section 3294, subd. (c)(1)].) “A conscious disregard of the safety of others may constitute malice within the meaning of section 3294 of the Civil Code. In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895-896.) 
“As amended to include [despicable], the statute plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, ‘malice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs’ interests. The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.” (College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.) The statute’s reference to despicable conduct represents a “new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.” (Ibid.) Despicable conduct is “conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. Such conduct has been described as ‘having the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.’” (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.) 
Here, the Complaint states that Defendants intentionally misrepresented that the subject property had passed building inspections and was building code compliant to induce Plaintiff into purchasing the subject property. The facts alleged in the Complaint sufficiently show that Defendants acted with a willful disregard for Plaintiff’s rights because the misrepresentation was intentional. Additionally, Defendants’ conduct rises to the level of despicable conduct because Defendants’ intentional misrepresentations rise to the level of conduct which would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary people. 
The motion to strike is denied on this ground.
Attorney’s Fees
Moving Defendants move to strike the demand for attorney’s fees, arguing that section 25 of the parties real estate purchase agreement only applies to Plaintiff and the seller, Defendant Park.
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that section 25 of the residential purchase agreement and joint escrow instructions provides for an award of attorney fees and costs to the prevailing party in any legal action involving the property. 
Plaintiff does not include the full text of the agreement or address the Moving Defendants’ arguments.
The motion to strike is granted on this ground.
