ORDER SUSTAINING WITH LEAVE TO AMEND DEFENDANT RICHARD SANDOR, M.D.’S DEMURRER AND DENYING HIS MOTION TO STRIKE

 

ORDER GRANTING IN PART DEFENDANT WARNER PARK RECOVERY CENTER, LLC’S MOTION TO STRIKE WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

 

 

 

 

I.        INTRODUCTION

This action arises out of an allegedly wrongful death. Plaintiff Melissa Seyed, successor in interest of Kia Hoghoughi (“Hoghoughi”)(collectively, “Plaintiffs”), alleges both in her representative and individual capacity that Defendants Sunshine Sober Living Support System, Inc. dba Sunshine Sober Living (“Sunshine”), Richard Goldberg (“Richard”), Wendy Goldberg (“Wendy”), Warner Park Recovery Center, LLC dba Warner Park Recovery Center (“Warner Park”), and Richard Sandor, M.D. (“Sandor”)(collectively, “Defendants”) caused or contributed to Hoghoughi’s death by fentanyl overdose. Plaintiffs allege Hoghoughi was admitted into the care and custody of Defendants on November 28, 2022, but that he overdosed on January 18, 2023.

Sandor now demurrers to Plaintiffs’ second, third, and fourth causes of action. Sandor also seeks to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations.

Warner Park also seeks to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ Complaint.     

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On April 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Fraud; (3) Unlawful Business Practices; (4) Dependent Adult Neglect; (5) Negligence; and (6) Wrongful Death.

On October 31, 2024, Warner Park filed the instant Motion to Strike (“Warner Park Motion”).

On November 6, 2024, Sandor filed the instant Demurrer and Motion to Strike (“Sandor Motion”).

On December 20, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition to Warner Park’s Motion (“Opposition Warner Park Motion”).

On December 20, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition to Sandor’s Demurrer (“Opposition Demurrer”) and Motion (“Opposition Sandor Motion”).

On December 27, 2024, Warner Park filed its Reply.

On December 30, 2024, Sandor filed his Replies.

III.     SANDOR’S DEMURRER

A.       Legal Standard.

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)[1] It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (See Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007 (citation omitted); see also Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).) 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.” (Id.) In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (See SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) Accordingly, “[w]hether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer.” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609-10.) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (See Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).  

B.      Sandor Did Not Properly Meet and Confer.

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a), emphasis added.) A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(3).)

Sandor’s counsel filed a declaration attesting to her meet and confer efforts, which consisted of one email to Plaintiffs’ counsel. (See Declaration of Julia Murray filed November 6, 2024 (“Murray Decl.”), ¶ 2, Ex. A.) Sandor’s counsel declares Plaintiffs’ counsel did not reply. (See Murray Decl., ¶ 2.) The Court does not find Sandor has satisfied his meet and confer obligations. The Code of Civil Procedure specifically requires in person, telephonic, or video conference communications. E-mail is insufficient. The Court will rule on Sandor’s Demurrer and Motion in the interest of judicial efficiency but all parties’ counsel are warned the Court is unlikely to do so in the future should they failure to meet and confer as required prior to any future filings.                     

C.      Analysis.

1.               Plaintiffs Fail to State Sufficient Facts to State a Fraud Cause of Action Against Sandor.

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Conroy v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2009) 45 Cal. 4th 1244, 1255.)

“[F]raud must be specifically pleaded. This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.” (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.) In addition, a cause of action of fraud against a corporation requires a plaintiff to allege the names of persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when. (See Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157-58.) 

Sandor argues Plaintiffs fail to allege Sandor made any representations and thus has not pleaded fraud with the requisite specificity. (See Demurrer, pg. 5.) 

In Opposition, Plaintiffs argue they allege Warner Park advertised Sandor’s care as part of its advertising pitch, with the cooperation of Sandor, and that Defendants, including Sandor, “misrepresented to Plaintiffs that Warner Park Recovery Center was a licensed medical facility.” (Opposition Demurrer, pp. 4-5.) Plaintiffs argue this is enough to allege fraud. (See id.)

The Court disagrees with Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs’ Opposition notably fails to address Sandor’s argument in Demurrer—that Plaintiffs’ Complaint does not allege Sandor made any representations himself. General allegations of a scheme to defraud by the Defendants as a collective, as Plaintiffs have elected to make here, are not enough to meet fraud’s specificity requirement.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Sandor’s Demurrer as to Plaintiffs’ second cause of action for fraud.

2.               Plaintiffs Fail to State Sufficient Facts to State an Unlawful Business Practices Cause of Action Against Sandor.

Unfair competition is defined as “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.”¿(Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200.) By proscribing “any unlawful” business act or practice, the unfair competition law (“UCL”) “borrows” rules set out in other laws and makes violations of those rules independently actionable.¿(See Zhang v. Superior Court (2013) 57 Cal.4th 364, 370.)¿A “violation of another law is a predicate for stating a cause of action under the UCL’s unlawful prong.”¿(Berryman v. Merit Property Management, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 1544, 1554.)

Sandor argues none of Plaintiffs’ allegations allege Sandor committed any acts and, in any event, Business & Professions Code § 17200 does not apply to medical care. (See Demurrer, pp. 6-7.)

In Opposition, Plaintiffs argue “Sandor was an integral part of the scheme set up to drive up profits by misleading potential residents. This misleading conduct, as alleged, is the exactly the sort of conduct prohibited by the Unfair Business Practices Act.” (Opposition Demurrer, pg. 6.)

The Court agrees with Sandor. Plaintiffs have failed to state sufficient facts to support an unlawful business practices cause of action against him. Plaintiffs do not allege any actual acts done by Sandor that could constitute a “business practice” of Sandor’s, instead focusing their third cause of action exclusively on the actions of businesses Warner Park and Sunshine. (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶ 84 (“[Warner Park] and [Sunshine] knew or should have known that their misrepresentations as to those services were untrue or misleading.”).) As currently alleged, Sandor is merely “affiliated with and providing care for the clients of” Warner Park but is not holding himself out to be a representative of Warner Park such that he could be liable for Warner Park’s allegedly unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business practices. (Complaint, ¶ 7.) General agency allegations such as these are insufficient to support a claim Sandor engaged in “business practices” as required by the law.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Sandor’s Demurrer as to Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for unlawful business practices.

3.               Plaintiffs Fail to State Sufficient Facts to State a Dependent Adult Neglect Cause of Action Against Sandor.

To state a cause of action for dependent adult abuse and neglect, a plaintiff must allege “[p]hysical abuse, neglect, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering.” (Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protections Act (“Act”), Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.07, (a)(1).) A plaintiff must prove: (1) that the defendant had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care; (2) that the defendant knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs; (3) that the defendant denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult's basic needs, either (a) with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the plaintiff alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or (b) with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (if the plaintiff alleges recklessness); and (4) that the defendant’s neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering. (See Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 407.) 

A “substantial caretaking or custodial role” must be alleged. (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 158 (“…it is the defendant's relationship with an elder or a dependent adult—not the defendant's professional standing or expertise—that makes the defendant potentially liable for neglect.”).) Whether a relationship gives rise to a custodial role is determined on a case-by-case basis. (See Oroville Hospital v. Superior Court (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 382, 405.) Admittance to an acute care facility is sufficient to make [a plaintiff] a “dependent adult” who would be entitled to the [Act's] protections. (See Stewart v. Superior Court (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 87, 102 (citing § 15610.23(b) and Health & Saf. Code § 1250(a)).) The Act still requires, however, “a separate analysis to determine whether such a [custodial] relationship exists.” (Winn, 63 Cal.4th at 164.) 

Furthermore, to constitute abuse and neglect within the meaning of the Act, a plaintiff “must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that defendant is guilty of something more than negligence; he or she must show reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct,” with recklessness referring “to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a “deliberate disregard” of the “high degree of probability” that an injury will occur,” and “[o]ppression, fraud and malice [involving] intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature.” (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 88-89, quotation marks omitted.)¿ 

Sandor argues “there are no specific allegations against [him] and how his actions or inactions meet the threshold requirements of this statute, instead he again appears to be lumped in with the multiple defendants.” (Demurrer, pg. 8.)

In Opposition, Plaintiffs argue “Defendant Sandor is not simply lumped in with the other defendants in this action. Rather, Defendant Sandor is the key figure responsible for the neglect.” (Opposition Demurrer, pg. 8.)

 Plaintiffs have once again failed to state sufficient facts to support a cause of action – this time for dependent abuse against Sandor. As Sandor argues, there are no specific allegations against any of the Defendants and therefore no allegations that Sandor in particular had a custodial relationship with Hoghoughi or that he acted recklessly, oppressively, fraudulently, or maliciously as required by the law. Plaintiff’s most pointed allegation against Sandor merely states the following: “On the day that Mr. Hoghoughi died, he had not seen his psychiatrist Defendant RICHARD SANDOR, M.D. for 20 days, and he had not received any clinical treatment for 5 days. Mr. Hoghoughi’s treatment plan had not been updated since December 5, 2022, 44 days before he died, despite his significant symptoms of high risk for relapse.” (Complaint, ¶ 30.) Without more, this allegation merely sounds in negligence and does not rise to the level of dependent abuse.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Sandor’s Demurrer as to Plaintiffs’ fourth cause of action for dependent adult neglect.

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Sandor’s Demurrer as to Plaintiffs’ second, third, and fourth causes of action.

IV.     MOTIONS TO STRIKE

          A.       Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (See id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (See id. at § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (See id. at § 437.)

B.      The Court Denies Both Sandor’s and Warner Park’s Motions.

1.        Sandor’s Motion.

Punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(a).) “Malice” is defined as conduct intended to cause injury to a person or despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (See Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. Cal., Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (See id.) “Fraud” is an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known by defendant, with intent to deprive a person of property, rights or otherwise cause injury. (See id.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.) 

“In any action for damages arising out of the professional negligence of a health care provider, no claim for punitive damages¿shall be included in a complaint or other pleading unless the court enters an order allowing an amended pleading that includes a claim for punitive damages to be filed. The court may allow the filing of an amended pleading claiming punitive damages on a motion by the party seeking the amended pleading and on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to¿Section 3294 of the Civil Code.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.13(a).) 

Sandor seeks to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations, Paragraph 106, p, 20 lines 23-25 and Paragraph 4 of the Complaint in the Prayer for Damages. (See Sandor Motion, pg. 2.) Sandor argues the instant action is, at its core, one for medical malpractice and Plaintiffs must obtain the Court’s permission to include punitive damages allegations pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.13. (See Sandor Motion, pg. 5.)

In Opposition, Plaintiffs appear to argue their punitive damages allegations are based not “in professional negligence” but that it “arises out of violations of the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act.” (Opposition Sandor Motion, pg. 4.) Plaintiffs argue they can therefore plead punitive damages without first obtaining this Court’s permission. (See id.)

In light of the Court’s decision to sustain Sandor’s Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act claim, the Court denies as moot Sandor’s Motion as it relates to Plaintiff’s punitive damages allegations in that cause of action. In addition, the Court is unaware of any authority, nor does Sandor cite any, to support his proposition that wrongful death claims cannot include punitive damages claims as a matter of law.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Sandor’s Motion.

2.       Warner Park’s Motion Is Granted In Part.

a.       Judicial Notice Is Inappropriate

Warner Park requests the Court take judicial notice of (1) Warner Park’s August 12, 2024 Statement of Information found on the California Secretary of State’s website; (2) California Department of Health Care Services’ (“DHCS”) Certification for Alcohol and Other Drug Programs 1.0 found on DHCS’ website; (3) DHCS’ Initial Treatment Provider Application (Form DHCS 6002) found on DHCS’ website; and (4) DHCS Behavioral Health Information Notice No. 24-001 found on DHCS’ website. (See Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”), referencing the Declaration of Michael Newborn filed October 31, 2024 (“Newborn Decl.”), ¶¶ 3-6, Exs. A-D.)

          The Court DENIES Warner Park’s RJN in its entirety. The Court DENIES Warner Park’s RJN of No. 1, above, as materials prepared by private parties that are on file with governmental agencies, such as the Secretary of State, are not official records of which judicial notice may be taken. (People v. Thacker (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 594, 598-599; Stevens v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 594, 608.) The Court also DENIES Warner Park’s RJN of Nos. 2-4, above, as judicial notice of Internet pages of even official entities may be improper absent evidentiary foundation showing the following elements: 1) Author, 2) date of creation, 3) purpose, 4) reliability, and 5) veracity. (See Hartwell Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 27 Cal.4th 256, 279, fn. 12; see also, e.g., Zumbrun Law Firm v. Cal. Legis. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1603, 1623 (“The website [of the California Legislature, www.leginfo.ca.gov ] is not the official, printed Government Code, and makes no promises regarding its accuracy.”)

b.       Warner Park’s Motion.

Warner Park seeks to strike Plaintiffs’ alter ego, agency, partnership, and joint venture allegations (paragraphs 12 (page 3, lines 21-25), 58 (in its entirety), 60 (page 12, lines 19-21), 61 (page 12, lines 23-26), 50 (page 10, lines 24-26), 90 (page 18, lines 19-20)) and unlawful business practices allegations (paragraphs 82, 84, and 85 (page 16, lines 24-27 and page 17, lines 5-8)). (See Warner Park Motion.)

Warner Park first argues Plaintiffs fail to plead the requisite elements of an alter ego theory because Plaintiffs fail to plead Defendants shared a unity of ownership or interest or that there would be an inequitable result if an alter ego relationship was not found. (See Warner Park Motion, pp. 5-8.) Warner Park next argues Plaintiffs fail to plead an agency or employment theory of liability because Plaintiffs “failed to plead which party is the principal and which is the agent, which is the employer and which is the employee, who controlled whom, or the nature of any agreement that existed between any of the parties.” (Id. at pg. 9.) Warner Park also argues Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged the existence of a partnership or joint venture agreement. (See id. at pp. 9-10.)

In Opposition, Plaintiffs argue “[t]he operation of a joint scheme lure in patients, along with misrepresentations and an insurance fraud scheme, gives rise to the existence of some sort of relationship between the various defendants. The specific nature of this relationship is known only to Defendants. The type of relationship between the various defendants and the nature of those relationship will be teased out in discovery.” (Opposition Warner Park Motion, pg. 4.)

The Court finds Plaintiffs’ allegations of an alter ego, agency, partnership, or joint venture relationship between the parties insufficiently overbroad at the pleading stage. The Court concedes the Court of Appeal has acknowledged “[c]omplaints in actions against multiple defendants commonly include conclusory allegations that all of the defendants were each other's agents or employees and were acting within the scope of their agency or employment. Prominent treatises, while recognizing that the Supreme Court has described such allegations as ‘egregious examples of generic boilerplate’ still advise that ‘such allegations may be necessary,’ especially ‘at the outset of a lawsuit, before discovery.’” (Barsegian v. Kessler & Kessler (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 446, 451 (citations omitted).) Nevertheless, Plaintiffs’ approach falls too far short of the mark to survive Warner Park’s Motion as to the alter ego allegations.[2]

Two general requirements must be met before the alter ego doctrine will be invoked: (1) there must be such unity of interest and ownership that the separate personalities of the corporation and the shareholder do not in reality exist, and (2) the result will be inequitable if the acts are treated as those of the corporation alone. (See Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538.) “The essence of the alter ego doctrine is not that the individual shareholder becomes the corporation, but that the individual shareholder is liable for the actions of the corporation. [Citation] For all other purposes, the separate corporate existence remains [citation].” (See Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 415.)

Plaintiffs do not properly plead an alter ego theory of liability. To do so, Plaintiffs must allege an individual, group of individuals, or a corporation is using another corporation to “to perpetrate fraud, circumvent a statute, or accomplish some other wrongful or inequitable purpose” such that it is appropriate for the Court to “treat the corporation's acts as if they were done by the persons actually controlling the corporation.”(Robbins v. Blecher (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 886, 892.) Individuals cannot be alter egos of each other. And to the extent Warner Park, Sunshine, or any of the individual Defendants are controlling the actions of either Warner Park or Sunshine such that piercing the corporate veil is appropriate, Plaintiffs must make clear who is doing what and how. Plaintiffs may not merely allege conclusory alter ego allegations against all Defendants as a group, as they have here, and expect to sort it all out later.

On the other hand, the Court is not persuaded by Warner Park’s argument that Plaintiff’s third cause of action for unlawful business practices misstates the law. Warner Park argues it is authorized to “to treat mental health disorders co-occurring with addiction, as reflected in, among other things, DCHS’ Certification Standards, the DHCS’ Initial Treatment Application for certification and licensure, and DHCS’ Behavioral Health Information Notices.” (Warner Park Motion, pg. 12, citing Newborn Decl., ¶¶ 4-6, Exs. B-D.) Because “[t]he sole allegation against [Warner Park] in Plaintiffs’ Unlawful Business Practices cause of action is that Warner Park Recovery falsely advertised its ability to provide mental health treatment without the necessary licensing[,]” Plaintiffs cannot maintain their third cause of action against Warner Park as a matter of law. (Warner Park Motion, pg. 12.)

To support its argument, Warner Park relies on both the materials it has requested this Court judicially notice (which this Court found were not appropriate for judicial notice) and other outside information, such as “DHCS Best Practice Guide section 3.1.3”. (See Warner Park Motion, pp. 10-13.) But such extrinsic evidence is improper on a motion to strike. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437 (“(a) The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.”).) Warner Park is attempting to turn a motion to strike into a motion for summary judgment. The Court declines to make such a leap at the pleading stage.  

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Warner Park’s Motion WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to Plaintiffs’ alter ego, agency, partnership, and joint venture allegations in paragraphs 12 (page 3, lines 21-25), 58 (in its entirety), 60 (page 12, lines 19-21), 61 (page 12, lines 23-26), 50 (page 10, lines 24-26), and 90 (page 18, lines 19-20). However, the Court DENIES Warner Park’s Motion as to Plaintiffs’ unlawful business practices allegations.

V.     CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Sandor’s Demurrer as to Plaintiffs’ second, third, and fourth causes of action.

However, the Court DENIES Sandor’s Motion to Strike.

In addition, the Court GRANTS Warner Park’s Motion WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to Plaintiffs’ alter ego, agency, partnership, and joint venture allegations in paragraphs 12 (page 3, lines 21-25), 58 (in its entirety), 60 (page 12, lines 19-21), 61 (page 12, lines 23-26), 50 (page 10, lines 24-26), and 90 (page 18, lines 19-20). However, the Court DENIES Warner Park’s Motion as to Plaintiffs’ unlawful business practices allegations.

Plaintiffs shall file their amended complaint on or before January 31, 2025.

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT TESLA MOTORS, INC.’S, MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

 

 

I.        BACKGROUND 

This is a Song-Beverly Act action filed by Plaintiff Felix Gleizer (“Plaintiff”) against Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc., (“Defendant”).

Defendant now moves to compel Plaintiff’s claims to arbitration.  

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; (3) Violation of The Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2; (4) Violation of The Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.22 - Tanner Consumer Protection Act.

On July 31, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration (“Motion”).

Plaintiff did not file an Opposition.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

“The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement.” (Avila v. Southern Cal. Specialty Care, Inc. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 835, 844.)

“California courts traditionally have maintained a strong preference for arbitration as a speedy and inexpensive method of dispute resolution… To this end, arbitration agreements should be liberally construed, … with doubts concerning the scope of arbitrable issues being resolved in favor of arbitration.” (Market Ins. Corp. v. Integrity Ins. Co. (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1095, 1098 (cleaned up).) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.... It seems clear that the burden must fall upon the party opposing arbitration to demonstrate that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to require arbitration of the dispute.” (Bono v. David (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 1055, 1062 (cleaned up).)

California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”), including a presumption in favor of arbitrability. (See Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-972.) The Court is empowered by Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.2[1] to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so.¿¿¿

The party opposing the petition to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense. (See Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.) Accordingly, under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (See Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)

Thus, in deciding a motion to compel arbitration, trial courts must first decide whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties, and then determine the second gateway issue of whether the claims are covered within the scope of the agreement. (See Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.             Request for Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice of Plaintiff’s Complaint.

///

///

B.             Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is Granted

The arbitration proponent must first recite verbatim, or provide a copy of, the alleged agreement. (See Sprunk v. Prisma LLC (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 785, 793.) A movant can meet this initial burden “‘by attaching a copy of the arbitration agreement purportedly bearing the opposing party’s signature.’” (Id. (quoting Rules of Court, rule 3.1330).) “[U]nless there is a dispute over authenticity, it is sufficient for a party moving to compel arbitration to recite the terms of the governing provision.” (Id. (citing Condee v. Longwood Mgmt. Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219).) “A voluntary acceptance of the benefit of a transaction is equivalent to a consent to all the obligations arising from it, so far as the facts are known, or ought to be known, to the person accepting.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1589.)

          Defendant has submitted evidence of a valid arbitration agreement between the parties. Defendant has attached a copy of its Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”) via the declaration of one of its Business Resolution Managers, Raymond Kim (“Kim”). (See Declaration of Raymond Kim filed July 31, 2024 (“Kim Decl.”), ¶ 3, Ex. 1.) Kim declares Plaintiff “placed an order for a 2019 Tesla Model 3...on or about June 11, 2019.  In placing that order, Plaintiff agreed to the terms of a [MVOA], thereby agreeing to be bound by its terms and conditions, which included an agreement to arbitrate.” (Kim Decl., ¶ 3.) Kim further declares Defendant made the MVOA available to Plaintiff via hyperlink during the sales process, and Plaintiff was required to attest he agreed to its terms before placing his order. (See id. at ¶¶ 3-4.)

          The MVOA’s arbitration provision states, in relevant part, as follows: “If you have a concern or dispute, please send a written notice describing it and your desired resolution to resolutions@tesla.com.  If not resolved within 60 days, you agree that any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you and Tesla will not be decided by a judge or jury but instead by a single arbitrator in an arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association (“AAA”) under its Consumer Arbitration Rules.” (Id. at ¶ 3, Ex. 1, pg. 3 (emphasis added).) The instant action is a statutory claim between Plaintiff and Defendant which arises out of the purchase or condition of the Vehicle. The dispute falls within the scope of Defendant’s MVOA’s arbitration provision. Defendant’s counsel also attests, uncontradicted, that Plaintiff refused the request to stipulate to arbitration. (See Declaration of Ali Ameripuour filed July 31, 2024, ¶ 3.) 

          Defendant has met its burden and Plaintiff has not submitted an opposition. Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion.

V.       CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and stays the action pending arbitration.

Accordingly, the Court sets a Status Conference re: Arbitration on January 9, 2026, at 8:30 a.m. The parties are ordered to file a Joint Status Update on or before January 2, 2026.
