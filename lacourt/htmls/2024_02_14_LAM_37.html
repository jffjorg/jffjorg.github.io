DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV25612    Hearing Date: February 14, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Wednesday, February 14, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   21STCV25612

CASE NAME:                        Mark A. Currie, et al. v. Farmers Insurance, et al. 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiffs Mark A. Currie, Michelle A. Currie, Michelle A. Currie, Leroy Turner, Ladiamond R. Blueford

TRIAL DATE:                        N/A

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion for Reconsideration of the January 8, 2024, ruling

OPPOSITION:                        31 January 2024

REPLY:                                  5 February 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                        Plaintiffs’ Motion for Reconsideration is denied.

Plaintiffs to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On July 13, 2023, Mark A. Currie, Michelle A. Currie, Michelle A. Currie, Leroy Turner, Ladiamond R. Blueford (collectively “Plaintiffs’) filed a Complaint against Fire Insurance Exchange (“Defendant” or “Fire”). This action relates to an insurance policy and alleged misrepresentations Fire made to Plaintiffs regarding coverage.

 

The operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleges ten causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Fraudulent Misrepresentation, (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, (4) Promissory Estoppel, (5) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (6) Fraud and Deceit, (7) General Negligence, (8) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, (9) Unfair Competition, and (10) Injunctive Relief.

 

On May 2, 2022, Plaintiffs dismissed Farmers Insurance Group and Foremost Insurance Group as Defendants. Plaintiffs also dismissed the third cause of action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty and the ninth cause of action for Unfair Competition.

 

On October 3, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, for  summary adjudication. On February 5, 2024, the court denied Plaintiffs’ request for summary judgment and summary adjudication.

 

On January 18, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a “Motion for Clarification and Reconsideration” of the January 8, 2024, ruling, which is noticed for hearing on February 14, 2024. Plaintiffs made no attempt to have the Motion heard before Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the alternative, for  summary adjudication. The matter is now before the court.

 

motion for reconsideration

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

 CCP § 1008 states, in pertinent part:¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

(a) When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make an application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

(b) A party who originally made an application for an order which was refused in whole or in part, or granted conditionally or on terms, may make a subsequent application for the same order upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. For a failure to comply with this subdivision, any order made on a subsequent application may be revoked or set aside on an ex parte motion.¿¿¿¿¿

II.        Request for Judicial Notice                    

 

The Court may take judicial notice of records of any court of record of the United States. (Evid. Code, § 452(d)(2).) The court grants Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice of the Shabani Declaration. A court may take judicial notice of contents of its own records. (Dwan v. Dixon (1963) 216 Cal.App.2d 260, 265; Foster v. Gray (1962) 203 Cal.App.2d 434, 439.) However, the court may only judicially notice the existence of the record, not that its contents are the truth. (Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1565.)

 

Plaintiffs request judicial notice of the following: 

 

1)     The Court’s Tentative Ruling made for Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication, hearing held December 20, 2023. A true and correct copy of this document attached as RJN Exhibit 1.

 

2)     The Court’s Minute Order dated January 8, 2024, RE Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication. A true and correct copy of this document attached as RJN Exhibit 2.

 

Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice is granted.

 

II.        Discussion

 

Plaintiffs seek clarification as to the January 8, 2024, ruling, whether the Plaintiffs met their burden as to certain causes of action, and whether the Plaintiff’s evidence was erroneously ruled as hearsay, lack of personal knowledge, and/or lack of authentication. Plaintiffs’ counsel asserts the different circumstances that warrant reconsideration is that Defendant’s evidentiary objections to Plaintiffs’ evidence were erroneously sustained, making Plaintiffs’ evidence inadmissible.  Defendant also failed to file a proposed order for the objections. The court finds that Plaintiffs failed to show that new or different circumstances warrant reconsideration of the January 8, 2024, ruling for several reasons.

 

First, by failing to challenge the Defendant’s objections at the hearing, Plaintiffs waived the right to challenge the validity of the Defendant’s objections to the Plaintiffs’ evidence. (See Lopez v. Baca (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1014; Vulk v. State Farm General Insurance Company (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 243, 253.)

 

Second, Plaintiffs fail to point out what evidence, if deemed admissible, would have established a triable issue of material fact, that would have warranted reversal of the grant of summary adjudication in favor of the Defendant as to the Plaintiffs’ second, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, and tenth causes of action. Without such an explanation, the court finds no reason why the January 8, 2024, ruling should be reconsidered.

 

Third, it was Plaintiffs’ obligation to properly lay the foundation and authenticate the exhibits they relied upon to oppose the Defendant’s motion for summary judgment. Plaintiffs’ former counsel, Angela Swan, submitted a declaration in support of Plaintiffs’ opposition to the Motion wherein she stated:

 

  • “The following facts are within my own personal knowledge, and/or are known to me based on my review of the documents referenced herein.”

 

  • “I am the Lead Attorney to the present case, Mark A. Currie et al. vs. Farmers Insurance Group, on behalf of Plaintiffs. As such, I am familiar with the pleadings, discovery, and other papers pertaining to this litigation, as discussed in further detail below.”

 

The Swan Declaration then proceeds to list Exhibits 1 to 30, without providing further details “sufficient to sustain a finding” that the exhibits are what Ms. Swan claims them to be. (Evid. Code, § 1400.) For example, Plaintiff’s Exhibit 19 is entitled “Water Loss Claim File Notes”, but no statement or declaration is offered as to who prepared or authored the claim notes. Ms. Swan fails to provide facts that would indicate that the notes are admissible under the business records exception to the hearsay rule. If the exhibits were provided by Defendant during discovery, then it was incumbent on Plaintiffs to point to deposition transcripts or discovery responses where Defendant admitted to the authenticity of the exhibits now being relied upon by Plaintiffs. While affidavits and declarations may be filed in support of a motion for summary judgment, such affidavits or declarations “shall be made by a person on personal knowledge, shall set forth admissible evidence, and shall show affirmatively that the affiant is competent to testify to the matters stated in the affidavits or declarations.” (CCP, § 437c(d) [italics added].)

 

The counteraffidavit concludes with the allegation that the ‘facts stated herein are within the personal knowledge of the affiant, and the affiant, if sworn as a witness, can testify competently thereto.’ Where the facts stated do not themselves show it, such bare statement of the affiant has no redeeming value and should be ignored.

(Snider v. Snider (1962) 200 Cal.App.2d 741, 754, see also Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 169.)

 

Fourth, while Plaintiffs Maeva A. Currie, Yamira A. Currie, Mark A. Currie, Michelle A. Currie, and LaDiamond R. Blueford all submitted declarations in support of their opposition to the Defendant’s Motion, each declaration had separate exhibits attached which do not appear in the Plaintiffs’ response to the Separate Statement. If the evidence is not cited in the Separate Statement, the evidence does not exist. (See Luebke v. Automobile Club of Southern California (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 694, 704.)

 

For the reasons above, the court found that Ms. Swan’s declaration and Plaintiffs’ declarations failed to show that the evidence the Plaintiffs relied upon to oppose the Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment—as well as the Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary judgment—was inadmissible evidence.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion for reconsideration is denied.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Reconsideration is denied. Plaintiffs to give notice.
