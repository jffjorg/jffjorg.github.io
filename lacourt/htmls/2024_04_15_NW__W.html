DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV02349    Hearing Date: April 15, 2024    Dept: W

GAL GAWY v. FRANCISCO SIBRIAN, et al.

 

Defendant’s MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S COMPLIANCE WITH DISCOVERY RESPONSES UNDER CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 2031.320(a)

 

Date of Hearing:        April 15, 2024                                     Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV02349

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 9, 2022, Plaintiff Gal Gawy filed a complaint against Defendants Francisco Sibrian and Los Angeles Unified School District for 1. Negligence 2. Negligent Entrustment 3. Neligent Hiring 4. Vicarious Liability 5. Violation of Vehicle Code 17001. Plaintiff alleges on October 1, 2021, Plaintiff Gawy was driving his vehicle traveling on the roadway when Defendant Sibrian was driving an agricultural vehicle and unsafely and negligently crashed into Plaintiff at or near Tampa and Vanowen. Plaintiff alleges Defendant failed to keep proper lookout, failed to keep a safe distance, and failed to travel at a speed safe for the existing traffic.

 

On July 21, 2023, Defendant LAUSD filed a cross-complaint against Gal Gawy and Joseph Gawy for indemnification, apportionment of fault, declaratory relief, and motor vehicle negligence.

 

Plaintiff filed a notice of death of Cross-Defendant Joseph Gawy on July 26, 2023. LAUSD dismissed Joseph Gawy from the Cross-Complaint on September 11, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Compliance with Discovery Responses Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.320(a) is GRANTED

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District moves the court for an order compelling Plaintiff to produce documents consistent with his responses to Defendant LAUSD’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One served on December 18, 2023. Defendant LAUSD also seeks sanctions in the amount of $375.00 against both Plaintiff, and Plaintiff’s attorney, Shahin Shawn Karimian, Esq. of Karimian Law Group, for the continued failure to comply to this authorized method of discovery.  

 

“If a party filing a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling under Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280 thereafter fails to permit the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling in accordance with that party’s statement of compliance, the demanding party may move for an order compelling compliance.”  (CCP §2031.320(a).) 

 

On July 21, 2023, Defendant LAUSD propounded Request for Production of Documents, Set One to Plaintiff. (Beckles Decl. ¶5, Exh. A.) After going back and forth, Plaintiff eventually served verified responses to the Request for Production of Documents, Set One on December 18, 2023. (Beckles Decl. ¶¶6-7, Exh. B.) Defendant LAUSD, however, was not served with Plaintiff’s document production. (Beckles Decl. ¶8.) Defendant LAUSD met and conferred several times requesting the missing document production; however, to date, Defendant LAUSD has not received Plaintiff’s discovery responses propounded on July 21, 2023. (Beckles Decl. ¶¶9-10.) No opposition has been filed.

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s motion pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.320. Plaintiff is ordered to produce documents to responses it stated it would and failed to do so.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.320(b) provides the court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel compliance with a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.  (CCP §2031.320(b).)

 

Defendant seeks sanctions in the amount of $375.00 on the grounds Plaintiff cannot provide any justification for its refusal to comply with his own agreement to produce responsive documents. The court GRANTS Defendant LAUSD’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel of record, Shahin Shawn Karimian, Esq. of Karimian Law Group.


Case Number: 23VECV04939    Hearing Date: April 15, 2024    Dept: W

PARHAM ZIYARI, et al. vs ESSEX WARNER CENTER, LP, et al.

 

DEFENDANT THE OAKS OF CALABASAS HOMEOWNERS ASSOCIATION'S DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFFS' VERIFIED THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        April 15, 2024                         Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV04939

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Essex Warner Center, LP and Essex Management Corporation

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Parham Ziyari and Saba Ziyari

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Ng Decl. ¶¶3-4.)  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from a landlord tenant dispute.  On February 21, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants Essex Warner Center, LP and Essex Management Corporation for 1. Contractual and Tortious Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability; 2. Breach of the Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; 3. Nuisance; 4. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; 5. Negligence; 6. Negligent Hiring, Training, Supervision, and Retention; 7. Violation of Civ. Code § 1940.2; 8. Violation of California Civil Code § 1942.5 (Retaliatory Eviction); 9. Violation of L.A.M.C. §§ 45.33 and 151.33; 10. Violation of Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30; 11. Fraud and Fraudulent Concealment; 12. Violation of the Resolution of the Board of Supervisors of the County of Los Angeles; 13. Premises Liability; and 14. Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 et seq. Unfair, Unlawful, and Deceptive Business Practices.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Essex Warner Center LP and Essex Management Corporation’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND, in part. Defendants’ Motion to Strike is MOOT.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Essex Warner Center, LP and Essex Management Corporation demur to the fourth, seventh, tenth, eleventh, and thirteenth causes of action in Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint on the grounds each cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to support a claim against Defendants and is too uncertain, vague and ambiguous to constitute a cause of action against Defendants.

 

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds none of the alleged conduct is outrageous nor intentional.

 

The elements of an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) intention to cause or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) severe emotional suffering; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress. (See Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 780; Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009.) To satisfy the element of extreme and outrageous conduct, defendant’s conduct “‘must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’” (Moncada, supra, 221 Cal.App.4th at 780 (quoting Tererice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883).)  

 

Defendants argue the allegations against them do not amount to outrageous conduct nor does it amount to any conduct that intended to harm Plaintiffs. Defendants contend courts have found no extreme and outrageous conduct where landlord was alleged to have failed to make repairs or do adequate maintenance. (See Rivera v. Johnson, 2022 Cal. Super. LEXIS 7718, *2; Gruver v. Ih2 Prop. W., 2021 Cal. Super. LEXIS 105245, *6.)[1] Moreover, there are no allegations, facts or evidence outside of conclusory allegations that Defendants intended to inflict any injury to Plaintiffs. Defendants contend there is nothing to even suggest that Defendants wished to harm Plaintiffs at all but instead, Defendants attempt to address Plaintiff’s concerns and actually fixing the issues allowing Plaintiffs to move back in.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the complaint sufficiently alleges multiple acts of outrageous conduct towards Plaintiffs. The FAC alleges Mr. Ziyari was initially informed that he could go to a hotel after the initial leak but eventually was subsequently informed that Defendants would not be paying for the hotel; that Defendants denied any responsibility for the flooding, and that Plaintiffs’ renters’ insurance would have to cover the cost. (FAC ¶82.) Moreover, Defendants had the audacity to serve Plaintiffs with papers threatening them with a “pending eviction,” constituting retaliatory conduct, which again caused Plaintiffs to consider the possibility of homelessness. (FAC ¶82.) According to Plaintiffs, these allegations go far beyond your “typical landlord-tenant disputes” and falls squarely within the bounds of “reckless conduct -- conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Davidson v. City of Westminster (1982) 32 Cal. 3d 197, 210.)

 

Both parties cite Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903. In Stoiber, the plaintiff-tenant alleged that her landlords knew of and failed to repair multiple defective conditions in the plaintiff’s apartment, including leaking sewage from the bathroom plumbing, defective and dangerous electrical wiring, structural weaknesses in the walls, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, dilapidated doors, and broken windows. (Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 912.)  Defendants were also cited by the health department for heavy cockroach infestation, broken interior walls, broken deteriorated flooring on front porch, falling ceiling, deteriorated and defective electrical wiring, lack of proper plumbing connection to sewage system in bathroom, sewage under bathroom floor, leaking roof, broken windows, and fire hazard.  (Id.) The trial court sustained the various defendants’ demurrers and granted motions for judgment on the pleadings on the basis that the cause of action for breach of the warranty of habitability was the exclusive remedy available to the plaintiff.  (Id. at 911.)  The court of appeal overruled the trial court, holding that a tenant may seek legal redress for breach of implied warranty of habitability and intentional infliction of emotional distress if the landlord’s acts are extreme and outrageous and result in severe mental distress. (Id. at 922.)  The court of appeal further found that, based on the facts alleged in the plaintiff's complaint, the issue of whether the landlords’ conduct was sufficiently extreme and outrageous was a factual issue that could not be determined on a demurrer or motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Id.) 

 

Plaintiffs’ allegations differ from Stoiber. While Plaintiffs have alleged several issues with the premises, Plaintiffs have not alleged Defendants knowingly allowed uninhabitable conditions to continue.¿Plaintiffs simply allege Defendants failed to maintain the plumbing system, refused to pay for property destroyed, and refused to pay for Plaintiffs hotel rooms. The complaint further alleges, however, Defendants sent a repairman to fix the leak.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Violation of Civil Code §1940.2

 

Defendants demur to the seventh cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1940.2 on the grounds Plaintiffs failed to state facts sufficient to support a claim against Defendants.

 

Civil Code section 1940.2 generally prevents landlords from using forceful, threatening, or menacing conduct that interferes with a tenant’s quiet enjoyment of the premises. Subsection (c) provides “An oral or written warning notice, given in good faith, regarding conduct by a tenant, occupant, or guest that violates, may violate, or violated the applicable rental agreement, rules, regulations, lease, or laws, is not a violation of this section. An oral or written explanation of the rental agreement, rules, regulations, lease, or laws given in the normal course of business is not a violation of this section.”

 

Defendants argue there is no violation of Civil Code §1940.2 by informing Plaintiff that they might evict them after Plaintiff seemingly suggests that they did not pay rent, resulting in the notice. As such, a notice that Plaintiffs may be facing eviction if they do not pay rent based on a violation of the applicable rental agreement and rules to pay rent timely, is not unlawful and thus, cannot be determined to be a violation of this statute.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue subsection (c) also requires the notice be given “in good faith”. Plaintiffs contend Defendants cannot credibly claim that they had any “good faith” reason to believe they could charge rent during Plaintiffs’ relocation and Defendants were responsible for providing a habitable premises.

 

The court finds the allegations are sufficient to support a claim of the written notice being made in bad faith. The FAC alleges the same day Plaintiffs informed Defendants that the Los Angeles Housing Department had assured them that Defendants owed them a duty to arrange for relocation, and to compensate them, Defendants served Plaintiffs with the pending eviction notice. A jury could conclude written warning notice was not in good faith.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Violation of Welf. & Inst. Code §15610.30

 

Defendants demur to the tenth cause of action for violation of Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.30 on the grounds Ms. Ziyari is not alleged to be an elder or adult dependent and moreover, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged Mr. Ziyari is a dependent adult.

 

Section 15610.23 includes in its definition of a “dependent adult” “a person, regardless of whether the person lives independently, between the ages of 18 and 64 years who resides in this state and who has physical or mental limitations that restrict his or her ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his or her rights, including, but not limited to, persons who have physical or developmental disabilities, or whose physical or mental abilities have diminished because of age.” (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.23(a).) 

 

Defendants argue while Plaintiffs attempt to allege he is a dependent adult, Plaintiff cannot claim that he is somehow a “dependent adult” who cannot carry out normal activities with physical and mental limitations or claim he is a decrepit individual but also claim in that same complaint, that he has a successful specialty foods business that he personally manages and runs and has suffered alleged loss. Moreover, Mr. Ziyari admits that he personally had an exchange with the alleged teenagers when he was allegedly assaulted and even attempted to locate security when it happened. Defendants also argue there are no allegations nor can there be any allegations that they knew he suffered from “severe panic attacks” and PTSD nor does the alleged conduct amount to wrongful use.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs first acknowledge the cause of action was inadvertently alleged by all Plaintiffs rather than just Mr. Ziyari. They will amend this accordingly. Plaintiffs further argue nothing that Mr. Ziyari has alleged contradicts the statutory definition of Welf. & Inst., Code § 15610.23(a). He is an individual aged 18-64 residing within California who has “physical or mental limitations,” i.e., disabilities, that “restrict” his ability to carry out normal activities, namely, severe panic attacks and PTSD.

 

The court finds the allegations insufficient. While a jury may find Plaintiff’s PTSD prevented him from carrying out normal activities, Welfare & Institutions Code section 15610.30 requires an allegation that the defendant knew or should have known that the conduct is likely to be harmful to the elder. (Welf. & Inst. §15610.30(b).) This is not alleged. Moreover, if Plaintiff is alleging kept charging Plaintiff rent for a wrongful use or intent to defraud, the complaint should clearly allege so.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the tenth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. 

 

Fraud and Fraudulent Concealment

 

Defendants demur to the eleventh cause of action for fraud and fraudulent concealment on the grounds Plaintiff’s cause of action for fraud does not state such claim with specificity or any of the requisite elements.

 

“[T]o establish fraud through nondisclosure or concealment of facts, it is necessary to show the defendant ‘was under a legal duty to disclose them.’” (OCM Principal Opportunities Fund v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 835, 845.) Nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud when: (1) there is a fiduciary relationship between the parties; (2) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. (See Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum Commission v. Insomniac, Inc. (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 803, 831.) In California, fraud must be pled with specificity. (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)  

 

 

Defendants argue the allegations are too general – they do not allege how, what, where and when such representations were made “in person” to Plaintiffs. Moreover, as to a corporate defendant, Plaintiff must allege the name of the people who made the misrepresentation, their authority to speak for the corporation, who received the communication, and when it was made. Moreover, there are no allegations that Defendants made any representations that the unit was going to be in absolutely perfect condition with absolutely no issues whatsoever during the entire tenancy; there are no allegations that Defendants knew the unit was “uninhabitable” or that they knew a flood was going to happen or that they knew mold was in the unit to claim that Defendants concealed this fact; no allegations had knowledge of any issues or intended to induce reliance on any alleged representations.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue Defendants ignore the numerous allegations in the FAC which do plead fraud and concealment, which are incorporated by reference in said cause of action at ¶ 129. Plaintiffs specifically pleaded fraud in FAC ¶¶ 61, 73, 112c(3), 122, 125, 130-136, and 147-158. Moreover, Plaintiffs are not required to plead the how, when, where, to whom, and by what means when defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy.

 

The court finds the allegations insufficient to support a claim for fraud. While less specificity is required when “it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[]” (Bradley v. Hartford Acc. & Indem. Co. (1973) 30 Cal.App.3d 818, 825), Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged Defendants intentionally concealed material facts not known to Plaintiffs and what was concealed.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Premises Liability

 

Defendants demur to the thirteenth cause of action for premises liability on the grounds Mrs. Ziyari has failed to allege any damages from the assault or from any alleged breach of duty for premises liability and is barred by the statute of limitations.

 

To establish premises liability, plaintiff must prove that the defendant breached a duty of care owed to the plaintiff that proximately caused injury and damages. “Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  The statute of limitations for premises liability is two years. (CCP §335.1.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff first states if the damages alleged against Mrs. Ziyari are not sufficient, it will seek leave to amend. As for statute of limitations, Plaintiffs argue the statute of limitations is tolled under the continuing violations doctrine including the ongoing security issues.

 

The court finds the allegations insufficient. Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged facts showing continuing violation. Plaintiffs are “required to demonstrate heightened foreseeability in the form of prior similar criminal incidents (or other indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults in the bar or its parking lot[.]” (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill (2005) 36 Cal.4th 224, 244.) Although at the pleading stage, Plaintiffs must allege as much.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the thirteenth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint on the grounds Plaintiffs have failed to plead specific facts sufficient to establish that Defendant acted with malice, fraud, or oppression, as required under California law to state a claim for punitive damages.

 

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.”  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 (citations omitted).)  Allegations that merely plead the statutory phraseology are wholly insufficient to state a basis for recovery of punitive damages.  (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041.)  Conclusory characterizations of defendant’s conduct as willful, intentional or fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of the necessary factual grounds for punitive damages.  (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.)     

 

Defendants argue none of the alleged conduct amount to malice, oppression or fraud and at best, can only be characterized as “unreasonable, negligent, grossly negligent or reckless” conduct which does not satisfy the highly culpable state of mind warranting punitive damages.

 

As the court has sustained the demurrer, the court finds the motion to strike moot.
