DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV23684    Hearing Date: October 25, 2024    Dept: T

Cross Motions to Enforce Settlement

Tentative Ruling:  Grant Defendant’s Motion

BACKGROUND 

This is a car collision case in which Plaintiff and Defendants agreed to settle the matter for $100,000, provided Plaintiff first complies with the conditions of the Settlement Agreement and Release. On March 4, 2024, Plaintiff signed the Release. (Declaration of Andrei Serpik (“Serpik Decl.”), ¶ 2, Ex. A.)

Paragraph 5 of the Release requires Plaintiff to provide Defendants with an executed copy of the CMS Affidavit. (Serpik Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. B.) However, Plaintiff returned an incomplete CMS Affidavit to Defendants and refused to provide the requested information because it was “private and confidential.” (Serpik Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. C.)

PLAINTIFF’S POSITION 

Plaintiff argues the Court should enforce his settlement agreement with Defendants to compel Defendants to tender the $100,000.00 value in the settlement agreement in addition and 10 percent interest, in addition to attorney fees and costs in the amount of $1,985.00. Plaintiff argues Defendants entered into a binding written agreement they cannot rescind. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Enforce Settlement, p. 3-4.)

DEFENDANTS’ POSITION 

Defendants argue this Court should compel Plaintiff Hector Calderon (“Plaintiff”) to complete the applicable portions of the CMS Affidavit form as required in Paragraph 5 of the Settlement Agreement and Release Plaintiff signed. (Defendants’ Motion to Enforce Settlement, p. 2.)  Defendants also request this Court award Defendants $2,172.50 in attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in bringing the instant motion. (Serpik Decl., ¶ 3).

In opposition to Plaintiff’s motion, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not fulfilled a material condition of the settlement agreement necessary for it to be enforceable by failing to provide a complete CMS Affidavit. Defendants also request this Court award Defendants in the amount of $1,950.00 for the costs incurred with opposing this motion. (Defendants’ Opposition to Motion to Enforce Settlement, p. 3.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Motion to Enforce Settlement

The Court is authorized to enter judgment pursuant to the stipulated settlement.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 664.6.)  In reviewing a motion to enforce a settlement, the Court determines “whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement.”  (Hines v. Lukes (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1182.)  “A settlement is enforceable under section 664.6 only if the parties agreed to all material settlement terms.  [Citations.]  The court ruling on the motion may consider the parties’ declarations and other evidence in deciding what terms the parties agreed to, and the court’s factual findings in this regard are reviewed under the substantial evidence standard.  [Citations.]  If the court determines that the parties entered into an enforceable settlement, it should grant the motion and enter a formal judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  (Id. at pp. 1182-1183; see also Osumi v. Sutton (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 1355, 1357 [“Strong public policy in favor of the settlement of civil cases gives the trial court, which approves the settlement, the power to enforce it”].)

II. Plaintiff’s Motion

Here, it is not appropriate in this case to grant Plaintiff’s motion to enforce the Parties’ settlement agreement. The settlement agreement required Plaintiff to provide an executed CMS Affidavit. (Para. 5) The most reasonable interpretation of this requirement is that the provided affidavit must be complete to be executed.  Plaintiff, by failing to provide a complete CMS Affidavit, accordingly fails to satisfy one of the material terms of the settlement agreement, which is a precondition to its enforcement. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion cannot be granted.  

III. Defendant’s Motion

Here, there is no dispute over whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement. Further the parties stipulated to this Court’s jurisdiction to enforce the settlement. (Para. 21).  As such, this Court has the power to make orders to effectuate the terms of this settlement.

The agreement can only be reasonably interpreted as obligating Plaintiff to provide a completed Affidavit.  Section 111 of the Medicare, Medicaid and SCHIP Extension Act of 2007 (MMSEA) contains mandatory reporting requirements with respect to Medicare beneficiaries who receive settlements, judgments, awards or other payment from liability insurance. The completed CMS Affidavit is necessary for Defendants to comply with these mandatory requirements.  Plaintiff’s refusal to provide this information thus fails to satisfy Plaintiff’s obligations under the settlement agreement.  

Plaintiff executed the settlement agreement and is obligated to comply with its terms.  Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s Motion to Enforce Settlement and orders Plaintiff to provide a complete and signed Affidavit.   

IV. Attorney Fees

Section 14 of the Parties’ settlement agreement provides that “In the event an action or a motion is brought by any SETTLING PARTY to enforce this AGREEMENT, the prevailing party in such action or motion shall be entitled to recover reasonable attorney fees and costs actually incurred in connection with such later action.” (Para. 14)  The plain language of Section 14 of the agreement thus anticipates the present motion and provides a basis for recovery to the prevailing party. Defendants are thus entitled to recover the reasonable attorney costs associated with bringing their motion and opposing Plaintiff’s motion.  

Defendants request $1,950.00 in sanctions for the costs associated with opposing Plaintiff’s motion. Defendants’ counsel reports charging $325.00 per hour and spending 4 hours preparing an opposition — and anticipates spending 2 hours reviewing Plaintiff’s reply and preparing and attending a hearing on this matter. (Serpik Decl., p. 3.)  Defendants also request the Court award $2,172.50 for the costs associated with their motion.  Defendants’ counsel reports spending 4.5 hours preparing this motion — and anticipated spending an additional 2 hours reviewing Plaintiff’s reply and preparing and attending a hearing on this matter. (Defendants’ Motion to Enforce Settlement, p. 6-7.) 

The Court finds that the hourly rate is reasonable, but finds significant overlap between Defendants’ motion and the opposition.  Further, Plaintiff did not file an opposition.  The Court finds five hours to be reasonable for defense counsel’s efforts in this manner.  The Court grants Defendants’ request for attorney fees in the amount of $1,625 and costs of $60.  

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement is DENIED.  Defendants’ motion to enforce settlement is GRANTED.  Defendants’ request for attorney fees and other costs is GRANTED in the amount of $1,685.

Case Number: 22STCV11887    Hearing Date: October 25, 2024    Dept: T

Bernabe Rosales v. Kim Cordeta

Motion for Attorney Fees

Moving Party: Plaintiff Bernabe Rosales 
Responding Party: Defendant Marek Hudec 
Tentative Ruling: DENY

BACKGROUND

On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel served an offer on Defendant Marek Hudec (“Defendant”) stating: “Plaintiff… offers to have judgment entered… against… Hudec in the amount of $100,000.00, with each party bearing their own respective attorney’s fees and costs through entry of judgment.” (Lee Declaration, ¶ 2; Exhibit A, Plaintiff Offer to Compromise; Exhibit B, September 15, 2023 Email.) Defendant allowed the Offer to expire without objecting or providing a written response. (Lee Declaration, ¶ 3.) On February 13, 2024, Plaintiff served a separate Offer to Compromise on Defendant Kim Cordeta (“Cordeta”) for $15,000.00. (Lee Declaration, ¶ 4; Exhibit C, Plaintiff Offer to Compromise to Cordeta; Exhibit D, February 13, 2024 Email.) Cordeta similarly allowed the offer to expire without objecting or providing a written response.

Plaintiff prevailed in a jury trial, which entered judgment against Defendant Hudec in the amount of $276,283.50 and found Cordeta jointly and severally liable for $15,000 of said judgment. (Lee Declaration, ¶ 9; Exhibit F, Judgment.) 

Maximilian Lee (“Attorney Lee”) spent 93.2 hours proving the matters in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission Nos. 1, 2, 5, 7, and 9. (Lee Declaration, ¶ 10.) Attorney Lee charges an hourly rate of $550. (Lee Declaration, ¶ 26.) Judith Camilleri (“Attorney Camilleri”) spent 33.4 hours proving the matters set forth in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission Nos. 1, 2, 5, 7, and 9. (Camilleri Declaration, ¶ 7.) Attorney Camilleri charges an hourly rate of $595. (Camilleri Declaration, ¶ 10.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Plaintiff argues he is entitled to attorney’s fees totaling $151,926 against Defendant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 2033.420, and that Defendant lacked reasonable grounds to believe he would prevail, and that Defendant had no good reason to admit Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission Nos. 1, 2, 5, 7, and 9. (Motion for Attorney Fees, p. 7.)

Plaintiff concedes that the issue of the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved were not exceptionally difficult. (Id. at 8.)

OPPOSITION

Defendant argues that he had reasonable grounds to believe he would prevail in refusing to admit Plaintiff’s requests for admission, and therefore should not be ordered to pay the expenses incurred by Plaintiff to prove them. (Opposition, p. 3.)

REPLY 

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s contradictory interrogatory responses and deposition testimony made clear that it was more likely than not that Defendant would be found negligent.  (Reply, p. 2.)
Plaintiff further argues Defendant could not reasonably have believed he would prevail on issues of causation and damages based on Defendant’s reliance on Dr. Lercel’s opinion that Plaintiff had a pre-existing meniscus and ACL injury to his right knee. (Id.)

ANALYSIS

I. Motion for Attorney Fees

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.420 states: 

“(a) If a party fails to admit the genuineness of any document or the truth of any matter when requested to do so under this chapter, and if the party requesting that admission thereafter proves the genuineness of that document or the truth of that matter, the party requesting the admission may move the court for an order requiring the party to whom the request was directed to pay the reasonable expenses incurred in making that proof, including reasonable attorney’s fees. (b) The court shall make this order unless it finds any of the following: (1) An objection to the request was sustained or a response to it was waived under Section 2033.290. (2) The admission sought was of no substantial importance. (3) The party failing to make the admission had reasonable ground to believe that that party would prevail on the matter. (4) There was other good reason for the failure to admit.”

If the party failing to admit the request for admissions has good cause in that the issues that were presented by the requested admissions were strenuously contested and difficult to resolve, the party propounding the requests may not recover the statutory costs of proving the truth of the matters therein, even if the matters were eventually proved to be true by uncontroverted evidence (Pappas v. Chang (2022) 75 Cal.App.5th 975, 992- 994; Yoon v. Cam IX Trust (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 388, 394; Universal Home Improvement, Inc. v. Robertson (2020) 51 Cal.App.5th 116, 129 132.) In evaluating whether a 'good reason' exists for denying a request to admit, “a court may properly consider whether at the time the denial was made the party making the denial held a reasonably entertained good faith belief that the party would prevail on the issue at trial.” (Laabs v. City of Victorville (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1275 1276; Orange County Water Dist. v. The Arnold Engineering Co. (2018) 31 Cal.App.5th 96, 116.)

“Whether a party has a reasonable ground to believe he or she will prevail, in the context of requests for admissions heavily reliant on expert opinion evidence.” This consideration depends on factors within the reasonable understanding of the party, including the expert’s qualifications and experience, whether the expert's opinions are likely admissible at trial, “whether the facts underlying the expert's opinions are supported by the evidence, whether the expert's methodology appears reasonable, and whether the expert's analysis is grounded in logic.” (Orange County Water Dist. v. The Arnold Engineering Co. (2018) 31 Cal.App.5th 96, 117-118) (italics added.) Moreover, a party’s reasonable belief that it will prevail based on a credible opinion from a qualified expert is not defeated by “the mere fact that an opposing party also has a credible opinion from a qualified expert.” Rather, “[s]omething about the state of the evidence must make the party's reliance on its own expert's opinion unreasonable.” (Id.) Whether a party has reasonable grounds to believe they will prevail is thus necessarily wholistic—requiring consideration of all the evidence known or reasonably available at the time the RFA responses are served. (Id. at 117-118.) 

It is not appropriate here to grant Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees because Defendant had reasonable grounds to believe that he
would prevail at trial on the issues covered by the subject requests for admission, and reasonably relied on his expert witness testimony in establishing that belief. 

Courts will generally find a party's reliance on expert opinion evidence unreasonable only where the expert opinion evidence would be inadmissible at trial or where the expert opinion evidence was fatally undermined by facts known to the party, though these circumstances are not exclusive. (Orange County Water Dist. v. The Arnold Engineering Co. (2018) 31 Cal.App.5th 96, 121.) Here, Defendant reasonably relied on the testimony of his expert witness, Dr. Gregory Lercel, in establishing a good faith belief he would prevail at trial. Conversely, Plaintiff’s motion tacitly acknowledges that Defendant had a legitimate evidentiary basis to believe he would prevail in attempting to justify Plaintiff’s request for a heightened attorney fee award. Plaintiff explicitly acknowledges Defendant’s case was not trivial, as Plaintiff had “overcome the fact that he struck the rear of Defendant Marek Hudec’s car while in the street, and jurors often assume that individuals who strike the rear of another person’s vehicle are always at fault” and that Plaintiff “had to overcome the opinions of Defendant’s expert witness Gregory Lercel, M.D.” (Motion, p.10.) Based on the record in this case, the Court thus finds that Defendant reasonably relied on the evidence available to him as well as the testimony of his expert witness to believe he would prevail at trial. 

RULING

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees is DENIED.
