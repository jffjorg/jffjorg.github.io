DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03196    Hearing Date: March 25, 2024    Dept: W

SEAN ROSE v. LOREN PROPERTIES, LLC, et al.

 

demurrer to the cross-complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        March 25, 2024                                              Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        23VECV03196

 

Moving Party:            Cross-Defendant Sean Rose  

Responding Party:     Cross-Complainants Loren Properties, LLC, Westside Habitats, LLC and John Patrick Graham

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 24, 2023, Plaintiff Sean Rose filed a complaint against Loren Properties, LLC, Westside Habitats, LLC, Rohit Mehta, and John Patrick Graham for negligence, premises liability, negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of employee, battery, and assault. Plaintiff Rose alleges Plaintiff, while standing within property he lawfully rented and controlled, was assaulted by Defendant, John Patrick Graham, while Mr. Graham was working as an employee on behalf of the other named Defendants.

 

On December 8, 2023, Loren Properties, LLC, Westside Habitats, LLC, and John Patrick Graham filed a Cross-Complaint against Rose for breach of contract, assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and elder abuse.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Cross-Defendant Sean Rose’s Demurrer to the Cross-Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

discussion

 

Cross-Defendant Sean Rose demurs to the Cross-Complaint by Loren Properties, LLC, Westside Habitats, LLC and John Patrick Graham on the grounds the complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action for breach of contract, assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and elder abuse. Cross-Complainant Patrick Graham oppose the demurrer and Cross-Complainants Loren Properties LLC and Westside Habitats LLC join in opposition.

 

Procedural Issues

 

Cross-Complainants oppose the demurrer on many grounds including the demurrer is untimely and Cross-Defendant Rose did not sufficiently meet and confer prior to filing the demurrer.

 

First, the court notes no proof of service has been filed but the summons was issued December 8, 2023 and the demurrer was not filed until February 2, 2024.  Furthermore, the court notes that Cross-Defendant was not granted an automatic thirty-day extension to file a responsive pleading as no declaration has been submitted concerning his meet and confer efforts.  (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) However, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473(a)(1), the Court may still consider a demurrer that is not timely. (Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742, 749.)  Notably, (1) Plaintiff failed to obtain a default judgment against Cross-Defendant and (2) the court finds that Cross-Complainants are not substantially prejudiced by the late demurrer.  Accordingly, the court exercises its discretion to decide the demurrer on the merits.  

 

Next, the court agrees contacting the pleading party the day before the moving party’s intention to file a demurer does not necessarily demonstrate a “reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (CCP § 2016.040.) Moreover, Section 430.41(a) requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer¿in person or by telephone¿with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer…” Cross-Defendant sent an e-mail. However, Section 430.41(a)(4) provides that any determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to sustain or overrule a demurrer.  In light of Cross-Complainants being able to file an opposition, the court will proceed to address the merits of the demurrer despite the insufficiency of the meet and confer. 

 

Breach of Contract

 

Cross-Defendant Rose demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract on the grounds Cross-Complainants have failed to allege a crucial element of their claim for breach of contract – damages.

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)  “A breach of contract is not actionable without damage.” (Bramalea California, Inc. v. Reliable Interiors, Inc. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 468, 473.)

 

Rose argues Cross-Complainants allege that they “have been damaged, in an amount to be determined at trial.” (Cross-Complaint ¶16.) Rose contends this, however, does not provide a concise amount of damages Cross-Complainants’ allegedly have suffered.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue the Cross-Complaint alleges actual damages resulting from Cross-Defendant's breach of the Lease Agreement. Looking at the Cross-Complaint as a whole, and incorporating by reference Paragraphs 6-8, Cross-Complainant was damaged in the amount of $39,940.00, which is the rent withheld by Cross-Defendant since December 2020. (Cross-Complaint ¶¶7-8.)

 

The court agrees the complaint sufficiently alleges a claim for breach of contract. “In reviewing the sufficiency of a complaint against a general demurrer, we are guided by long-settled rules. We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We also consider matters which may be judicially noticed. Further, we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318, citations and internal quotation marks omitted.) Here, the complaint alleges Cross-Defendant has an outstanding balance due and owing under the Lease in the amount of $39,940.00 and although Loren Properties LLC and Westside Habitats LLC repeatedly requested that Cross-Defendant pay his rental debt, Cross-Defendant has refused to fulfill his contractual obligation to pay the outstanding balance. (Cross-Complaint ¶¶7-8.) As a result of Cross-Defendant failing to make all payments and perform all obligations due under the Lease, Cross-Defendant breached the lease. (Cross-Complaint ¶¶14-15.)

 

Accordingly, Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Assault

 

Cross-Defendant Rose demurs to the second cause of action for assault on the grounds Cross Plaintiffs’ cause of action for assault is not properly pleaded.

 

“The essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant's conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff's harm.”  (Yun Hee So v. Sook Ja Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-69.)  

 

Rose argues here, Cross-Complainants’ fail to provide any factual allegations as to what Sean Rose did so that Mr. Graham fear that he was going to be touched offensively. Rose contends it is difficult to believe that he suffered a stroke when it was he who was arrested and it was he filmed talking coherently to first responder.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue the Cross-Complaint sufficiently alleges that Rose struck and punched Graham on the face and head and pinned him to the ground. (Cross-Complaint ¶10.) Moreover, the Cross-Complaint alleges Graham believed he was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner. (Cross-Complaint ¶19.) The court agrees this is sufficient to show assault on demurrer. The court further agrees whether it is difficult to believe Graham suffered a stroke is not a basis for a demurrer.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Battery

 

Cross-Defendant Rose demurs to the third cause of action for battery on the grounds Cross-Complainants’ present scant allegations supporting their cause of action.

 

“The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant’s conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.”  (Yun Hee So v. Sook Ja Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 669.) 

 

Rose argues the allegations are devoid of any facts showing that the alleged touching was done with any force. (See People v. Mansfield (1988) 200 Cal.App.3d 82, 88 [stating that touching must be done with force, no matter how slight).] Again, the court disagrees. Reading the Cross-Complaint as a whole, Cross-Complainants have alleged Rose struck, pushed, and pinned down Graham which falls squarely into the first element for battery.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Cross-Defendant Rose demurs to the fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds Cross-Plaintiffs’ present scant allegations supporting their cause of action.

 

The elements for a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) an actual and proximate causal link between the tortious conduct and the emotional distress.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050.)   

 

Rose argues Cross-Complainants have failed to provide and facts as to why or how the allegations actions were outrageous. Moreover, all that is provided are vague statements of outrageous conduct. There is no explanation as to what was done to Mr. Graham and how that conduct was intended to cause him emotional injury. There is no explanation as to what exactly, and why, such actions were beyond the pale such that the actions were outrageous.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue striking and punching on the face and head, and knocking and pinning an individual to the ground, thereby causing facial and head bruising, lacerations and a stroke, clearly amount to extreme and outrageous conduct.

 

The court agrees, for the purposes of a demurrer, Cross-Complainants have alleged facts sufficient to support a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress. The Cross-Complaint alleges Rose struck, hit and pinned down Graham with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of, causing emotional distress. Moreover, Graham has suffered as a result emotional distress as a result.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Elder Abuse

 

Cross-Defendant Rose demurs to the fifth cause of action for elder abuse on the grounds it is difficult to determine what exactly Graham alleges in this cause of action. Graham fails to cite any authority regarding a common law tort for Elder Abuse.

 

Cross-Defendant Rose argues presumably Graham seeks to allege that Rose violated the 1991 Elder Abuse Act. If so, pursuant to Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657, Graham must allege that Rose engaged in recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malicious conduct. (See Delaney v. Baker (1999) 20 Cal.4th 23, 31-32.) Here, there are no allegations of these crucial elements.

 

“The Elder Abuse Act's heightened remedies are available only in limited circumstances. A plaintiff must prove, by clear and convincing evidence, that a defendant is liable for either physical abuse under section 15610.63 or neglect under section 15610.57, and that the defendant committed the abuse with ‘recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice.’ ” (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 156.)  Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.07 defines abuse of an elder to include “[p]hysical abuse, neglect, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering. Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.63 provides a list detailing what defines “physical abuse”. Under section 15610.63, assault and battery are defined by their penal code definitions.  Thus, for purposes of the Elder Abuse Act, assault “is an unlawful attempt, coupled with a present ability, to commit a violent injury on the person of another.” (Pen. Code § 240; Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.63(a).) Battery is defined as “any willful and unlawful use of force or violence upon the person of another.” (Pen. Code § 242; Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.63(b).)

 

The court finds the allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. Cross-Complainants have sufficiently alleged physical abuse of an elder under Penal Code sections 240 and 242.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is OVERRULED.


Case Number: 23VECV03290    Hearing Date: March 25, 2024    Dept: W

ALEXANDER GHATAN v. BMW OF NORTH AMERICA, LLC, et al.

 

PLAINTIFF ALEXANDER GHATAN’S MOTION FOR AN ORDER THAT THE TRUTH OF THE REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION BE DEEMED ADMITTED BY DEFENDANT SHERMAN OAKS-B, INC. DBA BMW OF SHERMAN OAKS

 

Date of Hearing:        March 25, 2024                                 Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03290

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Alexander Ghatan

Responding Party:     Defendant Sherman Oaks-B, Inc. dba BMW of Sherman Oaks

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 28, 2023, Plaintiff Alexander Ghatan filed a complaint against BMW of North America, LLC and Sherman Oaks-B, Inc. dba BMW of Sherman Oaks for 1. Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; 2. Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; 3. Violation of The Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2; and 4. Violation of The Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.22 – Tanner Consumer Protection Act.  Plaintiff leased a new 2020 BMW X5 xDrive 40i from Defendants. Plaintiff alleges the Subject Vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty including, but not limited to, a malfunctioning steering intervention, vehicle key detection issues, and electrical system defects.

 

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint adding two additional causes of action for Negligent Repair and Strict Liability – Design Defect as well as punitive damages as a prayer for relief to this matter.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff Alexander Ghatan’s Motion for an Order that the Truth of the Requests for Admission be Deemed Admitted by Defendant Sherman Oaks-B, Inc. dba BMW of Sherman Oaks is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Alexander Ghatan moves for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Set One, propounded upon Defendant Sherman Oaks-B, Inc. dba BMW of Sherman Oaks be deemed admitted, and for monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel in the sum of $3,076.02. Ghatan makes the motion on the grounds that Defendant failed to respond to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set One.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s motion should be denied as moot because Defendant already served substantially compliant verified responses and document production. Given that Defendant served its substantially compliant responses and verifications, there is no prejudice to Plaintiff and it is clear his goal is solely to harass Defendant and not to achieve any further purpose since he has already been in possession of the discovery responses for almost three months now.

 

The court finds the motion for an order that the truth of the requests for admission be deemed admitted moot. If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (CCP § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (CCP § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (CCP § 2033.280(c).)

 

As Defendant has provided responses prior to the hearing, the court finds it would run against the interests of public policy to grant Plaintiffs’ motion as Defendant would suffer even when serving a substantially compliant response per Code of Civil Procedure sections 2033.220, 2033.240, and 2033.280.

 

Accordingly, because Defendant served a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion, Plaintiff’s request for admissions, set one be deemed admitted is DENIED.  

 

Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (CCP § 2033.280(c); see also CRC Rule 3.1348(a) (the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed).)

 

Plaintiff requests $3,076.02 in monetary sanctions for Defendant’s failure to timely and properly respond to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set One.

 

In opposition, Defendant also requests sanctions against Plaintiff for misusing the discovery process when he failed to meet and confer in good faith prior to filing this motion, continuously maintains the unreasonable position that he is entitled to proceed with his motion without substantial justification, employs discovery tactics solely to cause harassment and undue burden on BMW of Sherman Oaks, and brought the present frivolous and meritless motion.

 

The court denies both requests for sanctions.
