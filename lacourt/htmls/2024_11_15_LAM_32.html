DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV01640    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: 32

 

ANTONIO LEON, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

JAMES JABER, M.D., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  19STCV01640

  Hearing Date:  November 15, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant’s motion for reconsideration

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 22, 2019, Plaintiffs Antonio Leon and Blanca Leon filed this action against Defendants James Jaber, MD, Georgia Bode, MD, and Los Angeles Community Hospital, asserting causes of action for (1) medical malpractice and (2) loss of consortium. The First Amended Complaint was filed on May 8, 2019. Blanca Leon’s claims were dismissed, leaving Antonio Leon’s medical malpractice claim.

            The matter came on for trial between January 29, 2024 and February 9, 2024, after which the jury returned a verdict for Plaintiff against Defendant Bode. Judgment was entered on March 21, 2024 in the total amount of $12,478,222.95.    

            On August 8, 2024, Plaintiff levied Defendant’s deposit accounts at Citibank and Chase Bank. Citibank and Chase turned over $1,227,657.16 and $97,915.03 to the levying officer, respectively.

            On September 30, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s claim of exemption as to the Citibank funds in the amount of $384,143.02.

            On October 14, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for reconsideration of the Court’s order. Plaintiff filed his opposition on November 1, 2024.    

LEGAL STANDARD

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(a).)

Additionally, “the trial court retains the inherent authority to change its decision at any time prior to the entry of judgment.” (Darling v. Kritt (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 1148, 1156.) “[S]ection 1008 does not govern the court's ability, on its own motion, to reevaluate its own interim rulings.” (Ibid.) “[T]he only requirement of the court is that it exercise ‘due consideration’ before modifying, amending, or revoking its prior orders.” (Id. at p. 1157.)

DISCUSSION

Social security and pension benefits are exempt from levy. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 704.080, 704.110.) However, “[t]he exemption claimant has the burden of tracing an exempt fund.” (Id., § 703.080(b).) “The tracing of exempt funds in a deposit account shall be by application of the lowest intermediate balance principle unless the exemption claimant or the judgment creditor shows that some other method of tracing would better serve the interests of justice and equity under the circumstances of the case.” (Id., § 703.080(b).)     

The additional bank records attached to Defendant’s motion do not demonstrate that the Citibank accounts consist solely of social security and pension funds. (See Bode Decl., Ex. A-I.) Defendant claims that she has shown the lowest intermediate balance as follows: “As the deposited benefits accumulated, they were transferred to savings, including account ending in 3084 and certificates of deposit. Enough money has been deposited into the Bodes’ Citibank accounts to fully exempt the levied funds.” (Mtn. 6:11-14.) Defendant then concludes that because the total Social Security and LACERA deposits ($1,251,747.01) exceeds the levied amount ($1,227,657.16), the levied funds must necessarily be exempt. That is not how the lowest intermediate balance rule operates.

Plaintiff’s forensic accountant avers that Defendant has not accurately applied the lowest intermediate balance principle and explains the faults in Defendant’s methodology. (See Orrico Decl.) In particular, the expert notes the following:

 

“Judgment Debtor simply considered the balances in the Citibank Accounts as of the levy date and did not apply the LIBP on a day-to-day basis and did not identify non-exempt source funds. Thus, Judgment Debtor did not correctly apply the LIBP in her analysis and therefore, her analysis cannot be relied upon to provide an accurate accounting of Exempt Funds under the LIBP.”

(Id., ¶ 18.)

 

“[F]or $778,542 of the $1,227,657 of total levied funds, no support was provided. As such, it cannot be assessed or determined whether the source funds were exempt or non-exempt, and to the extent the source funds were exempt, whether such funds were depleted or remain in the account. Thus, it is unclear how Judgment Debtor concluded that $778,542 of the levied funds are exempt and therefore, her analysis cannot be relied upon to provide an accurate accounting of exempt funds under the LIBP.”

(Id., ¶ 20.)

Because Defendant has failed to properly trace the funds, the Court has no basis to find that any amount is exempt beyond the $384,143.02 conceded by Plaintiff. Accordingly, there is no reason to amend the Court’s order on the Citibank accounts.  

CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

TROCADERO INTERNATIONAL,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

DR. SEBI, LLC, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV31214

  Hearing Date:  November 15, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant/cross-complainant dr. sebi’s office, inc.’s motion for sanctions  

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff Trocadero International (TI) filed this action for breach of contract and fraud against Defendants Dr. Sebi, LLC; Dr. Sebi’s Office, Inc.; and Agustin Ruiz Miller. The complaint alleges that TI entered into an oral agreement with Defendants to provide packaging for Defendants’ tea product in exchange for $58,462.80. Defendants have allegedly failed to pay the balance of $30,082.80.

            On May 7, 2024, Dr. Sebi’s Office, Inc. (DSOI) filed a cross-complaint against TI, Richard Benichou, Alex Benichou, and Custom Co-Pak, LLC for breach of contract and fraud.

On June 20, 2024, DSOI filed two motions to compel TI’s responses to Requests for Production and Form Interrogatories. The Court denied these motions on August 23, 2024 because TI served its responses before the hearing, but the Court awarded sanctions of $1,050 due within 30 days.

On October 14, 2024, DSOI filed the instant motion for sanctions based on TI’s failure to pay the monetary sanctions previously awarded by the Court. TI filed its opposition on October 31, 2024. DSOI filed its reply on November 7, 2024.

DISCUSSION

            “[M]onetary sanction orders are enforceable through the execution of judgment laws.” (Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615.) “These orders have the force and effect of a money judgment, and are immediately enforceable through execution, except to the extent the trial court may order a stay of the sanction.” (Ibid.) Due to the availability of enforcement of judgment procedures, it is not necessary to impose further monetary sanctions.

            Terminating sanctions, which DSOI mentions in its reply, are unwarranted for a failure to pay monetary sanctions. DSOI has not been deprived of any discovery, thus termination would be disproportionate. Aghaian v. Minassian (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 603, 616-17 is distinguishable because the court in that case imposed issue and evidentiary sanctions for failure to respond to discovery, not for failure to pay sanctions.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant/Cross-Complainant Dr. Sebi’s Office, Inc.’s motion for sanctions is DENIED.


Case Number: 23STCV31867    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: 32

 

ODILE MADDEN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

GETTY CONSERVATION INSTITUTE, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV31867

  Hearing Date:  November 15, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant j. paul getty trust’s motion to compel mental examination

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On December 29, 2023, Plaintiff Odile Madden filed this employment discrimination action against Defendants Getty Conservation Institute and J. Paul Getty Trust. 

            On October 4, 2024, Defendant J. Paul Getty Trust filed the instant motion to compel a mental examination of Plaintiff. Plaintiff filed her oppositions on November 1, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by means of a physical or mental examination of (1) a party to the action, (2) an agent of any party, or (3) a natural person in the custody or under the legal control of a party, in any action in which the mental or physical condition (including the blood group) of that party or other person is in controversy in the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.020(a).) “If any party desires to obtain discovery by . . . a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court.” (Id., § 2032.310(a).) “The court shall grant a motion for a physical or mental examination under Section 2032.310 only for good cause shown.” (Id., § 2032.320(a).)

DISCUSSION

I. Good Cause

There is no dispute over Defendant’s right to take a mental examination, and Plaintiff has not refused to submit to a mental examination. Plaintiff “does not deny that her mental state is at issue.” (Opp. 5:20.) Thus, there is good cause for the examination. (See Vinson v. Sup. Ct. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 839 [“a party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy”].)   

II. Procedural Disputes

However, the parties are conflicted over the following conditions related to the examination: (1) the scope of questioning; (2) the release of test data to Plaintiff’s counsel; (3) Defendant’s right to take further examinations; and (4) Plaintiff’s right to strike portions of the doctor’s report.

            a. Scope

            The expert who will be conducting the examination, Dr. Ari Kalechstein, avers that “taking a detailed history is essential to the evaluation and often invaluable since it helps to guide the test selection process, allows for interpretation of test data within a suitable context, and may yield important clues to a correct diagnosis.” (Kalechstein Decl. ¶ 3.) Therefore, “clinical interviews typically involve questioning regarding the history of the subject, including the following information: basic descriptive data, developmental history, social history, relevant past medical history, current medical status, and the effect of the disorder.” (Ibid.) Dr. Kalechstein further avers that “[a]nother integral element of a neuropsychological evaluation is the administration of neuropsychological tests, which serve as objective measures of cognition, personality/emotional functioning, and behavior.” (Kalechstein Decl. ¶ 4.)

            Plaintiff argues that “the DME must be limited in scope to the emotional distress Dr. Madden has alleged to have suffered as a result of the wrongful termination” and that “[c]ognitive and neuropsychological tests are . . . irrelevant to any of the issues and damages asserted in this litigation.” (Opp. 4:15-16, 5:14-16.) However, a psychiatrist is the most qualified to determine the necessary scope of a mental examination. (Edwards v. Superior Court (1976) 16 Cal.3d 905, 911.) “Many questions which would be legally objectionable, if posed in a courtroom, might be very relevant in the formulation of a sound psychiatric judgment.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff cites no expert testimony rebutting Dr. Kalechstein’s assertions. Therefore, the Court defers to Dr. Kalechstein’s assessment of the necessary scope of examination.

            Additionally, Plaintiff’s proposed limitation presupposes that her emotional distress was solely caused by Defendant. However, Defendant is entitled ascertain evidence proving otherwise, which includes discovering alternative stressors. (See Vinson, supra, 43 Cal.3d at p. 840 [“by asserting a causal link between her mental distress and defendants' conduct, plaintiff implicitly claims it was not caused by a preexisting mental condition, thereby raising the question of alternative sources for the distress”].) Because the medical expert has averred to the necessary scope of the examination, Defendant’s proposed scope is not based on speculation as was the case in Tylo v. Superior Court (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1388.

            In sum, the scope of the examination may include Plaintiff’s history and the neurological tests that Dr. Kalechstein determines to be necessary.

            b. Test Data

            “The examiner and examinee shall have the right to record a mental examination by audio technology.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.530(a).) Furthermore, the party submitting to a mental examination may demand the following materials: “(1) A copy of a detailed written report setting out the history, examinations, findings, including the results of all tests made, diagnoses, prognoses, and conclusions of the examiner. (2) A copy of reports of all earlier examinations of the same condition of the examinee made by that or any other examiner.” (Id., § 2032.610(a).)

“[G]iven the trial court's broad discretion in discovery matters, the trial court . . . has the power to order disclosure of test materials and data to the plaintiff's attorney.” (Randy's Trucking, Inc. v. Superior Court (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 818, 835.) Test data is necessary to effectively cross-examine an expert and ensure the legitimacy of the examination. (See id. at pp. 834-35; Evid. Code, § 721.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s counsel is entitled to obtain the test materials, including the audio recording.

Defendant argues that releasing the test data and recording to anyone other than Plaintiff’s expert would compromise the integrity of the examination and result in ethical violations for Dr. Kalechstein. However, the American Psychological Association Code of Ethics states that a psychologist may release patient records under a court order. (APA Rule 9.04.) There is no indication that disclosure to counsel, especially under a protective order, would risk dissemination to the public or other misuse of the material.

In sum, Plaintiff’s counsel is entitled to obtain the test data and audio recording. Production shall be subject to a protective order.

c. Further Examinations

Plaintiff cannot condition this examination on Defendant waiving its right to compel future examinations. There is only one mental examination at issue in this motion, for which Plaintiff has already agreed there is good cause. Any further mental examinations would require a separate motion demonstrating good cause. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320(a).) The Court will not preemptively determine whether there is good cause for speculative future exams that are not at issue.  

d. Striking the Report

Plaintiff cites no authority allowing an examinee to strike portions of a test report. Plaintiff retains the right to object to the admissibility of the report or portions thereof, and to challenge the veracity of the report through cross-examination and her own evidence. But there is no precedent for authorizing an examinee to unilaterally strike portions of a report that he or she deems irrelevant.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant J. Paul Getty Trust’s motion to compel mental examination is GRANTED on the conditions set forth above.


Case Number: 24STCV18629    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: 32

 

MARIA TERESA SCOTT,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

DI LOGISTICS, LLC,

                        Defendant.

 

  Case No.:  24STCV18629

  Hearing Date:  November 15, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant’s motion to compel arbitration

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On July 23, 2024, Plaintiff Maria Teresa Scott filed this employment action against Defendant DI Logistics, LLC, asserting claims for violations of the FEHA, Labor Code, and UCL.

            On October 17, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel arbitration. Plaintiff filed her opposition on November 1, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists….” (Code Civ. Proc, § 1281.2.) “The party seeking arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement, and the party opposing arbitration bears the burden of proving any defense, such as unconscionability.” (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236.)

DISCUSSION

I. Prima Facie Proof of Agreement

            “The moving party ‘can meet its initial burden by attaching to the motion or petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the opposing party's signature.’” (Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165.)

            Here, Plaintiff hand signed an employment agreement on April 28, 2017 providing that “[a]ny dispute, controversy or claim arising out of, involving, affecting or related in any way to this Agreement or a breach of this Agreement, or in any way arising out of, involving, affecting or related to Employee's employment . . . shall be resolved by final and binding arbitration, pursuant to the Federal-Arbitration Act, in conformity with the procedures and the applicable rules of the American Arbitration Association.” (Santo Decl., Ex. A.)

            However, Plaintiff points out that the agreement is between herself and DI Overnite, LLC, not defendant DI Logistics. (See Santo Decl., Ex. A.) “Generally speaking, one must be a party to an arbitration agreement to be bound by it or invoke it.” (Pillar Project AG v. Payward Ventures, Inc. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 671, 675.) It appears from the face of the document that defendant DI Logistics is not a party to the agreement. Defendant mentions in passing that it is “also referred to as DI Overnite, LLC” but cites no actual evidence showing that the two are the same entity.  

            Absent evidence that DI Logistics and DI Overnite are the same entity, the Court finds that there is no arbitration agreement between Plaintiff and DI Logistics. Assuming DI Logistics is able to establish that it is the same entity as DI Overnite, the Court addresses Plaintiff’s other arguments below.    

 

II. Waiver

            “To establish waiver under generally applicable contract law, the party opposing enforcement of a contractual agreement must prove by clear and convincing evidence that the waiving party knew of the contractual right and intentionally relinquished or abandoned it.” (Quach v. California Commerce Club, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal.5th 562, 584.) “Its intentional relinquishment or abandonment of the right may be proved by evidence of words expressing an intent to relinquish the right or of conduct that is so inconsistent with an intent to enforce the contractual right as to lead a reasonable fact finder to conclude that the party had abandoned it.” (Ibid.)

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant has waived its right to arbitrate because it delayed in filing this motion. Defendant avers that it only discovered the arbitration agreement in September 2024 because it was undergoing a file transfer from Santa Fe Springs to Anaheim in August 2024, at around the same time this lawsuit was filed. (Santo Decl. ¶ 13.) The proof of service of the complaint indicates personal service on August 5, 2024. These facts show that Defendant was not aware of the arbitration agreement at the time the action was filed but promptly moved for arbitration upon discovering the agreement. Defendant filed this motion two months after being served with the lawsuit, while the case was still in its infancy.

Campbell v. Sunshine Behavioral Health, LLC (2024) 105 Cal.App.5th 419, cited by Plaintiff, is distinguishable. There, the defendant claimed to have discovered the arbitration agreement six months after the lawsuit had been filed, and then waited another six months to file a motion to compel. (Id. at p. 430.) The defendant failed to demonstrate that the plaintiff’s personnel file was inaccessible, much less for six months after the litigation began. (Ibid.) The court found that the defendant had known of the arbitration agreement from the outset of the case, meaning the defendant delayed for one year before filing the motion. (Ibid.)

By contrast, the facts here demonstrate that Plaintiff’s personnel file was inaccessible at around the time that the lawsuit was filed, because Defendant was moving its files from one location to another. (See Santo Decl. ¶ 13.) Furthermore, even assuming Defendant knew of the arbitration agreement from the outset of the case, there has only been a two-month delay. This is nowhere near the delay in Campbell. Plaintiff attempts to extend the perception of delay by arguing that Defendant has known of Plaintiff’s legal representation since November 2021. However, Plaintiff cites no authority attaching any significance to the date that an employer is notified of an employee’s legal representation. The relevant time period is the one between the filing of the lawsuit and the filing of the motion to compel, which in this case was two months. Defendant also propounded some discovery requests (Chami Decl., Ex. C), but there is no indication of any significant developments in relation to those requests. Plaintiff has not been deposed.  

Overall, Defendant’s actions are not so inconsistent with the right to arbitration that the right has been waived. The Court finds no waiver.

III. Unconscionability

a. Procedural Unconscionability

Procedural unconscionability “focuses on two factors: ‘oppression’ and ‘surprise.’ ‘Oppression’ arises from an inequality of bargaining power which results in no real negotiation and ‘an absence of meaningful choice.’ ‘Surprise’ involves the extent to which the supposedly agreed-upon terms of the bargain are hidden in the prolix printed form drafted by the party seeking to enforce the disputed terms.” (Zullo v. Superior Court (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 477, 484, internal citations omitted.)

            1. Contract of Adhesion

Plaintiff argues that the agreement is procedurally unconscionable because she had to sign it as a condition of employment. (See Scott Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.) However, the adhesive nature of a contract represents only a minimal degree of procedural unconscionability. (Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 695, 704.) “[A] compulsory predispute arbitration agreement is not rendered unenforceable just because it is required as a condition of employment.” (Lagatree v. Luce, Forward, Hamilton & Scripps (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1127.)

            2. Oppression

Plaintiff argues that the agreement is oppressive because she was told she needed to sign it in order to continue working for Defendant, and no one explained arbitration to her or offered to let her take the documents home. (Scott Decl. ¶ 3.) Plaintiff avers that she was never informed that she could seek outside advice before signing. (Ibid.) None of this renders the agreement unenforceable.

Defendant had no affirmative obligation to explain the agreement to Plaintiff. (See Brookwood v. Bank of America (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1667, 1674 [“No law requires that parties dealing at arm's length have a duty to explain to each other the terms of a written contract”].) Plaintiff does not rebut Defendant’s evidence that “[e]mployees were not provided a time restriction to review the arbitration agreement and they are free to submit the arbitration agreement to their own personal attorneys for review.” (See Santo Decl. ¶ 9.) The fact that no one explicitly offered these options to Plaintiff does not mean Plaintiff was restricted from exercising these options.

Although Plaintiff was told that she had to sign the documents in order to begin employment, there is no indication that anyone imposed a time limit on Plaintiff’s signature, and no indication that anyone prevented Plaintiff from leaving with the documents or seeking outside counsel. Ultimately, “one who signs an instrument which on its face is a contract is deemed to assent to all its terms.” (Marin Storage & Trucking, Inc. v. Benco Contracting & Engineering, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 1042, 1049.) “A party cannot use his own lack of diligence to avoid an arbitration agreement.” (Brookwood, supra, 45 Cal.App.4th at p. 1674.)

            3. Surprise

Plaintiff argues that she “could not understand the agreement as it is almost entirely filled with legal terms that are difficult to understand and hidden within a long document including other various policies.” (Scott Decl. ¶ 5.) However, the agreement is only two pages long with clearly delineated provisions. (See Santo Decl., Ex. A.) The arbitration clause is the third paragraph on the first page. (Ibid.) It is neither buried nor replete with complex legal jargon. The agreement is not “visually impenetrable,” nor does it “challenge the limits of legibility.” (See OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 128.)   

Plaintiff also argues that Defendant did not attach the arbitration rules to the agreement. (See Scott Decl. ¶ 7.) However, “the failure to attach the [arbitration] rules, standing alone, is insufficient grounds to support a finding of procedural unconscionability.” (Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1472.) The agreement clearly provides that it will proceed under AAA rules. (Santo Decl., Ex. A.) Plaintiff has no evidence that she was prevented from accessing the applicable AAA rules.

In sum, the Court finds a minimal degree of procedural unconscionability.

b. Substantive Unconscionability

Substantive unconscionability focuses on the actual terms of the agreement and evaluates whether they create overly harsh or one-sided results as to shock the conscience. (Suh v. Superior Court (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1504, 1515.)  

Plaintiff argues that the agreement “prohibits Ms. Scott from participating in any representative proceedings, including one under PAGA.” (Opp. 12:4-5.) The agreement does no such thing, and Plaintiff fails to cite or quote any provision of the agreement to support this argument. Plaintiff fails to explain how the agreement otherwise “precludes relief that Plaintiff would enjoy under the law.” (See Opp. 11:28-12:1.) Thus, the Court finds no substantive unconscionability.

With a minimal degree of procedural unconscionability and no substantive unconscionability, the agreement remains enforceable.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. The case is stayed in its entirety pending the outcome of arbitration.

           


Case Number: 24STCV20969    Hearing Date: November 15, 2024    Dept: 32

 

JULIO GOMEZ CARRERO,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

ARCHITECTURAL SURFACES GROUP, LLC, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  24STCV20969

  Hearing Date:  November 15, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant caesarstone usa, inc.’s demurrer and motion to strike (CRS# 2050, 6743)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff Julio Gomez Carrero filed this action against various defendants, asserting (1) negligence, (2) products liability – failure to warn, (3) products liability – design defect, (4) fraudulent concealment, and (5) breach of implied warranties.     

According to the complaint, from 2007 to 2024, Plaintiff worked as a cutter, fabricator and/or installer of Defendants’ stone products. (FAC ¶¶ 63-72.) As a result of this work, Plaintiff came into contact with “inherently hazardous stone products manufactured, imported, supplied, distributed, contracted, and/or brokered, by the named Defendants and Does 1-100.” (Id., ¶ 73.) Specifically, Plaintiff was “exposed to and inhaled stone dust containing silica and other toxins and carcinogens.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff allegedly developed lung disease and is at increased risk of developing other silica-related diseases. (Id., ¶ 74.) Plaintiff alleges that he “has had to receive substantial medical treatment, including hospitalizations and surgeries, including a lung biopsy and a possible future lung transplantation.” (Id., ¶ 75.)

            On September 30, 2024, Defendant Caesarstone USA, Inc. filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike. Plaintiff filed his opposition on November 1, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Ibid.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part of that pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b).) The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike (1) any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading and (2) all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.)

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.) The Court notes that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Spratt Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Demurrer

            a. Causation

In order for a complaint to properly plead causation in a toxic exposure case, the plaintiff must allege the following: 

(1) he was exposed to each of the toxic materials claimed to have caused a specific illness;

(2) he must identify each product that allegedly caused the injury;

(3) he must allege that as a result of the exposure, the toxins entered his body;

(4) he must allege that he suffers from a specific illness, and that each toxin that entered his body was a substantial factor in bringing about, prolonging, or aggravating that illness; and

(5) he must allege that each toxin he absorbed was manufactured or supplied by a named defendant.

(Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 80.) These facts must be pled “in ordinary and concise language” and may be pled “in a conclusory fashion if [the plaintiff’s] knowledge of the precise cause of injury is limited.” (Ibid.)

            Here, the complaint identifies the offending products attributable to Defendant Caesarstone and alleges that Plaintiff was exposed to toxic chemicals, specifically silica, within those products. (Compl. ¶¶ 62-73.) Plaintiff alleges that the exposure caused the toxins to enter his body through inhalation while he worked as a cutter, fabricator and/or installer of the stone products. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges that the toxins were a substantial factor in his illness, specifically silicosis and other lung diseases. (Id., ¶ 74.) Plaintiff alleges that each hazardous stone product was “manufactured, imported, supplied, distributed, contracted, and/or brokered, by the named Defendants.” (Ibid.) Therefore, the complaint satisfies the requirements of Bockrath, and Plaintiff has adequately pled causation.

            b. Failure to Warn

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff fails to allege his injuries could have been avoided if adequate warnings had been provided. However, the complaint alleges that the stone products “lacked warnings or contained warnings that were inadequate to apprise Plaintiff of their toxic hazards and their serious effects upon the human body, and they either lacked instructions for handling and use or lacked instructions adequate to prevent exposure and disease.” (Compl. ¶ 1259.) The complaint alleges that as a result of this, Plaintiff was exposed to toxins which entered his body, leading to injuries such as silicosis. (Id., ¶¶ 1264-1265.) This is adequate for pleading purposes.

            c. Substantial Factor

            Defendant argues that “Plaintiff has failed to allege that Caesarstone’s, (or any defendants’) products were used in a reasonably foreseeable way and were a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s alleged injury.” (Dem. 9:9-10.) However, as discussed above, Plaintiff has satisfied the Bockrath standard for pleading causation. Plaintiff alleges that he inhaled toxic stone dust while working as a cutter, fabricator and/or installer of the stone products. (Compl. ¶¶ 62-73.) This demonstrates a reasonably foreseeable use of the product and resulting injury.

The fact that Plaintiff attributes the same defect and wrongdoing to multiple defendants does not mean the claim is inadequately pled against Caesarstone. Despite grouping the defendants together, the allegations are sufficient “to put the defendant on notice about what the plaintiff is complaining and what remedies are being sought.” (See Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 415.)

           

d. Fraudulent Concealment

            “The elements of fraud that will give rise to a tort action for deceit are: ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974, quoting Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.) A fraudulent concealment claim is actionable in toxic exposure cases. (See Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198-1200.)

            Here, the complaint alleges that “Defendants fraudulently concealed from Plaintiff, JULIO GOMEZ CARRERO, that their stone products were toxic and that they contained silica, metals and other toxins that cause fibrotic lung disease upon inhalation.” (Compl. ¶ 85.) Plaintiff alleges that due to this concealment, he unknowingly inhaled toxic particles through the course of his work, leading to lung disease. (Id., ¶ 84.) Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants were aware of the toxic and fibrogenic nature of their stone products and that exposure to them causes silicosis.” (Id., ¶ 1301.) Defendants allegedly concealed these hazards “so that [Plaintiff] would use Defendants' stone products in his work.” (Id., ¶ 1307.) Plaintiff alleges that he “purchased and/or received toxic stone products from Defendants.” (Id., ¶ 1306.) These are allegations of ultimate fact which facially satisfy the elements of fraudulent concealment.   

Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to plead fraud with the requisite specificity. However, the rule of specificity “is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations.” (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.) “[I]t is harder to apply this rule to a case of simple nondisclosure. How does one show ‘how’ and ‘by what means’ something didn't happen, or ‘when’ it never happened, or ‘where’ it never happened?” (Ibid.) Plaintiff cannot provide specific details on nonexistent statements. For pleading purposes, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged that Defendant failed to disclose the hazardous nature of its stone products, thereby inducing Plaintiff to purchase or use Defendants’ products, leading to his injuries. (Compl. ¶¶ 1297-1329.)

II. Motion to Strike

            “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id., subd. (c)(1).) “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud is “intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Id., subd. (c)(3).)

            As discussed above, Plaintiff has adequately pled fraudulent concealment. Fraud is an independent basis for punitive damages. Additionally, even if the conduct did not constitute fraud, knowingly selling toxic products without disclosing their hazards may be considered a conscious disregard of Plaintiff’s rights and safety, as well as imposing unjust hardship. The specific identity of the corporate officers who ratified the concealment is a factual question for discovery. The complaint adequately pleads punitive damages.        

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Caesarstone USA. Inc.’s demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED.
