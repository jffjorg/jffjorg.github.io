Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV44536    Hearing Date: August 6, 2024    Dept: B

JADE CARRASCO, ET AL. V. POSITIVE INVESTMENTS, INC., ET AL.

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:  August 6, 2024                                Trial Date:       December 9, 2024

Department:        B                                                     Case No.:         21STCV44536

 

Moving Party:             Defendants Positive Investments, Inc. and PI Properties LLC  

Responding Party:       None  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 7, 2021, Plaintiff Jade Carrasco (“Carrasco”), by and through her Guardian Ad Litem Jesse Ronald Carrasco, and Jazelle Dimas (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Positive Investments, Inc. (“Positive”), PI Properties LLC (“PPL”), Becky Radgowski, and DOES 1 to 100 for injuries Plaintiffs sustained in a dog bite attack. The complaint alleges causes of action against Defendants for: (1) strict liability for dog bite, (2) premises liability, (3) general negligence, and (4) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

 

On March 8, 2022, Defendants Positive and PPL filed an answer to the complaint.

 

On May 17, 2022, Plaintiffs filed respective amendments to the complaint which identified: (1) Ariana Arambula as Doe 1; (2) Joseph M. Radgowksi as Doe 2; and (3) Mark A. Radgowski as Doe 3.

 

On April 25, 2023, Defendants Becky Radgowski, Joseph M. Radgowski, Mark A. Radgowski, and Ariana Arambula filed a joint answer to the complaint.

 

On October 25, 2023, Defendants Positive and PPL filed a cross-complaint against Becky Radgowski, Joseph Radgowski, Mark Radgowski, Ariana Arambula, and Jazelle Dimas alleging causes of action for: (1) indemnity, (2) contribution and apportionment of fault, (3) indemnity, and (4) contributory and apportionment of fault.

 

On November 27, 2023, Jazelle Dimas filed an answer to the cross-complaint.

 

On December 7, 2023, Defendants Positive and PPL (collectively, “Defendants”) filed and served the instant Motion for Summary Judgment. The motion for summary judgment was served on all interested parties. Defendants move for an order granting summary judgment on the entirety of Plaintiffs’ complaint.

 

On January 12, 2024, Becky Radgowski, Joseph Radgowski, Mark Radgowski, and Ariana Arambula filed respective answers to the cross-complaint.

On February 2, 2024, after being held in chambers without argument, the Court denied Plaintiff Carrasco’s ex parte application to continue the hearing date on the motion for summary judgment. (02/02/24 Minute Order.)

 

On February 6, 2024, after being held in chambers without argument, the Court granted Plaintiff Carasco’s ex parte application to continue the hearing date on the motion for summary judgment. (02/06/24 Minute Order.) The Court continued the hearing on the motion for summary judgment from February 21, 2024 to April 19, 2024. (02/06/24 Minute Order at p. 1.)

 

On February 15, 2024, the Court, on its own motion, continued the hearing on the motion for summary judgment from April 19, 2024 to August 6, 2024. (02/15/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) Plaintiff Carrasco was ordered to give notice. (02/15/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) Based on a review of the court file, it does not appear that Plaintiff Carrasco provided notice to of the Court’s February 15, 2024 minute order.

 

On June 6, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Michelle C. Kim to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz sitting in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East effective June 24, 2024.

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The Court GRANTS Defendants Positive Investments, Inc. and PI Properties LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment.  

 

DISCUSSION

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “may rely on factually devoid discovery responses to shift the burden of proof . . . .” (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590.)

“As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the defendant has met its burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th 826, 849.) 

“The summary judgment statute [is] not intended nor can it be used as a substitute for existing methods in the trial of issues of fact.” (Travelers Indemn. Co. v. McIntosh (1952) 112 Cal.App.2d 177, 182.) The summary judgment “procedure is drastic and should be used with caution.” (House v. Lala (1960) 180 Cal.App.2d 412, 415.) “Any doubt in granting summary judgment should be exercised against the moving party.” (Ibid.)

Issue No.1: First Cause of Action for Strict Liability  

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs cannot prevail on their first cause of action for strict liability because Defendants were not the owners of the dog.

“The owner of any dog is liable for the damages suffered by any person who is bitten by the dog while in a public place or lawfully in a private place, including the property of the owner of the dog, regardless of the former viciousness of the dog or the owner’s knowledge of such viciousness.” (Civ. Code, § 3342, subd. (a).)

The Undisputed Material Facts

Defendants present the following undisputed material facts: on August 30, 2018, Defendants, as landlords, entered into a lease agreement with Mark Radgowski, Ariana Arambula, Becky Radgowski, and Joseph Radgowski, as tenants for the single family home located at 128 S. Lark Ellen Ave., Covina, CA 91722 (the “Property”) at the rate of $2,550.00 per month (the “Lease”). (Defendants’ Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (DSUMF) at No. 1.) The Lease limited the occupancy of the Property to four people and prohibited the tenants from having any pet. (DSUMF at Nos. 2-3.) Defendants were not Plaintiffs’ landlords and Plaintiffs were not Defendants’ tenants in 2019. (DSUMF at No. 4.) On November 29, 2019, Plaintiffs first visited the Property and became aware of the dog living on the Property. (DSUMF at No. 5.) On or about December 1, 2019, Plaintiffs moved into the home on the Property. (DSUMF at No. 6.)

Each time Plaintiffs encountered the dog at issue in this case, the dog smelled Plaintiffs and walked away. (DSUMF at No. 7.) Plaintiffs considered Becky Radgowski their friend and landlord in 2019. (DSUMF at No. 8.) The dog lived on the Property during the time Plaintiffs lived on the Property. (DSUMF at No. 9.) Defendants were not aware that Plaintiffs moved into the home on the Property, and neither were Defendants aware of a minor child, Plaintiff Carrasco, living on the Property. (DSUMF No. 10.) The dog which attacked Plaintiff Carrasco was not owned by Defendants and the tenants were the owners or keepers of the dog at issue. (DSUMF at No. 11.)

On December 17, 2019, Plaintiff Carrasco was attacked by the dog on the Property. (DSUMF at No. 12.) Defendants were not aware that the tenants owned, or were alleged to have owned, a dog until after the incident. (DSUMF at No. 13.) Defendants were unaware of the dog’s propensity for viciousness. (DSUMF at No. 14.) Plaintiffs did not know the dog at issue was dangerous. (DSUMF at No. 15.) On December 17, 2019, Defendants were not present on the Property and did not witness the alleged incident. (DSUMF at No. 16.)

Analysis

Here, it is undisputed that Defendants did not own the dog at issue that caused Plaintiffs’ alleged injuries. Given that the instant motion is unopposed, Plaintiffs have not presented any evidence to create a triable issue of material fact as to Defendants being strictly liable.

Therefore, the Court finds that summary judgment is appropriate as to the first cause of action for strict liability.

Issue No. 2: Second Cause of Action for Premises Liability and Third Cause of Action for General Negligence

“To succeed in a negligence action, a plaintiff must show the following: (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a legal duty; (2) the defendant breached the duty; and (3) the breach proximately or legally caused the plaintiff’s damages or injuries.” (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.) “The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence.” (Jones v. Awad (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 1200, 1207.) “Whether an individual owes a duty of care is a question of law.” (Ibid.)  

“[T]he public policy of this state generally has precluded a landlord’s liability for injuries to his tenant or his tenant’s invitees from a dangerous condition on the premises which comes into existence after the tenant has taken possession. This is true even though by the exercise of reasonable diligence the landlord might have discovered the condition.” (Uccello v. Laudenslayer (1975) 44 Cal.App.3d 504, 510.) “A duty of care may not be imposed on a landlord without proof that he knew of the dog and its dangerous propensities.” (Lundy v. California Realty (1985) 170 Cal.App.3d 813, 821.)

Analysis

The Court references its recitation of the undisputed material facts from above and incorporates such facts herein. Defendants have presented evidence that they lacked knowledge about the presence of the dog and its dangerous propensities. Moreover, Defendants have presented evidence that they were not the owners of the dog. Thus, Defendants have presented evidence to show that they did not have a duty of care.

Plaintiffs have failed to oppose the motion and have therefore not shown a triable issue of material fact. As a matter of law, the Court finds that Defendants did not owe Plaintiffs a duty of care.

As such, the Court finds that summary judgment is appropriate as to the second and third causes of action in the complaint.

Issue No. 3: Fourth Cause of Action for Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

“[T]he negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort, but the tort of negligence.” (Burgess v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1064, 1072, citation omitted, emphasis in original.) “The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply.” (Ibid.) “Its existence depends upon the foreseeability of the risk and a weighing of policy considerations for and against imposition of liability.” (Ibid., citation omitted.)

The Court references its recitation of the undisputed material facts pursuant to the first cause of action and incorporates it herein. The Court references its analysis as to the second and third causes of action and incorporates it herein. As a matter of law, the Court finds that Defendants did not owe a duty to Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs have not opposed the instant motion and therefore have failed to create a triable issue of material fact.

Therefore, the Court finds that summary judgment is appropriate as to the fourth cause of action in the complaint.

Additionally, because Plaintiffs failed to oppose Defendants’ motion for summary judgment, the Court finds that they have conceded to all the arguments raised therein as “[c]ontentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority.” (Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.)

 

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.  

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice and submit a proposed judgment consistent with this ruling.

DISCUSSION



Any party may obtain discovery . . . by inspecting, copying, testing, or
sampling documents, tangible things, land or other property, and electronically
stored information in the possession, custody, or control of any other party to
the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010, subd. (a).) Where a party fails to
timely respond to demand for inspection, copying, testing or sampling “[t]he
party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is
directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege
or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd.
(a).) Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b) provides that “[t]he party making the
demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”



Code Civ. Proc. §
2023.010(d) provides that a misuse of the discovery process is “[f]ailing to
respond to or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(h) states that a
misuse of the discovery process includes “[m]aking or opposing, unsuccessfully
and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit
discovery.”   “The court may impose a
monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery
process, or any attorney advising that conduct” under Code Civ. Proc. §
2023.030(a).  A court has discretion to
fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty
Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771,
791.)



Appropriateness of
Compelling Responses

are essentially identical, the Court will address the Motions collectively.

to respond to such discovery. (Tan Decls., ¶ 10.) Plaintiff’s counsel declares
that a $60.00 filing fee was incurred as to each respective motion, her hourly
rate is $550.00, and one (1) hour of time is anticipated to be spent pursuant
to each of the Motions, which consists of 0.5 hours preparing the motion,
reviewing the opposition, and preparing the reply. (Tan Decls., ¶¶ 18-20.) 0.5
hours is expected to be spent at the hearing on the Motions. (Tan Decls., ¶
20.) Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions against Defendants Joseph and Mark
Radgowski in the amount of $610.00 each. (Tan Decls., ¶ 21.)



The Court finds that
Defendants Joseph Radgowski and Mark Radgowski have each failed to respond to
