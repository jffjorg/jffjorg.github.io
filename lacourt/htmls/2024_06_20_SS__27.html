DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV38710    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION TO STRIKE EXPERT DESIGNATION 

Hearing Date: 6/20/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV38710 CONCEPION VALADEZ vs NEWHOPE PINES APARTMENTS 

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Defendant Newhope Pines Apts 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO STRIKE EXPERT DESIGNATION IS GRANTED. 

Background

On October 8, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present case, alleging she fell from the stairs of Defendant's property and sustained severe injuries. The trial date was set for November 8, 2023. On September 19, 2023, Plaintiff served her designation of expert witness information; however, Defendant failed to designate any experts prior to the then-scheduled trial date.

On October 25, 2023, the Court issued a minute order for the final status conference continuing trial to May 15, 2024, but also stating that "All discovery is to remain closed and tied to the prior trial date of November 8, 2023. Only discovery related to expert depositions is continued to the new trial date of May 15, 2024." On March 26, 2024, Defendant served its Expert Designation, naming John K. Tyson as an expert. Plaintiff now moves the Court to strike the untimely designation. Defendant filed an opposition arguing that Plaintiff changed her theory of liability and Defendant mistakenly believed the Court’s order opened all expert related deadlines, including expert designation.

 

Legal Standards 

 

“Any party may demand a mutual and simultaneous exchange by all parties of a list containing the name and address of any natural person, including one who is a party, whose oral or deposition testimony in the form of an expert opinion any party expects to offer in evidence at the trial.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.210, subd. (b).)  The exchange of expert witness information shall include either of the following: (1) a list setting forth the name and address of a person whose expert opinion that party expects to offer in evidence at the trial, or (2) a statement that the party does not presently intend to offer the testimony of an expert witness.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.260, subd. (b).)  On objection of any party who has made a complete and timely compliance with Section 2034.260, the trial court shall exclude from evidence the expert opinion of any witness that is offered by any party who has unreasonably failed to do any of the following: (1) list that witness as an expert under Section 2034.260; (2) submit an expert witness declaration; (3) produce reports and writings of expert witnesses under Section 2034.270; or make that expert available for a deposition. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.300.)

CCP § 2034.230 states “(b) The demand shall specify the date for the exchange of lists of expert trial witnesses, expert witness declarations, and any demanded production of writings. The specified date of exchange shall be 50 days before the initial trial date, or 20 days after service of the demand, whichever is closer to the trial date, unless the court, on motion and a showing of good cause, orders an earlier or later date of exchange.”

Discussion

The central issue in this case concerns the deadline for designating expert witnesses. Plaintiff contends that, pursuant to the trial date set for November 8, 2023, the final day to designate an expert witness under section 2034.230(b) was either September 19, 2023 (Plaintiff’s designation), or October 9, 2023 (20 days following Plaintiff's designation/demand). Consequently, Defendant's expert designation on March 26, 2024, more than 5 months later, was untimely and should be stricken under CCP § 2034.300.

Defendant first argues that the court's order extending the expert deposition deadline to follow the trial date of May 15, 2024, implied an extension of all expert-related deadlines, including expert designation. However, the Court finds this argument unconvincing for two reasons. First, the minute order specifically states that only the “expert deposition” deadline is extended, leaving little ambiguity unlike broader terms such as "expert deadlines" or "expert discovery." Second, the trial continuance was agreed upon through an oral stipulation at the final status conference on October 30, 2023, by which time Defendant had already missed the October 9, 2023, deadline for expert designation. It is not a situation where Defendant was actively engaging in expert designation and deferred this action based on a misinterpretation of the court order.

Furthermore, Defendant contends that a shift in Plaintiff’s liability theory from inadequately lit stairs to violations of governmental regulations justifies their late expert designation. This assertion is based on Plaintiff's questions during Defendant’s PMQ deposition and Plaintiff’s expert designation on September 19, 2023, of a forensic engineer relating to the building’s compliance with government regulations. The Court is not persuaded because Plaintiff’s negligence claim in her complaint already indicated that the action would be based on the building's lack of compliance with government regulations. Furthermore, Plaintiff’s expert designation on September 19, 2023, would have given Defendant 20 days to designate its own expert witness, but Defendant failed to do so. 

 

Therefore, Defendant’s expert designation was untimely and Plaintiff’s motion to strike is GRANTED.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT 

Hearing Date: 6/20/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV07948 GABRIELA SABET-BAKTASH vs. CITY OF LOS ANGELES, et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Adam Kligfeld’s and Havi Kligfeld

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: THE COURT WILL HEAR FROM THE PARTIES ON THE MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT  


Background

 

This case arises from an alleged trip and fall occurring on August 28, 2020, at approximately 11:00 a.m., on the sidewalk in front of Defendants Adam Kligfeld’s and Havi Kligfeld’s residence located at 6601 Lindenhurst Avenue in Los Angeles. Plaintiff allegedly tripped over an uplift in the sidewalk adjacent to Defendants’ property. Defendants now move the Court for summary judgment or summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s two causes of action for negligence and premises liability.

 

Evidentiary Ruling 

Plaintiff’s Objections No. 2, 5, and 6 are overruled. At this time, the parties did not make any other objections to evidence that is likely to be material to the Court’s ruling on the Motion for Summary Judgment. (CCP § 437c(q).) 

 

Legal Standard

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.)

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

 

SIDEWALK ACCIDENT DECISIONS DOCTRINE

 

The “Sidewalk Accident Decisions” doctrine (the “sidewalk doctrine”) governs liability relating to property adjacent to a sidewalk. The sidewalk doctrine is based on case law explaining that sidewalks are public and thus adjacent landowners owe no duty to users of the sidewalk unless that property owner created the hazardous condition. Streets and Highways Code § 5610 provides that owners of lots fronting on a public street shall maintain any sidewalk in a condition that will not endanger persons and not interfere with the public convenience in the use of those works.  Pursuant to Jones v. Deeter (1984) 152 Cal.App.3d 798, 803, the duty imposed by this ordinance is owed to the city and not to persons using the sidewalk. “Under section 5610 the abutting owner bears the duty to repair defects in the sidewalk, regardless of whether he has created these defects. It was felt, however, that it would be unfair for such an owner to be held liable to travelers injured as a result of sidewalk defects which were not of the owner’s making. Thus, the ‘Sidewalk Accident Decisions’ doctrine arose; this doctrine holds that the abutting property owner is not liable in tort to travelers injured on the sidewalk unless the owner somehow creates the injurious sidewalk condition.” (Deeter, 152 Cal.App.3d at 803.) 

 

While generally a landowner is not liable for harm resulting to third parties from conditions or activities outside his property, on adjoining land or on the highway, street, or sidewalk, the landowner may be liable if the landowner created the defect or exercised dominion or control over the adjoining land (Jordan v. City of Sacramento (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1490-1491; Corcoran v. San Mateo (1953) 122 Cal.App.2d 355, 359; Schaefer v. Lenahan (1944) 63 Cal.App.2d 324, 326.) In settings where the planting trees or habitually trimming or caring for them, these abutting owners have the duty to maintain the trees in a safe condition to make sure the roots do not cause a tripping hazard. (Alcaraz v. Vece (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1149, 1162.) 

 

Furthermore, “a defendant need not own, possess and control property in order to be held liable; control alone is sufficient.” (Id.) A¿person¿is required to¿maintain¿“land in their possession and control in a reasonably safe condition.” (Ann M. v. Pacific Plaza Shopping Center¿(1993) 6 Cal.4th 666, 674.)¿¿In¿Alcaraz, supra., the court held that because the defendant¿maintained¿the lawn surrounding the meter box, a reasonable trier of fact could infer that the defendant exercised control over that strip of land and had a duty to warn others of the hazardous condition of the meter box when defendant had notice of the condition.¿(Alcaraz, 14 Cal.4th¿at 1162.)

 

1.  Ownership Of the Sidewalk

Defendants introduced a declaration stating that Defendants did not own the sidewalk or the tree whose roots allegedly caused the displacement. (Declaration of Adam Kligfeld ¶ 3.) Defendants have met their initial burden as to the issue of ownership.

Plaintiff disputes ownership of the tree and sidewalk based on the lack of official government documents or surveys provided by the moving party. (Avrit Declaration 1:6-25, 11:9-12:7.) Plaintiff did not provide any evidence, such as a deed or property drawings, showing that the sidewalk was owned by Defendant. To raise a triable issue of fact, Plaintiff needs to set forth specific facts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) If Plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted. (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) Pointing out that certain evidence was not presented by the moving party does not set forth specific facts to raise a triable issue of fact. Plaintiff needs to provide affirmative evidence that Defendants owned the sidewalk and tree at issue.

 

2.  Creation Of the Displacement

Under the sidewalk liability doctrine, Defendants can be liable for the incident if Defendants created the dangerous condition. Defendants installed drip irrigation on both sides of the sidewalk. (UMF 13.) Although Defendants stated that installing irrigation tubes on a timer system for drip irrigation significantly reduced the amount of water going anywhere in/on the parkway where the tree is located (UMF 14, Declaration of Adam Kligfeld ¶ 7), Defendants failed to show that they did not cause the alleged dangerous condition. There was no expert declaration or evidence showing how the reduced amount of water on the parkway or the tree did not cause the displacement, or that the displacement was instead caused by some other factors unrelated to Defendants. Thus, Defendants failed to meet their initial burden.

Further, Plaintiff introduced expert witness testimony declaring that Defendant’s drip irrigation caused the uplift. “During my visit on October 10, 2023, I excavated the ground on both sides of the sidewalk in an effort to determine the cause of the uplifted sidewalk by determining extent of the intrusion of the roots of the sycamore tree which was growing adjacent to the sidewalk in the parkway area. The excavations I conducted were sufficient to permit me to formulate the opinions stated in this Declaration. I was able to determine that one of the sycamore roots that traveled under the sidewalk at the subject location measured 5.5 inches in diameter and another measured 4 inches in diameter. It is my opinion that these tree roots uplifted the sidewalk, and that this condition existed since at least 2016. I was able to determine that Defendants Kligfield irrigated both sides of the sidewalk, and thereby contributed to the growth of the tree roots that uplifted the sidewalk. By watering and then reducing the water, the tree roots would grow in search of water and that is what led to the uplift. What Defendants Kligfeld did was to provide more water to the soil area surface which encouraged surface root growth as evidenced by the photos I took and those I reviewed. This sidewalk uplift was caused by the homeowners Defendants Kligfeld.” (Monfette Decl. 6:12-21.)

Defendant’s liability under the sidewalk decision doctrine arises when 1) defendant created the dangerous condition, or 2) defendant retained control over the dangerous condition and failed to warn when it had notice. Defendant can be liable under either one of these two prongs. As shown in the foregoing analysis, Defendants here have failed to meet their initial burden, and Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact regarding the first prong of the creation of the dangerous condition. Summary judgment is therefore denied as to Defendants’ liability under the sidewalk decision doctrine, and an analysis on the issue of control and notice need not to be conducted.

Defendants also contend they have no liability for the sidewalk adjacent to their property based on a 2015 settlement agreement in the case of Mark Willits, et al. v. City of Los Angeles, Case No. CV10-05782-CBM, D. Ct. (CDCA)[1] Pursuant to that agreement, the City allocated funds for the installation, repair, and remediation of pedestrian facilities, including the installation of missing curb ramps and the repair of damage caused by tree roots to sidewalks. The agreement does not create new obligations contrary to those in case law. It merely allocates funds for obligations that have always presumptively been the City's responsibility. Nothing in the agreement relates to third-party homeowners' obligations, nor does it alleviate Defendant’s liability under the sidewalk liability doctrine, the controlling law relating to Defendants’ liability in the present case.

 

TRIVIAL DEFECT DOCTRINE 

 

“Property owners are required to maintain land in their possession and control in a reasonably safe condition and to use due care to eliminate dangerous conditions on their property.  But a property owner is not liable for damages caused by a minor, trivial, or insignificant defect on its property.  The so-called ‘trivial defect doctrine’ recognizes that persons who maintain walkways, whether public or private, are not required to maintain them in an absolutely perfect condition.  The duty of care imposed on a property owner, even one with actual notice, does not require the repair of minor defects.”  (Fajardo v. Dailey (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 221, 226 [cleaned up].)  

 

“In limited circumstances a court may determine a walkway defect is trivial as a matter of law.  Where reasonable minds can reach only one conclusion—that there was no substantial risk of injury—the issue is a question of law, properly resolved by way of summary judgment.  But where sufficient evidence has been presented so that reasonable minds may differ as to whether the defect is dangerous, summary judgment is inappropriate.”  (Id. at p. 226.)

 

California Courts have developed two substantially similar tests to determine whether a sidewalk defect is trivial, i.e., not dangerous, as a matter of law. In Stathoulis v. City of Montebello (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 559, 567-68, the Court of Appeal set out a two part test. “First, the court reviews evidence regarding the type and size of the defect.  If that preliminary analysis reveals a trivial defect, the court considers evidence of any additional factors such as the weather, lighting and visibility conditions at the time of the accident, the existence of debris or obstructions, and plaintiff’s knowledge of the area.  If these additional factors do not indicate the defect was sufficiently dangerous to a reasonably careful person, the court should deem the defect trivial as a matter of law ....” (See also Nunez v. City of Redondo Beach (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 749, 758 [adopting two-step framework]; Huckey v. City of Temecula (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 1092, 1105 [same].)

 

More recently, in Stack v. City of Lemoore (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 102, 110, the Court of Appeal read Government Code section 830.2 to require a more “holistic” approach, stating, “[a]lthough we agree with the premise that the size of the defect is the primary determinant of triviality, as discussed below, we modify the prevailing two-step framework into a holistic, multi-factor analysis.”  The Court will follow Stack’s holistic approach.

 

1.  Size of the Defect

 

 “Sidewalk elevations ranging from three-quarters of an inch to one and one-half inches have generally been held trivial as a matter of law.” (Huckey, supra, 37 Cal.App.5th at p. 1107; see also Stathoulis v. City of Montebello (2008)164 Cal.App.4th 559, 568 [“Several decisions have found height differentials of up to one and one-half inches trivial as a matter of law.”]) “There is no firmly fixed arbitrary measurement in inches below which a defect is trivial as a matter of law and above which it becomes a question of fact whether or not the defect is dangerous. This is because a court should not rely solely upon the size of the defect ... although the defect’s size ‘may be one of the most relevant factors’ to the court’s decision.” (Id. at pp. 112–113 [cleaned up].)

 

2.  Aggravating Factors

 

The Court next considers additional factors.  “Beyond size, additional factors courts typically consider in assessing a sidewalk condition’s triviality as a matter of law are: the nature and quality of the defect (including whether it has jagged breaks or cracks); whether anything was obstructing or concealing the defect (for instance, an object, debris, or other substance); the lighting and weather conditions at the time of the incident; whether the defect has caused other accidents; and plaintiff's familiarity with the area.” (Stack, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 115.)

 

3.  Analysis

 

“It is well within the common knowledge of lay judges and jurors just what type of a defect in a sidewalk is dangerous.” (Caloroso v. Hathaway (2004) 122 Cal.App.4th 922, 928.) “Regardless of whether a [expert] witness can be found to opine on the subject of a dangerous condition, the court must independently evaluate the circumstances” (Id.)  

Defendants introduced evidence showing that the uplift was measured to be 1 inch to 1.25 inches at its highest point. (Def’s Exh. B, photograph Nos. 12-15.) On the date of the incident, the sky was clear and the weather was dry. (Def’s Exh. C, Sabet-Baktash Depo., 18:2-17.) Defendants were also not aware of anyone falling on the sidewalk at issue prior to Plaintiff’s incident. (Def’s Exh. D, Deposition of Havi Kligfeld, 89:22-90:6.) The displacement, at its highest point, measured 1.25 inches, which is within the range of ¾ of an inch to 1 ½ inches where the court has found the displacement to be trivial.

Plaintiff’s essential argument is that, according to Plaintiff’s expert, Brad Avrit’s, measurement, the displacement measures from 1.33 to 1.5 inches. (Avrit Decl., pg. 35-40, pictures of Avrit measuring the displacement.) The two sidewalk slabs on both ends of the displacement are titled at an angle, 3.3 and 7.2 degrees. (Avrit Decl. pg. 5.) As a result of the incline or tilt, the displacement at its apex has a practical effect of being higher than 1.5 inches. Additionally, the displacement was obstructed by shade and overgrown vegetation.

Obviously, the parties' measurements of the displacement differ. Defendants measured the displacement as ranging from 1 inch to 1.25 inches, while Plaintiff’s measurement ranged from 1.3 to 1.5 inches. The difference in measurement is related to the incline or tilt of the sidewalk slabs. Defendants measured the displacement using a tape measure that hugged the displacement with no space in between. Plaintiff’s expert measured the displacement using a right-angle ruler, with the base of the ruler sitting on one sidewalk slab at an angle and a level placed on top of the other sidewalk slab, also at an angle. Plaintiff’s measurement takes the meeting point between the level and the right-angle ruler. (See Avrit Decl., pg. 35-36.) As a result of the slabs’ incline, Plaintiff’s ruler is slightly tilted, creating a visible gap between the displacement and the ruler, resulting in a higher measurement than Defendants’. Plaintiff’s measurement has already taken  account of the incline. 

As to the visibility of the sidewalk at issue, Avrit points to Figure 6 of his declaration, which shows the shading of the area as it likely would have appeared at the time of day that Plaintiff fell. Plaintiff relies on Figure 7 to show that the uplift is difficult to see. However, the expert does not state whether the photo was taken at the time of the incident when Plaintiff fell. Examination of Figure 6 shows that, although there is shade cast on the displacement, it does not seem to impact the visibility of the sidewalk or the displacement. Although there is some vegetation growing out of the property next to the displacement, the vegetation is located at the edges of the displacement, and neither the vegetation itself nor the shadow it casts obscures the visibility of the displacement. (Figure 8 and Pg. 32-33, 39 to Avrit Decl.)

Furthermore, Plaintiff presented no evidence of cracks, jagged edges, or debris covering the displacement. There were no prior incidents reported, and it is undisputed that the incident occurred on a clear day with a dry sidewalk surface and no rain.

PETITION FOR RELIEF

Hearing Date: 6/20/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV18116 GEOFF LOUI, et al. vs STATE OF CALIFORNIA 

Moving Party: Defendant STATE OF CALIFORNIA

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: PETITION FOR RELIEF IS GRANTED 

 

Background

 

Defendant, the People of the State of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation (“Caltrans”), has moved the Court for an order relieving it from the provisions of Government Code Section 945.4 on account of failure to file a timely claim against the County of Los Angeles.

 

Legal Standard

 

Under the Government Claims Act, a plaintiff bringing suit for monetary damages against a public entity or employees thereof must first present a claim to the public entity (“government claim”), which must be acted upon or deemed rejected by the public entity. (Government Code §§ 945.4, 950.2, 950.6(a).) Government Code section 911.2(a) states that “a claim relating to a cause of action for death or for injury to person or to personal property . . . shall be presented . . . not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.”

 

If a plaintiff fails to file a government claim within the six-month period, he or she may apply to the public entity for permission to file a late claim. (§ 911.4(a).)  An application for permission to file a late claim must be presented within a reasonable time, and not later than one year after the cause of action’s accrual. (§ 911.4(b).)

 

If the public entity denies the application for permission to file a late claim, the plaintiff may file a civil petition for relief from section 945.4’s requirement of timely claim presentation prior to suit. (§ 946.6.) The petition must be filed within six months after the application to the public entity is denied or deemed to be denied. (§ 946.6(b).) The petition must show: (1) that an application was made to the public entity under section 911.4 and was denied or deemed denied; (2) the reason for failure to timely present the claim to the public entity within the time limit specified in section 911.2; and (3) the information required by section 910. (§ 946.6(b).) 

 

The court shall grant relief only if it finds that (1) the application to the public entity for leave to file a late claim was made within a reasonable time not to exceed one year after accrual of the claim as specified in section 911.4(b), (2) the application was denied or deemed denied by the public agency pursuant to section 911.6,  and (3) one or more of the following is applicable: (a) the failure to timely present the claim was through mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, unless the public entity establishes that it would be prejudiced in the defense of the claim if the court relieves the petitioner from the requirements of section 945.4; (b) the person who sustained the alleged injury, damage or loss was a minor during all of the time specified in section 911.2 for the presentation of the claim; (c) the person who sustained the alleged injury, damage or loss was physically or mentally incapacitated during all of the time specified in section 911.2 for the presentation of the claim and by reason of that disability failed to present a claim during that time; or (d) the person who sustained the alleged injury, damage or loss died before the expiration of the time specified in section 911.2 for the presentation of the claim. (§ 946.6(c).)

 

“Government Code section 946.6 is a remedial statute intended to provide relief from technical rules which otherwise provide a trap for the unwary. The remedial policy underlying the statute is that wherever possible cases should be heard on their merits. Thus, a denial of such relief by the trial court is examined more rigorously than where relief is granted and any doubts which may exist should be resolved in favor of the application.”¿(Munoz, supra, 33 Cal.App.4th at p. 1778 (citations omitted).) Because of the remedial character of the statute, “the modern trend of judicial decisions favors granting relief unless absolutely forbidden by statute.”¿(Ibid.)¿ 

 

The excusable neglect standard assumes that there has been some sort of inattention or carelessness and asks “whether a reasonably prudent person might have made the same error under the same or similar circumstances.”¿(Id. at p. 1783 (citations omitted).) In applying this standard, a court must also examine whether the party or her counsel were “otherwise diligent in investigating and pursuing the claim.”¿(Id. at pp. 1782-1783; see also Dep't of Water & Power v. Superior Court (2000) 82 Cal. App. 4th 1288, 1293.)

 

Discussion

On June 2, 2022, Plaintiffs Geoff Loui and Rich Herschinger filed their complaint alleging that Plaintiffs were cycling eastbound at or near 26104 Pacific Coast Highway, Malibu, CA 90265 when they allegedly came across a road defect which caused them to lose control of their bicycle, resulting in a crash that caused them to  sustain injuries. The State of California through its Department of Transportation (Caltrans) filed its answer on October 28, 2022.

Since 2022, Defendant has met and conferred with Plaintiffs' counsel. During these conversations, Plaintiffs’ counsel stated that the County of Los Angeles had provided a declaration that it had not performed any work on the road at issue and Plaintiffs dismissed the County on August 15, 2022, pursuant to that declaration. In performing due diligence, Defendant’s counsel discovered that Los Angeles County Public Water Works had an Annual Encroachment Permit to perform work at the site of the incident. Counsel contacted the representative listed on the permit, Ramy Mattar, on August 29, 2023, and he responded on August 30, 2023, that the County had no records of work performed at the location identified in this case. In December 2023, Caltrans received subpoenaed documents from Los Angeles County showing records that the County did actually perform work in the area of the accident to repair a 74” crack in a water main. Discovery of this information was delayed by both the mistake of counsel and Los Angeles County’s active denial of its involvement in this case, both by declaration made to Plaintiffs’ counsel and representations made to Caltrans’ counsel.

On December 20, 2023, Caltrans made a written application to the County of Los Angeles for permission to file claim. On January 2, 2024, Couty rejected Caltrans’ claim on the basis of a late claim. On January 12, 2024, Caltrans filed a request for a leave to file a late claim to the County of Los Angeles. County has not provided a response within 45 days as required under Gov. Code §911.6. On April 22, 2024, Caltrans moved the Court for relief.

All the requirements for relief have been met. Through the County’s active denial of its involvement in this case, both by declaration made to Plaintiff’s counsel and representations made to Caltrans’ counsel, Caltrans’ counsel did not learn of the County’s involvement until the County produced subpoenaed documents in December 2023. On December 20, 2023, Caltrans made a written application to the County of Los Angeles for permission to file a claim. This claim was rejected in January 2024. Caltrans made another application to file a late claim, but the County did not respond to Caltrans’ request, and the request is presumed denied under Gov. Code § 911.6. Within six months of filing the initial application, Caltrans, on 4/22/24, filed the present petition with the Court for relief.

The Court finds that Caltrans was diligent in investigating and pursuing the claim given the circumstances of the case, and a prudent person might have made the same error under similar circumstances, especially given the County’s representations to both Caltrans and Plaintiff. Furthermore, the County was served but did not file an opposition alleging prejudice. Given the overriding principle to hear cases on their merits, the Court grants Caltrans’ petition for relief.

Tentative Ruling 

 

Hearing Date:                6/20/2024 at 1:30 p.m.   

 

Case No./Name.:         22STCV26982, RANDY MEJIA AND LIZBETH VARGAS v. LUIS VALENZUELA

 

Motion:                              MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Moving Party:                 Randy Mejia and Lizbeth Vargas, Plaintiffs

 

Responding Party:      Unopposed

 

Notice:                                Sufficient

   

Ruling:                               PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT IS GRANTED

 

Background

 

On August 19, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against defendant regarding a motor vehicle accident. Thereafter, Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint, adding minors Lizbeth Vargas and Randy Mejia as plaintiffs, through their guardian ad litum, Erendira Tabares. Randy Mejia’s proposed guardian ad litum was accepted by the court. Yet, Lizbeth Vargas’s proposed guardian ad litum was rejected by the court. Then, this motion to amend was filed after Lizbeth Vargas, during the period between the date of the of the first amended complaint and the date of the application for Lizbeth Vargas’s guardian ad litum, became an adult, legally abandoning her title as a minor. Since the first amended complaint lists Lizbeth Vargas as a minor while in fact Lizbeth Vargas is now an adult, the court clerk has not accepted and filed a summons for the case. Thus, the absence of a summons has prevented plaintiff from serving this case upon the Defendant.

 

In this motion, Plaintiffs seek to change plaintiff Lizbeth Vargas “Randy Mejia and Lizbeth Vargas, minors by and through their Guardian ad Litem, Erendira Tabares” to “Lizbeth Vargas, an individual, and Randy Mejia, a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Erendira Tabares.”

 

Legal Standard 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”  Amendment may be allowed at any time before or after commencement of trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) “[T]he court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings. The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.” (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428 (internal citations omitted).) “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend . . .”  (Morgan v. Sup. Ct. (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) The type of prejudice the court is to be concerned with should be something beyond simply having to cope with a potentially successful new legal theory of recovery that has been revealed during discovery. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486.) Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.” (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)  

 

DISCUSSION and CONCLUSION

 

There is no prejudice that will come into effect if this motion is granted. Here, plaintiffs have been unable to serve defendant because of the technical discrepancy between Lizbeth Vargas’s actual age with Lizbeth Vargas’s status articulated in the first amended complaint. Courts are to be wary of prejudice that include “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.” (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.) A technical discrepancy that needs to be corrected in order to enable service of process does not raise a concerning issue of prejudice.

 

Furthermore, this motion is unopposed, giving more credence to the notion that no prejudice is present. Moreover, the California Code of Civil Procedure has explicitly said that "The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect." (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(a)(1)).

 

Thus, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Leave to Amend is GRANTED. The Court hereby orders that the proposed amended pleading be filed and served within five court days of today.

MOTION TO DEEM RFA ADMITTED AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 6/20/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV11630 JESSICA MENA OROMAN, et al. vs JOHN BABER

Moving Party: Plaintiffs 

Responding Party: Defendant John Baber

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO DEEM RFA ADMITTED AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED.

 

Legal Standard

¿ 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ CCP § 2033.010.¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared . . . .”¿ CCP § 2033.250(a).¿ 

¿ 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. CCP § 2033.280(a). The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. CCP § 2033.280(b). The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. CCP § 2033.280(c). Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to deem RFA admitted does not have any meet and confer requirements.¿

 

Discussion

 

On November 3, 2023, Plaintiffs served Requests for Admission (Set One) upon Defendant John Baber. Defendant’s deadline to respond was December 8, 2023. As of May 17, 2024, when the present motion was filed, Defendant had not responded to Plaintiffs’ discovery request.

¿ It is undisputed that the initial responses were not served within the timeframe mandated by CCP § 2033.250(a). Furthermore, no opposition was filed or any other filing showing that responses have been served prior to the hearing. Thus, the present motion is GRANTED and Plaintiffs’ RFA, Set One, is deemed admitted.

 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. CCP § 2033.280(c). Defendant’s failure to serve discovery responses forced Plaintiffs to file the present motion and incur attorney’s fees.

 

Plaintiffs request sanctions in the amount of $1804.00, and the Court finds this amount reasonable. Consequently, the Court sanctions Defendant in the amount of $1700. Defendant and his counsel, jointly and severally, are ORDERED to pay sanctions of $1700 to Plaintiffs within 20 days of today’s date.

Ruling: MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL IS GRANTED

 

Background

 

Samer Habbas represents Plaintiff. Habbas moves to be relieved as counsel, citing an irremediable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. No opposition has been filed.

 

Legal Standard¿ 

¿ 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.) 

¿ 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Forms MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion) and MC-052 (Declaration), and include MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).) 

¿ 

Analysis and Conclusion¿ 

¿ 

Habbas has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Habbas seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).) The court notes that Plaintiff’s address has been confirmed by counsel as current in the past 30 days.

 

The Court notes that the next hearing is an FSC set for 4/1/25, which should be sufficient time for Plaintiff to retain new counsel. Alternatively, if Plaintiff or new counsel needs additional time to prepare for trial, sufficient time exists for Plaintiff to take action to seek a continuance of the trial. Thus, the present motion to be relieved as counsel is GRANTED effective upon service on Plaintiff.

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO TAKE SUBSEQUENT DEPOSITIONS AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 6/20/24 

CASE NO./NAME: BS170239 DANIEL GEOULLA VS CALIFORNIA AUTOMOBILE INSURANCE COMPANY 

Moving Party: Respondent CALIFORNIA AUTOMOBILE INSURANCE COMPANY 

Responding Party: Claimant

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling:

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO TAKE SUBSEQUENT DEPOSITIONS IS GRANTED

REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED

 

Background

The claimant's uninsured motorist claim arises out of a minor rear-end accident that occurred on October 3, 2013. As a result of the accident, the claimant alleges injuries to his neck, back, knees, ankle, and wrist, with ongoing cervical complaints and a surgical recommendation from his treating orthopedic surgeon, Dr. Fardad Mobin. Respondent has taken Claimant’s deposition three times: on 4/22/16 for 3.5 hours, on 7/6/17 for 15 minutes, and on 10/5/18 for an undisclosed amount of time.

In April 2020, Respondent received discovery responses, including Dr. Mobin’s 12/12/18 report. From the report, Respondent learned for the first time that Claimant was involved in two accidents in 2017 and 2018, after the present 2013 accident. Further, on May 3, 2024, Claimant produced two reports from Dr. Andrew Fox reflecting Claimant’s visits with Dr. Fox in 2020. In the reports, Dr. Fox compared Claimant’s images taken in July 2020 with Claimant’s prior images; Dr. Fox noted changes in the claimant's cervical spine. Dr. Fox stated that Claimant most likely needed definitive treatment, including surgery, which would cost upwards of $200,000. Based on learning of Dr. Fox’s report and Claimant’s subsequent accidents, Respondent moves the court to compel Claimant’s 4th deposition.

Procedural Issues

Claimant argues that the moving papers are deficient in several ways. First, the motion should have been brought under § 2025.610 instead of § 2025.450. Respondent concedes this point but argues that the Court should excuse this procedural/clerical error because Claimant has already responded to the merits under § 2025.610. Since Claimant has already responded to the merits of the motion under § 2025.610 and is therefore not prejudiced, the Court will excuse Respondent’s clerical error.

Second, Claimant argues that a separate statement should have been filed for a motion to compel further deposition and for issue and evidentiary sanctions. This is a motion to compel a subsequent deposition, not a motion to compel further deposition where counsel instructs the client not to answer. A separate statement is not required. Although a separate statement is required for issue or evidentiary sanctions, as the Court will discuss below, evidentiary sanctions are not justified under the current facts where the Discovery Act provides for an incremental approach to sanctions aimed at inducing compliance and not punishment.

Leave To Take Subsequent Depositions

Pertinent parts of CCP 2025.610 provide the following:

(a) Once any party has taken the deposition of any natural person, including that of a party to the action, neither the party who gave, nor any other party who has been served with a deposition notice pursuant to Section 2025.240 may take a subsequent deposition of that deponent.

 

(b) Notwithstanding subdivision (a), for good cause shown, the court may grant leave to take a subsequent deposition, and the parties, with the consent of any deponent who is not a party, may stipulate that a subsequent deposition be taken.

Claimant does not dispute that he was involved in two accidents in 2017 and 2018 after the present incident, nor does Claimant dispute that Respondent learned of Claimant’s 2017 and 2018 accidents in 2020, and of Dr. Fox’s assessment of the progression of issues relating to Claimant’s cervical spine in 2024. Claimant argues that he has not made any bodily injury claims or seen any doctors other than in this current action. The subsequent incidents only resulted in property damage claims. Nonetheless, because Claimant was involved in two subsequent accidents and Dr. Fox noticed changes in the cervical spine before and after these alleged accidents, Respondent has a basis to ask Claimant questions relating to these accidents related to the cervical spine for the assessment of damages.

The motion is not moot as Claimant alleges. Even though Respondent took Claimant’s third deposition in 2018, Respondent did not ask questions about the two subsequent accidents, as Respondent only learned of them in 2020. Claimant argues that Respondent waited six years to take Claimant’s deposition. Respondent can conduct discovery before the discovery deadline, even if it’s six years later. Part of the reason for this delay is the appeal and as Respondent has stated, the rationale for taking Claimant’s deposition now is the recent receipt of Dr. Fox’s report, where he noted changes in Claimant’s cervical spine. Thus, the court finds good cause to grant Respondent leave to take the subsequent deposition of Claimant. CCP § 2025.610 does not empower the court to compel claimant to sit for his deposition, as no notice of deposition has been served and the last deposition took place in 2018.

Regarding sanctions, the Court finds that both parties acted with substantial justification. The issue of whether there is good cause for a fourth deposition is up to the Court to decide, and both parties have provided sufficient rationale for their positions.
