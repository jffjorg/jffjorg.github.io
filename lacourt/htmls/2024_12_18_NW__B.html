DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV40020    Hearing Date: December 18, 2024    Dept: B

Hearing:             12/18/2024

Re:                         EMILY ROBINSON, ET AL. VS JONATHAN PRESTO (21STCV40020)

 

 

Petitioner Emily Robinson’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

 

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Emily Robinson’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from a June 1, 2019, motor vehicle accident in which Plaintiffs Emily Robinson (“Petitioner”), Brandon Grimes (“Brandon”), and Ben Robinson (“Ben”)(collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege that Defendant Jonathan Presto (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 20 operated their motor vehicle in a negligent or reckless manner as to collide with Plaintiff’s vehicle and cause injuries thereof. On October 29, 2021, Plaintiffs brought suit alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence.

 

On March 30, 2023, minor Estelle Grimes (“Claimant”), by and through her guardian ad litem Emily Robinson, brought suit against Defendant and Does 1 through 20 alleging one cause of action for negligence. Petitioner was pregnant with Claimant at the time of the underlying incident and alleges that, as a result of the accident, Claimant suffered injuries which manifested themselves at birth.

 

On April 13, 2023, Petitioner was approved as guardian ad litem for Claimant.

 

On December 5, 2023, the two cases were consolidated.

 

On June 7, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On June 12, 2024, Petitioner filed a notice of settlement.

 

On November 26, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability on behalf of Claimant.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $100,000.00 from Defendant.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Cal. Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Ibid. at 1606.) 

 

Probate Code section 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows: 

 

The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator), costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or incompetent person. (Prob. Code § 3601(a).) 

 

In cases pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 7.955(a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (CRC Rule 7.955(a)(2).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $100,000.00. Of the $100,000.00 settlement, $640.05 will be used to reimburse medical expenses, $15,463.33 will be used for attorney’s fees, and $3,896.82 will be used for costs. (See MC-350 Item 16.) Therefore, the net settlement amount available for Claimant is $80,000.00. (Ibid.)

The Petition sets forth information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise pursuant to CRC Rule 7.950. (MC-350 Attachments 6, 8, and 11b(6).) It also provides information bearing upon the reasonableness of the settlement apportionment. (See ibid. at Attachment 11b(6).) Additionally, attorney’s fees are reasonable as they represent approximately 15% of the total settlement amount. (MC-350 Attachment 13a.) Claimant’s counsel provides their retainer agreement as Attachment 17a. Costs are reasonable considering the extent of litigation.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the petition to confirm minor’s compromise.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

 

Hearing Date: DECEMBER 18, 2024

Trial Date: JANUARY 27, 2025

 

I.       BACKGROUND

On May 19, 2022, Plaintiffs Evan Pugrad (“Pugrad”), Trenton Hearin (“Hearin”), and Caroline Draper (“Draper”)(collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendant Nancy Martinez (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 50 alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence. Plaintiffs allege that, on or about May 22, 2020, Defendant negligently operated her vehicle, colliding with Plaintiffs’ vehicle and causing injuries thereof.

On June 5, 2024, the action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On October 30, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions.

The motion is unopposed.

 

II.    LEGAL STANDARD

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 permits the Court, in its discretion, to impose the following types of terminating sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process”:

(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

(2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

(3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

(4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.

(CCP § 2023.030(d).)

Conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process” includes failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (CCP § 2023.010(d) and (g).) “Imposition of sanctions for misuse of discovery lies within the trial court's discretion, and is reviewed only for abuse.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 991.) “The trial court should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery[.]” (Ibid. at 992.) Sanctions should be tailored to the harm created by the withheld discovery and should not be used as punishment. (Ibid.) The court should also consider whether the discovery misconduct was “willful” and whether the requesting party was prejudiced by the other’s failure to comply. (Do It Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 37.)

Lastly, courts have held that terminating sanctions are warranted when a party repeatedly fails to propound adequate discovery responses, even after the court imposed monetary sanctions. (Liberty Mutual Fire Ins. Co. v. LcL Administrations, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1093.) For example, in Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, the Court of Appeal held that five motions to compel created a record “replete with evidence of [appellant’s] failures to answer discovery requests.” (Ibid. at 279–280.)

 

III.   DISCUSSION

Defendant makes this motion on the grounds that Plaintiffs failed to comply with the Court’s October 14, 2024, order to provide discovery responses and pay sanctions. (Anderson Decl. ¶ 6; Exh. A [ruling].) Additionally, Defendant claims that Plaintiffs have “abandoned their case,” making dismissal appropriate pursuant to CCP § 2023. The Court notes that, due to a significant breakdown in the attorney-client relationship, Plaintiffs’ former counsel was relieved in November 2024. Plaintiffs have remained entirely unresponsive in this action since serving Defendant in October 2023.

“A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) Here, Plaintiffs did not comply with the Court’s October 14, 2024, order. Additionally, Plaintiffs have to date refused to participate in this litigation, indicating that less severe sanctions would fail to produce Plaintiffs’ compliance. Therefore, the Court finds terminating sanctions appropriate.

 

IV. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED. Plaintiffs’ operative complaint is dismissed without prejudice.

 LEGAL STANDARD

BACKGROUND



This action arises from a May 30, 2020, motor vehicle
accident in which Plaintiffs Rakeem Chadwick (“Rakeem”), Harlem Chadwick, a
minor (“Harlem”), Brooklynn Dixon, a minor (“Brooklynn”), by and through their
guardian ad litem Wonda Hale (“Petitioner”) allege that Defendant Victor
Delgado (“Defendant”) operated their motor vehicle in a negligent or reckless
manner as to collide with Plaintiff’s vehicle and cause injuries thereof. On
May 27, 2022, Plaintiffs brought suit alleging two causes of action: (1) motor
vehicle negligence; and (2) general negligence.



On June 5, 2024, this action was
reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys
Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.



On November 1, 2023, Petitioner filed
a notice of settlement.



On November 21, 2024, Petitioner filed the
instant Petitions to Confirm Minor’s Compromise of Claim or Action or
Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability on
behalf of Harlem and Brooklynn.



 



ANALYSIS



Petitioner
seeks court approval for a settlement under which Harlem and Brooklynn would each
receive $15,000.00 from Defendant.



 LEGAL
STANDARD



An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or
incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Cal. Prob.
Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 372.) For this
purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is
granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co.
(1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is
voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Ibid. at 1606.) 



 



Probate Code section 3600, et seq., governs how
the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to Probate Code section 3601,
the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as
follows: 



 



The court making the order or giving the
judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further
order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or
otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator),
costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall
be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the
benefit of the minor or incompetent person. (Prob. Code § 3601(a).) 



 



In cases pursuant to Probate Code section 3601,
“unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use
a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s
fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a
minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 7.955(a)(1).)
In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any
representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of
the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on
the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except
where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a
disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later
events.” (CRC Rule 7.955(a)(2).)



 



DISCUSSION



 With rare
exceptions, a child who is a party to a suit must appear through a guardian ad
litem or similar representative. (CCP § 372.) CCP § 372 represents a
recognition by the legislature that whenever a minor is involved in litigation,
the minor's rights cannot be protected unless a guardian ad litem or a similar
representative act for the minor. The guardian ad litem is an officer of the
court, and he or she has the right to control the lawsuit on the minor's
behalf. Among his or her powers are the right to compromise or settle the
action, to control the procedural steps incident to the conduct of the
litigation, and, with the approval of the court, to make stipulations or
concessions that are binding on the minor, provided they are not prejudicial to
the latter's interests. (In re Josiah Z. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 664, 678.)



 Here,
Petitioner filed applications to be approved as guardian ad litem (“GAL”) for
Harlem and Brooklynn on June 22, 2022. They were both rejected, and Petitioner
has failed to file another GAL application since. Therefore, Petitioner does
not have the authority to compromise Harlem and Brooklynn’s rights in the
instant action.  



CONCLUSION



 



Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES the
Petitions to confirm minor’s compromise.

RON A ROSEN JANFAZA vs omid khorshidi

 

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DISMISSAL

 

Date of Hearing:          December 18, 2024                Trial Date:       None

Department:               B                                              Case No.:         BC707602

 

Moving Party:             Defendant/Cross-Complainant Omid Korshidi

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Ron A. Rosen Janfaza

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 25, 2018, Plaintiff Ron A. Rosen Janfaza (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Omid Khorshidi (“Khorshidi”) and Does 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”), alleging six causes of action: (1) assault and battery; (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (3) negligent hiring, training, supervision and retention of employees; (4) negligence; (5) false imprisonment; and (6) violation of the Bane Civil Rights Act. The complaint alleges that, on May 26, 2016, Defendants violently beat Plaintiff, causing Plaintiff to sustain injuries and damages as a result.

 

On June 22, 2020, Khorshidi filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff and Roes 1 to 5 alleging two causes of action: (1) assault; and (2) battery. Khorshidi alleges that, at the time of the underlying incident, he was an attorney who had just appeared on behalf of multiple clients that had fired Plaintiff and whom had a pending fee dispute with Plaintiff. (Cross-Complaint ¶ 6.) Khorshidi alleges that Plaintiff threatened Khorshidi and struck him, causing Khorshidi to sustain injuries as a result. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 7 and 8.) On July 6, 2020, Khorshidi amended their cross-complaint to add a cause of action for violation of the Bane Civil Rights Act. On January 3, 2022, Khorshidi filed a third amended cross-complaint.

 

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On July 26, 2024, a jury trial was called for hearing. (See July 26, 2024, Minute Order.) Khorshidi did not appear at trial. (Ibid.) The Court denied Khorshidi’s request for a short trial continuance as he failed to show good cause. (Ibid.) Consequently, the Court dismissed Khorshidi’s cross-complaint without prejudice.

 

On September 16, 2024, Khorshidi filed the instant motion to set aside the dismissal.

 

On September 17, 2024, Khorshidi filed a notice of appeal from the dismissal.

 

On December 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition.

 

On December 11, 2024, Khorshidi filed their reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Section 473(b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief [from dismissal]. [Citation.]” (Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.) An application for relief under this section must be made no more than six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect of the moving party or its attorney. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) Additionally, an application for relief under this section “shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed herein, otherwise the application shall not be granted.” (CCP § 473(b).) Relief under this section is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Ibid.) When dismissal is the attorney’s fault, the six-month period starts to run from the date of the entry of dismissal. (CCP § 473(b); Sugasawara v. Newland (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 294, 295.)

DISCUSSION

Jurisdiction

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court addresses whether it retains jurisdiction to rule upon the instant motion after Khorshidi filed a notice of appeal on September 17, 2024.

 

“Generally, the filing of a notice of appeal ‘divests the trial court of further jurisdiction in the cause.’” (Avenida San Juan Partnership v. City of San Clemente (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1267.) “[T]he perfecting of an appeal stays proceedings in the trial court upon the judgment or order appealed from or upon the matters embraced therein or affected thereby, including enforcement of the judgment or order, but the trial court may proceed upon any other matter embraced in the action and not affected by the judgment or order.” (CCP § 916(a).) An appeal is perfected when the notice of appeal is filed. (Kroger Co. v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 952, 959.)

 

Here, Khorshidi filed their notice of appeal from dismissal on September 17, 2024. The notice of appeal and instant motion both seek relief from the same dismissal and, consequently, proceedings regarding relief from dismissal are stayed in this trial court pursuant to CCP § 916. Therefore, the trial court does not retain jurisdiction to rule upon the instant motion.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Omid Korshidi’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is DENIED as the Court lacks jurisdiction pursuant to CCP § 916.
