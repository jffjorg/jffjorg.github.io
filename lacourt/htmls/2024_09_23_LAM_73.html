DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCV05644    Hearing Date: September 23, 2024    Dept: 73

9/23/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

ABDOLMOHAMMADIYON v. REAL PROPERTY TRUSTEE, INC., et al. (24STCV05644)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Amy Mousavi (Mousavi Law, PC)

Counsel for Defendants Fidelity Recovery LLC and Jack Shut/moving party:  Gregory Bodell (Kozberg & Bodell LLP)

 

(1)   DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRERS TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT (filed 06/11/24)

(2)   DEFENDANT FIDELITY RECOVERY LLC’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF TO APPEAR FOR DEPOSITION AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 07/18/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s first and second causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s third, fourth and fifth causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 6, 2024, Plaintiff Matthew Abdolmohammadiyon filed this action against Defendants Real Property Trustee, Inc.; Fidelity Recovery, LLC; Olesya Shut and Jack Shut.

 

On May 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Wrongful Foreclosure – Invalid Deed of Trust

2.      Wrongful Foreclosure – Misrepresentation of Loan

3.      Fraud

4.      Accounting

5.      Conversion

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff owns the real property located at 1456 S. Bonnie Beach Place, Commerce, California 90023, legal description: LOT 13 IN BLOCK 4 OF TRACT NO. 4301, IN THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, STATE OF CALIFORNIA, AS PER MAP RECORDED IN BOOK 50 PAGES(S) 98 AND 99 OF MAPS, IN THE OFFICE OF THE COUNTY RECORDED OF SAID COUNTY, Assessor Parcel Number: 5241-022-002 (“Bonnie Property”). In December 2018, Plaintiff and Defendant Jack Shut (“Jack”) agreed that Jack would lend Plaintiff $100,000 as a hard money loan with ten percent (10%) interest and no maturity date. Jack requested Plaintiff sign a promissory note. However, the promissory note contained different terms not agreed upon, including that that the principal loaned was $120,000, not $100,000. On December 17, 2018, Plaintiff executed a Short Form Deed of Trust and Assignment of Rents (“Deed of Trust”) at the direction of Jack. However, there was no Notary Public present when Plaintiff signed the Deed of Trust. Plaintiff alleges the notary public’s signature was the product of fraud and that the Deed of Trust is invalid. Plaintiff alleges a dispute arose between Plaintiff and Jack. Then in or around January 2024, Jack closed his account so that Plaintiff could not deposit funds, and together with his wife and their companies, Fidelity and Property Trustee, Jack recorded a Notice of Default on the Bonnie Property. Plaintiff alleges he had been paying $1,000/month towards the loaned funds; however, the Defendants have not provided Plaintiff with any credit for the nearly $60,000 that Plaintiff has paid.

On June 11, 2024, Defendants Fidelity Recovery LLC and Jack Shut both filed Demurrers to the FAC. Both Demurrers argue the following:

·       The First Cause of Action for Wrongful Foreclosure (Invalid Deed of Trust) fails to state a cause of action because it does not allege a sale of the property in issue and a deed of trust need not be acknowledged to be enforceable

·       The Second Cause of Action for Wrongful Foreclosure (Misrepresentation of Loan) fails to state a cause of action because it also does not allege a sale of the property in issue and fails to include required allegations of the misrepresentations

·       The Third Cause of Action for Fraud fails to state a cause of action because it does not include the required specifics of any misrepresentation

·       The Fourth Cause of Action for Accounting fails to state a cause of action because it is not a cause of action but a remedy

·       The Fifth Cause of Action for Conversion fails to state a claim against Fidelity as it does not contain any charging allegations against Fidelity

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition to both Defendants’ Demurrers, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s causes of action are sufficiently pled

·       Plaintiff Has Plead Alter Ego Theory Against, Inter Alia, Fidelity and Jack Shut

 

Defendants filed a joint reply maintaining that Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action.

 

On July 18, 2024, Defendant Fidelity Recovery LLC filed a Motion to Compel Plaintiff to appear for deposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff served a notice for Jack’s deposition after Defendant Fidelity Recovery LLC (“Fidelity”) already had served a deposition notice of Plaintiff and set Jack’s deposition before the date that Fidelity had set Plaintiff’s deposition. The motion became necessary because Plaintiff refused to withdraw his underset deposition of Jack. Fidelity and Jack served an objection to the notice and filed a motion for protective order against his the taking of his deposition as noticed by Plaintiff, which motion is set to be heard on November 1, 2024 (the earliest date available on the Court’s regular calendar).

·       Appendix Rule 3A of the Los Angeles County Bar Association’s Rules of Civility and the decision of the California Supreme Court in Rosemont v. Superior Court (1964) 60 Cal.2d 709 (“Rosemont”) provide that Plaintiff is not allowed to underset Fidelity’s deposition of Plaintiff.

·       The Court thus is requested to (a) set Plaintiff’s deposition on a date certain and (b) rule that Jack’s deposition may not be taken before Plaintiff’s deposition. The Court should also impose monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and his attorneys.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition. Defendant filed a reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Defendants’ Demurrer

 

a.       Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

b.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests the Court take judicial notice of: The Transcript of Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application For A Temporary Restraining Order And Order To Show Cause Hearing of March 12, 2024.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)

 

The Court takes judicial notice of Plaintiff’s request.

 

c.       Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating that the parties exchanged several emails regarding the alleged defects in the FAC. (Bodell Decl, ¶ 2.) While the Court finds the moving party did not satisfy its obligation to meet and confer in person or by telephone, the failure to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5 subd. (a)(4).) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

d.      First and Second Causes of Action: Wrongful Foreclosure – Invalid Deed of Trust and Wrongful Foreclosure – Misrepresentation of Loan

 

Defendants demur to the first and second causes of action for Wrongful Foreclosure – Invalid Deed of Trust and Wrongful Foreclosure – Misrepresentation of Loan.

 

“The elements of a wrongful foreclosure cause of action are: (1) The trustee or mortgagee caused an illegal, fraudulent, or willfully oppressive sale of real property pursuant to a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust; (2) the party attacking the sale (usually but not always the trustor or mortgagor) was prejudiced or harmed; and (3) in cases where the trustor or mortgagor challenges the sale, the trustor or mortgagor tendered the amount of the secured indebtedness or was excused from tendering.”  (Citrus El Dorado, LLC v. Chicago Title Co. (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 943, 948, cleaned up.)

 

Defendants argue that the first and second causes of action fail because Plaintiff does not allege that the property in issue was sold, which is a required element of a wrongful foreclosure claim.

 

In Plaintiff’s opposition he does not dispute that the property has not been sold. Plaintiff contends that the foreclosure sale has been halted. However, Plaintiff states he should not have to wait until the foreclosure of his property to bring an action to stop the foreclosure. Plaintiff cites to Stockton v. Newman (1957) 148 Cal.App.2d 558, 564 in support. However, in Stockton, the court discussed granting a preliminary injunction to preclude defendants from selling property pursuant to purported notice of default under trust deed. The Stockton court does not support an assertion that a plaintiff can allege a wrongful foreclosure cause of action before the property has been sold. Therefore, since Plaintiff has not alleged a required element of a wrongful foreclosure cause of action, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action fail on this ground.

 

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC’s first and second causes of action with leave to amend.  

 

e.      Third Cause of Action: Fraud

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for fraud.

 

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

The FAC alleges the following. On or about December 2018, Plaintiff needed money and asked Jack to lend him the money. Jack agreed to lend Plaintiff $100,000 at 10%. However, Jack required that Plaintiff sign a Promissory Note in favor of Fidelity that reflected a $120,000 principal and 12% interest. Plaintiff informed Jack that the Promissory Note did not reflect their agreement. However, Jack informed Plaintiff that these were typographical errors that would not be controlling terms. Jack assured Plaintiff that he would only be required to pay the $100,000 principal at 10% interest but that he needed to sign the Promissory Note. A few days after signing the Promissory Note, Jack, through another company transferred $100,000 to Plaintiff. Plaintiff reasonably relied on these representations because Plaintiff and Jack have had a working relationship in the past. Further, Plaintiff relied on these representations because Jack only remitted the $100,000 principal once the Promissory Note was signed. The actual transfer supports Jack’s representations that their agreement was controlling. Plaintiff has been relying on those representations because he has been paying Jack, directly, the monthly payment of $1,000. Jack and Fidelity then used the Promissory Note and Deed of Trust (and the misrepresentations contained therein) to initiate foreclosure proceedings against the Bonnie Property. Jack and Fidelity knew, or should have known, that the representations they made to Property Trustee and other third parties about the validity of the Promissory Note and Deed of Trust were false, misleading, or suppressed the truth. (FAC, ¶¶ 85-98.)

 

The Court finds these allegations sufficiently state a cause of action for fraud against Jack. Defendants do not appear to dispute that Plaintiff has stated a cause of action for fraud against Defendant Jack. Instead in their motion, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not adequately pled fraud against Fidelity. Defendants argue that Plaintiff does not allege how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered by Fidelity.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that he has alleged that an alter ego relationship between Jack and Fidelity. The FAC alleges that Defendants Jack and Olesya “wholly control, and manipulate FIDELITY, its business affairs, its money, and that at all times herein mentioned, FIDELITY was and is a mere shell, instrumentally, and conduit through which OLESYA and JACK carried on business, exercising control and dominance of such businesses to such an extent that any separateness between OLESYA and JACK on the one hand, and FIDELITY on the other hand does not, and at all relevant times did not exist.” (FAC, ¶ 7.)

 

The Court finds these allegations sufficient to establish an alter ego relationship between Jack and Fidelity. In Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 235-36, the court stated, there is not a heightened pleading standard for alter ego theory; it is adequate for a plaintiff to allege “ultimate rather than evidentiary facts” and that “less particularity [of pleading] is required where the defendant may be assumed to possess knowledge of the facts at least equal, if not superior, to that possessed by the plaintiff, which certainly is the case” when analyzing alter ego theory at the pleading stage. Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged fraud against Jack and Fidelity.

 

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC’s third cause of action.

 

f.        Fourth Cause of Action: Accounting

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for accounting.

 

“A cause of action for an accounting requires a showing that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.  An action for accounting is not available where the plaintiff alleges the right to recover a sum certain or a sum that can be made certain by calculation.”  (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179, cleaned up.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff is not entitled to an accounting because Plaintiff has identified all of the payments that he made, thus, the money is easily identified.

 

However, Plaintiff argues that it is unclear as to how much money, if any, of the money that Plaintiff paid was directed to Fidelity. Plaintiff alleges he paid Jack $1,000 a month pursuant to the agreement between them two. (FAC, ¶ 44.) Yet, none of the Defendants have provided the requested accounting. (FAC, ¶¶ 38, 39, 45.) Plaintiff alleges Fidelity has refused to provide an accurate accounting of the payment Plaintiff has remitted to Jack. (FAC, ¶¶ 38, 39, 45.) Plaintiff asserts that Fidelity, through Property Trustee, recorded a Notice of Default implying that Fidelity received some money from Jack on behalf of Plaintiff. However, absent an accounting, it remains unclear as to how much money, if any, of the $60,000 Plaintiff paid was directed to Fidelity.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently pled a cause of action for accounting. The FAC alleges a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due that can only be ascertained by an accounting. 

 

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC’s fourth cause of action.

 

g.       Fifth Cause of Action Conversion

 

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for conversion against Fidelity.

 

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.”  (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed to allege conversion by Fidelity. Defendants argue that only Defendant Jack is alleged to have done anything regarding exercising wrongful dominion and control over Plaintiff’s property.

 

However, as discussed above, the Court finds that Plaintiff has alleged an alter ego relationship between Jack and Fidelity. Thus, the Court does not sustain Defendants’ demurrer on this ground.

 

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC’s fifth cause of action.

 

B.      Motion to Compel Deposition

 

Defendant Fidelity moves to compel the deposition of Plaintiff.

 

a.       Legal Standard for Motion to Compel Deposition

 

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) A party desiring to take an oral deposition shall give a notice in writing which states the specification of reasonably particularly of any materials to be produced by the deponent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.220, subd. (a)(4).) A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) The party served with a deposition notice waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).)  

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action … without having served a valid objection … fails to appear for examination, … or to produce for inspection any document, … described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document … described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)   

 

b.      Discussion

 

Here, on May 31, 2024, Fidelity noticed Plaintiff’s deposition to occur on June 25, 2024. (Def’s Exh. 5.) However, on June 19, 2024, Fidelity continued the date for Plaintiff’s deposition to July 17, 2024. (Def’s Exh. 6.) Fidelity did this because its counsel was engaged in a jury trial in Santa Monica and had not expected the trial to run so long. (Bodell Decl., ¶ 3.) On June 19, 2024, Plaintiff noticed the deposition of Defendant Jack Shut for July 15, 2024. (Def’s Exh. 7.) On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff served objections to Defendant’s deposition notice of Plaintiff. (Def’s Exh. 11.) Plaintiff did not appear for his duly noticed deposition on July 17, 2024. (Bodell Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff did not serve valid objections. Therefore, Defendant moves for an order compelling Plaintiff to attend deposition and rule that Jack’s deposition shall not proceed until after Plaintiff’s deposition.

 

However, the Court finds that Plaintiff served valid a objection to his deposition notice, thus warranting denial of Defendant’s motion to compel. Plaintiff objected to Defendant’s deposition notice on the following grounds:

1.      The date was unilaterally set without regard to the availability of the witness, parties or counsel;

2.      Notice of Deposition is in violation of CCP §2025.250(a);

3.      Propounding party is in violation of the Court’s Order; and

4.      The Notice of Deposition is not complete pursuant to CCP §2025.220.

 

A valid objection under Section 2025.410 involves an objection regarding procedural irregularities associated with a deposition notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.410 [“Any party served with a deposition notice that does not comply with Article 2 (commencing with Section 2025.210) waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity”], 2025.210, et seq. [proscribing various procedural requirements with respect to deposition notices, including, but not limited to, when a deposition notice may be served, the font size and contents of a deposition notice, and the permissible locations where the deposition may be conducted.)

 

First, the Court notes that Plaintiff’s first objection that the date was unilaterally set is not a valid objection because it was not made on grounds of procedural irregularities. On the other hand, Plaintiff’s second objection is made on the grounds that the location of Plaintiff’s deposition was further than the allotted distance under CCP §2025.250(a). Section 2025.250, subd. (a) provides that the deposition must occur within 75 miles of the deponent’s residence, or within the county where the action is pending and withing 150 miles of the deponent’s residence. (C.C.P. § 2025.250, subd. (a).) Here, Plaintiff’s deposition was noticed to occur in Los Angeles, California. (Def’s Exh. 5.) However, the parties dispute as to whether Plaintiff resides in Commerce, California or Florida. In a declaration submitted by Plaintiff in relation to another motion, Plaintiff had stated he alternatives living in California and Florida. (Motion, Decl. of Abdolmohammadiyon.) Nevertheless, the Court finds that Plaintiff had set forth a valid objection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a) [“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . ., without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination . . . , the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance”].) Since Plaintiff had served a valid objection, the Court finds that denial of Defendant’s motion to compel is warranted.

 

Based on this ground, Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is DENIED.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s first and second causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s third, fourth and fifth causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is DENIED.


Case Number: 24STCV08748    Hearing Date: September 23, 2024    Dept: 73

9/23/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

ILTF, INC. v. LAN VENTURE GROUP LLC (24STCV08748)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Raymond Brenneman (Law Office of Raymond Brenneman)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Kevin Spainhour (Retitlelaw, APC)

 

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT (filed 07/08/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint’s fourth and fifth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff ILTF, Inc. filed this action against Defendant Lan Venture Group LLC.

 

Plaintiff alleges causes of action for:

1.      Breach of Contract

2.      Service Mark Infringement

3.      False or Misleading Advertising

4.      Intentional Misrepresentation

5.      Negligent Misrepresentation

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff was organized in 2007 and distributes and sells frozen yogurt under the brand name, “Tutti Frutti”, via a network of exclusive, authorized independent retailers. Plaintiff contracts with independent retailers who agree to exclusively sell Tutti Frutti frozen yogurt products. Defendant entered into a Tutti Frutti Product Distribution Agreement dated July 24th, 2008 (the “Contract”), thus becoming an authorized Tutti Frutti frozen yogurt retailer. The wrongful sale by Defendant of non-Tutti Frutti frozen yogurt products while simultaneously holding themselves out to the public as a Tutti Frutti retailer and utilizing Plaintiff’s intellectual property and the Tutti Frutti brand’s goodwill and name recognition to its benefit has financially harmed Plaintiff and diluted the value of the Tutti Frutti brand.

On July 8, 2024, Defendant filed a Demurrer to the Complaint, arguing:

·       Each of Plaintiff’s causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action

·       First cause of action for breach of contract fails because:

o   The Contract is illegal and Void because Plaintiff was not a Licensed Franchisor. The Court will not enforce an Illegal Contract.

·       Second and third causes of action fail because the Complaint contradicts the express language of the Contract and it is evident that Lan has been authorized to use the service marks since 2009. It has therefore not falsely advertised Tutti-Frutti frozen yogurt.

·       Plaintiff’s fraud claims all fail because Plaintiff does not allege fraud with the required specificity.

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Judicial Notice is improper

·       Nevertheless, the Contract is not illegal and void. The Consent Order did not invalidate or revoke existing agreements between ILTF and Tutti Frutti store operators. Thus, Plaintiff has pled breach of contract

·       Defendant has no right to use Plaintiff’s service marks. The contract only allows service marks to promote authorized Tutti Frutti frozen yogurt. Defendant violated this by using unauthorized frozen yogurt

·       Plaintiff’s fraud claims are sufficiently pled. Plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts

·       Leave to amend should be granted

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action.

·       The putative contract is for the sale of a franchise and Plaintiff was not licensed to sell franchises in California when the contract was executed or at any time ever.

·       As to Plaintiff’s fraud claims, Plaintiff does not plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

B.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of the following:

1.      Consent Order dated February 27, 2019 executed by Mary Ann Smith, Deputy Commissioner to Jan Lynn Owen, Commissioner of Business Oversight

2.      Tutti Frutti Franchising, Inc., Franchise Disclosure Document

3.      Statement in support of Stop Order denying Effectiveness of Franchise Registration dated August 30, 2021

4.      Letter from Holmes Lofstrom dated August 23, 2019 to Department of Business Oversight

5.      History of ILTF, Inc., that Defendant downloaded from the Secretary of State’s website.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)

 

Therefore, the Court takes judicial notice of Defendant’s requests 1-5. However, although the Court may take judicial notice of the documents, the documents are only judicially noticeable to show their existence and what orders were made. The facts and findings within the documents are not judicially noticeable. (See Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 885.)

 

C.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

On June 7, 2024, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration in support of automatic extension to file its demurrer, stating that the parties had begun the meet and confer process but had not concluded it yet. (Spainhour Decl.) However, the Court notes that it appears Defendant did not attach a meet and confer declaration to its Demurrer. Thus, while the Court finds the moving party did not satisfy its obligation, the failure to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5 subd. (a)(4).) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

D.     First Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

 

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) “The terms of a contract are reasonably certain if they provide a basis for determining the existence of a breach and for giving an appropriate remedy.” (Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 777.) “Where a contract is so uncertain and indefinite that the intention of the parties in material particulars cannot be ascertained, the contract is void and unenforceable.” (Ibid., internal quotations omitted.)

 

The Complaint alleges that on or about July 24, 2008, the parties entered into a written Tutti Frutti Product Distribution Agreement (the “Contract”). Plaintiff attached a copy of the Contract to the Complaint. Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant failed and refused, and continue to refuse, to tender their performance as required by the Contract in that they sold frozen yogurt made from frozen yogurt mix acquired from Plaintiff’s direct competitors.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s claim fails because the Contract is illegal and void because Plaintiff was not a licensed franchisor. In support, Defendant cites to the Consent Order dated February 27, 2019, ordering Plaintiff to “desist and refrain from the further offer or sale of franchises in violation of Corporations Code section 31110.” (RJN Exhibit #1, Page 3.) Defendant contends that the Consent Order states that Plaintiff unlawfully sold Tutti Frutti franchise investments in California beginning in 2007 to 2016. (RJN Exhibit #1, Pages 2-3.) However, as discussed above, while the Court may take judicial notice of the Consent Order, it is only judicially noticeable to show their existence and what orders were made. The facts and findings within the documents are not judicially noticeable. (See Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 885.) Thus, the Court takes judicial notice only of the order that was made and not as to any of the facts and findings, such as whether Plaintiff unlawfully sold franchise investments in 2007 to 2016.

 

According to the Consent Order, Plaintiff was ordered to “desist and refrain from the further offer or sale of franchises in violation of Corporations Code section 31110” and “to make an offer of rescission of all franchise agreements entered into with the franchisees identified in Schedule B.” (RJN Exhibit #1, Pages 3-6 [emphasis added].) Therefore, the Consent Order did not invalidate or revoke existing franchise agreements between Plaintiff and the Tutti Frutti store operators. Thus, the Court does not find that the parties’ Contract is illegal and void.

 

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action.

 

E.      Second and Third Causes of Action: Service Mark Infringement and False or Misleading Advertising

 

Defendant demurs to the second and third causes of action for Service Mark Infringement and False or Misleading Advertising.

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendant has, without Plaintiff’s consent, used Plaintiff’s Service Marks in advertising, marketing, inside and outside store signage, and product branding of frozen yogurt that Defendant has sold, and continues to sell, in California and has provided California consumers with yogurt cups, receipts and other materials that incorporate these Service Marks. (Complaint, ¶ 24.) The Complaint further alleges that for several years, and continuing to the date of this complaint, Defendants have advertised their store location as a retailer of Tutti Frutti-brand frozen yogurt. This advertising consists of storefront signage, social media postings, and internet advertising. These advertisements were and are disseminated to the public in California. (Complaint, ¶ 31.) Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s advertising was untrue or misleading, and likely to deceive the public, in that: the frozen yogurt sold by Defendant is not Tutti Frutti-brand frozen yogurt but is, instead, a counterfeit of unknown origin, quality, or safety. (Complaint, ¶ 33.)

 

Defendant argues these causes of action fail because the Contract expressly allows Defendant to use Plaintiff’s service marks. Defendant relies on Paragraph 6 of the Contract titled Use of Trademarks and Tradenames, Intellectual Property Rights, which provides in relevant part: “During the term of this Agreement, Distributor is authorized by Supplier to use the Trademarks solely in connection with distributor’s promotion of Products...” (Complaint, Ex. B, p. 2.)

 

However, as Plaintiff points out, the Contract specifies that Defendant is authorized by Plaintiff to use the Trademarks solely in connection with Defendant’s promotion of frozen yogurt products approved by Plaintiff. Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant used Plaintiff’s trademark and service marks to promote products not authorized by Plaintiff, in violation of the parties’ Contract. Therefore, the Court does not find Defendant’s argument persuasive.  

 

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendant’s demurrer to the second and third causes of action.

 

F.       Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action: Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation

 

Defendant demurs to the fourth and fifth causes of action for Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation.

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation include “[m]isrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, and with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented; ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation by the party to whom it was directed; and resulting damage.”  (Hydro-Mill Co., Inc. v. Hayward, Tilton & Rolapp Ins. Associates, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1154, cleaned up.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) 

 

“California courts have recognized a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, i.e., a duty to communicate accurate information, in two circumstances.  The first situation arises where providing false information poses a risk of and results in physical harm to person or property.  The second situation arises where information is conveyed in a commercial setting for a business purpose.”  (Friedman v. Merck & Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 454, 477.)

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.  (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Defendant represented to Plaintiff that they would use their best efforts to sell only Tutti Frutti-brand frozen yogurt and that they would not sell any competing frozen yogurt products in their store. (Complaint, ¶ 38.) Defendant made the representations with the intention of inducing Plaintiff to enter into, and later maintain, the Contract, thereby permitting them the use and benefit of Plaintiff’s goodwill and intellectual property, including, but not limited to, the Service Marks. (Complaint, ¶ 39.) When Defendant made the alleged representations, they had no reasonable grounds for believing the representations to be true. (Complaint, ¶ 49.)

 

The Court does not find that Plaintiff alleged sufficient facts to state either cause of action. Plaintiff vaguely alleges representations were made, but does not specify what was said/represented, by whom, or when. In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice. (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 74.) The Court does not find Plaintiff has pled its fourth or fifth causes of action with the required specificity.

 

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the fourth and fifth causes of action with leave to amend.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint’s fourth and fifth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.
