DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV01065    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: S27

This case is set for trial on 4/08/24, which is only six days after the hearing on this motion.  CCP §2024.020(a) makes clear that all discovery motions must be heard at least fifteen days prior to trial.  The motion is summarily denied because of its proximity to the trial date. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative.  If the parties do not submit on the tentative, they should arrange to appear remotely.


Case Number: 23CMCV00946    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Complaint

Plaintiff, San Tropez Homeowners Association filed this action against Defendants, PAMC Services, Inc., LBCM, Inc., and Christina McCandless for conversion, breach of confidential relationship, negligence, and unfair competition.  Plaintiff alleges it entered into a management agreement with a non-party, who assigned its rights and obligations under the agreement to LBCM.  LBCM later assigned its rights and obligations to PAMC.  Plaintiff alleges LBCM, PAMC, and its principal, McCandless, breached various duties arising under the management agreement. 

 

2.     Cross-Complaint

On 11/06/23, LBCM filed a cross-complaint against McCandless, PAMC, and Principle Association Management, Inc.  Plaintiff’s cross-complaint includes claims for express indemnification (against PAMC), equitable indemnification (against all cross-defendants), equitable indemnification (against all cross-defendants), and declaratory relief (against all cross-defendants).  As it relates to Moving Cross-Defendant, Principle, the cross-complaint alleges Principle failed to use reasonable care in managing the Material Contract with Plaintiff.  At ¶10, LBCM allege the “material contract” was between LBCM and Plaintiff (San Tropez). 

 

3.     Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Cross-Defendant filed a meet and confer declaration with the demurrer.  The meet and confer declaration is sufficient, and the Court will rule on the demurrer on its merits.

 

c.     Request for Judicial Notice

Principle seeks judicial notice of the complaints in this action and the related actions.  The RJN is granted.

 

d.     Analysis

Principle correctly notes, in its demurrer, that the crux of the cross-complaint is that Principle should indemnify LBCM in connection with the breach of the “material agreement,” which ran between LBCM and San Tropez.  Principle correctly notes that it was not a party to the material agreement, and therefore had no duties under the agreement. 

 

LBCM, in opposition to the demurrer, argues it is being sued by San Tropez in tort, and is seeking indemnification based on those tort claims.  Principle, in reply, correctly notes that none of the allegations in the cross-complaint relate to tort claims.  At ¶¶15 and 16, LBCM alleges Principle is liable for failure to use reasonable care in managing the material contract; again, Principle is not a party to the material contract.  ¶¶21 and 22 contain the same allegations, and ¶¶25 and 26 allege declaratory relief is necessary for the reasons set forth above.  Those are the only paragraphs in the cross-complaint that reference Principle.

 

Because the cross-complaint alleges solely that Principle must indemnify LBCM for its role in “managing the material contract,” and because Principle was not a party to the material contract, the demurrer is sustained.  Leave to amend is granted if and only if LBCM can allege some non-contractual basis for its claims against Principle. 

 

If LBCM wishes to amend its cross-complaint, it must do so within twenty days.  Principle must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.

Case Number: 23LBCV00223    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Robert Joshua Rodrigo filed 23LBCV00223 against Defendants, Miguel Angel Gonzales, Mission Fleet Services, LLC, and Mike Gonzalez for damages arising out of an automobile accident.  The accident occurred on 4/02/21, and Plaintiff filed the complaint on 2/06/23.    

 

Plaintiff, American Family Connect Property and Casualty Ins Co filed 23LBCV01170 against Defendants, Michael Gonzalez, Rosario Gonzalez, Miguel Angel Gonzalez, Mission Fleet Services, LLC, and Robert Joshua Rodrigo for declaratory relief.  American Family alleges the Gonzalez family removed the subject vehicle from their insurance policy prior to the collision, then attempted to add the vehicle back to the policy retroactively a few days after the collision, fraudulently declaring that the vehicle had sustained no damage during the period of time when it was not insured.  American Family seeks a declaration that it does not owe coverage for the subject accident and that it is entitled to an award of its fees and costs expended in connection with the litigation concerning the accident. 

 

Both cases have been assigned, since their inception, to Department S27. 

 

2.     Motion to Consolidate

a.     Parties’ Positions

Plaintiff seeks an order consolidating the two actions for all purposes, contending the two actions arise out of the same automobile accident. 

 

Defendants filed timely opposition to the motion.  They contend the two actions do not require resolution of overlapping issues of fact or law, such that consolidation is not appropriate.  They argue they will be prejudiced by consolidation, because suggesting Defendants were not insured could prejudice the jury against them in the collision case. 

 

Any reply to the opposition was due on or before 3/26/24.  The Court has not received timely opposition papers and will not consider late-filed reply papers. 

 

b.     Law Governing Consolidation

CCP §1048 grants discretion to the trial courts to consolidate actions involving common questions of law or fact.  The trial court's decision will not be disturbed on appeal absent a clear showing of abuse of discretion.  (Feliner v. Steinbaum (1955) 132 Cal.App.2d 509, 511.)  A consolidation of actions does not affect the rights of the parties.  The purpose of consolidation is to avoid unnecessary costs or delay, avoid duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both action, and avoid inconsistent results by hearing and deciding common issues together.  (See Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485.)  Each case presents its own facts and circumstances, but the court generally considers the following: (1) timeliness of the motion: i.e., whether granting consolidation would delay the trial of any of the cases involved; (2) complexity: i.e., whether joining the actions involved would make the trial too confusing or complex for a jury; and (3) prejudice: i.e, whether consolidation would adversely affect the rights of any party. (See State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1956) 47 Cal.2d 428, 430–431.)  In deciding whether to grant a motion to consolidate, the court should weigh whether the common issues predominate over the individual issues and whether any risks of jury confusion or prejudice to the parties outweighs the reduction in time and expense that would result from consolidation. (Todd-Stenberg v. Shield (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978.) 

 

Consolidation is a procedure for uniting separate lawsuits for trial, where they involve common questions of law or fact and are pending in the same court.  (See CCP § 1048.)  The purpose is to enhance trial court efficiency (i.e., to avoid unnecessary duplication of evidence and procedures); and to avoid the substantial danger of inconsistent adjudications (i.e., different results because tried before different juries, or a judge and jury, etc.).  (See Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978–979.) 

 

A consolidation of actions does not affect the rights of the parties.  The purpose of consolidation is merely to promote trial convenience and economy by avoiding duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both actions.  (See Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485.)  In deciding whether to grant a motion to consolidate, the court should weigh whether the common issues predominate over the individual issues and whether any risks of jury confusion or prejudice to the parties outweighs the reduction in time and expense that would result from consolidation. (Todd-Stenberg v. Shield (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978.) 

 

c.     Analysis

The Court finds there are insufficient overlapping issues of fact or law to support consolidating the two actions.  The issue in 23LBCV00223 is who caused the automobile accident.  The issue in 23LBCV01170 is whether the Gonzalezes were insured at the time of the accident.  Neither the issue nor the evidence will overlap, and therefore the motion to consolidate is denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV00357    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Young Pil filed this action against Defendant, General Motors, LLC for violations of the Song-Beverly Act.  The parties entered into a settlement, pursuant to which Defendant would pay Plaintiff $30,000, a stipulation that Plaintiff can keep the vehicle through the end of the lease period, and an agreement that Plaintiff is entitled to attorneys’ fees and costs. 

 

2.     Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and Costs 

a.     Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiff, with his moving papers, seeks judicial notice of various other trial court lemon law decisions on motions for attorneys’ fees.  The purpose of the RJN is to show that other trial courts have awarded similar billing rates to attorneys with similar experience.  The RJN is granted.

 

b.     “Canned” Work

The majority of Defendant’s argument in opposition to the motion is that Plaintiff is billing an excessive number of hours for what is essentially “canned” work – work that is repeated in connection with every lemon law case litigated.  The Court tends to agree.  Defendant has shown, at pages 7-10 of the opposition brief, that the work done in this case is substantially the same as the work Plaintiff’s attorney does in every lemon law case.  The Court will not repeat the argument or showing in this ruling, but agrees that the billing is excessive.  The Court is inclined to reduce the billing by 1/3 in light of its finding that the billing is padded and redundant with other cases. 

 

c.     Pre-Engagement Work

Defendant argues time prior to attorney engagement is not compensable, citing Dominguez v. American Suzuki Motor Corp. (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 53, 58 in support of its position.  Plaintiff correctly notes in reply that Defendant is misconstruing the holding of Dominguez.  The crux of the holding in Dominguez was that a potential plaintiff in a lemon law suit is not entitled to fees if the defendant settles the case prior to filing.  This is in keeping with the purpose of the lemon law statutes, which is to encourage a car manufacturer, when faced with a meritorious claim, to buy back the vehicle without the need for litigation.  The Court therefore finds the pre-engagement time was also a component of the fees expended, and will not reduce that time to zero, as suggested by Defendant. 

 

d.     Motion to Compel

Defendant also argues Plaintiff’s time spent drafting a motion to compel should be reduced to zero because the case settled prior to the hearing on the motion.  Plaintiff, in reply, shows that the case settled AFTER the motion was filed; indeed, Defendant made its settlement offer after the motion was filed.  The Court will therefore also not reduce this time to zero.

e.     Costs

Plaintiff seeks to recover $900.59 in costs, and Defendant challenges $173.11 of those costs.  Costs are recoverable by way of a memorandum of costs, and any challenge must be made by way of a motion to strike or tax costs.  CRC 3.1700.  Plaintiff has set forth no authority permitting it to recover costs via noticed motion, and the Court knows of none.  The request for costs is therefore denied.  The ruling is without prejudice to Plaintiff’s right to seek recovery of costs in a manner permitted by the Rules of Court. 

 

f.      Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motion is granted, but the requested $41,350.59 in fees is reduced by 1/3, and Plaintiff is awarded $28,026.51 in fees.  Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV01779    Hearing Date: April 2, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Complaint

Plaintiff, Clara Castillo filed this action against Defendants, LunaSol Holdings, LLC and Weststar Property Management, Inc. for negligence and premises liability arising out of injuries she sustained when a sliding glass door shattered.

 

2.     Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel filed a meet and confer declaration with the demurrer.  In the declaration, he declares the attorneys met and conferred by email.  §430.41(a) requires meet and confer to be by phone or in person.  The Court finds further meet and confer would not likely resolve the parties’ issues, but asks Counsel to comply with the “by phone or in person” language in the statute in the future in connection with this and other actions. 

 

c.     Timeliness of Demurrer

Plaintiff filed proof of personal service on Defendant on 10/11/23.  The POS indicates Defendant was personally served on 10/10/23.  Defendant filed its demurrer on 12/08/2, almost two months later, and after the time to do so had expired per CCP §430.40(a).

 

Pursuant to Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742, 750, the Court has discretion to consider a late-filed demurrer.  The Court will do so at this time for two reasons.  First, Defendant, in reply, shows that Plaintiff did not timely respond to attempts to meet and confer, which protracted the time to file the demurrer.  Second, and more importantly, if the Court were to overrule the demurrer on the sole ground that it was not timely filed, this would likely result in Defendant filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings and raising the same arguments articulated in the demurrer, which would merely protract the litigation timeline.  It is not in the interest of judicial economy to leave the issues posed by the demurrer unsettled pending the filing and hearing of a motion for judgment on the pleadings. 

 

d.     Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant seeks judicial notice of various official records showing it purchased the subject property on 9/29/21, three days after the incident occurred.  Plaintiff argues the Court can take judicial notice of the fact that the records are what they purport to be, but not of the truth of the facts found in the documents. 

 

Two cases discussing whether and when the contents of a website can be the proper subject of judicial notice are Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 887-890 and Scott v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 743, 752-753, 760.  In Jolley, a purchase agreement between the FDIC and a bank was posted on the FDIC website, but the Court of Appeals held that the facts in the agreement were not the proper subject of judicial notice; the Court specifically noted that the party opposing judicial notice had testified that the proffered agreement was not the full agreement the parties had entered into, and a longer version with different terms also existed. 

 

In Scott, the Court of Appeals held that various documents on the FDIC website WERE the proper subject of judicial notice, noting that there was no dispute that the copy of the agreement on the website was the actual agreement, and that there was no dispute concerning the legal effect of the document. 

 

The Court is inclined to grant the request for judicial notice.  The request includes four different documents, specifically the subject grant deed, the assessor’s office website showing the date of sale, the property report showing the date of sale, and the final closing statement showing the date of sale.  All four of the documents show the same date of sale.  It does not appear that the date of sale is reasonably in dispute.  Notably, Plaintiff does not allege that the documents are incorrect.  If Plaintiff wishes to allege specific facts showing the date is incorrect, the Court will grant her leave to do so (as discussed fully below). 

 

e.     Merits

Plaintiff argues that, even if the RJN is granted and the Court accepts that Defendant purchased the property three days after the subject incident, the Court should overrule the demurrer for two reasons. 

 

First, she argues the complaint alleges the defendants owned, operated, maintained, and/or managed the property, and Defendant has not negated the possibility that, even though it did not own the property, it may have operated, maintained, and/or managed the property.  Plaintiff’s argument is nonsensical.  All documents attached to the RJN show that Defendant purchased the property three days after the subject incident.  The complaint is devoid of a specific factual allegation showing that Defendant had some sort of management or other role in operating the property prior to purchasing the property.  Pursuant to Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-550, a mere boilerplate pleading is not sufficient when the allegation at issue is implausible, and essential facts supporting otherwise implausible allegations must be pled. 

 

Thus, if Plaintiff wishes to base her claims against Defendant on the allegation that it maintained, managed, or operated the premises PRIOR TO purchasing the premises, Plaintiff must plead specific facts supporting this conclusion.

 

Plaintiff’s second argument is that Defendant may have assumed the seller’s liabilities during the purchase of the property.  As Defendant correctly notes in reply, a claim for premises liability can only be asserted against someone who owns, maintains, manages, or operates the property; control of the property at the time of the incident is the key determining factor.  Any agreement to assume liabilities would run between Defendant and the seller, but would have nothing to do with Plaintiff’s ability to assert a claim. 

 

f.      Conclusion

The demurrer is sustained.  Defendant showed that it purchased the subject property three days after the incident, such that it is not liable for negligence or premises liability.  Plaintiff failed to articulate any reason why the complaint should be permitted to stand.

 

Leave to amend is granted if and only if Plaintiff can plead specific facts showing that either (a) something about the otherwise judicially noticeable documents is incorrect, and/or (b) Defendant managed, maintained, or operated the property prior to purchase.  If Plaintiff cannot plead specific facts showing either of the above, then she must not amend her complaint as against this demurring defendant.
