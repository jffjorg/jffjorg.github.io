DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV43562    Hearing Date: January 24, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Wednesday, January 24, 2023

CASE NUMBER:                   19STCV43562

CASE NAME:                        Iris Echeverria, et al. v. Kia Motors America Inc.

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Kia Motors America Inc. 

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiffs Iris Echeverria and Carlos Castano

TRIAL DATE:                        Post Judgment

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion to Strike Costs

OPPOSITION:                        22 October 2023

REPLY:                                  28 October 2023

 

TENTATIVE:                         Defendant Kia’s Motion to Strike is denied. Defendant Kia’s Motion to Tax Costs is granted in part and $192.02 will be taxed from Item 13.

                                                                                               

 

Background

 

on December 4, 2019, Iris Echeverria and Carlos Castano (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Kia Motors America (“Kia” or “Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10. The Complaint alleged three causes of action related to violations of the Song-Beverly Act: (1) breach of express warranty, (2) breach of implied warranty, and (3) breach of Civil Code § 1793.2.

 

After a trial that began August 14, 2023, the jury rendered a verdict on August 31, 2023. The jury found in favor of the Plaintiffs as to the first and third causes of action, but not the second cause of action. (8/31/23 Verdict.) The jury awarded Plaintiff damages in the total sum of $149,435.80. 

On September 15, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a memorandum of costs seeking to recover $65,003.69 in costs. On October 3, 2023, Defendant Kia filed a Motion to Tax Costs. Plaintiffs oppose the motion. The matter is now before the court.

motion to tax costs

I.         Legal Standard

 

Under the Song-Beverly Act, the prevailing party can recover “costs and expenses . . . based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794(d).)  

 

Assuming the “prevailing party” requirements are met, the trial court has no discretion to order each party to bear his or her own costs of suit.¿ (Michell v. Olick (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1194, 1198; Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 129.) 

 

After judgment is entered, the prevailing party “who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment or dismissal, or within 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a).) “The memorandum of costs must be verified by a statement of the party, attorney, or agent that to the best of his or her knowledge the items of cost are correct and were necessarily incurred in this case.” (Id.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

In turn, the losing party may file a motion to strike or tax costs. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b).) Procedurally, “[a]ny notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum.” (Id.) “Unless the objection is made to the entire cost memorandum, the motion to strike or tax costs must refer to each item objected to by the same number and appear in the same order as the corresponding cost item claimed on the memorandum of costs and must state why the item is objectionable.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(2).) “Allowable costs shall be¿reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (CCP § 1033.5(c)(2).)¿¿ 

 

II.        Discussion

 

The Plaintiffs’ Memorandum of Costs seeks to recover $65,0003.69 in costs. Defendant Kia now moves to strike the Memorandum of Costs or tax $60,051.00 in costs as follows:

 

1)     $199.70 in Filing and Motion fees, claimed under Item 1;

2)     $3,262.15 in Deposition costs, claimed under Item 4;

3)     $2,004.28 in Service of Process costs, claimed under Item 5;

4)     $70.00 in Ordinary Witness fees, claimed under Item 8(a);

5)     $21,228.36 in Expert fees, claimed under Item 8(b);

6)     $1,474.27 in Models, Blowups, and Photocopies of Exhibits, claimed under Item 11;

7)     $17,145.00 in Court Reporter fees, claimed under Item 12; and

8)     $14,667.24 in “Other” costs, claimed under Item 13.

 

Defendant Kia argues that the Plaintiffs’ cost should be stricken because the costs are excessive and not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation. (CCP § 1033.5(c)(2).

 

A verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services therein listed were necessarily incurred. (See Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Serv., Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266 (Rappenecker).) A party seeking to tax costs must provide evidence to rebut this prima facie showing.  (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1266.) Mere statements unsupported by facts are insufficient to rebut the prima facie showing that costs were necessarily incurred. (Ibid.) “If the items in a cost memorandum appear proper, the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence the expenses were necessarily incurred by the defendant. The burden of showing an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable falls on the objector.” (Benach v. County of Los Angeles (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 836, 858.) Therefore, the burden is on Defendant Kia to show that Plaintiffs’ costs were not reasonably incurred.

 

A.        Item 1: Filing and Motion Fees

 

Defendant Kia objects to $199.70 in costs incurred relating to filing and motion fees because the fees were unnecessarily incurred. Defendant Kia alleges that Plaintiffs filed and “downloaded” various matters including an ex parte motion and other motions that did not advance the litigation such that all filing fees should be stricken except the $435.00 fee for filing the initial complaint.

 

Plaintiffs as the prevailing parties are entitled to recover costs, including but not limited to filing fees. (CCP § 1033.5(a)(1).) Defendant Kia fails to point to any case law or statute that limits Plaintiffs’ fee recovery to only those arguments or motions that were successfully argued or granted. (See Sundance v. Municipal Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 268, 273 [“[A] a practical matter, it is impossible for an attorney to determine before starting work on a potentially meritorious legal theory whether it will or will not be accepted by a court years later following litigation”].)

 

Therefore, filing and motion fees will not be taxed.

B.        Item 4: Deposition Costs

 

Defendant Kia seeks to tax $3,262.15 in deposition costs on the basis that only Plaintiff’s deposition, the person most knowledgeable of Kia of Downtown LA, the person most knowledgeable of Kia, and one deposition of Kia’s expert, Albert Perez, were the only depositions necessary in this action. Defendant Kia argues that there was no need to take the deposition of the dealership personnel and service advisors because they kept contemporaneous records in their repair orders. Therefore, Kia argues that deposition costs should be taxed.

 

Plaintiffs assert that Defendant Kia refused to stipulate to the admissibility of the repair orders at trial so the depositions were necessary to make them admissible at trial. (Devabose Decl. ¶ 6, Ex. C.) Plaintiffs also state it was necessary to depose personnel who worked on the subject vehicle and have the transcripts to prepare for trial as the personnel could be called by either party to testify at trial or to rebut expert testimony. Furthermore, deposition costs are a permitted cost. (CCP § 1033.5(a)(3).) Plaintiffs also provide copies of their internal spreadsheet and relevant invoices to collaborate these costs and show their reasonableness. (Devabose Decl. ¶¶ 4-8, Ex. A-e.)

 

The court finds the deposition costs were reasonably incurred and will not be taxed.

 

C.        Item 5: Service of Process Costs

 

Defendant Kia similarly asserts that $2,004.28 in service of process costs were not reasonably incurred because the depositions of various Kia personnel were not necessarily incurred. However, as stated above, the court found that the deposition costs were necessarily incurred; then so too were the service of process costs to serve the deposition notices.

 

Accordingly, Item 5 will not be taxed.

 

D.        Item 8: Ordinary and Expert Fees

 

Defendant Kia objects to the $70.00 paid in ordinary witness fees to Frank Gonzalez, “who no longer works for Kia Downtown LA,” because Plaintiff failed to provide receipts. However, a verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence and Defendant has failed to rebut this evidence and show that the costs were not reasonably incurred. (Rappenecker, supra, 93 Cal.App.3d at p. 266.) Therefore, ordinary witness fees will not be taxed.

 

Defendant Kia argues that the Plaintiffs’ case did not require an expert and that expert fees based on 53 hours of work, at varying rates, are unreasonable and excessive. Defendant Kia also argues that expert fees are not an allowable cost except when authorized by law. (CCP § 1033.5(b)(1).)

Plaintiffs point out that expert witness fees are recoverable under the Song-Beverly Act even though they are not recoverable under CCP § 1033.5.

 

The Legislature intended the word ‘expenses’ to cover items not included in the detailed statutory definition of ‘costs,’ and the legislative history of the statute indicates that the Legislature exercised its power to determine selectively the types of actions and circumstances in which expert witness fees should be recoverable as costs so as to permit the recovery of expert witness fees by prevailing buyers under the [Song-Beverly Act].


(Jensen v. BMW of North America, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112, 119 (Jensen).)

 

Plaintiffs further assert that their expert witness fees were reasonably incurred because Anthony Micale's fees include time spent reviewing documents to prepare for the vehicle inspection, traveling to the vehicle inspection, preparing to discuss the vehicle inspection, repair orders, and preparing for trial. Plaintiffs include a spreadsheet of the fees incurred by Mr. Micale in this matter and Mr. Micale’s invoices. (Devabose Decl. ¶¶ 4-5, Ex. A-B.) Plaintiffs also explain that Mr. Micale’s fees vary because his rate varies depending on the task. Mr. Micale’s expertise was also necessary to determine the subject vehicle’s defect, the existence of those defects, what Kia knew, and how Kia chose to respond to the Plaintiffs’ demands under the Song-Beverly Act.

As Defendant Kia fails to show that the expert witness costs were unreasonably incurred, they will not be taxed.

 

E.        Item 11: Models, Blowups, and Photocopies of Exhibit Costs

Defendant Kia argues that $1,474.27 should be taxed from Item 11 because the costs were not reasonably incurred since Plaintiffs failed to provide any receipts and how each item was used at trial to aid the jury.

In opposition, the Plaintiffs provide invoices for the costs in Item 11. (Devabose Decl. ¶ 7, Ex. D.) The court finds that Defendant Kia has failed to meet its burden of showing that the costs in Item 11 were not reasonably incurred.

Therefore, Item 11 will not be taxed.

F.        Item 12: Court Reporter Fees

 

Defendant Kia states that $17,145.00 in court reporter costs were excessive because under the Los Angeles Superior Court fee schedule, court reporters receive $764.00 for proceeding over 4 hours and $382.00 for proceedings between 1 and 4 hours, and a per diem of $30.00 for proceedings under 1 hour. Defendant Kia states that none of the hearings in this case lasted more than one hour and that court transcripts not ordered by the court are not allowable costs. (CCP, § 1033.5(b)(5).) Therefore, court reporter fees should be taxed.

 

Court reporter fees are an allowable cost under the CCP § 1033.5(a)(11). “Any allowable costs must be ‘reasonably¿necessary¿to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation,’ and reasonable in amount.¿(CCP § 1033.5(c)(2), (3).)” (Bender v. County of Los Angeles¿(2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 968, 990.) Plaintiffs assert that the court reporter costs were necessary in case there was a dispute between the parties as to what occurred at said proceedings and as evidence for later motions. Second, Defendant Kia signed and acquiesced to the use of a court reporter at the hearings. Third, the court does not automatically provide a court reporter for every hearing and does not guarantee their availability such that the Plaintiffs needed to retain independent court reporters to transcribe the proceedings leading up to and through trial. The Plaintiffs also provide a spreadsheet detailing the costs for the court reporter fees and the supporting invoices. (Devabose Decl. ¶ 8, Ex. E.)

 

As explained above, the Song-Beverly Act and not CCP § 1033.5 governs this action. (Jensen, supra, 35 Cal.App.4th at p. 119.) The Song-Beverly Act permits the recovery of “costs and expenses . . . based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794(d).) Here, Defendant Kia fails to show that the court reporter costs and transcripts were not reasonably incurred and that certain hearings did not require a court reporter. Moreover, Defendant Kia signed and acquiesced to the use of a court reporter such that Kia is barred from arguing only the court’s minute order should have been used to ascertain what was said at the hearings. Furthermore, Defendant Kia fails to cite case law to show that privately engaged court reporters must charge fees pursuant to the Los Angeles Superior Court fee schedule for court reporter services for the fees to be reasonable.

 

As Defendant Kia has failed to show that the court reporter fees were not reasonably incurred, Item 12 will not be taxed.

 

G.        Item 13: Other Costs

 

Defendant Kia seeks to strike $14,667.24 in other costs incurred.

 

a.         Attorney Service and Messengers

 

Defendant Kia argues that attorney service and messenger fees were not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation but fails to explain why.

 

Plaintiffs assert that the attorney service and messenger fees were related to the “prosecution of this action” for purposes of filing documents such as the Complaint and Case Management Statement. (See Ladas v. California State Automobile Association (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 776.) Moreover, the use of a lower-cost attorney service to file documents was more cost-effective than sending an attorney incurring a lodestar hourly rate. Thus, the court agrees that attorney service and messenger fees were reasonably incurred.

 

                        b.         PGI Invoices

 

Defendant Kia objects to the $1,366.50 in costs due to Plaintiffs’ billing for “PGI Invoices” on the basis that the costs do not appear proper. In response, the Plaintiffs provide PGI invoices, which indicate that the costs were incurred to locate the addresses of Riley Hope and Frank Gonzalez. (Devabose Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. A.) Defendant Kia states that TLOxp service from TransUnion provides public background checks on individuals and that such a search should cost less than $100.00. (Reply at 7: 23-26.) However, Defendant Kia fails to provide a declaration showing that address location service costs should be in the $100.00 range. The fact that Defendant Kia finds the costs excessive is not sufficient to rebut Plaintiffs’ Memorandum of Cost attesting that the costs were reasonably incurred.

 

Therefore, the PGI invoice costs will not be taxed.

 

c.         Travel Expenses

 

Plaintiffs also assert that travel costs, including parking and mileage, are recoverable costs under the Civ. Code § 1794(d) because the costs were reasonably incurred. Exhibit A to the Declaration of Deepak Devabose includes a spreadsheet itemizing the travel expenses. Most travel expenses related to mileage and parking to attend hearings; thus these costs were reasonably incurred in the prosecution of this action. (Civ. Code § 1794(d).) However, the court finds that meal expenses were not reasonably incurred.

 

While section 1033.5, subdivision (a)(3) allows the cost of taking and transcribing depositions and ‘travel expenses to attend depositions,’ it does not mention meals eaten while attending local depositions. Nor can meal expenses be justified as ‘necessary to the conduct of the litigation’ since attorneys have to eat, whether they are conducting litigation or not.

 

(Ladas, supra, 19 Cal.App.4th at p. 774 [italics added].)


The court finds that meal expenses were not reasonably incurred and should be taxed.

 

  • $10.04 for meal for “KE” incurred on 3/21/23
  • $15.17 for meal for “KE” incurred on 6/21/23
  • $7.64 for meal for “JMP” incurred on 8/14/23
  • $9.27 for feal for “JMP” incurred on 8/15/23
  • $8.40 for meal for “JMP” incurred on 8/16/23
  • $64.10 for meal for “BPS and JMP” incurred on 8/17/23
  • $5.85 for meal for “JMP” incurred on 8/17/23
  • $6.55 for meal for “JMP” incurred on 8/18/23
  • $65.00 for meal for “BPS and JMP” incurred on 8/21/23
  •  

Accordingly, $192.02 will be taxed from Item 13.

 

d.         Mediation Costs

 

Defendant Kia fails to explain why mediation costs should be taxed and why such costs are not recoverable under the Song-Beverely Act. Plaintiffs’ opposition further asserts this case was resolved for more than what Defendant Kia offered at mediation such that the costs were reasonable.

 

Therefore, mediation costs will not be taxed from Item 13.

 

e.         Interpreter Fees

 

Defendant Kia argues that interpreter fees are not recoverable because under CCP § 1033.5(a)(12), interpreter fees are only recoverable when the matter involves court interpreter fees authorized by the court for an indigent person represented by a qualified legal services project.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the costs recoverable under the Song-Beverly Act are more expansive than under CCP § 1033.5 because it includes “costs and expenses” “incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code § 1794(d).) Plaintiffs only understood Spanish, which is why interpreters were needed. A spreadsheet of the interpreter costs is included in Exhibit A to the Declaration of Deepak Devabose.

 

In reply, Defendant Kia argues that the interpreter costs do not follow the rates set forth in the Payment Policies for Independent Contractor Interpreters published by the Judicial Council. However, those rates pertain to the payment for independent contractor interpreters retained by the California trial courts rather than by independent counsel.  Defendant Kia again fails to point to case law or statutes that require the Plaintiffs’ counsel to use interpreters that charge rates similar to those set by the Judicial Council.

 

The court finds that the interpreter costs were reasonably incurred in prosecuting this action. Therefore, the interpreter costs will not be taxed.

 

H.        plaintiffs’ Request for Attorney’s Fees for Opposing Motion

 

The California Supreme Court in Serrano v. Unruh¿(1982) has held that Plaintiff’s counsel is entitled to recovery attorney’s fees for opposing fee motions, such as Defendant’s Motion to Tax Costs. (32 Cal.3d 621, 639 [“We hold therefore that, absent circumstances rendering the award unjust, fees recoverable under section 1021.5 ordinarily include compensation for all hours reasonably spent, including those necessary to establish and defend the fee claim.”].) Therefore, the court agrees that Plaintiffs are entitled to recover attorney’s fees for opposing this Motion.

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel states he spent 0.5 hours reviewing Defendant Kia’s Motion and declaration, 3.0 hours drafting this opposition, and all relevant exhibits, and drafting the supporting declaration, and 1.0 hour preparing to attend this hearing remotely. (Devaboos Decl. ¶ 12.) In total, Plaintiffs’ counsel states he spent 5.0 hours billed at an hourly rate of $425.00 hours opposing this Motion and incurred $2, 125.00 in fees.

 

Defendant Kia opposes the Plaintiffs’ fee motion request on the basis that fees are excessive and Plaintiffs are not entitled to a fee request. The court disagrees and grants the Plaintiffs’ fee request in the sum of $1,912.50 for 4.5 hours of work.

 

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Kia’s Motion to Strike is denied. Defendant Kia’s Motion to Tax Costs is granted in part and $192.02 will be taxed from Item 13. Plaintiffs’ request for opposing this Motion is granted in the sum of $1,912.50 for 4.5 hours of work.

 

Defendant to give notice.
