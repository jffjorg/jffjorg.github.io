DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 20STCV36701    Hearing Date: December 11, 2023    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles 

Department 40 

 

ROSALINDA GALVEZ,  

 

Plaintiff, 

 

v. 

 

UNIVERSITY OF SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA, et al, 

 

Defendants.

 

 

  Case No.:         20STCV36701

 

  Hearing Date:  12/11/23 

 

  Trial Date:       8/20/24 

 

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 

 

Defendants University of Southern California and Keck Medical Center of USC’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication. 

 

 

Background 

 

This is a medical malpractice action.  Plaintiff Rosalinda Galvez (“Plaintiff”) alleges that she was under the direct care of Defendants University of Southern California (“USC”); Keck Medicine of USC; Keck Hospital of USC; Keck School of Medicine of USC; Niels Kokot, M.D.; John Carmichael, M.D.; Sebina Bulic, M.D.; Efrain Martinez-Alvernia, Res.; Janet Choi, Res.; Clay Wu, Res.; Rajkumar Dasgupta, M.D.; Trevor Angell, M.D.; Reshma Patel, Res.; Braden Barnet, M.D.; Alyssa Lampe, Res.; Okeefe Simmons, Res.; Andrew Ippoliti, M.D.; Saeed Kalantari, Res.; Peggy Nguyen, M.D.; Benjamin Emanuel, D.O.; Joshua Bakhsheshian, Res.; Frank Attenelo, M.D.; Oliver Gantz, Res.; Zhipeng Zhang, Res.; Alexander Markarian, M.D.; Ronica Yalamanchili, Res.; Salina Teja, M.D.; Jing Shan, Res., and Does 1 through 30 (collectively “Defendants”) from May 29, 2019 to August 19, 2019.  Plaintiff alleges that while under the care of Defendants, Defendants told her she had cancer, but never advised her that the tumor that was the basis of the cancer diagnosis was benign.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants also never obtained informed consent from her regarding the proposed treatment to be administered or any alternatives and unilaterally administered treatment, including steroid treatment.  Plaintiff further alleges that, on or about June 3, 2019, she suffered heart failure due to diabetic ketoacidosis and respiratory distress from Defendants’ administration of a large amount of insulin and/or steroids and as a result, had to be intubated.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants punctured and ripped portions of Plaintiff’s tongue during the intubation procedure, resulting in significant blood loss and permanent damage and disfigurement of her tongue. 

 

On September 25, 2020, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants for (1) professional negligence (medical malpractice); (2) negligent hiring and retention; (3) negligent infliction of emotional distress; (4) respondeat superior (vicarious liability); and (5) breach of fiduciary duty.

  

On October 5, 2021, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint. 

 

On December 10, 2021, the Court sustained with leave to amend Defendant Keck Medical Center of USC’ Demurrer to Plaintiffs First Amended Complaint’s second cause of action for negligent hiring and retention.

 

On January 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint.  Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) professional negligence (medical malpractice); (2) failure to obtain informed consent; and (3) breach of fiduciary duties.

Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication 

 

Summary Adjudication Legal Standard: “The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)

 

As to each cause of action as framed by the complaint, a defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action ... cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 850-851; Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) \

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 850-851.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint sets forth two causes of action against moving Defendants—professional negligence (medical malpractice) and failure to obtain informed consent.

 

First Cause of Action, Professional Negligence (Medical Malpractice): GRANTED. 

 

Defendants move for summary judgment or summary adjudication on the grounds that Plaintiff’s causes of action for medical malpractice fails to raise a triable issue of material fact because the care and treatment complied with the requisite standard of care, and that no act or omission by Defendants caused Plaintiff’s alleged injuries. 

 

In a medical negligence action, a plaintiff must establish the following elements: “(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of [the] profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional’s negligence.”  (Galvez v. Frields (2001) 88 Cal.App.4tha 1410, 1420; Simmons v. West Covina Medical Clinic (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 696, 701-02.)  “Both the standard of care and defendants’ breach must normally be established by expert testimony in a medical malpractice case.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008), 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

A medical professional breaches the duty of professional care by failing to act in accordance with the prevailing industry standard of care.  (See Folk v. Kilk (1975) 53 Cal.App.3d 176, 186.)  “The standard of care against which the acts of a physician are to be measured is a matter peculiarly within the knowledge of experts; it presents the basic issue in a malpractice action and can only be proved by their testimony …, unless the conduct required by the particular circumstances is within the common knowledge of the layman.’”  (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 410.)

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment in a medical malpractice action must “present evidence that would preclude a reasonable trier of fact from finding that it was more likely than not that their treatment fell below the standard of care.”  (Johnson v. Super. Ct. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 297, 305.)  “When a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports [the] motion with expert declarations that [their] conduct fell within the community standard of care, [the defendant] is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”  (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985.)  An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider. (Starr v. Mooslin (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 988, 999.) 

 

Here, Defendants present the declaration of Richard L. Ruffalo, M.D. (“Dr. Ruffalo”), a physician licensed to practice medicine in California who is board certified in anesthesiology and advanced cardiac life support.  (Declaration of Richard L. Ruffalo, M.D. (“Ruffalo Decl.”), ¶¶ 2,4.)  Dr. Ruffalo is familiar with the applicable standard of care within the professional community in Southern California as it applies to anesthesiology.  (Ruffalo Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

Dr. Ruffalo testifies that the materials he has reviewed include the operative complaint and Plaintiff’s medical records from Keck Medicine of USC.  (Id. at ¶ 6.)  Dr. Ruffalo recounts that Plaintiff was admitted to Mountain View Hospital on May 27, 2019 due to complaints of left frontal headaches that were progressively worsening.  (Id. at ¶ 8(a).)  Plaintiff received immediate and appropriate care beginning on her initial transfer from Mountain View Hospital to USC on May 27, 2019, until her discharge from USC on August 19, 2019.  (Id. at ¶ 9(a).) 

 

On June 3, 2019, Plaintiff went into respiratory failure requiring medication-assisted treatment, including emergent intubation and full vent support due to acute encephalopathy from diabetic ketoacidosis. Bassam Yaghmour, M.D. (“Dr. Yaghmour”) and Peter Chung, M.D. (“Dr. Chung”) performed the endotracheal intubation procedure on Plaintiff. Dr. Wu performed the arterial line insertion procedure.  Plaintiff was reported to have tolerated the procedure well and it was noted that Plaintiff did not have any complications.  Plaintiff was deemed critically ill with the following active issues: acute hypoxemic respiratory failure requiring intubation with full vent support; acute metabolic acidosis/lactic acidosis/diabetic ketoacidosis; and acute encephalopathy/brain mass/cerebral edema.  Plaintiff was subsequently moved to the ICU.  This was an emergent lifesaving treatment that was appropriately performed within the standard of care and any reasonable person would consent to this treatment.  (Id. at ¶ 9(b).) 

 

On June 4, 2019, Plaintiff went into acute respiratory failure.  During this time, Plaintiff had an inability to clear her secretions and a loss of protective reflexes.  Dr. Wu performed an endotracheal intubation procedure.  Dr. Wu also reintubated Plaintiff due to Plaintiff’s failed ventilation and respiratory distress. (Id. at ¶ 9(c).)  Following this procedure, Dr. Wu monitored Plaintiff frequently.  (Id. at ¶ 9(d).) 

 

On June 6, 2019, Dr. Wu and Ricardo Juarez, M.D. (“Dr. Juarez”) examined Plaintiff and noted that Plaintiff’s endotracheal tube was secured.  Plaintiff’s endotracheal tube secretions and oral secretions were noted to be minimal.  Following Plaintiff’s surgery, Dr. Wu appropriately ordered a radiology examination of Plaintiff’s chest view due to Plaintiff being intubated.  No negative impressions regarding the endotracheal tube were noted.   (Id. at ¶ 9(e).) 

 

On June 13, 2019, Dr. Wu and Dr. Juarez noted that Plaintiff had a swollen tongue and some ulcers on her tongue.  Dr. Wu and Dr. Juarez recommended Plaintiff to undergo a percutaneous tracheostomy due to upper airway edema, specifically the tongue swelling, to which Plaintiff agreed.  Dr. Wu changed Plaintiff’s ventilator mode for the bedside tracheostomy.  Dr. Wu, Dr. Yaghmour, and Lucas Cruz, M.D. (“Dr. Cruz”) subsequently performed a surgical percutaneous tracheostomy placement on Plaintiff due to her respiratory failure.  Plaintiff was reported not to have active bleeding following her tracheostomy.  It was further reported that Plaintiff tolerated the procedure well and there were no immediate complications reported.  (Id. at ¶ 9(f).) 

 

Based on Dr. Ruffalo’s review of the materials, education, knowledge, training, and experience, Dr. Ruffalo opines to a reasonable degree of medical certainty that moving Defendants and its medical providers complied with the requisite standard of care.  (Id. at ¶ 9.)  It is also Dr. Ruffalo’s opinion that no negligent action or omission by Defendants caused or contributed  to Plaintiff’s claimed injuries and damages.  (Id. at ¶¶ 9-10.) 

 

Further, Defendants present the declaration of Steven Edelman, M.D. (“Dr. Edelman”), a physician licensed to practice in California who is board certified in internal medicine and has actively practiced in the field of endocrinology since completing residency.  (Declaration of Steven Edelman, M.D.  (“Edelman Decl.”), ¶¶ 2, 4.)  Dr. Edelman is familiar with the applicable standard of care relating to the practice of endocrinology and the claims set forth in this case.  (Edelman Decl., ¶5.)

 

Dr. Edelman testifies that the materials he has reviewed include the operative complaint and Plaintiff’s medical records from Keck Medicine of USC.  (Ibid.)  Dr. Edelman recounts that Plaintiff was admitted to Mountain View Hospital on May 27, 2019 due to complaints of left frontal headaches that were progressively worsening.  (Id. at ¶ 7(a).)  Plaintiff received immediate and appropriate care beginning on her initial transfer from Mountain View Hospital on May 27 up until her discharge from USC on August 19.  (Id. at ¶ 8(a).)

 

Defendants made a correct presumptive diagnosis, before definitive lab work was completed, of a large pituitary tumor (macroadenoma)which was later proven to be correct.  This early evaluation of her differential diagnosis was important and led to a successful outcome.  (Id. at ¶ 8(b).) 

 

Throughout her hospital course, Defendants ordered multiple head scans (CT and MRI) for Plaintiff to monitor her progress after cabergoline was initiated. Defendants made the appropriate decision to not send the patient to surgery because of the extreme mass of the lesion and that it was encasing the carotid arteries.  Her prolactin went from 73,00 to near normal at the time of her discharge on August 19.  (Id. at ¶ 8(c).) 

 

Plaintiff’s hospital admission was complicated by diabetic ketoacidosis most likely brought on by the need to use steroids to reduce tumor size as she was experiencing emergent neurologic symptoms.  Plaintiff did have a history of glucose intolerance which put her at risk as well as her having sepsis early on as reported in the medical records.  She was treated in the ICU appropriately with insulin, fluids, and laboratory monitoring data with no sequalae.  (Id. at ¶ 8(d).) 

 

The ICU team along with the critical care physicians attended to her respiratory failure appropriately, which quite frankly saved her life.  Had she been in a small community hospital without the support staff that USC has, she may have had a poor outcome.  (Id. at ¶ 8(e).) 

 

In addition, her macroglossia was assessed appropriately by an ENT physician who did an oral exam as well as a bilateral nasal endoscopy.  Although the exact etiology of her tongue issue was never determined except perhaps self-induced by tongue biting, she was examined multiple times during her visit, monitored closely, and ultimately improved.  No matter what the etiology of her tongue swelling was, she received very appropriate care.  (Id. at ¶ 8(f).)

 

Based on Dr. Edelman’s review of the materials, education, knowledge, training, and experience, Dr. Edelman opines that moving Defendants and its medical providers complied with the requisite standard of care.  (Id. at ¶ 8.)  It is also Dr. Edelman’s opinion that no negligent action or omission by Defendants caused or contributed  to Plaintiff’s claimed injuries and damages.  (Id. at ¶¶ 8-9.) 

 

The foregoing is “evidence which, if uncontradicted, would constitute a preponderance of evidence that an essential element of the plaintiff’s case cannot be established.”  (Kids’ Universe v. In2Labs (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 870, 879.)  The expert opinions of Dr. Ruffalo and Dr. Edelman are evidence that moving Defendants complied with their duties under California law and the standard of care for a reasonably careful medical professional under similar circumstances.  The expert opinions are also evidence that no acts or omissions by moving Defendants caused or contributed to the alleged injuries of Plaintiff.  With this evidence, Defendants have satisfied their initial burden of showing that one or more elements of each of the causes of action in the FAC cannot be established.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  

 

This shifts the burden to Plaintiff to show that there is a triable issue of one or more material facts as to the causes of action.  (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff has failed to make this showing because Plaintiff has submitted no expert declaration or other competent evidence rebutting the Dr. Ruffalo and Dr. Edelman’s declarations.  Instead, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ expert declarations are based on inadmissible hearsay because they are based on Plaintiff’s medical records.  That is not the case.  Authenticated hospital and medical records can be admitted under the business records exception to the hearsay rule.  (Garibay v. Hemmat (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 735, 741.)  Plaintiff also argues that Defendants’ experts failed to address a laceration on Plaintiff’s tongue.  However, that is also not the case.  Dr. Ruffalo and Dr. Edelman testified that they reviewed the medical records and rendered their opinions on Plaintiff’s claims concerning her tongue in reaching their conclusions. 

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion for summary judgment will be granted as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for professional negligence (medical malpractice).

 

Second Cause of Action, Failure to Obtain Informed Consent: GRANTED. 

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s cause of action for failure to obtain informed consent fails to raise a triable issue of material fact because Plaintiff consented to the treatment, and or a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would have consented to the treatment. 

 

The necessary elements for lack of informed consent are: (1) the defendant performed a medical procedure on her; (2) the defendant failed to disclose certain information to her that was material to her decision when soliciting her consent to a medical procedure; (3) a reasonable person in the plaintiff’s position would not have agreed to the procedure if she had been adequately informed; and (4) breach of the duty to disclose certain information caused damaged to the plaintiff.  (See Jameson v. Desta (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1144, 1164; Saxena v. Goffney (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 316, 324 [“A claim based on lack of informed consent—which sounds in negligence—arises when the doctor performs a procedure without first adequately disclosing the risks and alternatives”]; Cobbs v. Grant (1972) 8 Cal.3d 229, 245 [“There must be a causal relationship between the physician’s failure to inform and the injury to the plaintiff.  Such causal connection arises only if it is established that had revelation been made consent to treatment would not have been given”].)  

Like the standard of care defense in traditional medical malpractice actions, a doctor defending a claim that his patient lacked informed consent may assert a defense that the relevant disclosure “was not required within his medical community.”  (Cobbs v. Grant (1972) 8 Cal.3d 229, 240.)  The rationale underlying the applicability of the standard of care defense to lack of informed consent claims is that the tort of lack of informed consent sounds in negligence.  (See id. at pp. 240-241.)

 

Here, Dr. Ruffalo’s expert declaration includes his testimony that this was an emergency lifesaving treatment that was appropriately performed within the standard of care and any reasonable person would consent to this treatment.  (Ruffalo Decl., ¶ 9(b).)  Defendants’ submitted evidence also shows that when Plaintiff was admitted to Keck Medical Center of USC, she signed consent forms indicating that she consented to care and treatment provided by Defendants including lifesaving treatment.  (Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Fact ¶ 345.)   

 

Because Defendants provide credible expert witness testimony from a doctor knowledgeable in the relevant field stating that Defendants met the applicable standard of care and any reasonable person would consent to the lifesaving treatment, and because Plaintiff has presented no expert witness testimony in response, Defendants’ motion for summary judgment will be granted as to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for failure to obtain informed consent.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendants University of Southern California and Keck Medical Center of USC’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication is GRANTED.

Showtime/CBS’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

Legal Standard

Either prior to trial or at the trial—and barring statutory provisions otherwise—the plaintiff or the Defendants may move for judgment on the pleadings where the appropriate ground for such a motion is the same as that arguable by general demurrer, namely, the failure to state a cause of action or defense. (Dobbins v. Hardister (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 787, 791 A motion for JOP is used to challenge a pleading in the same manner as a general demurrer—that is, the challenged pleading (1) establishes that the court does not have subject-matter jurisdiction or (2) does not allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action or defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1); see Marzec v. Public Empls. Ret. Sys. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 889, 900; International Assn of Firefighters v. City of San Jose (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1179, 1196.)

To sufficiently allege a cause of action, a complaint must allege all the ultimate facts—that is, the facts needed to establish each element of the cause of action pleaded. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212 (Committee on Children’s Television), superseded by statute as stated in Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) Courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) However, this analysis “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in White v. Cridlebaugh (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 506, 521.)

As with a demurrer, the grounds for a motion for judgment on the pleadings must appear on the face of the pleading or be based on facts capable of judicial notice. (Bufil v. Dollar Fin. Grp. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1202, disapproved on other grounds in Noel v. Thrifty Payless, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 955, 985-986, fn. 15.) The only significant difference between the two motions is that a motion for judgment on the pleadings is brought after the time to file a general demurrer has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f); Caldera Pharms. v. Regents of the Univ. of Cal. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 338, 350; International Assn of Firefighters v. City of San Jose, supra, at p. 1196.)

In reply, Defendant Showtime/CBS reiterates their position that:

(1) The action is time barred under CCP §583.210.  Showtime/CBS argues their general appearance on June 9, 2017 did not trigger the exception under CCP §583.220 because they had not yet been named as DOEs on the 8th and 9th causes of action.  Showtime/CBS argues the FAC is an “incomprehensible mess,” because DOEs 1 and 2 are allegedly “men Rueda recognized from the Wildcard Gym,” and at other times DOE 1 is a restaurant patron.  Showtime/CBS argues any attempt to amend and avoid dismissal per CCP §583.210 would be futile.

(2) there is no recognized cause of action for attempted extortion;

(3) the facts alleged do not support imposition of vicarious liability for the conduct of third parties.   

The Court finds in favor of Defendant Showtime/CBS.  Defendant Showtime/CBS’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED without leave to amend as to the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion and GRANTED without leave to amend as to the 9th cause of action for IIED. 

(1)  CCP §583.210 does not bar the 8th and 9th cause of action

CCP §583.210(a) provides, “The summons and complaint shall be served upon a defendant within three years after the action is commenced against the defendant.  For the purposes of this subdivision, an action is commenced at the time the complaint is filed.”  “If the summons has not been served and return made within three years after the commencement of the action, and if the case does not come within a statutory or judicially-declared exception, dismissal of the complaint under Code of Civil Procedure section 581a, subdivision (a), is mandatory and jurisdictional.  It is not necessary to show that the defendant has been prejudiced by the delay.  (Botsford v. Pascoe (1979) 94 Cal.App.3d 62, 66 (interpreting predecessor statute to CCP §583.210).)

An exception to the three-year deadline under CCP §583.210 is set forth in CCP §583.220.  “The time within which service must be made pursuant to this article does not apply if the defendant enters into a stipulation in writing or does another act that constitutes a general appearance in the action.”  (CCP §583.220.)

“A general appearance is not necessarily a formal, technical step or act. The term is applied to various acts which, under established principles of procedure, are deemed to confer jurisdiction of the person.  Whether there has been a general appearance will often depend upon an examination of all the circumstances.  A general appearance must be express or arise by implication from the defendant's seeking, taking, or agreeing to some step or proceeding in the cause beneficial to himself or detrimental to the plaintiff, other than one contesting the jurisdiction only.”  (Botsford v. Pascoe (1979) 94 Cal.App.3d 62, 67.) 

In order for the exception under CCP §583.220(a) to apply, the general appearance must have been made in the “action” in which the defendant seeks dismissal.  Thus, where a defendant generally appeared on a cross-complaint within three years of filing the underlying complaint, but did not appear within three years on the underlying complaint, CCP §583.220 did not apply and the underlying complaint was properly dismissed pursuant to CCP §583.210.  (Botsford, supra, 94 Cal.App.3d at 67.)  “[F]or purposes of section 581a, subdivision (a), an ‘action’ on a complaint should be distinguished from an “action” on a related cross-complaint and that a general appearance in one such action will not necessarily constitute a general appearance in the other. A complaint and a cross-complaint in a single lawsuit are for most purposes treated as independent actions.”  (Id.) (applying predecessor statute to CCP §583.210.)

On March 24, 2016, Plaintiff filed the complaint in this action.  On August 25, 2016, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint.  The FAC named Defendant Showtime/CBS as Defendants.  (FAC, ¶¶6-7.)  On June 9, 2017, Defendant Showtime/CBS filed a joint answer to the First Amended Complaint.  On September 11, 2019, Plaintiff filed DOE Amendments to the Complaint naming CBS as DOE 1 and Showtime as DOE 2. 

Based on these facts, which appear from the record and are cited by Plaintiff and Defendant in reply, Showtime/CBS made a general appearance within three years of filing of the complaint.  The complaint was filed on March 24, 2016 and Showtime/CBS generally appeared in the action on June 9, 2017, a little over a year later.  Pursuant to CCP §583.220, the three-year time limit set forth in CCP §583.210 would therefore not apply. 

Showtime/CBS’s reply does not adequately respond to Plaintiff’s argument that CCP §583.220 applies.  Showtime/CBS argue that of their general appearance in this action, they were not yet named as DOE defendants and they were specifically not yet named in the 8th and 9th causes of action.  Showtime/CBS argue they could not make a general appearance on causes of action to which they had not yet been named.

Showtime/CBS offers no authority that supports their position that “action” under CCP §583.220 is properly interpreted to mean “causes of action.”  Showtime/CBS cite to Botsford v. Pascoe (1979) 94 Cal.App.3d 62, 67, Brumley v. FDCC California, Inc. (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 312 and Barrington v. A. H. Robins Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 146.  None of these cases stand for the proposition that “action” in CCP §583.220 means “cause of action.” 

Botsford states that for purposes of CCP §583.220, a complaint and a cross-complaint in a single litigation are separate “actions” for purposes of CCP §583.220.  Here, there is no question that Showtime/CBS generally appeared in the “action,” i.e. the underlying complaint, within three years of the original filing. 

Barrington held that a complaint that was amended to charge a DOE defendant with a new cause of action arising from different operative facts did not need to be filed within three years of the original filing.  (Barrington, supra, 39 Cal.3d 146, 149.)  Barrington held that an amendment adding a new cause of action against a DOE defendant based on different operative facts did not relate back to the original complaint, and therefore, the three-year period to serve CCP §581.210 did not begin to run until the amended complaint adding that new cause of action was filed.  (Id. at 157.) 

Brumley followed Barrington’s application of the relation back doctrine to a motion to dismiss based on the five-year period for bringing an action to trial.  For purposes of calculating the five-year period to bring an action to trial, as to amended causes of action asserting different rights and damages from those alleged in the original complaint, the date of the “action was commenced” was the date on which the amendments were filed, not the date the original action was filed.  (Brumley, supra, 156 Cal.App.4th at 316.)

Neither Barrington nor Brumley support Showtime/CBS’s position that their general appearance in June 2017 does not trigger the exception under CCP §583.220.  Those cases merely stand for the proposition that, for purposes of calculating the three-year period under CCP §583.210 or the five-year period under CCP §§583.310 and 583.360, the relation back doctrine applies.  As to amendments that do not relate back, to calculate these statutory deadlines, the action commenced when the amendment was filed, not when the original complaint was filed.

Showtime/CBS also argue that this Court already found the action time-barred under CCP §583.210 in connection with Showtime/CBS’s SLAPP motion.  (Defendant Showtime/CBS’s RJN, Ex. B, November 13, 2020 Minute Order.)  On the second prong of the SLAPP analysis, Judge Sotelo found the action was time-barred based on CCP §583.210, because Defendants were not served as DOE defendants within three years of the filing of the original complaint and nothing prevent Plaintiff from doing so based on the evidence presented by Showtime/CBS on the second prong of SLAPP.  (Defendant Showtime/CBS’s RJN, Ex. B, p. 10 of 11.) 

Based on the Rueda’s Opposition to the Showtime/CBS SLAPP and the November 13, 2020, Rueda never argued that the June 9, 2017 answer triggered CCP §583.220. Rueda argued that the September 2016 demurrer qualified as a general appearance.  (Defendant Showtime/CBS’s RJN, Ex. B, p. 10 of 11; Plaintiff’s Opposition to Showtime/CBS SLAPP filed on October 16, 2020.)  While the November 13, 2020 Minute Order references the argument that the September 6, 2016 demurrer was a general appearance, it does not address that argument nor does it reference CCP §583.220.  As such, the specific argument raised here, that the June 9, 2017 answer triggered the exception under CCP §583.220, was never previously addressed by this Court. 

Moreover, Defendants fail to cite any authority holding that the findings on the second prong of SLAPP have any binding effect outside of the SLAPP motion.  The soundness of the second prong findings was also not at issue in Rueda’s appeal of the Showtime/CBS SLAPP motion.  (Defendant Showtime/CBS’s RJN, Ex. A, p. 16 (“Rueda has not challenged the trial court’s determination that he has no reasonable probability of prevailing on the claims that arise from Davidson’s conduct…Accordingly, we need not analyze the issue.”)

Defendant Showtime/CBS’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings based on CCP §583.210 is denied. 

 

(2) Attempted extortion is not a recognized cause of action. 

As discussed in connection with the Motions for Judgment of the Pleadings of Defendants Pacquiao and Davidson, the Court finds there is no recognized cause of action for attempted extortion.  For reference that discussion is reproduced here.

The parties do not disagree that there exists a cause of action in California for extortion. (Fuhrman v. California Satellite Systems (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 408, 426, disapproved of on other grounds in Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205.) However, as both parties acknowledge, to bring such a claim one must show that he or she paid the money or took whatever action is demanded by the extortioners. As noted in Fuhrman, a claim for actual extortion was recognized by the Supreme Court as “[h]owever denominated (e.g., extortion, menace, duress) . . a cause of action for the recovery of money obtained by the wrongful threat of criminal or civil prosecution.” (Fuhrman, supra, 179 Cal.App.3d at p. 426.) In that case, the court affirmed dismissal of the cause of action on that ground. (Ibid. [“[t]he fatal flaw in plaintiff’s action is that she apparently never paid the money defendants demanded in their letters”].) The question here is whether there is a cause of action in California for attempted extortion; that is, the kind of claim set forth in Fuhrman in which the threat is made but the victim refuses to pay.

The Fuhrman case considered whether the cause of action in its case – essentially, attempted extortion, or extortion without payment -- could be upheld on some other theory, and noted that it “could be construed as one for the recovery of damages for the severe emotional distress caused by defendants’ ‘outrageous’ conduct. As such, it is simply a variation of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress.” (Id. at p. 428.) There, as here, the plaintiff had already alleged such a claim in addition to the extortion claim, and the Court dismissed the extortion claim. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff nonetheless urges this Court to find a cause of action for attempted extortion. In the November 13, 2020 ruling on the Showtime/CBS SLAPP motion, Judge Sotelo addressed the argument that attempted extortion is not a recognized cause of action by stating, “[t]he Court finds that Rueda could allege his attempted extortion claim:  Rueda claims that percentage of proceeds were promised to him—it was his money.  An attempt to withhold his claimed earnings may be attempted extortion.”  However, in the very next sentence, Judge Sotelo acknowledged that the issue was moot given his findings regarding Showtime/CBS’s affirmative defenses based on litigation privilege and statute of limitations.  Therefore, Judge Sotelo’s statement regarding Rueda’s ability to allege attempted extortion was advisory or dicta, and not a ruling on the specific issue of whether such a cause of action exists under California law. 

Attempted extortion is a crime.  (Penal Code §524.)  “There are at least three different ways alleged violations of criminal law can result in civil actions. First, and perhaps most commonly, violation of a criminal statute can be used to establish a breach of the standard of care or other element of an ordinary tort cause of action.  Second, and pertinent to [plaintiff]'s argument on appeal, a criminal statute can expressly or impliedly give rise to a private right of action for its violation.  Third, under some circumstances, a governmental or quasi-governmental agency can sue to enjoin further breaches of the statute on a public nuisance or related theory.”  (Animal Legal Defense Fund v. Mendes (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 136, 141.) 

While Plaintiff does not cite any controlling authority expressly recognizing that attempted extortion is a civil cause of action, Plaintiff cites to federal cases recognizing attempted extortion as a cause of action under California law.  (Monex Deposit Co. v. Gilliam (C.D. Cal. 2009) 666 F.Supp.2d 1135, 1137; Leibman v. Prupes (C.D. Ca. 2015) 2015 WL 898454 *16, fn. 6; TaiMed Biologics, Inc. v. Numoda Corp. (N.D. Cal. 2011) 2011 WL 1630041 *5; Baker v. FirstCom Music (C.D. Cal. 2018) 2018 WL 2572725 *11.  However, the Court is not bound by federal decisions on matters of California law.  (Haynes v. EMC Mortgage Corp. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 329, 335; Qualified Patients Assn. v. City of Anaheim (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 734, 764 [“[D]ecisions of the lower federal courts are not binding precedent [citation], particularly on issues of state law.”) 

Monex argued recognition of a civil cause for extortion was proper even when there was no payment of monies (thus, akin to the “attempted extortion alleged here), given the California Supreme Court’s adoption of section 874A of the Restatement (Second) of Torts, which states:  “When a legislative provision protects a class of persons by proscribing ... certain conduct but does not provide a civil remedy for the violation, the court may, if it determines that the remedy is appropriate in furtherance of the purpose of the legislation and needed to assure the effectiveness of the provision, accord to an injured member of the class a right of action, using a ... new cause of action analogous to an existing tort action.”  (Monex, supra, 666 F.Supp.2d at 1137.)  Monex concluded, “[B]ecause the civil tort for extortion is derived from the crime of extortion, and because recognizing a claim for civil extortion furthers the purpose of California Penal Code section 523, the Court declines to dismiss the third cause of action.”  (Id.) 

The Court does not find Monex persuasive, because Monex did not identify any analogous, existing tort action that would support a claim for “attempted extortion,” despite citation to the Restatement (Second) of Torts section 874A.  (Monex, supra, 666 F.Supp.2d at 1137.)  While the specific claim before it was for civil extortion, as noted, the plaintiff alleged that it “never gave in to defendants’ alleged extortion(s),” and thus the claim was for attempted extortion.  (Id. at 1136.)  The Court finds the Fuhrman court’s opinion better reasoned (and actually to have precedential effect) which finds that the tort of attempted extortion, by contrast, is more analogous with a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  (Id. at p. 428.) The Court finds that none of the other remaining federal authorities cited are persuasive. (Leibman, supra, 2015 WL 898454 *16, fn. 6 [not analyzing the difference between extortion and attempted extortion and relying on Monex]; TaiMed Biologics, Inc. v. Numoda Corp., supra, 2011 WL 1630041 *5 [citing Monex’s analysis]; Baker v. FirstCom Music (C.D. Cal. 2018) 2018 WL 2572725 *11 [not analyzing the difference between extortion and attempted extortion and replying on Monex].) 

Plaintiff also cites to an isolated ruling from a Los Angeles County trial court.  (Chang v. Ruy (2019) 2019 WL 13194157 *5 [Rico, J].)  “Trial courts do not make binding precedents.” (9 Witkin (6th ed. 2021) California Procedure, Appeal §507.)  “When collected and readily available for study,” trial court orders “may be cited for their persuasive value and are occasionally followed in the absence of controlling higher authority.” (Id.)  However, an isolated ruling that is not part of a readily available compilation is not citable for its persuasive value. (Santa Ana Hosp. Med. Center v. Belshé (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 830.) Nor does the Court find the Chang case persuasive, even if it was citable for such a purpose. The trial court there also cited Monex but just as did the court in Monex, as noted above, the superior court failed to consider the requirement of tethering the new cause of action to an analogous, existing tort action.

Plaintiff also argues attempted extortion exists as a cause of action, because it was implicitly recognized in Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299.   Plaintiff relies on the concurring opinion of Justice Werdegar.  The issue of whether attempted extortion is a recognized cause of action was not before the Flatley court.  Indeed, the majority opinion focused on whether the conduct arose to the level of criminal extortionate conduct (id. at p. 330), and the issue of whether a civil claim for extortion requires the payment of money did not arise either in the majority or the concurring opinion. “It is axiomatic that cases are not authority for propositions that are not considered.”  (California Building Industry Assn. v. State Water Resources Control Bd. (2018) 4 Cal.5th 1032, 1043.)  The Court cannot imply a finding in Flatley on an issue that was never considered or presented to the Flatley court. 

Plaintiff fails to identify an analogous, recognized civil tort claim to attempted extortion.  As did the court in Fuhrman, this court finds that the most analogous tort claim would be a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress, which is separately pleaded as the 9th cause of action. Plaintiff also fails to identify any other recognized tort that is stated based on the allegations in the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion.  Finally, Plaintiff does not argue or establish any of these three grounds to recognize a private, civil right of action for attempted extortion based on Penal Code §524. 

Plaintiff fails to establish that attempted extortion is recognized cause of action under California law.  Defendant Pacquiao’s and Davidson’s Motions for Judgment on the Pleadings are GRANTED as to the 8th cause of action for Attempted Extortion. 

(3)  The IIED claim is not sufficiently alleged as to Showtime/CBS

Defendant argues the IIED claim fails against them, because Plaintiff fails to allege any facts that would make them vicariously liable for the conduct of third parties, e.g. the Wildcard members.  The IIED claim incorporates ¶¶1-45 and 91-99 of the FAC.  However, the July 26, 2023 opinion, the Court of Appeals only struck the incorporated “general allegations.”  The Court interprets “general allegations” to refer to ¶¶1-45. 

Based on the allegations in support of the IIED claim, Plaintiff alleges DOES 1-6 and “goons from the Wildcard Gym” “harassed, stalked, best and terrorized Rueda on behalf of Roach and Paquiao.  (FAC, ¶92.)  Plaintiff alleges, “Roach and Pacquiao knew their agents Davidson, DOES 1-6 and the goons from the Wildcard Gym would make the above described threats or take the above-described actions.”  (FAC, ¶94.)  Plaintiff alleges Pacquiao and Davidson intentionally caused Plaintiff to suffer severe emotional distress by “threat[en]ing…physical harm to himself and property…and causing him to live in fear for his safety and livelihood.”  (FAC, ¶101.)  Plaintiff alleges he “felt fearful for his personal and physical safety.  He could not go out in public for fear that Wildcard Gym members and Pacquiao’s Posse would physically attack him at anytime.”  (FAC, ¶102.)  Plaintiff also alleges that the DOE defendants were acting as agents of the other named Defendants. (FAC, ¶12.)  Plaintiff specifically alleges the threatening and menacing behavior at ¶42, that the behavior was carried out by persons he recognized as being members of the Wildcard Gym owned by Roach and patronized by Pacquiao’s fans.  (FAC, ¶42.)  Plaintiff alleges the menacing behavior occurred just after Davidson approached him with an offer to settle his claims, and he believes was intended to intimidate him into accepting Davidson’s offer.  (Id. at ¶¶41 and 44.) 

“Under the doctrine of respondeat superior, the innocent principal or employer is liable for the torts of the agent or employee, committed while acting within the scope of employment. It is immaterial that the employee acts in excess of authority or contrary to instructions.”  (3 Witkin, Summary (11th ed. 2020), Agency §175.)  “The courts have approved a general statement to the effect that the wrongdoer was the agent or employee of the defendant principal, and “was acting in the scope of his employment.  [¶] The general averment is sufficient not only in negligence cases but also where the cause of action is based on an intentional tort.”   (5 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), ¶918. 

“An allegation of agency constitutes an averment of ultimate fact, which we accept as true on a demurrer.  But where factual allegations are based on information and belief, the plaintiff must allege information that leads the plaintiff to believe that the allegations are true.”  (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1077, 1106.)  “[G]eneral allegations of agency that are contradicted by, or inconsistent with, facts specifically pleaded, will be disregarded.”  (5 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), Plead §918.)

Plaintiff’s FAC fails to allege any facts that would support a finding of agency making Showtime/CBS liable for the actions of the alleged “goons” from Wildcard Gym or any other unidentified persons for harassing, threatening, stalking or menacing Plaintiff.  Plaintiff’s general allegation of agency is insufficient as to Showtime/CBS for the simple reason that his DOE amendments render his FAC unclear, or, as uncharitably characterized by Showtime/CBS, an “incomprehensible mess.” 

There are no allegations directed specifically at Showtime/CBS to hold them vicariously liable for the actions of these third party “goons.”  Plaintiff relies on the DOE allegations, including the general allegation of agency that “each DOE defendant was acting as an agent…for each of the other Defendants…”  Showtime/CBS were named as DOEs 1 and 2 in the 9th cause of action for IIED.  However, the general allegation of agency seems to allege that Showtime/CBS were acting as agents for the other named Defendants.  Plaintiff must allege that the “goons,” who are themselves DOE defendants or unidentified persons, were acting as Showtime/CBS’s agents when they harassed and intimidated Plaintiff. 

More importantly, Plaintiff alleges that DOEs 1-3, which would include Showtime and CBS, were “men.”  (FAC, ¶42.)  Plaintiff alleges DOEs 1-6 directly engaged in the alleged harassment.  (FAC, ¶43.)  Showtime and CBS are corporate entities, not “men.”  They could not have physically “pointed at Rueda and laughed” or “forcefully grabbed” Rueda’s wrist.  “A corporation can only act through its servants, agents and employees.”  (Alvarez v. Felker Mfg. Co. (1964) 230 Cal.App.2d 987, 998.)  Plaintiff must allege facts that would make Showtime/CBS vicariously liable for the acts of these “men.”  Plaintiff does not achieve this by naming Showtime/CBS as DOE 1 and 2.  The allegations as to DOEs 1-2 allege direct liability as to DOEs 1-2 as actual participants in the alleged harassment, intimidation and physical threats.

“[G]eneral allegations of agency that are contradicted by, or inconsistent with, facts specifically pleaded, will be disregarded.”  (5 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), Plead §918.) Given the conflicting and inconsistent DOE allegations, the general allegation of agency is also disregarded. 

Defendant argues on reply that Plaintiff was required to comply with CRC Rule 3.1324(a)-(b) to obtain leave to amend.  However, CRC Rule 3.1324’s requirements apply to a noticed motion for leave to amend.  Plaintiff did not file such a noticed motion, nor was he required to in the face of an MJP.  “[I]t is an abuse of discretion to grant a motion for judgment on the pleadings without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action.” (Dudley v. Department of Transportation (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 255, 260.)  Plaintiff bears the burden of demonstrating the defect can be cured by amendment. (Id..) Such a showing need not be made in the trial court, but may be made to the reviewing court. (Id..)

Defendant Showtime/CBS’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is granted with 20 days leave to amend as to the 9th cause of action for IIED.

Legal Standard

Either prior to trial or at the trial—and barring statutory provisions otherwise—the plaintiff or the Defendants may move for judgment on the pleadings where the appropriate ground for such a motion is the same as that arguable by general demurrer, namely, the failure to state a cause of action or defense. (Dobbins v. Hardister (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 787, 791 A motion for JOP is used to challenge a pleading in the same manner as a general demurrer—that is, the challenged pleading (1) establishes that the court does not have subject-matter jurisdiction or (2) does not allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action or defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1); see Marzec v. Public Empls. Ret. Sys. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 889, 900; International Assn of Firefighters v. City of San Jose (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1179, 1196.)

To sufficiently allege a cause of action, a complaint must allege all the ultimate facts—that is, the facts needed to establish each element of the cause of action pleaded. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212 (Committee on Children’s Television), superseded by statute as stated in Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) Courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) However, this analysis “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in White v. Cridlebaugh (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 506, 521.)

As with a demurrer, the grounds for a motion for judgment on the pleadings must appear on the face of the pleading or be based on facts capable of judicial notice. (Bufil v. Dollar Fin. Grp. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1202, disapproved on other grounds in Noel v. Thrifty Payless, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 955, 985-986, fn. 15.) The only significant difference between the two motions is that a motion for judgment on the pleadings is brought after the time to file a general demurrer has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f); Caldera Pharms. v. Regents of the Univ. of Cal. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 338, 350; International Assn of Firefighters v. City of San Jose, supra, at p. 1196.)

Defendants Davidson and Pacquiao (“Defendants”) filed separate motions for judgment on the pleadings of the 8th and 9th causes of action for attempted extortion and IIED that make the nearly the same substantive arguments.  Both Defendants argue:

(1) there is no such cause of action as “attempted extortion”;  

(2) the attempted extortion claim is based on Davidson’s statements to Rueda that have already been deemed by this Court and the Court of Appeal to be statements protected by the litigation privilege; and

(3) as to the alleged harassing conduct of third parties, Plaintiff fails to allege sufficient facts that would make either Davidson or Pacquiao liable for their actions, e.g. agency, conspiracy allegations.

In addition to the arguments above, Defendant Pacquiao alone makes the following arguments:

(1) even if attempted extortion were a cause of action, Plaintiff never relinquished any of his claims to a finder’s fee in response to the attempted extortion; and

(2)  there are no allegations of conduct by Pacquiao that would support either the 8th or 9th cause of action, nor are there sufficient allegations to support imposition of vicarious liability on Pacquiao for the conduct of either Davidson or non-parties.

In response, Plaintiff filed separate oppositions to Pacquiao’s and Davidson’s MJPs that are essentially identical.  Plaintiff argues:

(1) attempted extortion is a cause of action and the Court indicated it is a recognized cause of action in its prior ruling on the 2nd prong of CBS/Showtime’s SLAPP;

(2) the appellate opinion issued on the CBS/Showtime SLAPP does not apply to Davidson or Pacquiao, as neither was a party to that appeal and the doctrine of “law of the case” only applies to parties to the appeal;

(3) Davidson specifically waived any right to rely on the CBS/Showtime SLAPP, because he inexplicably withheld evidence and argument from his SLAPP that CBS/Showtime later presented in their SLAPP;

(4) even without the statements allegedly made by Davidson that were stricken in the CBS/Showtime SLAPP, judgment on the pleadings cannot be granted because Plaintiff alleges other conduct that supports the extortion and IIED claims.

(5) based on Supreme Court authority, vicarious liability for both the extortion and IIED claims are sufficiently pled based on the bare allegation that DOES 1-6 and the Wildcard Gym were the agents of both Davidson and Pacquiao. 

(6) the IIED claim is sufficiently pled based on the physical stalking, harassment and threats by third parties who were acting as the agents of Davidson and Pacquiao.

In their replies, Pacquiao and Davidson both reiterate that attempted extortion is not a recognized cause of action in California.  Defendants argue that nothing in the language or history of Penal Code §524 indicates an intent to provide for a civil right of action, nor has any California Court of Appeal ever held that such a claim exists. 

In addition, Davidson argues in reply that the prior ruling on the CBS/Showtime SLAPP never found that a claim for attempted extortion exists in California.  Defendant Davidson argues the court indicated that issue was moot.

Davidson also argues in reply that, even if attempted extortion were a recognized cause of action, no extortionate threat is alleged.  Davidson argues that the Court of Appeal already deemed his statements protected by the litigation privilege in connection with the Showtime/CBS SLAPP.  Davidson argues that conduct therefore cannot form the basis of Plaintiff’s attempted extortion claim.  Davidson argues the Court of Appeal never limited its ruling to the Showtime/CBS.  Davidson argues even if law of the case does not apply, Plaintiff should be estopped from relitigating whether Davidson’s statements are protected by the litigation privilege.  Davidson argues he has never waived any rights with respect to the assertion that his statements were subject to the litigation privilege.  Davidson argues that even if the appellate opinion did not find the call between Davidson and Susser privileged, it found Davidson’s allegedly extortionate threats to Plaintiff privileged. 

Pacquiao echoes Davidson’s arguments regarding the impact of the appellate opinion on Showtime/CBS’s SLAPP motion.  Pacquiao argues Davidson’s alleged statements were already deemed by the Court of Appeal to be protected by the litigation privilege and cannot form the basis of a claim for attempted extortion.  Pacquiao also argues on reply that, without the Davidson statements, there is no claim for attempted extortion based on the allegations of physical intimidation and harassment by Wildcard gym members and DOEs.

Both Pacquiao and Davidson argue in reply that there are insufficient factual allegations to support a finding of agency.  Defendants argue that Plaintiff therefore fails to allege attempted extortion or IIED against based on the conduct of third parties. 

The Court finds in favor of Defendants on the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion and in favor of Plaintiff on the 9th cause of action for IIED.  Defendant Pacquiao’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is granted as to the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion and is denied as to the 9th cause of action for IIED.  Defendant Davidson’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is granted as to the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion and denied as to the 9th cause of action for IIED. 

 

(1)  Attempted extortion is not a recognized cause of action. 

The parties do not disagree that there exists a cause of action in California for extortion. (Fuhrman v. California Satellite Systems (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 408, 426, disapproved of on other grounds in Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205.) However, as both parties acknowledge, to bring such a claim one must show that he or she paid the money or took whatever action is demanded by the extortioners. As noted in Fuhrman, a claim for actual extortion was recognized by the Supreme Court as “[h]owever denominated (e.g., extortion, menace, duress) . . a cause of action for the recovery of money obtained by the wrongful threat of criminal or civil prosecution.” (Fuhrman, supra, 179 Cal.App.3d at p. 426.) In that case, the court affirmed dismissal of the cause of action on that ground. (Ibid. [“[t]he fatal flaw in plaintiff’s action is that she apparently never paid the money defendants demanded in their letters”].) The question here is whether there is a cause of action in California for attempted extortion; that is, the kind of claim set forth in Fuhrman in which the threat is made but the victim refuses to pay.

The Fuhrman case considered whether the cause of action in its case – essentially, attempted extortion, or extortion without payment -- could be upheld on some other theory, and noted that it “could be construed as one for the recovery of damages for the severe emotional distress caused by defendants’ ‘outrageous’ conduct. As such, it is simply a variation of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress.” (Id. at p. 428.) There, as here, the plaintiff had already alleged such a claim in addition to the extortion claim, and the Court dismissed the extortion claim. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff nonetheless urges this Court to find a cause of action for attempted extortion. In the November 13, 2020 ruling on the Showtime/CBS SLAPP motion, Judge Sotelo addressed the argument that attempted extortion is not a recognized cause of action by stating, “[t]he Court finds that Rueda could allege his attempted extortion claim:  Rueda claims that percentage of proceeds were promised to him—it was his money.  An attempt to withhold his claimed earnings may be attempted extortion.”  However, in the very next sentence, Judge Sotelo acknowledged that the issue was moot given his findings regarding Showtime/CBS’s affirmative defenses based on litigation privilege and statute of limitations.  Therefore, Judge Sotelo’s statement regarding Rueda’s ability to allege attempted extortion was advisory or dicta, and not a ruling on the specific issue of whether such a cause of action exists under California law. 

Attempted extortion is a crime.  (Penal Code §524.)  “There are at least three different ways alleged violations of criminal law can result in civil actions. First, and perhaps most commonly, violation of a criminal statute can be used to establish a breach of the standard of care or other element of an ordinary tort cause of action.  Second, and pertinent to [plaintiff]'s argument on appeal, a criminal statute can expressly or impliedly give rise to a private right of action for its violation.  Third, under some circumstances, a governmental or quasi-governmental agency can sue to enjoin further breaches of the statute on a public nuisance or related theory.”  (Animal Legal Defense Fund v. Mendes (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 136, 141.) 

While Plaintiff does not cite any controlling authority expressly recognizing that attempted extortion is a civil cause of action, Plaintiff cites to federal cases recognizing attempted extortion as a cause of action under California law.  (Monex Deposit Co. v. Gilliam (C.D. Cal. 2009) 666 F.Supp.2d 1135, 1137; Leibman v. Prupes (C.D. Ca. 2015) 2015 WL 898454 *16, fn. 6; TaiMed Biologics, Inc. v. Numoda Corp. (N.D. Cal. 2011) 2011 WL 1630041 *5; Baker v. FirstCom Music (C.D. Cal. 2018) 2018 WL 2572725 *11.  However, the Court is not bound by federal decisions on matters of California law.  (Haynes v. EMC Mortgage Corp. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 329, 335; Qualified Patients Assn. v. City of Anaheim (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 734, 764 [“[D]ecisions of the lower federal courts are not binding precedent [citation], particularly on issues of state law.”) 

Monex argued recognition of a civil cause for extortion was proper even when there was no payment of monies (thus, akin to the “attempted extortion alleged here), given the California Supreme Court’s adoption of section 874A of the Restatement (Second) of Torts, which states:  “When a legislative provision protects a class of persons by proscribing ... certain conduct but does not provide a civil remedy for the violation, the court may, if it determines that the remedy is appropriate in furtherance of the purpose of the legislation and needed to assure the effectiveness of the provision, accord to an injured member of the class a right of action, using a ... new cause of action analogous to an existing tort action.”  (Monex, supra, 666 F.Supp.2d at 1137.)  Monex concluded, “[B]ecause the civil tort for extortion is derived from the crime of extortion, and because recognizing a claim for civil extortion furthers the purpose of California Penal Code section 523, the Court declines to dismiss the third cause of action.”  (Id.) 

The Court does not find Monex persuasive, because Monex did not identify any analogous, existing tort action that would support a claim for “attempted extortion,” despite citation to the Restatement (Second) of Torts section 874A.  (Monex, supra, 666 F.Supp.2d at 1137.)  While the specific claim before it was for civil extortion, as noted, the plaintiff alleged that it “never gave in to defendants’ alleged extortion(s),” and thus the claim was for attempted extortion.  (Id. at 1136.)  The Court finds the Fuhrman court’s opinion better reasoned (and actually to have precedential effect) which finds that the tort of attempted extortion, by contrast, is more analogous with a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  (Id. at p. 428.) The Court finds that none of the other remaining federal authorities cited are persuasive. (Leibman, supra, 2015 WL 898454 *16, fn. 6 [not analyzing the difference between extortion and attempted extortion and relying on Monex]; TaiMed Biologics, Inc. v. Numoda Corp., supra, 2011 WL 1630041 *5 [citing Monex’s analysis]; Baker v. FirstCom Music (C.D. Cal. 2018) 2018 WL 2572725 *11 [not analyzing the difference between extortion and attempted extortion and replying on Monex].) 

Plaintiff also cites to an isolated ruling from a Los Angeles County trial court.  (Chang v. Ruy (2019) 2019 WL 13194157 *5 [Rico, J].)  “Trial courts do not make binding precedents.” (9 Witkin (6th ed. 2021) California Procedure, Appeal §507.)  “When collected and readily available for study,” trial court orders “may be cited for their persuasive value and are occasionally followed in the absence of controlling higher authority.” (Id.)  However, an isolated ruling that is not part of a readily available compilation is not citable for its persuasive value. (Santa Ana Hosp. Med. Center v. Belshé (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 830.) Nor does the Court find the Chang case persuasive, even if it was citable for such a purpose. The trial court there also cited Monex but just as did the court in Monex, as noted above, the superior court failed to consider the requirement of tethering the new cause of action to an analogous, existing tort action.

Plaintiff also argues attempted extortion exists as a cause of action, because it was implicitly recognized in Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299.   Plaintiff relies on the concurring opinion of Justice Werdegar.  The issue of whether attempted extortion is a recognized cause of action was not before the Flatley court.  Indeed, the majority opinion focused on whether the conduct arose to the level of criminal extortionate conduct (id. at p. 330), and the issue of whether a civil claim for extortion requires the payment of money did not arise either in the majority or the concurring opinion. “It is axiomatic that cases are not authority for propositions that are not considered.”  (California Building Industry Assn. v. State Water Resources Control Bd. (2018) 4 Cal.5th 1032, 1043.)  The Court cannot imply a finding in Flatley on an issue that was never considered or presented to the Flatley court. 

Plaintiff fails to identify an analogous, recognized civil tort claim to attempted extortion.  As did the court in Fuhrman, this court finds that the most analogous tort claim would be a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress, which is separately pleaded as the 9th cause of action. Plaintiff also fails to identify any other recognized tort that is stated based on the allegations in the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion.  Finally, Plaintiff does not argue or establish any of these three grounds to recognize a private, civil right of action for attempted extortion based on Penal Code §524. 

Plaintiff fails to establish that attempted extortion is recognized cause of action under California law.  Defendant Pacquiao’s and Davidson’s Motions for Judgment on the Pleadings are GRANTED as to the 8th cause of action for Attempted Extortion. 

 

(2) Defendants fail to establish that the affirmative defense of litigation privilege applies to them based on the Showtime/CBS SLAPP, nor would the 9th cause of action for IIED fail to state a claim if the litigation privilege applied to them

The following discussion applies to the remaining 9th cause of action for IIED.

In the July 26, 2023 Court of Appeal opinion on the Showtime/CBS SLAPP (“July 26, 2023 Opinion”), the Court of Appeal held that Davidson’s alleged conduct was not illegal as a matter of law, rejecting Rueda’s position that SLAPP did not apply per the illegality exception set forth in Flatley.  (Plaintiff’s RJN, Ex. A, July 26, 2023 Opinion, p. 13.)  The Court of Appeal indicated that the appeal did not “challenge the trial court’s conclusion that Davidson’s actions…were undertaken in anticipation of litigation contemplated in good faith and under serious consideration.  That point is accordingly waived.”  (Plaintiff’s RJN, Ex. A, July 26, 2023 Opinion, pp. 13-14.) 

The Court of Appeal also wrote that “Rueda has not challenged the trial court’s determination that he has no reasonable probability of prevailing on the claims that arise from Davidson’s conduct that we have concluded, for purposes of this appeal, is protected by the anti-SLAPP statute (in that it is not illegal as a matter of law).  We accordingly need not analyze the issue; we can and do hold there was no error in granting the anti-SLAPP motion as to those claims.”  (Plaintiff’s RJN, Ex. A, July 26, 2023 Opinion, p. 16.) 

Defendants Pacquiao and Davidson argue the 9th cause of action fails to state a claim against them to the extent based on Davidson’s conduct, because those statements are protected by the litigation privilege. Defendants argue the finding of litigation privilege applies to them based on the doctrine of law of the case. 

Even if Davidson’s statements are protected by the litigation privilege, Plaintiff’s IIED claim is based on conduct other than Davidson’s statements. (FAC, ¶101.  As made clear in the July 26, 2023 opinion, the 8th and 9th causes of action for attempted extortion and IIED were based on Davidson’s protected statements and unprotected “conduct of Wildcard gym members, Palmer, or other nonparties.”  (Plaintiff’s RJN, Ex. A, July 26, 2023 Opinion, p. 22.)  The Court of Appeal reversed the ruling to the extent it granted Showtime/CBS’s SLAPP motion as to the entirety of the 8th and 9th causes of action for this precise reason:  “The trial court’s order is reversed, and the cause is remanded with directions to enter a new and different order granting the CBS Defendants’ special motion to strike the eighth and ninth causes of action (including the general allegations incorporated therein) except insofar as those causes of action include claims for vicarious liability based on the conduct of ‘Wildcard Gym members,” Palmer, or other nonparties.’” (Plaintiff’s RJN, Ex. A, July 26, 2023 Opinion, p. 22.)

As discussed below in connection with the remaining IIED claim, a cause of action is stated based solely on the alleged conduct of “Wildcard Gym members, Palmer, or other nonparties” physically intimidating and harassing Rueda.  Thus, even if the Davidson statements are protected by the litigation privilege as to both Pacquiao and Davidson, there remains a portion of the IIED claim that is properly stated.  A general demurrer cannot be brought to a part of a cause of action.  If there are sufficient allegations to entitle plaintiff to relief, other allegations cannot be challenged by general demurrer.  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1167 (disapproved on other grounds by Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 948.) 

Moreover, Defendants fail to establish that the law of the case doctrine applies to them.  “The decision of an appellate court, stating a rule of law necessary to the decision of the case, conclusively establishes that rule and makes it determinative of the rights of the same parties in any subsequent retrial or appeal in the same case.”  (9 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), Appeal § 480.)  Law of the case does not apply to issues not analyzed by the prior appeal or necessary thereto. (Morohoshi v. Pacific Home (2004) 34 Cal.4th 482, 491-491 (law of the case precluded plaintiff from taking issue with holding that defendant was vicariously liable under the rationale of nondelegable duty for negligence of co-defendant, which was decided in prior appeal, but law of the case did not apply to issue of whether same defendant could be held vicariously liable for same co-defendant’s breach of co-defendant’s own duty of care, which was not analyzed in prior appeal).)

“[L]aw of the case does not apply to arguments that might have been but were not presented and resolved on an earlier appeal.”  (Leider v. Lewis (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1121, 1130.)  “A most perplexing problem arises where the point could have been determined in the first appeal, but counsel did not raise it and the appellate court's opinion does not expressly mention it. May a point be deemed impliedly decided for the purpose of the doctrine of the law of the case?  The general rule holds that the doctrine is applicable only to points raised below. In other words, stated negatively: ‘The doctrine of the law of the case (in this state) does not extend to the facts or the points of law which might have been but were not presented and determined on a prior appeal.’”  (9 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), §497.) 

Law of the case also only applies to “those parties who appealed and whose rights were affected by that prior appeal.”  (Bigbee v. Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. (1983) 34 Cal.3d 49, 57.)  Thus, in Bigbee, law of the case based on a prior appeal brought by two defendants did not apply to remaining two co-defendants, who were not parties to the prior appeal.  (Id.)  “Pacific Telephone and Western Electric were the only defendants who were parties to the Bigbee I proceeding. The rights of Western Industrial and Decker were not determined in that proceeding. Therefore, in order to determine whether summary judgment was properly granted as to them, this court must consider anew the question of foreseeability.”  (Id.)

As Plaintiff points out, neither Davidson nor Pacquiao were parties to Rueda’s appeal of the Showtime/CBS SLAPP motion.  On reply, Pacquiao fails to offer any satisfactory response to this argument.  On reply, Davidson argues Plaintiff should be estopped from denying that the litigation privilege protects the alleged statements.  Davidson also argues his rights were encompassed by the July 26, 2023 Opinion.  Defendants fail to adequately respond to Plaintiff’s argument that the “same parties” requirement of law of the case is not met.  The requirement is not “same parties or whose rights were affected by that prior appeal.”  The requirement is those parties who appealed and whose rights were affected by that prior appeal.”  Defendants fail to offer any authority holding otherwise.

Defendants argue the Court of Appeal did not limit its order striking the Davidson statements and the general incorporated allegations from the FAC to Showtime/CBS.  Defendants argue it would be ridiculous to say that the order only applied to Showtime/CBS.  The Court disagrees.  The motion was filed by Showtime/CBS and the relief granted would only logically be as to Showtime/CBS. 

This is not to say that there is no legal doctrine pursuant to which Defendants Pacquiao and Davidson could take advantage of Judge Soleto’s second prong determinations on the Showtime/CBS SLAPP motion.  The Court only finds that Defendants Pacquiao and Davidson have not raised any such legal doctrine on this motion for judgment on the pleadings. 

(3)  The IIED sufficiently states a claim against Pacquiao and Davidson

Defendants argue the IIED claim fails against them, because Plaintiff fails to allege any facts that would make them vicariously liable for the conduct of third parties, e.g. the Wildcat members.  The IIED claim incorporates ¶¶1-45 and 91-99 of the FAC.  Even if the July 26, 2023 opinion applied to Defendants Pacquiao and Davidson, the Court of Appeal only struck the incorporated “general allegations.”  The Court interprets “general allegations” to refer to ¶¶1-45. 

Based on the allegations in support of the IIED claim, Plaintiff alleges DOES 1-6 and “goons from the Wildcard Gym” “harassed, stalked, best and terrorized Rueda on behalf of Roach and Paquiao.  (FAC, ¶92.)  Plaintiff alleges, “Roach and Pacquiao knew their agents Davidson, DOES 1-6 and the goons from the Wildcard Gym would make the above described threats or take the above-described actions.”  (FAC, ¶94.)  Plaintiff alleges Pacquiao and Davidson intentionally caused Plaintiff to suffer severe emotional distress by “threat[en]ing…physical harm to himself and property…and causing him to live in fear for his safety and livelihood.”  (FAC, ¶101.)  Plaintiff alleges he “felt fearful for his personal and physical safety.  He could not go out in public for fear that Wildcard Gym members and Pacquiao’s Posse would physically attack him at anytime.”  (FAC, ¶102.)  Plaintiff also alleges that the DOE defendants were acting as agents of the other named Defendants. (FAC, ¶12.)  Plaintiff specifically alleges the threatening and menacing behavior at ¶42, that the behavior was carried out by persons he recognized as being members of the Wildcard Gym owned by Roach and patronized by Pacquiao’s fans.  (FAC, ¶42.)  Plaintiff alleges the menacing behavior occurred just after Davidson approached him with an offer to settle his claims, and he believes was intended to intimidate him into accepting Davidson’s offer.  (Id. at ¶¶41 and 44.) 

“Under the doctrine of respondeat superior, the innocent principal or employer is liable for the torts of the agent or employee, committed while acting within the scope of employment. It is immaterial that the employee acts in excess of authority or contrary to instructions.”  (3 Witkin, Summary (11th ed. 2020), Agency §175.)  “The courts have approved a general statement to the effect that the wrongdoer was the agent or employee of the defendant principal, and “was acting in the scope of his employment.  [¶] The general averment is sufficient not only in negligence cases but also where the cause of action is based on an intentional tort.”   (5 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), ¶918. 

“An allegation of agency constitutes an averment of ultimate fact, which we accept as true on a demurrer.  But where factual allegations are based on information and belief, the plaintiff must allege information that leads the plaintiff to believe that the allegations are true.”  (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1077, 1106.)  “[G]eneral allegations of agency that are contradicted by, or inconsistent with, facts specifically pleaded, will be disregarded.”  (5 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), Plead §918.)

For purposes of this motion for judgment on the pleadings, the Court accepts as true Plaintiff’s allegation that the DOE defendants, i.e. including the “goons” from Wildcard Gym, were agents of the named Defendants, including Pacquiao and Davidson.  This allegation of ultimate fact must be accepted as true, even if it is a general allegation and regardless of Plaintiff’s ultimate ability to prove it.  Indeed, “we accept as true even improbable alleged facts, and we do not concern ourselves with the plaintiff's ability to prove the factual allegations.”  (Marina Pacific Hotel and Suites, LLC v. Fireman's Fund Insurance Company (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 96, 104–105.) 

Moreover, Plaintiff is not alleging an ordinary agency akin to a real estate broker or an employee.  Plaintiff is alleging that Defendants Pacquiao and Davidson instructed these DOEs to illegally intimidate and harass Plaintiff into accepting the settlement offer proposed by Davidson.  Plaintiff supports his general allegation of agency with specific allegations of the circumstances indicating that the “goons” were acting on behalf of Pacquiao and Davidson.  Whether Plaintiff can prove the claims is of course not germane to a decision at this stage of the case. 

Defendant Pacquiao’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings of the 9th cause of action for IIED is DENIED. Defendant Davidson’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings of the 9th cause of action for IIED is DENIED.   

 

Conclusion

Defendant Pacquiao’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion and DENIED as to the 9th cause of action for IIED is DENIED.

Defendant Davidson’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings of the 9th cause of action is GRANTED as to the 8th cause of action for attempted extortion and DENIED as to the 9th cause of action for IIED.
