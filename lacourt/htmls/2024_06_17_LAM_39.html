DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV34326A    Hearing Date: June 17, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 17, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21STCV34326

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant, Cross-Plaintiff, and Cross-Defendant Purchasing 411, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendants and Cross-Defendants Gredale LLC and Stop C-19 LLC

 

 

BACKGROUND

Defendant, Cross-Plaintiff, and Cross-Defendant Purchasing 411, Inc., which the parties refer to as Royalty Distribution, negotiated a purchase of gloves from Defendant, Cross-Plaintiff, and Cross-Defendant Stop C-19 LLC (“Stop C-19”).  Royalty Distribution sent a deposit to Stop C-19, which transferred the deposit to Defendant and Cross-Defendant Gredale LLC (“Gredale”).  Royalty Distribution contends Gredale and Stop C-19 failed to deliver the gloves and wrongfully retained Royal Distribution’s deposit.  Royalty Distribution moves for summary adjudication of its claims for money had and received and conversion against Gredale and Stop C-19.  Royalty Distribution also moves for summary judgment on Gredale and Stop C-19’s cross-complaint against Royalty Distribution, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action therein.  Gredale and Stop C-19 oppose the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. . . . There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) 

DISCUSSION

Royalty Distribution’s second cause of action for money had and received

To state a claim for money had and received against Gredale and Stop C-19, Royalty Distribution must show that they owe it a certain sum that they obtained for Royalty Distribution’s benefit.  (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460.)

Royalty Distribution presents evidence it paid Stop C-19 $3,952,000 when it placed two orders with Stop C-19 for nitrile exam gloves.  (Declaration of Jonathan Noori, ¶¶ 5-6, Exhibits F-G.)  Royalty Distribution also asserts, in response to a request from Gredale, Royalty Distribution made a deposit for its purchases.  (Declaration of Jonathan Noori, ¶ 9, Exhibit A.)  Gredale and Stop C-19 partially refunded Royalty Distribution but retained the unused deposit in the amount of $1,173, 250, despite their inability to provide the gloves Royalty Distribution ordered.  (Declaration of Jonathan Noori, ¶¶ 11, 13, Exhibits E, J; Declaration of Arman Shatrang, ¶¶ 4-6.) 

This evidence meets Royalty Distribution’s burden of showing Gredale and Stop C-19 are in possession of the unused deposit, which belongs to Royalty Distribution, and have no defense to Royalty Distribution’s claim for money had and received.  Thus, Royalty Distribution has shifted the burden to Gredale and Stop C-19 to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether they have affirmative defenses to Royalty Distribution’s claim.

In opposition, Gredale and Stop C-19 argue Stop C-19’s performance was impossible.  But, as pointed out by Royalty Distribution, Gredale and Stop C-19 did not plead this defense in their answer to Royalty Distribution’s cross-complaint.  In failing to do so, Gredale and Stop C-19 waived he impossibility and impracticability defenses.  (See California Academy of Sciences v. County of Fresno (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1436, 1442 [“A party who fails to plead affirmative defenses waives them”].)  Also, it is established the impossibility and impracticability defenses does not apply to noncontract causes of action.  (See West Pueblo Partners, LLC v. Stone Brewing Co., LLC (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1179, 1188 [“the doctrines of impossibility and impracticability . . . are common law defenses to contract performance”].) Thus, those defenses would not apply to Royalty Distribution’s action for money had and received. 

The circumstances in this case do not permit Gredale and Stop C-19 to retain Royalty Distribution’s unused deposit regardless of it being impossible or impractical for those parties to perform under the agreement. 

Gredale and Stop C-19 contends Royalty Distribution cancelled the contract with Stop C-19 due to delays that resulted from pandemic closures in China and port congestion.  In this regard, they rely on Commercial Code section 2615, which concerns impracticality.  Even if the court assumes Gredale’s and Stop C-19’s contention in this regard were correct, the delays entitled Royalty Distribution to cancel the contract, and it was not foreclosed from demanding Gredale and Stop C-19 return its deposit.  (See Cal. Com. Code, § 2616, subd. (1) [“Where the buyer receives notification of a material or indefinite delay  . . . he may by written notification to the seller as to any delivery concerned . . . (a) Terminate and thereby discharge any unexecuted portion of the contract”]), and it was not prevented from seeking reimbursement.    

Gredale and Stop C-19, therefore, fail to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether they have affirmative defenses to Royalty Distribution’s claim for money had and received.

Royalty Distribution’s seventh cause of action for conversion

To state a cause of action for conversion, Royalty Distribution must allege it has a right of ownership of an identifiable sum of money in Gredale’s and Stop C-19’s possession.  (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119;  PCO, Inc. v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro, LLP (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 384, 395, 397.) 

As set forth above, Royalty Distribution has presented evidence Gredale and Stop C-19 are in possession of the unused deposit, which belongs to Royalty Distribution.  Royalty Distribution has met its burden by showing Gredale and Stop C-19 have no defense to Royalty Distribution’s claim for conversion.  Royalty Distribution then has shifted the burden to Gredale and Stop C-19 to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether they have affirmative defenses to Royalty Distribution’s claim for conversion.

            In opposition, Gredale and Stop C-19 rely on the doctrines of impossibility and impracticality.  Again, these are defenses to breach of contract claims, not a conversion cause of action.  However, even if these doctrines applied, they do not excuse Gredale and Stop C-19  retaining Royalty Distribution’s deposit.  Thus, Gredale and Stop C-19 fail to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether they have affirmative defenses to Royalty Distribution’s claim for conversion.

Gredale’s and Stop C-19’s first cause of action for breach of contract in its cross-complaint

To state a cause of action for breach of contract against Royalty Distribution, Gredale and Stop C-19 must allege the existence of a contract between the parties, Gredale's and Stop C-19’s performance under the contract or an excuse for its nonperformance, Royalty Distribution’s breach of the contract, and resulting damages.  (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. N. Y. Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)As set forth above, Royalty Distribution has presented evidence Gredale and Stop C-19 failed to perform under the contract, as they did not provide the gloves Royalty Distribution ordered.  It was that inability on Gredale's and Stop C-19’s part that compelled Royalty Distribution to seek to be reimbursed the deposit it had provided them.  This evidence meets Royalty Distribution’s burden to show Gredale and Stop C-19 cannot prevail on their claim for breach of contract when they failed to perform themselves.
Royalty Distribution, therefore, had effectively shifted the burden to Gredale and Stop C-19 to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether they could prevail on the breach of contract cause of action.  Further, Royalty Distribution’s evidence that Gredalet and Stop C-19 gave it a partial refund and committed to providing a full refund establishes Gredale and Stop C-19 waived Royalty Distribution’s performance under the contract. Gredale and Stop C-19 have not refuted this.  The fact Gredale and Stop C-19 did not intend to hold Royalty Distribution to performing under the contract undermines any contention they were willing and able to fulfill their  commitments, and this further satisfies Royalty Distribution’s burden of showing Gredale and Stop C-19 cannot prevail on their claim for breach of contract. 

Given these circumstances, Gredale and Stop C-19’s reliance on the doctrines of impossibility and impracticality in their opposition to Royalty Distribution’s motion is unavailing.  For the reasons set forth above, Royalty Distribution was not precluded from canceling the agreement and demanding a return of the unused deposit.  Gredale's and Stop C-!9’s contentions regarding the impossibility of performing the agreement would serve to possibly excuse their own failures to perform under the agreement, but it would not impact any claim Royalty Distribution itself breached the agreement.  Moreover, even if impossibility or impracticality excused Gredale and Stop C-19 from performing under the agreement, that would not lead to Royalty Distribution having to pay them under the terms of the parties’ contact, especially when there is no contention or proof the parties expressly agreed to terms that called for such payment under those circumstances.

Therefore, Gredale and Stop C-19 fail to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether they can prevail on their claim for breach of contract against Royalty Distribution.

Gredale’s and Stop C-19’s second cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations

Royalty Distribution purports to move for summary adjudication of Gredale’s and  Stop C-19’s second cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations.  However, Gredale and Stop C-19 assert that cause of action against Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant JG Medical, LLC, not Royalty Distribution.  Therefore, we do not consider its applicability to Royalty Distribution.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Gredale and Stop C-19 fail to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Royalty Distribution can prevail against them on its causes of action for money had and received and conversion and as to whether Gredale and Stop C-19 can prevail on its cause of action for breach of contract against Royalty Distribution.  Therefore, the Court grants Royalty Distribution’s motion for summary adjudication of its causes of action for money had and received and conversion and its motion for summary judgment on Stop C-19’s cross-complaint alleging breach of contract against Royalty Distribution. 

Royalty Distribution is ordered to give notice of the court’s order and to file a proof of service of same.

MOTION

Motion for Sanctions

MOVING PARTY

Defendant, Cross-Plaintiff, and Cross-Defendant Purchasing 411, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendants and Cross-Defendants Gredale LLC and Stop C-19 LLC

 

MOTION

Defendant, Cross-Plaintiff, and Cross-Defendant Purchasing 411, Inc., which the parties refer to as Royalty Distribution, negotiated a purchase of gloves from Defendant, Cross-Defendant, and Cross-Plaintiff Stop C-19 LLC (“Stop C-19”).  Royalty Distribution sent a deposit to Stop C-19, which transferred the deposit to Gredale LLC (“Gredale”).  Royalty Distribution contends Stop C-19 and Gredale failed to deliver the gloves and wrongfully retained Royal Distribution’s deposit.  Royalty Distribution moves for monetary sanctions against Gredale and Stop C-19.  Royalty Distribution also moves for an order to show cause why Gredale and Stop C-19 should not be held in contempt of court.  Gredale and Stop C-19 oppose the motion.

 

ANALYSIS

In a contempt proceeding, “an affidavit must be presented to the court stating the facts constituting the contempt, an order to show cause must be issued, and hearing on the facts must be held by the judge.” (Arthur v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1965) 62 Cal.2d 404, 407–408.)  When the court receives the affidavit, it issues an order to show cause (OSC) why the person should not be held in contempt.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1212.)  The alleged contemnor may respond to the affidavit and OSC by submitting counter affidavits or declarations.  (Lyon v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1968) 68 Cal.2d 446, 452.) 

In its order of November 15, 2023, the court ordered Gredale and Stop C-19 to pay sanctions in the amount of $3,000 to Royalty Distribution within 30 days.  To date, Gredale and Stop C-19 have not paid the sanctions.  (Declaration of Dan Terzian, ¶ 16.)  However, to be punishable as contempt, the failure of Gredale and Stop C-19 to pay the sanctions must be a conscious refusal, meaning Gredale and Stop C-19 are able to pay the sanctions, but they refuse to do so.  (Chapman v. Superior Court (1968) 261 Cal.App.2d 194, 200.) 

Royalty Distribution’s affidavit in support of the motion must recite the specific facts supporting the conclusion Gredale and Stop C-19 are in contempt of court, including their ability to pay the sanctions.  (See In re de la Parra (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 139, 144.)  In connection with its moving papers, Royalty Distribution does not advance any evidence regarding the financial condition of Gredale and Stop C-19 to show they can pay the sanctions.  As such, the court will not issue the order to show cause why the court should not hold Gredale and Stop C-19 in contempt.  While Royalty Distribution purports to present evidence of Stop C-19’s financial condition in its reply brief, the court will not consider that evidence, as Gredale and Stop C-19 have not had the opportunity to respond to it.  (See In re Marriage of Hoffmeister (1984) 161 Cal.App.3d 1163, 1171.)

Royalty Distribution also seeks sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.  That statute permits the court to award attorney fees a party incurred “as a result of bad-faith actions or tactics that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (a).)  There is no evidence before the court of Gredale’s and Stop C-19’s failure to pay the sanctions was in bad faith or frivolous, or that it was designed to cause unnecessary delay.  The Court, therefore, declines to award sanctions under Section 128.5.

The Court also declines to impose additional monetary sanctions under the Civil Discovery Act.  The court finds the only purpose of such sanctions in this instance would be punitive, which is improper.  (See Ghanooni v. Super Shuttle of Los Angeles (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 256, 262.)  Royalty Distribution’s remedy is to enforce the prior sanctions order as a monetary judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 680.230. 680.270, 699.510, subd. (a); see also Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615 [because a monetary sanction order has the force and effect of a money judgment, it is enforceable through the execution of judgment laws].)

Royalty Distribution is ordered to give notice of this order and file proof of service of same.
