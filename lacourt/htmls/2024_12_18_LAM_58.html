DEPARTMENT 58 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCV05755    Hearing Date: December 18, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce G. Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             December 18, 2024

Case Name:                Phillips v. Fidler

Case No.:                    24STCV05755

Matter:                        Motion to Consolidate

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Emir Phillips

Responding Party:      Defendants Gary Fidler, Esq. and Gary D. Fidler, APLC

Tentative Ruling:      The Motion to Consolidate is denied.

 

 

This is defamation action. The Complaint alleges causes of action for defamation, false light, negligent and intentional emotional distress.

 

On March 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Related Case seeking to relate this action with case number 24SMUD00429.

 

Now, Plaintiff Emir Phillips seeks to consolidate this action with 24SMUD00429. Defendants Gary Fidler, Esq. and Gary D. Fidler, APLC filed an opposition. Defendants Barham 22nd Street, LLC and Amanda Robertson filed a joinder to the opposition.

 

The motion to consolidate is denied.

 

DISCUSSION

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court has not related these matters; if the matters have not been related, they cannot be consolidated. 

 

Local Rule 3.3(g) sets forth the procedural requirements for motions to consolidate applicable to the instant case; it states, in relevant part:

 

“Cases may not be consolidated unless they are in the same department. A motion to consolidate two or more cases may be noticed and heard after the cases, initially filed in different departments, have been related into a single department, or if the cases were already assigned to that department.”

 

Plaintiff Phillips seek to consolidate these two cases on the grounds that they involve common questions of law and fact. Further, Plaintiff contends there is an overlap between the parties. Lastly, Plaintiff argues that consolidating these cases will avoid unnecessary costs and delay, and avoid the potential of inconsistent judgments.

 

The trial court has discretion to consolidate actions involving common questions of law or fact. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048.) The purpose of consolidation is “to promote trial convenience and economy by avoiding duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both actions.” (Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485.)   

 

Consolidation is appropriate when the actions involve common questions of law or fact and to avoid unnecessary costs or delay. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (a).) In deciding whether to grant a motion to consolidate, the court should weigh whether the common issues predominate over the individual issues and whether any risks of jury confusion or prejudice to the parties outweighs the reduction in time and expense that would result from consolidation. (Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 979.)¿ 

 

The Court declines to relate these two matters. Even had they been related, the Court would deny the motion to consolidate. Each case involves different parties. More importantly, there are no overlapping legal issues and no risk of conflicting judgments or waste of judicial resources.

 

On February 22, 2024, Defendants Gary Fidler, Esq. and Gary D. Fidler & Associates, APLC filed an Unlawful Detainer action at the Santa Monica Courthouse, Case No. 24SMUD00429 (UD Action) on behalf of their client, Defendant Barham 22nd Street, LLC, the owner of the subject property, against Plaintiff Phillips, as well as two other defendants, Hannah Mock and Elena Escudero.

 

Then, on March 7, 2024, Plaintiff Phillips filed the present civil lawsuit against Defendants Gary Fidler, Esq., Gary D. Fidler, APLC, Barham 22nd Street, LLC and Amanda Robertson, alleging various causes of action including defamation, false light, negligent and intentional infliction of emotional distress, and invasion of privacy. In response, Defendants filed an anti-SLAPP motion, which the court granted as to all causes of action except the invasion of privacy claim.

 

As noted above, the parties are not identical although there is some overlap. Further, the issues involved in the unlawful detainer action and invasion of privacy cause of action do not involve similar legal questions or underlying evidence. Thus, consolidation would not promote judicial economy.

 

Additionally, whereas here, the case to be consolidated is an unlawful detainer action,  consolidation would result in removing the summary proceeding nature of the unlawful detainer action. In Martin–Bragg v. Moore, the court explained that an action for unlawful detainer can co-exist with other causes of action “only ‘so long as the entire case is treated as an ordinary civil action, not as a summary proceeding.’ ” (Martin-Bragg v. Moore (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 367, 387; Lynch & Freytag v. Cooper (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 603, 608.) Because the civil action here does not involve questions of title or ownership that would directly inform on the UD Action, removing the summary proceeding nature of the UD action is not justified. (Cf. Martin–Bragg v. Moore, supra, 219 Cal.App.4th at 393-396.) Thus, the motion to consolidate must be denied for these substantive reasons.

 

Conclusion

 

 Accordingly, the motion to consolidate is denied.


Case Number: 24STCV19837    Hearing Date: December 18, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce G. Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             December 18, 2024

Case Name:                Los Angeles Police Protective League v. Carranza

Case No.:                    24STCV19837

Matter:                        (1.) Demurrer

                                    (2.) Demurrer

Moving Party:             (1.) Defendant Lilian Carranza

                                    (2.) Defendant Marc Reina

Responding Party:      (1.) Plaintiff Los Angeles Police Protective League

                                    (2.) Plaintiff Los Angeles Police Protective League    

Tentative Ruling:      (1.) Defendant Lilian Carranza’s demurrer is sustained.

                                    (2.) Defendant Marc Reina’s demurrer is sustained.

 

            Plaintiff Los Angeles Police Protective League (LAPPL), an employee organization, commenced this action against Defendants Lillian Carranza (Carranza) and Marc Reina (Reina). Plaintiff alleges Defendants Carranza and Reina both attempted, through fraudulent and unlawful means, to complete surveys and receive emailed communications from the LAPPL that both knew they were not entitled to receive. The operative pleading, the First Amended Complaint (FAC), alleges causes of action for (1.) violations of Penal Code section 502, (2.) unfair competition, (3.) fraud, (4.) violations of Penal Code section 502, (5.) unfair competition, (6.) fraud, and (7.) conspiracy.

 

            In October 2024 Defendants filed separate Special Motions to Strike the Complaint under the anti-SLAPP statute, Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16. On December 17, 2024, the Court heard argument on both motions.  The special motions to strike were denied.

 

            Now, Defendants Carranza and Reina separately demur to the Complaint. Plaintiff opposed both demurrers.

 

            The demurrers are sustained in their entirety.

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “ ‘ “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law . . . .” ’ ”  (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

Discussion

 

Subject Matter Jurisdiction:

 

Defendants argue the Court lacks subject matter jurisdiction to adjudicate the claims in the FAC because they involve the collective bargaining process under the Meyers–Milias–Brown Act (MMBA) and must first be adjudicated by Public Employment Relations Board (PERB). As such, they maintain, the FAC is barred.

 

Government Code section 3509 is the statute governing PERB’s exclusive jurisdiction. Subdivision (b) of section 3509 provides in part, “A complaint alleging any violation of this chapter or of any rules and regulations adopted by a public agency pursuant to Section 3507 or 3507.5 shall be processed as an unfair labor practice charge by the board. The initial determination as to whether the charge of unfair practice is justified and, if so, the appropriate remedy necessary to effectuate the purposes of this chapter, shall be a matter within the exclusive jurisdiction of the board.”

 

The general scope of PERB's exclusive jurisdiction has been described by the California Supreme Court as follows: “The administrative agency authorized to adjudicate unfair labor practice charges under the MMBA is [PERB]. Subject to certain exceptions, local public agencies and their employees must exhaust their administrative remedies under the MMBA by applying to PERB for relief before they can ask a court to intervene in a labor dispute.” (City of San Jose v. Operating Engineers Local Union No. 3 (2010) 49 Cal.4th 597, 601.)

 

Where, as here, the issue is whether the superior court or PERB has exclusive initial jurisdiction over a labor dispute, “what matters is whether the underlying conduct on which the suit is based—however described in the complaint—may fall within PERB’s exclusive jurisdiction. [Citations.]” (El Rancho Unified School Dist. v. National Education Assn. (1983) 33 Cal.3d 946, 954, fn. 13.)

 

Defendants argue that the FAC presents a labor issue implicating collective bargaining agreements between the City of Los Angeles, the Plaintiff, and the Defendants.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff disputes this characterization, arguing that the FAC alleges violations of the Penal Code, the Business and Professions Code, and fraud, all of which exist independent of, and are distinct from, any potential unfair labor practice claim.

 

Contrary to Plaintiff’s argument, the label of the cause of action does not control. However, importantly, the substance underlying these causes of action do not fall within PERB’s exclusive jurisdiction. That is, Plaintiff does not assert that Defendants’ conduct violates the MMBA or any labor practice. Rather, the allegations involve unlawful access to LAPPL’s private emails and participation in surveys for the purely personal interests of Defendants. That Plaintiff is an employee association and Defendants are employers/managers is incidental to the claims alleged.

 

The demurrers on the grounds of lack of jurisdiction and exhaustion are overruled.

 

First and Fourth Causes of Action for Violation of Penal Code Section 502:

 

            Defendants argue that the first and fourth causes of action for violation of Penal Code section 502 fails because the allegations do not state a claim, the claims are barred by governmental immunities, and the statute of limitations.

 

Penal Code section 502 of the Computer Data Access and Fraud Act (CDAFA) broadly prohibits accessing, use, or copying of computerized data, but requires that such conduct be “without permission.” (Pen. Code, § 502, subd. (c).)

 

            Specifically, Penal Code section 502, subdivision (c), describes multiple offenses related to the use of computers and computer data. An offender under this expansive provision includes one who “(1) Knowingly accesses and without permission alters, damages, deletes, destroys, or otherwise uses any data, computer, computer system, or computer network in order to either (A) devise or execute any scheme or artifice to defraud, deceive, or extort, or (B) wrongfully control or obtain money, property, or data. [¶] (2) Knowingly accesses and without permission takes, copies, or makes use of any data from a computer, computer system, or computer network, or takes or copies any supporting documentation, whether existing or residing internal or external to a computer, computer system, or computer network. [¶] (3) Knowingly and without permission uses or causes to be used computer services. [¶] (4) Knowingly accesses and without permission adds, alters, damages, deletes, or destroys any data, computer software, or computer programs which reside or exist internal or external to a computer, computer system, or computer network. [¶] (5) Knowingly and without permission disrupts or causes the disruption of computer services or denies or causes the denial of computer services to an authorized user of a computer, computer system, or computer network. [¶] (6) Knowingly and without permission provides or assists in providing a means of accessing a computer, computer system, or computer network in violation of this section. [¶] [or] (7) Knowingly and without permission accesses or causes to be accessed any computer, computer system, or computer network.”

 

First, Defendants argue that there are no allegations in the FAC that Defendants took, copied, used or accessed any specific data in Plaintiff LAPPL’s computers or networks.

 

Not so. The FAC alleges that Defendants accessed Plaintiff’s computer data without permission; it is alleged that they completed at least one survey and transmitted it to the LAPPL after utilizing false credentials.

 

            Next, Defendants next contend that that the allegations do not show a violation of Penal Code section 502 because Plaintiff LAPPL has not alleged that it sustained an injury as defined by the statute.

 

            Section 502 defines “injury” as “any alteration, deletion, damage, or destruction of a computer system, computer network, computer program, or data caused by the access, or the denial of access to legitimate users.”

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues the FAC alleges that Defendants submitted survey results utilizing false credentials, thereby altering the survey results and damaging their effectiveness. Plaintiff argues that these allegations meet the statutory definition of altering or damaging the data compiled by the LAPPL from its members.

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to allege the requisite injury. The only “injury” alleged as result of Defendants’ actions under Section 502 are that they participated in the survey which produced “data” after completion, but is not in itself deletion of or damage to data as defined by the statute.[1]

 

Courts have held that “damage or loss” under this statutory scheme “contemplates some damage to the computer system, network, program, or data contained on that computer, as opposed to data generated by a plaintiff while engaging with a defendant's website.” (See Heiting v. Taro Pharm. USA, Inc. (C.D. Cal. 2023) 709 F. Supp. 3d 1007, 1021 [emphasis added] [rejecting the argument that the plaintiff's loss of data, including “IP address information and other identifying information,” is “damage” or “loss” under the CDAFA]; see also In re Google Inc. Cookie Placement Consumer Privacy Litigation (3d Cir. 2015) 806 F.3d 125, 148-149, 152 [rejecting the plaintiffs' argument that they suffered loss under the CDAFA through the defendants' “capturing and making economic use of [the plaintiffs' personally identifiable information]” thus “depriving the plaintiffs of their own ability to sell their internet usage information”); Cottle v. Plaid Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2021) 536 F.Supp.3d 461, 488 [holding damage or loss under the CDAFA did not include “loss of the right to control [the plaintiffs'] own data, the loss of the value of their data, and the loss of the right to protection of the data”]; Doe v. Meta Platforms, Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2023) 690 F.Supp.3d 1064, 1081-1083 [holding the plaintiffs' allegations that their protected information was diminished in value did not qualify as damage or loss under the CDAFA]; Pratt v. Higgins (N.D. Cal., July 17, 2023, No. 22-CV-04228-HSG) 2023 WL 4564551, at *9 [finding the defendant's access to the plaintiff's communications, notes and medical information did not create the type of loss contemplated by the CDAFA's private cause of action].)

            The demurrers to the Penal Code section 502 causes of action are sustained.

 

Defendants also argue that the claim fails because a defendant cannot face a civil lawsuit for violations of Section 502 until he or she has been criminally prosecuted and convicted. Defendants cite no legal authority for this proposition, aside from the California State Administrative Manual, section 5300.2. Importantly, however, the plain language of the statute does not support Defendants’ interpretation. Section 502 does not require – as a condition precedent to civil liability – a criminal conviction.

 

The demurrers on this ground are overruled. 

Governmental Immunities:

 

Defendants also argue that their statements in completing the subject surveys are protected by the prosecutorial and discretionary immunities provided in Government Code Sections 818.2, 820.4, 821 and 821.6. Defendants provided legal analysis only with respect to Government Code section 821.6.

 

            Government Code section 821.6 provides that public employees are not “liable for injury caused by his instituting or prosecuting any judicial or administrative proceeding within the scope of his employment, even if he acts maliciously and without probable cause.” (Gov. Code, § 821.6; Richardson-Tunnell v. Schools Ins. Program for Employees (SIPE) (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 1056, 1062.) “California courts construe section 821.6 broadly in furtherance of its purpose to protect public employees in the performance of their prosecutorial duties from the threat of harassment through civil suits.” (Gillan v. City of San Marino (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1048.) “Investigations are considered to be part of judicial and administrative proceedings for purposes of section 821.6 immunity.” (Richardson-Tunnell v. Schools Ins. Program for Employees (SIPE), supra, 157 Cal.App.4th at 1062.)

 

Defendants argue that, as commanding officers, Defendants completed the subject surveys as a means of addressing complaints within the department and furthering policies of fairness and integrity within the LAPD; as a result, Defendants’ actions are protected by discretionary immunity.

 

This argument is not well taken. Defendants point to no specific allegations in the FAC that support Defendants’ assertion that their participation in the surveys were part of either an ongoing judicial or administrative proceeding. While such facts may be developed through discovery (although it seems unlikely that the acts alleged in the complaint fell within Defendants’ job duties with respect to an official proceeding), they are not alleged in the complaint. At the pleading stage, Defendants’ immunity argument fails.

 

The demurrers based on immunity are overruled.

 

Statute of Limitations:

 

Defendants also argue that causes of action are barred by the statute of limitations. First, Defendants argue that the alleged conduct falls outside of Penal Code section 502’s three-year statute of limitations. The section specifically states that no plaintiff may bring an action under Penal Code section 502 “unless it is initiated within three years of the date of the act complained of, or the date of the discovery of the damage, whichever is later.” (Pen. Code, § 502, subd. (e)(5).)

 

The statute of limitations for fraud is also three years. (Code Civ. Proc., § § 338, subd. (d).) Lastly, the statute of limitations for liability under Business and Professions Code section 17200 is four years. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17208.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants began accessing its emails in or around 2016, more than eight years before Plaintiff brought this lawsuit and far outside the statute of limitations for any of the causes of action. (FAC ¶¶ 10-13 [Carranza], ¶ 17 [Reina].)

 

Plaintiff argues that the surveys were intended to be anonymous and thus, there are no allegations or inferences that would establish that completing the survey would be sufficient to give notice to LAPPL of the fraud Defendants were perpetrating. While the FAC indicates the survey was anonymous (FAC ¶ 15), the FAC remains barred by the statute of limitations on its face. Plaintiff has alleged no facts supporting a theory of delayed discovery.

 

The demurrers based on the actions being time-barred are sustained.

 

Third and Sixth Causes of Action for Fraud:

 

            Defendants argue that that the FAC fails to allege how Defendants intended to defraud Plaintiff and how Plaintiff relied on the representation to its detriment.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff contends that it has had to expend fees to identify the intrusion by Defendants and ensure the security of its systems, including the confidentiality of its surveys and their result. (FAC ¶¶ 25, 34, 48, 57.)

 

This alleged injury, however, was not the result of reliance on the representation. It was an action to prevent further injuries in the future. That is, there is no specific allegation that Plaintiff relied on the content of misrepresentation in the survey to its detriment. Rather, in discovering that the integrity of the survey had been compromised, Plaintiff took steps to ensure it would not recur. This is not detrimental reliance.

 

The demurrers to the fraud claim are sustained.

 

Second and Fifth Cause of Action for Violation of Business and Professions Code Section 17200:

 

Business and Professions Code section 17200 prohibits “any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice ….” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A business practice need only satisfy one of the three criteria—unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent—to be considered unfair competition. (McKell v. Wash. Mut., Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1470-1471.)

 

            Here, the FAC alleges a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 based on same facts supporting the violation of Penal Code section 502 and the fraud cause of action. (FAC ¶¶ 30-35, 53-58.)

 

            This claim is derivative of Plaintiff’s previous claims. As such, Plaintiff has failed to allege any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent conduct to support its claim for unfair business practices. The demurrers to the cause of action based on section 17200 are sustained.  

 

Seventh Cause of Action for Conspiracy:

 

            First, as argued by Defendants, “[c]onspiracy is not a cause of action, but a legal doctrine that imposes liability on person who, although not actually committing a tort themselves, share with the immediate tortfeasors a common law or design in its perpetration.” (Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 510.)

 

Because this theory of liability is entirely dependent on the existence of other cognizable claims and it cannot stand without a predicate cause of action – the demurrers to this “cause of action” are sustained.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Carranza’s demurrer to the FAC is sustained. Defendant Reina’s demurrer to the FAC is also sustained. Plaintiff shall have leave to amend. Plaintiff shall serve and file an amended pleading on or before January 20, 2025.
