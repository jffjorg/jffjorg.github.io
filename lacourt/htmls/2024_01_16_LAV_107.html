DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00914    Hearing Date: January 16, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc.

 

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

Pavlovich, et al.

                       

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  22VECV00914                   107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Motion to Compel Further Responses from Defendant Mirko Pavlovich to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (Set One); Request for Monetary Sanctions of $4,650

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Defendant Mirko Pavlovich to Special Interrogatory Nos. 15-35 (Set One) is GRANTED.  Pavlovich is ordered to serve further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 15-35 within 20 days.  Pavlovich and counsel Sina are ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $3560 within 20 days.  Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc. to give notice.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            Plaintiff is suing as assignee of State Compensation Insurance Fund (“Assignor”).  Assignor provided Defendant Mirko Pavlovich with worker’s compensation insurance pursuant to two policies of insurance, Policy Nos. 9107037-17 and 9258320-19.  Defendant Pavlovich breached the insurance policies by failing to pay the premiums due thereon.  Plaintiff alleges an outstanding balance of $94,360.60 on Policy No. 9107037-17 and $23,928.87 on Policy No. 9258320-19. 

 

            On July 1, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint alleging (1) breach of contract; (2) open book account; (3) account stated; and (4) reasonable value.  Defendant Pavlovich filed an answer on November 7, 2022.  A non-jury trial is scheduled for July 1, 2024.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Applicable Law

 

CCP §2030.300

(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

            (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

            (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

            (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.

(b)(1) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

(2) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.

(d) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(e) If a party then fails to obey an order compelling further response to interrogatories, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of, or in addition to, that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

 

Application to Facts

 

            Meet and Confer

 

            Plaintiff served the Special Interrogatories on November 15, 2022 and granted an extension to respond until January 18, 2023.  (Jun Dec., ¶¶1, 2.)  Defendant did not serve his first set of responses until January 27, 2023, after the extended deadline expired.  (Jun Dec., ¶3.)  Parties met and conferred by correspondence and telephonically from February through April 2023 on these initial responses.  Parties attended an IDC with the Court on June 1, 2023.  (Jun Dec., ¶6.)  At the IDC, Defendant agreed to produce further responses by July 7, 2023 and the agreement was set forth in the Court’s July 7, 2023 minute order.  (Jun Dec., ¶6.) 

            Defendant failed to provide further responses by July 7, 2023 in violation of the Court’s June 1, 2023 order.  (Jun Dec., ¶6.)  Defendant finally provided untimely further responses on August 15, 2023.  (Jun Dec., ¶8.)  Parties met and conferred by email and telephonically regarding these further responses from September through November 1, 2023.  (Jun Dec., ¶¶9-10).  At the November 1, 2023 meet and confer, Defendant indicated that Plaintiff should proceed with its motions to compel further responses on discovery and insisted the responses were sufficient.  (Jun Dec., ¶10.) 

            Parties have engaged in extensive meet and confer efforts regarding Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (Set One).  Plaintiff establishes compliance with the meet and confer requirement under CCP §2030.300(b). 

 

Defendant’s responses are insufficient and further responses are required

 

Defendant responded with the same two-sentence paragraph to each of the Special Interrogatories at issue:  “Bates 60-67 provide the names of each employee, the hourly rate and type of labor.  From these documents, Plaintiff can just as easily—if not a lot easier with its vast knowledge of SCIF auditing—as Defendant (who is a mom and pop construction company) prepare an abstract, compilation, summary and data of the figures.” 

 

The one-paragraph response does not fully respond to the interrogatories, which ask that Defendant identify specific errors by the Assignor in connection with the audit of Policy Nos. 9107037-17 and 9258320-19 and information regarding the specific employees and/or independent contractors purportedly hired by Defendant.  Defendant may have been attempting to respond to the interrogatory pursuant to CCP §2030.230, which allows a responding party the option to answer an interrogatory by referring to CCP §2030.230 and specify the writings from which the answer may be derived or ascertained, if “the answer to an interrogatory would necessitate the preparation or the making of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary of or from the documents of the party to whom the interrogatory is directed, and if the burden or expense of preparing or making it would be substantially the same for the party propounding the interrogatory as for the responding party.” 

However, Defendant lost the right to respond pursuant to CCP §2030.230 by failing to timely respond to the special interrogatories:  “If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply:  (a) The party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories…”  Defendant also failed to reference section 2030.230 in his responses.  Defendant’s response is therefore patently insufficient even if he intended to rely on CCP §2030.230. 

 

            Finally, Defendant failed to oppose this motion.  If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories and RFAs. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (Wolcher) (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221; Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Sup.Ct. (Stendell) (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  Defendant failed to justify his failure to fully respond to the special interrogatories. 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatory Nos. 15-35 is GRANTED.  Defendant Pavlovich is ordered to serve responses within 20 days.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $4,560 against Defendant Pavlovich and his counsel, Reza Sina.  The requested sanctions are based on 6 hours of motion preparation, 3 hours of anticipated time to review any opposition, prepare a reply and prepare and attend the hearing on this motion at $500/hr.  No opposition was filed.  Plaintiff is awarded $3,500 in sanctions and $60 in filing fees for a total of $3,560.  

           

CONCLUSION

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Defendant Mirko Pavlovich to Special Interrogatory Nos. 15-35 (Set One) is GRANTED.  Pavlovich is ordered to serve further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 15-35 within 20 days.  Pavlovich and counsel Sina are ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $3560 within 20 days.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Demurrer to Complaint

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

[Tentative] Ruling:   Sustained Demurrer with Leave To Amend as to the Sole Cause of Action.

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Hamid Kohan Darvish filed this Complaint on April 11, 2023, arising from a motor vehicle collision against Defendants Khelika Bell (“Bell”), El Camino Real Charter High School (“Charter School”), Friends of ECR, a Nonprofit Public Benefit Corporation (“FOE”), City of Los Angeles (“City”), and Does 1 through 10 (collectively, “Defendants”). (Complaint p.5.) Plaintiff alleges injuries arising from Defendants’ negligence. (Id.)

 

Plaintiff alleges the following: On November 6, 2022, Plaintiff was on his way to a local high school carnival. (Id.) In attempt to access entrance to the event when he was struck by Defendant Khelika Bell. (Id.) Consequentially, Plaintiff suffered injuries and was rushed to the nearest hospital. (Id.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants El Camino Real Charter High School, Friends of ECR, and the City of Los Angeles, failed to provide safe access, created a substantial increase in local traffic, created an environment of driving distractions for local traffic, and failed to migrate any, and all, safety hazards, created by the event causing injuries and severe damages to Plaintiff. (Id.)

 

On August 24, 2023, Defendant Friends of ECR, a Nonprofit Public Benefit Corporation filed this demurrer without a motion to strike to the Complaint.

 

On December 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed his opposition.

 

On January 8, 2024, Defendant filed its reply.

 

II. MOVING PARTY’S GROUNDS FOR THE DEMURRER

           

            Defendant FOE demurs to the only cause of action for negligence on the grounds that the complaint fails to set forth facts sufficient to state a cause of action against Defendant FOE. (Not. of Dem. p.2-3.) Moreover, the Defendant asserts that the claim is uncertain, vague, and unintelligible because Plaintiff’s allegations do not demonstrate that Defendant owed a duty to Plaintiff. (Id.) Additionally, Defendant asserts that the allegations do not demonstrate any causal connection between Defendant FOE’s alleged acts and Plaintiff being hit by a car. (Id.)

           

III. ANALYSIS

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to¿overrule or sustain a demurrer.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

Here, on August 16, 2023, Defendant FOE’s counsel emailed Plaintiff’s counsel meet and confer letter. (Dec. Jacobson ¶1.) After not receiving a response, on August 17, 2023, Defendant FOE’s counsel called Plaintiff’s counsel. (Id.) Although the parties never met and conferred, Defendant FOE’s counsel’s attempts to meet and confer likely satisfied the requirement.  

 

Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal. App. 4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.)

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal. 3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal. App. 4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal. 2d 240, 245).

 

Negligence

 

Defendant FOE argues that the Court should sustain this Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint because Plaintiff fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action for negligence and the cause of action is uncertain.

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligence are “duty, breach, causation and damages.” (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)¿The complaint alleges that “[o]n November 6, 2022, Plaintiff was on his way to a local high school carnival. . . [I]n attempt to access entrance to the event when he was struck by Defendant Khelika Bell. Consequentially, Plaintiff suffered injuries and was rushed by ambulance to the nearest hospital. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants El Camino Real Charter High School, Friends of ECR, and the City of Los Angeles, failed to provide safe access, created a substantial increase in local traffic, created an environment of driving distractions for local traffic, and failed to migrate any, and all, safety hazards, created by the event causing injuries and severe damages to Plaintiff.” (Complaint p.5.)

 

Although Plaintiff’s complaint attempts to satisfy the causation and damages elements by stating that all Defendants “failed to provide safe access, [which] created a substantial increase in local traffic, created an environment of driving distractions for local traffic, and failed to migrate any, and all, safety hazards, created by the event causing injuries and severe damages to Plaintiff.” (Complaint p.5.) Plaintiff fails to provide sufficient facts to show what duty Defendant FOE owed. For instance, Plaintiff lists all Defendants and states that they “failed to provide safe access” but fails to specify why they Defendant FOE owed a duty in the first place.

 

Defendant FOE argues that Plaintiff failed to show that FOE owed any duty to Plaintiff, or that a causal connection exists between any alleged negligence by FOE and the claimed injuries that Plaintiff alleges were caused by the pedestrian-versus-auto accident. (Mot. to Dem. p.5.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the negligence elements are adequately pled because the paragraph in GN-1 is sufficient to show negligence and causation. (Opp. p.6.) Plaintiff also argues that a claim of ordinary negligence need only be pled in general terms. (Id.) In support, Plaintiff relies on Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC, McGarvey v. Pacific Gas & Electric Company, Bonnano v. Central Contra Costa Transit Authority, and Pedeferri v. Seidner Enterprises. Although these cases provide support as to premises liability and pedestrian-versus-auto-accident liability, they fail to show why Defendant FOE owed a duty to Plaintiff. Moreover, Plaintiff argues that the demurrer procedurally fails because it does not specify the pleading’s uncertainties with reference to page and line numbers. (Opp. p.3.)

 

However, Defendant argues that the allegations fail to provide the specific factual basis to support a duty of care or a direct causal link to the injury sustained because they are too general. (Reply p.2.) Specifically, Defendant argues that the distracted driving allegations and failure to mitigate safety hazards are too general and do not “detail how Defendant FOE’s specific actions or inactions caused Plaintiff’s injuries.” (Reply p.3.) Moreover, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s argument about the deficiency in the demurrer is unfounded because it included page numbers and line numbers. The Court agrees. 

 

Defendant FOE also argues that FOE had a defined and limited role in the carnival’s organization which would prevent them from owing any duty to manage traffic conditions. (Reply p.2.) However, it is improper for the Court to dispute the facts at this stage and this argument will be disregarded. 

 

The reasoning in Hegyes v. Unijian Enterprises Inc. is instructive. The court in Hegyes found plaintiff’s complaint to be deficient because he failed to allege facts in his complaint showing that defendant owed a duty and instead relied on causation. (Hegyes v. Unjian Enters. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 1103, 1111.) The court reasoned that the “existence or nonexistence of ‘duty’ is the initial obstacle which must be mastered before any liability for negligence is legally permissible.” (Id.) Significantly, the court stated that “[a] complaint which lacks allegations of fact to show that a legal duty of care was owed is fatally defective.” (citation omitted.) (Id.) The court further reasoned that the “existence of such a duty is properly challenged by demurrer and is a question of law for the court.” (citation omitted.) (Id.) With respect to FOE, the allegations do not suggest the existence of a legal duty. Absent allegations of fact from which it can be inferred that FOE owed a legal duty of care to plaintiff and breached that duty, the complaint fails to state a cause of action for negligence.

 

Therefore, these alleged facts are insufficient to state a cause of action for negligence because Plaintiff does not provide facts as to what duty Defendant FOE owed. Moreover, the complaint is uncertain because it is ambiguous as to the duty owed by Defendant FOE.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to the sole cause of action.

 

Leave to Amend

 

“Generally, it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) “Plaintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” (Id.)

 

Here, Defendant argues that leave should not be granted because there is no possibility of curing a defective complaint by amendment because of its contractual obligation. (Reply p.4.) In support, Defendant cites Bily v. Arthur Young & Co. However, in Bily the issue arose from a financial auditor’s negligent preparation of an audit which led to economic injury and did not discuss any pedestrian-auto-accident liability. (Bily v. Arthur Young & Co. (1992) 3 Cal.4th 370, 398 [11 Cal.Rptr.2d 51, 834 P.2d 745].)

 

Plaintiff could amend his complaint to specify what duty Defendant FOE owed. Moreover, Plaintiff has not made any amendments to his Complaint.


As such leave to amend will be granted.  

Accordingly, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to the sole cause of action.
